# Style Guide: Jack Vance — *T'sais* (The Dying Earth)

> **Source sample:** "T'sais," Jack Vance, ~2,400 words (excerpt, OCR-converted)
> **Genre context:** Dying-earth science-fantasy; picaresque dark adventure
> **Narrative POV:** Third-person limited (close to T'sais), with omniscient asides for world-history

---

## 1. Sentence Structure

### 1.1 Length

- **Average sentence length:** ~13 words
- **Range:** 1–55 words
- **Standard deviation:** ~10 words
- **Distribution shape:** Strongly bimodal. Dense cluster of short declarative punches (3–8 words) and a secondary cluster of medium narrative sentences (15–25 words), with occasional long sweeps for world-history or environmental set-pieces. The short end is the signature: Vance fires off fragments and terse statements like a drummer hitting accents between longer phrases.

### 1.2 Sentence Types (ratio)

- **Simple (single independent clause):** 45%
- **Compound (coordinated independent clauses):** 25%
- **Complex (independent + dependent clause):** 20%
- **Compound-complex:** 10%

The high simple-sentence ratio is deceptive — Vance achieves complexity not within sentences but *between* them, using rapid juxtaposition of short declarations to build composite meaning.

### 1.3 Opening Patterns

- **Subject-first (SVO):** 55%
- **Prepositional/adverbial opener:** 12%
- **Participial phrase opener:** 5%
- **Dependent clause opener:** 5%
- **Inverted/verb-first:** 3%
- **Conjunction opener (And, But, For, etc.):** 12%
- **Other (fragments, interjections, appositives):** 8%

Conjunction openers deserve special note: Vance frequently begins with "And" to chain action forward at pace, giving sequences a breathless, tumbling quality ("And in spite of T'sais' horrified outcries, they dragged her across the moor…").

### 1.4 Clause Stacking

- **Typical clause count per sentence:** 1–3
- **Stacking method:** Predominantly right-branching coordinate chains joined by commas and "and." Subordination is shallow — rarely more than one dependent clause before resolution. When Vance does stack deeply, it is through *appositional* layering (parenthetical asides, inserted descriptions) rather than syntactic nesting.
- **Maximum comfortable depth before resolution:** 3 clauses — beyond this, Vance breaks to a new sentence rather than embedding further.

## 2. Paragraph Structure

### 2.1 Length

- **Average paragraph length:** 3–4 sentences / ~50 words
- **Range:** 1–12 sentences
- **Distribution shape:** Heavily front-loaded toward short paragraphs (1–3 sentences), with occasional long set-piece paragraphs for landscape description or historical digression. Single-sentence paragraphs are common and deliberate.

### 2.2 Internal Pattern

- **Typical opening move:** Action or perception — a character does something or notices something. Rarely a topic sentence; Vance drops the reader into motion.
- **Typical closing move:** A sharp image, a terse emotional beat, or a pivot-verb launching the next paragraph. Final sentences tend to be shorter than mid-paragraph sentences ("And T'sais wept." / "The bleeding figure of Liane remained by the dying fire.").
- **Paragraph-level rhythm:** Build through accumulation of coordinated clauses, then snap shut with a short declarative. The shape is a rising wave that breaks on a blunt final stroke.

### 2.3 Transitions Between Paragraphs

- **Dominant method:** Direct continuation of action, or spatial/temporal shift via brief marker ("A few minutes later," "presently"). Associative leaps are rare — Vance prefers linear forward motion.
- **Frequency of hard breaks vs. linked flow:** 35% hard / 65% linked. Hard breaks occur at scene shifts or tonal pivots.

## 3. Word Choice

### 3.1 Register & Etymology

- **Latinate vs. Germanic ratio:** ~40/60 Latinate/Germanic. The base texture is blunt Germanic monosyllables (struck, flung, sprang, crept, howled), with Latinate and exotic vocabulary deployed for *effect* — ornament atop a plain scaffold.
- **Preferred register:** Mixed by function. Action uses plain, hard, Anglo-Saxon verbs. Description uses elevated or exotic vocabulary. Dialogue ranges from colloquial coarseness ("you stinking animals") to formal archaic ("I brook no annoyance").
- **Archaism frequency:** Occasional — not structural, but flavoring.
  - **Type:** Vocabulary choices (brook, sennight-adjacent coinages), formal dialogue constructions ("What means this?"), and deliberate stiffness in speech patterns. True archaism is light; Vance more often *invents* formality than borrows it from Middle English.

### 3.2 Specificity & Concreteness

- **Abstraction level:** Highly concrete and sensory. Abstract concepts (love, beauty, evil, wickedness) appear as *topics of dialogue or thought* but are never the texture of narration itself. Narration is built from physical particulars.
- **Noun specificity:** Very high. "Rapier" not "sword." "Brand" not "torch." "Mead" not "drink." "Pandy-suits" not "clothes." Vance almost never uses a generic noun when a specific one exists.
- **Verb specificity:** Very high and kinetic. "Strutted," "caroled," "ogled," "lurching," "stumbled," "sprang," "crouched," "panted." Verbs carry the energy; adverbs are largely unnecessary because the verb already encodes manner.

### 3.3 Syllable Profile

- **Average syllables per word:** ~1.4
- **Polysyllabic word frequency (3+ syllables):** ~8–10%
- **Placement tendency for polysyllabic words:** Sentence-middle or embedded in appositives/proper nouns. Polysyllabic words serve as rhythmic variation against the monosyllabic base, not as terminal weight. Invented proper nouns (Embelyon, Pandelume, Modavna, Golickan Kodek) carry much of the polysyllabic load.

### 3.4 Repetition & Variation

- **Deliberate repetition:** Frequent — a key device.
  - **Type:** Immediate echo for emphasis ("Dead! My wife is dead!"), anaphoric stacking in action sequences ("He caroled, he danced"), and key-word repetition across a passage to hammer theme ("love" repeated with escalating irony in the rogues' scene; "tighter, tighter, tighter" in the Golickan Kodek history).
- **Synonym variation:** Low to moderate. Vance reuses key words deliberately rather than reaching for synonyms. Characters are named by name, not varied with epithets.

### 3.5 Word Selection Method

When generating **new** words not drawn from the sample, select by:

1. **Etymology filter:** Default to short Germanic roots for action and narration. Deploy Latinate or exotic roots for proper nouns, descriptive color, and moments of elevation.
2. **Syllable target:** 1–2 syllables as baseline; polysyllabic words only when rhythmic variation is needed or for coined/exotic terms.
3. **Sensory channel:** Prioritize visual and kinesthetic vocabulary — things seen and things *done to bodies.*
4. **Formality gate:** Would this word appear in a 1950s adventure magazine written by someone who also read Montaigne? If it feels too clinical, too modern-casual, or too academic, reject it.
5. **Position rule:** Place rarer/heavier words mid-sentence as ornamental variation. End sentences on plain, blunt words for percussive closure.

## 4. Syntax Patterns

### 4.1 Modifier Placement

- **Adjective position:** Predominantly pre-noun. Occasional post-noun for emphasis or archaic flavor ("eyes glazing," "woman of the night"). Stacked pre-noun clusters are rare — typically one adjective per noun, two at most ("luminous hazel eyes," "gay green cloak").
- **Typical adjective density:** 0.5–1 per noun phrase. Vance is selective — adjectives are deployed for precise sensory hits, not atmospheric accumulation.
- **Adverb position:** Rare. When present, typically sentence-initial as a scene-setting marker ("Slowly, with many backward glances…") or mid-clause for tempo.
- **Adverb frequency:** Near-absent in action narration. Verb specificity replaces adverbs almost entirely.

### 4.2 Inversion & Unusual Word Order

- **Frequency:** Occasional — signature but not dominant.
- **Type:** Object-fronting for emphasis ("Unpleasant; we must ignore them."), predicate-adjective fronting, and inverted constructions in dialogue ("What means this?").
- **Trigger context:** Dialogue and moments of heightened formality or irony. Narration stays mostly SVO.

### 4.3 Apposition & Interjection

- **Appositives:** Frequent — a core Vancean device.
  - **Typical position:** Mid-sentence between commas, often as parenthetical world-building: "Modavna Moor, a place of history, a tract which had borne the tread of many feet." Also sentence-final for elaboration: "the prowling erbs (creatures mixed of beasts, man and demon)."
- **Parenthetical interjections:** Moderate.
  - **Punctuation used:** Commas for light insertions; parentheses for encyclopedic asides about creatures or history; dashes for dramatic interruption.

### 4.4 Parallelism

- **Frequency:** Structural — one of the most recognizable Vance signatures.
- **Typical form:** Tricolon and paired clauses. Extended coordinate lists joined by commas. Balanced antithesis in dialogue ("It is sharp and hard. You must lay it away. I am a kind man, but I brook no annoyance.").
- **Typical list length when present:** 3 items (tricolon dominates). Lists of 4+ appear in world-building catalogs (creatures, places).

## 5. Figurative Language

### 5.1 Density

- **Figures per paragraph (average):** 0.5–0.8
- **Figures per page (average):** 4–6

Vance is *economical* with figures. Most paragraphs contain none. When a figure appears, it is brief and precise, not extended.

### 5.2 Type Preference (ratio)

- **Simile:** 50%
- **Metaphor:** 15%
- **Personification:** 20%
- **Synecdoche/metonymy:** 10%
- **Other (transferred epithet, zeugma):** 5%

The high personification count comes largely from the rapier, which is treated as a semi-autonomous agent throughout.

### 5.3 Source Domains

1. **Animals / predation** — "like a plumed cock," "fiend-cat," "fought like bulls"
2. **Light / color / fire** — "hundred-colored fire," "luminous hazel eyes," "blaze which glowed deep"
3. **Landscape / geology** — "bushes grew like blots," "rocky crags," spatial metaphors
4. **Body / anatomy** — physical descriptions grounded in body-level detail
5. **Architecture / ruin** — "tumbled ruins," stone structures, ancient roads

### 5.4 Construction Pattern

- **Typical simile form:** Short "like X" — rarely elaborated beyond a phrase ("strutted toward her like a plumed cock," "lofted into the air like a bird").
- **Metaphor integration:** Embedded in verb choice rather than stated. Vance's metaphors hide in his verbs — the rapier "sprang up by itself," "lunged itself," "wished to stab." The reader absorbs metaphor through action, not through declarative comparison.
- **Length:** Single phrase. Extended conceits are absent. Vance treats figures as spice, not architecture.

## 6. Punctuation

### 6.1 Comma Behavior

- **Density:** Moderate — approximately 1.5–2 per sentence.
- **Oxford comma:** Yes (consistent in lists).
- **Comma splices:** Deliberate stylistic choice, used for acceleration in action ("He caroled, he danced." / "lurching, falling, cursing").

### 6.2 Semicolons

- **Frequency:** Moderate.
- **Primary use:** Joining related independent clauses for pacing control — the semicolon functions as a pause shorter than a period but stronger than a comma, keeping momentum while acknowledging a conceptual break ("Unpleasant; we must ignore them.").

### 6.3 Dashes

- **Type preferred:** Em dash.
- **Frequency:** Moderate.
- **Primary use:** Dramatic interruption in dialogue and narration, and framing parenthetical action ("And in sudden doubt they relaxed their holds. But still she could not reach her rapier."). Also used for terminal dramatic emphasis — cutting off a thought at its peak.

### 6.4 Colons

- **Frequency:** Rare.
- **Primary use:** Introducing a quoted speech or a dramatic reveal after a buildup.

### 6.5 Other Notable Punctuation

- **Ellipses:** Rare — used for trailing speech ("Release me—" functions more as dash-interrupt).
- **Exclamation marks:** Frequent *in dialogue only.* Narration never uses them. Dialogue is exclamatory, theatrical, overwrought — the contrast with flat narration is a key Vance signature.
- **Question marks in narration:** Rare but present — used for T'sais' internal questions ("Was not killing wicked?"), reflecting her naïve epistemology.

## 7. Rhythm & Cadence

### 7.1 Stress Patterns

- **Dominant tendency:** Iambic-to-trochaic base, with frequent spondaic clusters in action sequences (hard monosyllabic verbs stacked: "sprang back," "struck a light," "fought like bulls").
- **Regularity:** Loosely rhythmic — Vance writes prose that *scans* without being metrically regular. The ear catches patterns, but they shift before becoming predictable.

### 7.2 Sentence-Level Pacing

- **Acceleration technique:** Shortening sentences to fragments; comma-spliced verb chains; asyndeton (dropping conjunctions: "lurching, falling, cursing"); stacking simple SVO declarations.
- **Deceleration technique:** Appositional expansion; parenthetical world-history asides; polysyllabic proper nouns; dependent-clause openers.
- **Typical pacing shape within a paragraph:** Fast-slow-fast. Open with a short action sentence, expand briefly with description or context, close with another short punch. Alternatively: steady build through coordinate clauses, then a short snap at the end.

### 7.3 Sound Devices

- **Alliteration:** Occasional and unforced — appears when it serves rhythm rather than as a deliberate ornamental strategy ("flung her fists," "prone bodies," "sighing forest courses").
- **Assonance/consonance:** Occasional — Vance is sensitive to vowel music in descriptive passages ("opalescent dimming," "illimitable dark expanse") but does not foreground it.
- **Onomatopoeia:** Rare — verbs do the sonic work instead ("gasped," "snarled," "howled").

## 8. Dialogue

### 8.1 Tag Behavior

- **Tag frequency:** Most lines tagged.
- **Preferred tag verb:** Varied and expressive — "gasped," "snarled," "cried," "screamed," "panted," "fretted," "crowed." Plain "said" is nearly absent. Tags carry emotional and physical information; they function as micro-action beats.
- **Tag position:** Post-speech dominant, occasionally mid-speech for interruption.

### 8.2 Speech Patterns

- **Average speech-turn length:** 15–25 words / 1–2 sentences. Longer speeches are rare and reserved for villains monologuing or rogues bantering.
- **Interruption/trailing off:** Moderate — dashes interrupt speech at dramatic moments.
- **Dialect or idiolect markers:** Heavy but *invented* rather than mimetic. Each character has a distinct verbal register:
  - **Liane:** Affected, preening, self-regarding ("I am the perfect man; so you shall see.")
  - **T'sais:** Direct, naïve, syntactically simple ("I am no witch! Release me, you stinking animals!")
  - **Rogues:** Coarse, bantering, rhetorical-question heavy ("Hey, lads?")
  - **Victims:** Hysterical, fragmented, curse-laden.

### 8.3 Dialogue-to-Narration Ratio

- **Approximate ratio:** 35% dialogue / 65% narration within dialogue-containing passages. Narration dominates overall; dialogue comes in concentrated bursts at dramatic peaks, then recedes.

## 9. Description Method

### 9.1 Sensory Distribution

- **Visual:** 60%
- **Auditory:** 15%
- **Tactile/kinesthetic:** 18%
- **Olfactory:** 5%
- **Gustatory:** 2%

Visual dominates, but *kinesthetic* is the secret engine — Vance describes the world in terms of physical contact, weight, resistance, and bodily movement. You feel the moor-wind, the mud, the buffet, the cold.

### 9.2 Camera Behavior

- **Default distance:** Medium — room-scale or clearing-scale. Close enough to see faces and postures, far enough to frame the scene.
- **Movement pattern:** Zoom in from medium establishing shot to close physical detail, then pull back. In the moor passage, the camera starts wide (landscape), narrows to the road, then snaps to close-up (the ambush).
- **Focal priority:** Action first, then environment as needed. Characters are placed in space through what they *do*, not through pre-positioned description. Environment is sketched with a few sharp strokes — never catalogued.

### 9.3 Description Density

- **Lines of pure description before action/dialogue resumes:** 2–4 sentences on average. The Golickan Kodek history passage (~5 sentences of pure description) is the exception — a set-piece digression deliberately held long for grandeur, then snapped back to T'sais with a terse transition.
- **Integration method:** Single-sentence brushstrokes woven into action. Pure description blocks are rare and reserved for establishing new locations. The signature method is the *appositional insert* — a descriptive phrase dropped into an action sentence as a comma-bounded aside: "three bearded, scarred rogues of the moor; they wore gray pandy-suits, stained and fouled by mud and filth."

## 10. Vance-Specific Signatures

> *Patterns not captured by generic template categories.*

### 10.1 Encyclopedic Aside

Vance interrupts forward action with parenthetical catalogs of world-detail, delivered in a dry, authoritative, almost academic register: "(creatures mixed of beasts, man and demon), the gids, who leapt twenty feet across the turf and clasped themselves to their victims." These asides feel like footnotes from a dying-earth naturalist. They are syntactically parenthetical (commas, dashes, or actual parentheses) and tonally *detached* from the surrounding narrative urgency.

### 10.2 Ironic Distance

The narrator maintains a cool, faintly amused distance from suffering and chaos. Violence is described with precise physical detail but without emotional amplification. The effect is sardonic rather than grim — the narrator observes horror the way a naturalist observes predation. This distance is achieved through:
- Flat, declarative summary of extreme events ("The woman spoke not. Her eyes were wide, and stared glassily upwards.")
- Juxtaposition of violence with vanity or absurdity (Liane preening immediately after torture)
- Understated transitions from horror to travelogue

### 10.3 The Animate Object

Vance frequently grants agency to objects — most notably the rapier, which "sprang up by itself," "lunged itself," and "wished to stab." This is not metaphor; in-world, the rapier may genuinely be semi-sentient. But the *syntactic* effect is the same regardless: objects act as subjects, and characters are dragged behind them. This produces a distinctive sense of human beings caught in the machinery of a world that acts upon them.

### 10.4 Theatrical Dialogue Register

Characters speak in a register that is slightly *too* formal, *too* self-aware, *too* performed for the situation — as though everyone in the Dying Earth is simultaneously living their life and narrating it. Liane does not simply threaten; he *orates.* The rogues do not simply menace; they *banter about* menacing. This theatrical quality is achieved through:
- Complete, grammatical sentences even under duress
- Rhetorical questions and call-and-response patterns ("Hey, lads?")
- Self-referential commentary ("I am in perspiration with this work")

## Usage Notes

When writing new prose using this guide:

1. **Sentence construction:** Default to short, blunt SVO sentences. Let compound sentences emerge from chained actions joined by "and" or commas. Keep subordination shallow. Fire off single-sentence paragraphs without hesitation.
2. **Word selection:** Choose the most specific concrete noun available. Choose the most kinetic verb available. Avoid adverbs — if you need one, your verb is wrong. Run candidate words through the filter in §3.5: Germanic roots for action, Latinate/exotic for color and naming.
3. **Paragraph shaping:** Short paragraphs (2–4 sentences) are the norm. Build through accumulated action, close with a blunt declarative. Reserve longer paragraphs for landscape description or historical aside.
4. **Figurative language:** Use sparingly — one figure per two paragraphs at most. Keep similes to a single "like X" phrase. Hide metaphors inside verb choices. Never extend a figure beyond one sentence.
5. **Punctuation:** Use semicolons to join related actions at pace. Use comma splices deliberately for acceleration. Reserve exclamation marks for dialogue only. Use em dashes for dramatic interruption.
6. **Rhythm check:** Read aloud. Sentences should land with monosyllabic weight at their ends. If a sentence trails off on a soft polysyllabic word, restructure it. The overall rhythm should feel like a hand drum: steady pulse with sharp accents.
7. **Dialogue:** Give every character a distinct verbal tic or register. Use expressive dialogue tags — never plain "said" more than once per page. Keep speeches short. Let characters be theatrical and self-aware.
8. **Description:** Sketch environments in 2–3 brushstrokes, not panoramic detail. Integrate description into action. Use appositional inserts for world-building color. Maintain ironic narrative distance from violence and suffering.
9. **Encyclopedic asides:** When introducing creatures, places, or historical events, drop briefly into a dry, authoritative, almost clinical tone — as though quoting from an in-world reference text — then snap back to the action without transition.
