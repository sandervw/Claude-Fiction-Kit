# Style Guide: Jack Vance — *T'sais* (The Dying Earth)

> **Source:** Merged composite guide drawn from analyses of "T'sais," Jack Vance (~6,400 words of source text)
> **Genre context:** Dying-earth science-fantasy; mannered picaresque adventure with melancholic undertone
> **Narrative POV:** Third-person limited anchored to T'sais, with omniscient flexibility for world-history and catalog passages; emotional states inferred from visible signs

---

## 1. Sentence Structure

### 1.1 Length

- **Average sentence length:** 15 words
- **Range:** 2–56 words
- **Standard deviation:** ~10 words
- **Distribution shape:** Bimodal — short declarative punches (2–10 words) against a middle band (10–20 words), with a long tail of accumulative sentences (30+ words). Typical texture: short-short-short-long.

### 1.2 Sentence Types (ratio)

- **Simple (single independent clause):** 39%
- **Compound (coordinated independent clauses):** 26%
- **Complex (independent + dependent clause):** 24%
- **Compound-complex:** 11%

The high simple-sentence ratio is deceptive — Vance achieves complexity *between* sentences, not within them.

### 1.3 Opening Patterns

- **Subject-first (SVO):** 56% — dominant mode, direct and propulsive
- **Conjunction opener (And, But, For):** 12% — "And" dominates, lending a chronicle-like additive cadence
- **Prepositional/adverbial opener:** 12% — spatial or temporal framing
- **Dependent clause opener:** 6%
- **Participial phrase opener:** 4% — simultaneous action
- **Inverted/verb-first:** 4% — dramatic emphasis or archaic coloring
- **Other (fragments, demonstratives, temporal markers):** 6%

### 1.4 Clause Stacking

- **Typical clause count per sentence:** 1–3
- **Stacking method:** Right-branching coordinate chains joined by commas, semicolons, or "and." Clauses extend rightward rather than nesting. Subordination is shallow; deep stacking achieved through *appositional* layering rather than syntactic embedding.
- **Maximum comfortable depth before resolution:** 3–4 clauses. Sentences grow *wide* rather than *deep*.

## 2. Paragraph Structure

### 2.1 Length

- **Average paragraph length:** 3–4 sentences / ~43 words
- **Range:** 1–11 sentences
- **Distribution shape:** Heavily front-loaded toward short paragraphs (1–3 sentences). Single-sentence paragraphs common for action beats and dialogue. Narration clusters at 2–4 sentences. Occasional set-piece catalog paragraphs run longer.

### 2.2 Internal Pattern

- **Typical opening move:** Action or concrete perception — direct continuation of prior motion. Rarely a topic sentence; the paragraph drops mid-observation.
- **Typical closing move:** A striking terminal image or clipped declarative. The most vivid element comes last; final sentences tend to be the paragraph's shortest.
- **Paragraph-level rhythm:** Accumulative build followed by emphatic close. Medium sentences stack detail, then the paragraph snaps shut with a short, sharp sentence. In catalog paragraphs, accumulation builds until a final sentence brakes the motion.

### 2.3 Transitions Between Paragraphs

- **Dominant method:** Direct continuation of action or dialogue; spatial/temporal shift via brief marker ("presently," "one morning," "dawn came") for scene shifts.
- **Frequency of hard breaks vs. linked flow:** 34% hard / 66% linked.

## 3. Word Choice

### 3.1 Register & Etymology

- **Latinate vs. Germanic ratio:** ~40/60 Germanic-dominant. Germanic monosyllables carry the load for action and physical description ("struck," "flung," "snatched"); Latinate vocabulary deployed selectively for atmosphere and the numinous ("enigmatic," "insensible," "demoniac").
- **Preferred register:** Elevated-precise — a plain Germanic chassis with selective Latinate ornamentation. Formal enough to feel literary, plain enough to feel effortless.
- **Archaism frequency:** Occasional — flavoring, not structural
  - **Type:** Vocabulary ("rapier," "doffed," "bereft," "manse," "unguent"), lightly archaic phrasing and syntax (inverted dialogue constructions, adverbial phrases like "the while," "hence"). Pronouns and verb conjugations remain modern — the effect is *elevated* rather than *antique*. Vance more often *invents* formality than borrows it.

### 3.2 Specificity & Concreteness

- **Abstraction level:** Highly concrete and sensory. Abstract concepts appear in dialogue or sorcerous thought but never as the texture of narration.
- **Noun specificity:** Maximum — "rapier" not "sword," "crags" not "rocks," "pelgrane" not "creature." Vance selects the most precise noun available, inventing specific nouns for invented things.
- **Verb specificity:** Very high and kinetic — "lurched," "strode," "coppered," "pranced," "clambered," "snatched." Verbs encode manner and sensation directly; adverbs are largely unnecessary.

### 3.3 Syllable Profile

- **Average syllables per word:** ~1.4
- **Polysyllabic word frequency (3+ syllables):** ~9%
- **Placement tendency for polysyllabic words:** Clause-end or sentence-end for rhythmic weight against the monosyllabic base. Polysyllables are rare enough that each one registers. Invented proper nouns carry much of the polysyllabic load.

### 3.4 Repetition & Variation

- **Deliberate repetition:** Occasional, but structural when present
  - **Type:** Key-word echo for thematic anchoring ("strange soil… strange air… strange and new"), anaphoric "And" in catalog passages, verbatim repetition of ritual phrases. Not decorative — repetition serves accumulation or motif.
- **Synonym variation:** Low-to-moderate. Vance reuses character names and key nouns rather than hunting for synonyms, but varies verb choices freely.

### 3.5 Word Selection Method

When generating **new** words not drawn from the sample, select by:

1. **Etymology filter:** Default to short Germanic roots for action and physical description. Deploy Latinate or exotic roots for atmosphere, abstraction, and the numinous.
2. **Syllable target:** Favor monosyllables and disyllables (~91%). Reserve trisyllabic+ words for rhythmic weight at clause-end, never more than one per sentence.
3. **Sensory channel:** Prioritize visual and kinesthetic vocabulary. The world is seen and felt more than heard or smelled.
4. **Formality gate:** Would this word appear in a 1950s literary adventure-fantasy for an educated audience? If it sounds like journalism, modern-casual prose, or academic jargon, reject it.
5. **Position rule:** Place heavier or rarer words at clause-end or sentence-end. Keep sentence openings blunt and Germanic. End sentences on concrete, short words.

## 4. Syntax Patterns

### 4.1 Modifier Placement

- **Adjective position:** Predominantly pre-noun; stacked pairs common ("dark trees," "grave blue eyes") but clusters of three+ are rare. Occasional post-noun placement for archaic flavor or emphasis.
- **Typical adjective density:** 1–2 per noun phrase. Many noun phrases pass unmodified — Vance trusts his nouns. One exact modifier over several vague ones.
- **Adverb position:** Pre-verb, sentence-initial, or mid-clause when used; never stacked. Usually embedded in the verb's own specificity instead.
- **Adverb frequency:** Sparing — manner encoded in verb choice. Adverbs appear mainly for temporal pacing ("presently"). Perhaps 1 per 5–6 sentences.

### 4.2 Inversion & Unusual Word Order

- **Frequency:** Occasional — a recognizable signature flavor element
- **Type:** Verb-subject inversion in dialogue tags ("said T'sais"); object-fronting for emphasis; predicate-adjective fronting ("alien was it to her mind"); scenic reveals with inversion ("Ahead shone a glimmering sea").
- **Trigger context:** Dialogue tags (almost always inverted), scenic reveals, moments of wonder or horror, elevated passages. Inversion functions as a spotlight.

### 4.3 Apposition & Interjection

- **Appositives:** Frequent — a core Vancean device for identification, recharacterization, and world-building detail.
  - **Typical position:** Mid-sentence between commas, or post-noun after an em dash, sometimes stacking in cascade. Can extend into mini-catalogs in set-piece passages.
- **Parenthetical interjections:** Rare — em dashes for dramatic insertions; commas for lighter qualifications; parentheses reserved for encyclopedic asides only.

### 4.4 Parallelism

- **Frequency:** Structural — one of the most recognizable Vance markers, especially prominent in action, description, and catalog passages.
- **Typical form:** Tricolon constructions, anaphoric catalog lists ("And one… And another…"), paired clauses, serial verb chains, balanced antithesis in dialogue, polysyndetic coordinate lists.
- **Typical list length when present:** 3 items (tricolon is the default). Catalogs may extend to 6–8 items for world-building inventory.

## 5. Figurative Language

### 5.1 Density

- **Figures per paragraph (average):** ~0.5
- **Figures per page (average):** 3–5

Vance is economical with figures. The prose achieves atmosphere through precise literal description rather than metaphorical elaboration. Many paragraphs contain none; figures land hard because of their rarity.

### 5.2 Type Preference (ratio)

- **Simile:** 38% — the most overt mode, typically brief "like X" constructions
- **Metaphor (embedded in verb/noun choice):** 27% — the dominant *functional* mode
- **Personification:** 22% — applied to landscape, light, objects
- **Synecdoche/metonymy:** 10% — part for whole, especially light and color standing in for mood
- **Other (transferred epithet, synesthesia, compressed epithet):** 3%

### 5.3 Source Domains

1. **Light, color, and fire** — illumination as emotional signal; sun, copper, refracted hues, flame
2. **Geology, architecture, and ruin** — crags, columns, spires, dust; the mineral world
3. **Body, anatomy, and sensation** — eyes, face, skin; tactile experience; decay
4. **Animals, predation, and the organic** — character rendered through animal comparison; botanical
5. **Sleep, death, water, and weather** — enchantment as living death; dissolution; atmospheric depth

### 5.4 Construction Pattern

- **Typical simile form:** Brief "like X" or "as X" — compact, 2–5 words. One vehicle, no elaboration. The comparison arrives, does its work, and withdraws.
- **Metaphor integration:** Embedded in verb and noun choice rather than stated as formal comparison. The verb carries the metaphoric freight ("the sun ballooned upward"; "coppered her skin"; "rasped her nerves"). This is the dominant figurative mode.
- **Length:** Single phrase or single verb. Extended conceits are absent. Figures flash and vanish.

## 6. Punctuation

### 6.1 Comma Behavior

- **Density:** Moderate — approximately 2 per sentence on average, rising to 3–4 in longer constructions
- **Oxford comma:** Generally consistent in lists
- **Comma splices:** Deliberate stylistic choice for compressed parallel action and serial verb chains. The splice is an acceleration device.

### 6.2 Semicolons

- **Frequency:** Moderate — roughly 1 per 10 sentences on average; heavier in catalog and expository passages, sparser in dialogue scenes
- **Primary use:** Joining parallel independent clauses; separating items in complex lists. In set-piece descriptions, a load-bearing punctuation mark.

### 6.3 Dashes

- **Type preferred:** Em dash
- **Frequency:** Moderate, varying by passage type
- **Primary use:** Appositional insertion, dramatic interruption or reveal, compressed identifying phrases, and interrupted speech. Can be paired to frame a parenthetical.

### 6.4 Colons

- **Frequency:** Rare
- **Primary use:** Introducing a dramatic revelation, pronouncement, or formal elaboration.

### 6.5 Other Notable Punctuation

- **Ellipses:** Rare — trailing speech more often indicated by em dash. When present, Vance uses spaced dots (. . .).
- **Exclamation marks:** Dialogue only, reserved for genuine alarm or theatrical intensity. Absent in narration. The contrast between exclamatory dialogue and flat narration is a key signature.
- **Question marks in narration:** Rare — used for free indirect discourse reflecting T'sais' internal questioning ("Was this beautiful?"), or occasional rhetorical/dramatic use.

## 7. Rhythm & Cadence

### 7.1 Stress Patterns

- **Dominant tendency:** Loosely iambic flow with trochaic interruptions at sentence openings and spondaic clusters for gravity ("sprang back," "black dust," "dim red"). Dactylic variation in description. The underlying pulse is da-DUM but breaks freely.
- **Regularity:** Loosely rhythmic — enough regularity to feel controlled, enough variation to avoid sing-song. Shaped by ear, not metrical enough to scan as verse, but controlled enough that deviations feel deliberate.

### 7.2 Sentence-Level Pacing

- **Acceleration technique:** Shortening to bare declarative sentences; asyndeton; comma-spliced compound predicates; dropping subordination; stacking dialogue exchanges without tags.
- **Deceleration technique:** Extending with coordinate "and" chains; polysyndeton; embedding appositives between dashes or commas; cataloging details in parallel phrases; trailing subordinate or prepositional chains.
- **Typical pacing shape within a paragraph:** Steady build through accumulated detail, then snap shut with a blunt final sentence. The signature is controlled build followed by percussive close.

### 7.3 Sound Devices

- **Alliteration:** Occasional and unforced — background texture supporting rhythm without calling attention to itself. Paired adjectives and proper nouns are primary vehicles.
- **Assonance/consonance:** Occasional — woven into key phrases and proper nouns, sensitive to vowel music in description but never foregrounded.
- **Onomatopoeia:** Rare — verbs do the sonic work ("gasped," "thudded," "rasping"). Creature sounds when present.

## 8. Dialogue

### 8.1 Tag Behavior

- **Tag frequency:** Most lines tagged, either with verb or action beat. Tagless exchanges for clear two-speaker contexts.
- **Preferred tag verb:** "Said" dominates (~60%), with "asked" for questions. Expressive alternatives earned by context ("gasped," "snarled," "mused," "chuckled") — Vance deploys vivid tags more in set-piece scenes and "said" more in rapid exchanges. No purely decorative tags.
- **Tag position:** Post-speech dominant, with frequent verb-subject inversion ("said T'sais," "cried Pandelume"). Mid-speech tags for longer turns. Pre-speech tags are rare.

### 8.2 Speech Patterns

- **Average speech-turn length:** 15–20 words / 1–2 sentences for exchanges; up to 100+ words for expository monologue or legend-telling. Asymmetry between mentor-orator and student-questioner is characteristic.
- **Interruption/trailing off:** Rare — marked by em dash when present. Characters tend to speak in complete, controlled utterances.
- **Dialect or idiolect markers:** Heavy but *invented* rather than mimetic. Each speaker has a distinct verbal register achieved through rhythm, attitude, and word selection — not spelling or phonetic distortion. Authority figures use longer, more complex constructions with weary formality. Naïve characters use short declaratives. Villains are theatrical and self-aware.

### 8.3 Dialogue-to-Narration Ratio

- **Approximate ratio:** 40% dialogue / 60% narration within dialogue-containing passages. The ratio shifts by scene: some are dialogue-driven (65%+), others narration-dominated (85%+). Dialogue arrives in concentrated bursts, then recedes.

## 9. Description Method

### 9.1 Sensory Distribution

- **Visual:** 68% — color, light, shape, distance dominate massively. Vance's prose is a painter's prose.
- **Auditory:** 13% — specific sounds tied to concrete sources (bells, voices, metal)
- **Tactile/kinesthetic:** 14% — the secret engine; the world described through physical contact, weight, resistance, movement
- **Olfactory:** 3% — minimal; implied rather than foregrounded
- **Gustatory:** 2% — essentially absent

### 9.2 Camera Behavior

- **Default distance:** Medium — room-scale or clearing-scale. Landscape-scale for travel passages. Close enough to see faces and postures, far enough to frame the scene.
- **Movement pattern:** Follows the acting character or pans slowly across a scene. Close-ups for faces and key details. In catalog scenes, the camera inventories the space, pausing on each figure for one identifying detail. Action uses rapid cuts; scene-setting uses slow pans.
- **Focal priority:** Environment-first in establishing shots, character-first in dialogue and action. Once established, the environment fills in around movement.

### 9.3 Description Density

- **Lines of pure description before action/dialogue resumes:** 1–4 sentences on average; up to 5–8 in set-piece catalog passages.
- **Integration method:** Single-sentence brushstrokes woven into forward motion. Pure description blocks are rare and reserved for establishing new locations or catalog inventory. Once the scene is set, description is delivered through *appositional inserts* — descriptive phrases dropped into action sentences as comma-bounded or dash-bounded asides. Vance front-loads description, then thins it as action takes over.

## Usage Notes

When writing new prose using this guide:

1. **Sentence construction:** Alternate short punches (3–12 words) with medium compounds (15–25 words). Reserve long accumulative sentences (30+) for catalogs. Default to SVO openings (~56%); use conjunction openers ("And" especially) ~12%. Keep subordination shallow; favor right-branching coordination. Use single-sentence paragraphs freely. Target the ~39/26/24/11 type ratio.

2. **Word selection:** Run candidates through the five-step filter in §3.5. Germanic monosyllables are the backbone; Latinate polysyllables are color accents. Choose the most specific concrete noun and the most kinetic verb — if you need an adverb, your verb is wrong. Place polysyllabic words sparingly and at clause-end for rhythmic weight.

3. **Paragraph shaping:** Keep most paragraphs to 2–4 sentences. Use single-sentence paragraphs as rhythmic punctuation. Open with action or image, not topic sentences. Close on a concrete image or the shortest, sharpest sentence in the paragraph. Permit one extended catalog paragraph per major scene.

4. **Figurative language:** Roughly 1 figure per 2–3 paragraphs. Embed metaphor in verb choice rather than formal comparison. Similes use "like X" in 2–5 words. Draw from light, landscape, body, and decay. Never extend beyond one sentence.

5. **Parallelism & catalog:** Deploy tricolons for gravity. Use "And one… And another…" for crowd inventory, one detail per figure. The most audible Vance rhythm.

6. **Punctuation:** Commas for pacing and serial verb chains; periods for force. Semicolons for balanced parallel clauses. Em dashes for appositional insertion and dramatic reveals. Exclamation marks for sharp dialogue only. Spaced ellipses (. . .) for trailing implication.

7. **Dialogue:** Invert tags ("said T'sais"). Use "said"/"asked" for the majority; expressive tags only when they precisely describe the speech act. Lightly elevated register — formal enough for another era, natural enough to breathe. Keep speeches short (15–20 words). Differentiate characters by rhythm, attitude, and verbal register — not vocabulary distortion.

8. **Rhythm check:** Read aloud. Short sentences land like struck bells. Long ones accumulate mass through additive coordination. Listen for spondaic clusters; if the prose sounds too smooth, add one. Paragraphs should build to a culminating image, then snap shut. If a sentence trails off on a soft polysyllable, restructure it.

9. **Tone:** Cool precision underwritten by melancholic wonder. Detached, faintly sardonic clarity — the narrator observes with a lapidary eye, never editorializing or emoting. Violence rendered flatly; absurdity juxtaposed without comment. Never sentimental, never cynical. Late afternoon light: warm but fading.

10. **Encyclopedic asides:** When introducing creatures, places, or historical events, drop briefly into a dry, authoritative, almost clinical tone — as though quoting from an in-world reference text — then snap back to the action without transition.
