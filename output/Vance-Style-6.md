# Style Guide: Jack Vance — "T'sais" (The Dying Earth)

> **Source sample:** "T'sais," Jack Vance, ~1,720 words (excerpt, OCR-converted)
> **Genre context:** Dying-earth science-fantasy; picaresque dark fantasy
> **Narrative POV:** Third-person limited (anchored to T'sais), with omniscient excursions for catalog passages

---

## 1. Sentence Structure

### 1.1 Length

- **Average sentence length:** 20 words
- **Range:** 2–67 words
- **Standard deviation:** 13 words
- **Distribution shape:** Bimodal — frequent short punches (1–8 words, 17%) against a broad middle (9–20 words, 47%), with a long tail of sweeping accumulative sentences (36+ words, 8%). The effect is staccato bursts alternating with flowing panoramic passages.

### 1.2 Sentence Types (ratio)

- **Simple (single independent clause):** 25%
- **Compound (coordinated independent clauses):** 30%
- **Complex (independent + dependent clause):** 30%
- **Compound-complex:** 15%

### 1.3 Opening Patterns

- **Subject-first (SVO):** 56%
- **Conjunction opener (And, But, Nor):** 17% — "And" alone accounts for 14%, giving prose a chronicle/biblical cadence. This is the single most distinctive syntactic habit.
- **Prepositional/adverbial opener:** 10%
- **Participial phrase opener:** ~1%
- **Dependent clause opener:** ~5%
- **Inverted/verb-first:** ~2%
- **Other (demonstrative, existential, appositional):** ~9%

### 1.4 Clause Stacking

- **Typical clause count per sentence:** 1–4
- **Stacking method:** Right-branching coordinate chains joined by commas and "and." Catalog/list structures are common — Vance stacks appositional noun phrases rather than subordinating them. Nested embedding is rare; clauses extend rightward like beads on a string.
- **Maximum comfortable depth before resolution:** 4–5 clauses, but resolution often comes through a final image or detail rather than a grammatical closing.

---

## 2. Paragraph Structure

### 2.1 Length

- **Average paragraph length:** 2.6 sentences / 41 words
- **Range:** 1–10 sentences
- **Distribution shape:** Skewed short. Many single-sentence paragraphs used for dialogue beats or dramatic punctuation. Occasional long set-piece paragraphs (the Sabbath catalog, the demon inventory) run 8–10 sentences.

### 2.2 Internal Pattern

- **Typical opening move:** Direct continuation of action or a new visual focal point. Rarely a topic sentence — Vance opens with the next thing that happens or the next thing the viewpoint character sees.
- **Typical closing move:** A striking image, a sensory detail, or a clipped final clause that lands like a verdict. Paragraphs often end on the most vivid or grotesque element.
- **Paragraph-level rhythm:** Short opener, accumulative middle, emphatic close. Set-piece paragraphs build through catalog accretion — each clause adds a new figure or detail, escalating toward a culminating image.

### 2.3 Transitions Between Paragraphs

- **Dominant method:** Temporal progression ("Now," "presently," "suddenly") or spatial shift. Dialogue paragraphs transition through direct continuation (speech–response–speech).
- **Frequency of hard breaks vs. linked flow:** 40% hard / 60% linked. Hard breaks typically signal a new visual set-piece or a shift in scene focus.

## 3. Word Choice

### 3.1 Register & Etymology

- **Latinate vs. Germanic ratio:** 70/30 Germanic-dominant. The prose muscle is Anglo-Saxon — short, concrete, physical verbs and nouns. Latinate words appear selectively for atmosphere, exoticism, or formal gravity ("saturnalia," "communicants," "libidinous," "delirium," "dissolution").
- **Preferred register:** Mixed — a baseline of clean, direct prose elevated by precise archaic or exotic vocabulary deployed at intervals. Neither uniformly elevated nor colloquial; the register shifts with content.
- **Archaism frequency:** Occasional
  - **Type:** Vocabulary ("doffed," "sennight"-class words), occasional verb forms ("spake"-adjacent constructions), and syntax (inverted constructions in dialogue: "Are not the stars beautiful?"). Never so dense as to feel mannered.

### 3.2 Specificity & Concreteness

- **Abstraction level:** Highly concrete and sensory. Abstract concepts (beauty, love, horror) are named directly but always grounded by surrounding physical detail.
- **Noun specificity:** Very high. "Pelgrane," not "creature." "Hatchet beak," not "sharp beak." "Furze bush," not "bush." Vance names things precisely, often inventing specific nouns for invented things.
- **Verb specificity:** Very high. "Pranced," "shuffled," "reeled," "breasting," "clambering," "doffed," "spattered." Verbs carry sensory and kinetic information; generic movement verbs ("went," "moved," "came") appear only in subordinate positions.

### 3.3 Syllable Profile

- **Average syllables per word:** 1.44
- **Polysyllabic word frequency (3+ syllables):** 9%
- **Placement tendency for polysyllabic words:** End-of-clause or end-of-sentence for weight and rhythm ("saturnalia," "amphitheater," "exultation"). Polysyllabic words also appear as proper nouns (place and creature names) distributed throughout.

### 3.4 Repetition & Variation

- **Deliberate repetition:** Frequent
  - **Type:** Anaphoric "And" at sentence openings — the signature Vance device in catalogue passages. Also key-word echo ("danced" repeated for Javanne; "face" repeated in the demon catalog). Ritual/incantatory repetition in set-piece descriptions.
- **Synonym variation:** Moderate. Vance repeats key words deliberately for accumulative force, but varies descriptive language around them.

### 3.5 Word Selection Method

When generating **new** words not drawn from the sample, select by:

1. **Etymology filter:** Default to Germanic/Anglo-Saxon roots for verbs and physical nouns. Reserve Latinate vocabulary for naming abstract states, exotic/arcane concepts, or formal set-piece passages.
2. **Syllable target:** Favor monosyllables and disyllables (91% of vocabulary). Place trisyllabic or longer words at clause-end for rhythmic weight.
3. **Sensory channel:** Prioritize visual and kinesthetic vocabulary. Descriptions should feel *seen* and *felt* — color, shape, movement, texture.
4. **Formality gate:** Would this word appear in a 1940s–50s adventure pulp written by someone with a broad vocabulary and a taste for the exotic? If it sounds like a modern self-help book or a Victorian novel, discard it.
5. **Position rule:** Place heavier/rarer words at sentence-end or clause-end. Open sentences with plain, direct language.

## 4. Syntax Patterns

### 4.1 Modifier Placement

- **Adjective position:** Predominantly pre-noun. Post-noun adjectives reserved for emphasis or archaic flavor ("a sight grotesque and strange"). Stacked pre-noun clusters are common but short — typically 2 adjectives maximum ("great broad face," "hard leathern body").
- **Typical adjective density:** 1–2 per noun phrase. Key noun phrases get precise, compressed description; minor nouns pass unmodified.
- **Adverb position:** Sentence-initial or mid-clause. Never stacked.
- **Adverb frequency:** Sparing. Vance prefers to encode manner in verb choice rather than adverb attachment.

### 4.2 Inversion & Unusual Word Order

- **Frequency:** Occasional
- **Type:** Object-fronting for dramatic emphasis; predicate-adjective fronting in descriptive passages ("Foul face after face T'sais saw"). Verb-subject inversion in dialogue for archaic register ("Are not the stars beautiful?").
- **Trigger context:** Elevated or dramatic passages; formal dialogue.

### 4.3 Apposition & Interjection

- **Appositives:** Frequent — this is a core device. Vance identifies characters and creatures by stacking appositional phrases: "the Prince Datul Omaet of Cansaspara, the city of fallen pylons across the Melantine Gulf."
  - **Typical position:** Post-noun, mid-sentence between commas. Often extended into mini-catalogs.
- **Parenthetical interjections:** Rare
  - **Punctuation used:** Dashes

### 4.4 Parallelism

- **Frequency:** Structural — parallelism drives the catalog passages
- **Typical form:** Anaphoric catalog lists ("And one... And another... And these two... And the dreamy-eyed witch..."). Also paired and tricolon constructions in action sequences ("hacking, slashing, stabbing").
- **Typical list length when present:** 3–6 items. Catalogs can extend to 8+.

## 5. Figurative Language

### 5.1 Density

- **Figures per paragraph (average):** 0.5 — Vance is restrained. Figures appear at key moments rather than saturating the prose.
- **Figures per page (average):** 3–4

### 5.2 Type Preference (ratio)

- **Simile:** 65%
- **Metaphor:** 15%
- **Personification:** 10%
- **Synecdoche/metonymy:** 5%
- **Other (compressed metaphor-as-epithet):** 5%

### 5.3 Source Domains

1. **Machinery and objects** — "wings creaking like rusty hinges"; mechanical, tactile comparisons
2. **Animals and natural phenomena** — "like birds alighting"; "like spume"
3. **Botanical/organic** — "hair like a furze bush"; "like a stream"
4. **Architectural/geological** — amphitheater, crags, dais; the landscape as stage
5. **Fire and light** — fires, purple bolts, green glow, darting firelight

### 5.4 Construction Pattern

- **Typical simile form:** "like X" — compact, usually 2–5 words. Rarely extended.
- **Metaphor integration:** Embedded in verb and noun choice rather than stated. "Breasting over the crags like spume" — the metaphor lives in "breasting" as much as in "like spume."
- **Length:** Single phrase. Vance does not extend conceits; figures flash and vanish.

## 6. Punctuation

### 6.1 Comma Behavior

- **Density:** Moderate-heavy — approximately 1.7 per sentence
- **Oxford comma:** Inconsistent (likely absent in original; OCR may obscure)
- **Comma splices:** Rare but present as deliberate stylistic choice in rapid-action sequences

### 6.2 Semicolons

- **Frequency:** Moderate (7 in ~1,700 words)
- **Primary use:** Joining parallel independent clauses in catalog passages; separating items in complex lists

### 6.3 Dashes

- **Type preferred:** Em dash
- **Frequency:** Moderate-heavy (12 in ~1,700 words)
- **Primary use:** Appositional insertion and dramatic interruption. Often frames a compressed identifying phrase: "—gargoyle creatures, with wings creaking like rusty hinges." Also used for dramatic pause before a revelation.

### 6.4 Colons

- **Frequency:** Absent in sample
- **Primary use:** N/A

### 6.5 Other Notable Punctuation

- **Ellipses:** Rare (1 instance) — used for trailing speech, bitter irony ("He chuckled weakly, bitterly . . .")
- **Exclamation marks:** Rare (5 instances) — reserved for genuine alarm or exclamation, mostly in dialogue ("The pelgrane!")
- **Question marks in narration:** Absent; questions appear only in dialogue

## 7. Rhythm & Cadence

### 7.1 Stress Patterns

- **Dominant tendency:** Iambic flow in medium sentences; trochaic punch in short declaratives. The prose has a natural spoken cadence — not metrical, but clearly shaped by ear.
- **Regularity:** Loosely rhythmic. Vance writes for the sound of sentences without imposing meter.

### 7.2 Sentence-Level Pacing

- **Acceleration technique:** Shortening clauses; asyndeton in action ("hacking, slashing, stabbing"); fragment sentences; removing subordination.
- **Deceleration technique:** Polysyndeton (repeated "And"); stacking appositional phrases; extending catalog lists; embedding sensory detail.
- **Typical pacing shape within a paragraph:** Steady build to a vivid closing image. Set-piece paragraphs accelerate through accumulation — each new clause adds mass and speed until the final image brakes the motion.

### 7.3 Sound Devices

- **Alliteration:** Frequent — a consistent background texture. "Slender shadow," "stone steps," "small sparks," "Modavna Moor," "sad Sabbath." Rarely ostentatious; the alliteration supports rhythm without calling attention to itself.
- **Assonance/consonance:** Occasional. Most evident in proper nouns and invented names, which Vance shapes for phonetic texture ("Cansaspara," "Melantine Gulf," "Cobalt Mountain").
- **Onomatopoeia:** Occasional — "creaking," "flapping," "scrabbling." Used for creatures and physical action.

---

## 8. Dialogue

### 8.1 Tag Behavior

- **Tag frequency:** Most lines
- **Preferred tag verb:** Varied — "whispered," "sighed," "laughed harshly," "murmured," "inquired," "assured," "chuckled," "cried." "Said" appears but does not dominate. Vance uses tag verbs to do characterization work.
- **Tag position:** Post-speech dominant, occasionally mid-speech for longer utterances

### 8.2 Speech Patterns

- **Average speech-turn length:** 10–20 words / 1–2 sentences. Dialogue is compact. Characters speak in brief, pointed utterances.
- **Interruption/trailing off:** Rare — one trailing ellipsis instance
- **Dialect or idiolect markers:** Light. Elevated register in dialogue (inverted questions, formal vocabulary) marks the archaic/dying-earth setting. No phonetic dialect. Characters are distinguished by attitude and content rather than speech pattern.

### 8.3 Dialogue-to-Narration Ratio

- **Approximate ratio:** 15% dialogue / 85% narration within dialogue-containing passages. Narration dominates; dialogue punctuates rather than drives.

## 9. Description Method

### 9.1 Sensory Distribution

- **Visual:** 70%
- **Auditory:** 15%
- **Tactile/kinesthetic:** 10%
- **Olfactory:** 3%
- **Gustatory:** 2%

### 9.2 Camera Behavior

- **Default distance:** Medium — room-scale or clearing-scale, enough to frame a scene
- **Movement pattern:** Wide establishing shot, then rapid specific cuts to individual figures and grotesque details. In the Sabbath scene, the camera pans across the crowd in inventory fashion, pausing on each figure long enough for one identifying detail before moving on.
- **Focal priority:** Environment first (the amphitheater, the fires, the dais), then figures in aggregate, then individual figures in close-up. Characters emerge from setting, not the reverse.

### 9.3 Description Density

- **Lines of pure description before action/dialogue resumes:** 3–8 sentences in set-piece passages; 1–2 in transitional passages
- **Integration method:** Catalog set-pieces for major scenes — long paragraphs that inventory a space and its inhabitants through accumulated appositional detail. Between set-pieces, description is woven into action as single-sentence brushstrokes.

## Usage Notes

When writing new prose using this guide:

1. **Sentence construction:** Alternate short declarative sentences (2–10 words) with medium compound sentences (15–25 words). Reserve long accumulative sentences (35+ words) for catalog and set-piece descriptions. Open 14–17% of sentences with "And" to maintain the chronicle cadence.
2. **Word selection:** Run candidate words through the five-step filter in §3.5. Default to Germanic monosyllables for action; deploy Latinate polysyllables sparingly for atmosphere and exotic naming. Choose verbs that encode manner of movement — never "went" when "clambered," "pranced," or "reeled" is available.
3. **Paragraph shaping:** Keep paragraphs short (2–3 sentences) for action and dialogue. Permit one extended catalog paragraph (6–10 sentences) per major scene. Close paragraphs on a vivid image.
4. **Figurative language:** Use similes ("like X") at 1 per 2 paragraphs. Keep them compact (2–5 words). Draw from machinery, animal, and botanical domains. Embed secondary metaphor in verb choice rather than stating it.
5. **Punctuation:** Use em dashes for appositional interruption and dramatic framing. Allow semicolons in catalog passages to join parallel clauses. Keep exclamation marks for genuine alarm.
6. **Catalog technique:** When describing a crowd, gathering, or landscape full of figures, use the anaphoric "And one... And another... And these..." pattern, giving each figure exactly one identifying detail or compressed appositive before moving to the next.
7. **Rhythm check:** Read sentences aloud. Short sentences should land like a struck bell. Long sentences should accumulate mass and forward motion. The paragraph should feel like it *arrives* somewhere.
