# Style Guide: Jack Vance — *T'sais* (The Dying Earth)

> **Source sample:** "T'sais," Jack Vance, ~1,150 words (excerpt from cottage scene through moor walk)
> **Genre context:** Dying-earth science fantasy; planetary romance with melancholic undertone
> **Narrative POV:** Third-person limited, centered on T'sais but occasionally shifting to Etarr's perspective within scene

---

## 1. Sentence Structure

### 1.1 Length

- **Average sentence length:** 11 words (narration), ~18 words (dialogue turns)
- **Range:** 2–46 words
- **Standard deviation:** 9.0 words (narration)
- **Distribution shape:** Heavily skewed toward short declarative sentences (median 8 words), with a long tail of extended descriptive or monologue sentences. The typical texture is short-short-short-long.

### 1.2 Sentence Types (ratio)

- **Simple (single independent clause):** 50%
- **Compound (coordinated independent clauses):** 25%
- **Complex (independent + dependent clause):** 18%
- **Compound-complex:** 7%

### 1.3 Opening Patterns

- **Subject-first (SVO):** 65%
- **Prepositional/adverbial opener:** 7%
- **Participial phrase opener:** 0% (essentially absent)
- **Dependent clause opener:** 0%
- **Inverted/verb-first:** 3%
- **Conjunction opener (And, But, For):** 20%
- **Other (temporal marker: "Presently," "One morning"):** 5%

**Note:** The high conjunction-opener rate is a signature feature. Vance uses "And" to chain narrative beats with a chronicle-like cadence, not as filler. "But" appears for pivot moments.

### 1.4 Clause Stacking

- **Typical clause count per sentence:** 1–2
- **Stacking method:** Right-branching coordinate chains joined by "and"; occasionally parallel phrases in list form separated by commas
- **Maximum comfortable depth before resolution:** 3 clauses (longer sentences in Etarr's monologue reach 4, but these are exceptional and dramatic)

---

## 2. Paragraph Structure

### 2.1 Length

- **Average paragraph length:** 2 sentences / 18 words (highly variable due to dialogue dominance)
- **Range:** 1–7 sentences
- **Distribution shape:** Strongly bimodal — single-sentence dialogue paragraphs are the most common unit; narration paragraphs cluster at 2–4 sentences

### 2.2 Internal Pattern

- **Typical opening move:** Direct continuation from prior beat (action or dialogue response); occasional scene-setting detail for transitional paragraphs
- **Typical closing move:** A single striking image or observation that lands with finality; often a short declarative sentence after longer buildup
- **Paragraph-level rhythm:** Short declarative opener → one or two elaborating sentences → concluding image or summary beat. The paragraph "resolves" like a musical phrase.

### 2.3 Transitions Between Paragraphs

- **Dominant method:** Direct continuation (dialogue exchange drives most transitions); temporal markers ("Presently," "One morning") for scene shifts
- **Frequency of hard breaks vs. linked flow:** 20% hard / 80% linked

## 3. Word Choice

### 3.1 Register & Etymology

- **Latinate vs. Germanic ratio:** 35/65 Germanic-dominant. Germanic words carry action and observation; Latinate words appear for atmosphere, abstraction, and proper-noun-adjacent exoticism ("degradations," "calculation," "celebrant").
- **Preferred register:** Elevated-plain — formal enough to feel literary, plain enough to feel effortless. The diction is precise but never ornate.
- **Archaism frequency:** Occasional
  - **Type:** Vocabulary choices ("aghast," "blithely," "sennight"-adjacent register) and lightly archaic phrasing ("I knew nothing of this," "laden with a thousand fragrances") rather than thee/thou verb forms.

### 3.2 Specificity & Concreteness

- **Abstraction level:** Highly concrete and sensory, with sudden pivots to philosophical abstraction in dialogue ("Why revenge?" / "infinite dark")
- **Noun specificity:** High — "poplar trees," "flint," "crags," "star-charts" rather than generic equivalents
- **Verb specificity:** High — "coppered," "knit," "strode," "clasped" preferred over neutral alternatives. Verbs do physical work even in emotional scenes.

### 3.3 Syllable Profile

- **Average syllables per word:** 1.32
- **Polysyllabic word frequency (3+ syllables):** 6%
- **Placement tendency for polysyllabic words:** Sentence-end or pre-pause position for weight. Polysyllabic words are rare enough that each one registers. Invented proper nouns (Ascolais, Tenebrosa, Modavna) supply much of the polysyllabic texture.

### 3.4 Repetition & Variation

- **Deliberate repetition:** Rare but structural when present
  - **Type:** Key-word echo across paragraphs ("beauty" recurs through the moor-walk sequence as a thematic anchor; "evil" threads through Etarr's monologue). Not anaphoric or incantatory — more like a motif.
- **Synonym variation:** Moderate — Vance will reuse "said" and "eyes" freely but varies descriptive vocabulary.

### 3.5 Word Selection Method

When generating **new** words not drawn from the sample, select by:

1. **Etymology filter:** Default to Germanic roots for action, observation, and physical description. Use Latinate roots for emotional/philosophical abstraction, exotic atmosphere, and character judgment.
2. **Syllable target:** Favor monosyllables and disyllables (~94% of total). Reserve polysyllabic words for sentence-end emphasis or exotic flavor.
3. **Sensory channel:** Prioritize visual (color, light, shape) and tactile/kinesthetic vocabulary. Vance's world is seen and felt more than heard or smelled.
4. **Formality gate:** Would this word appear in a 1950s literary adventure story written for an educated general audience? If it sounds too academic, too slangy, or too medievally archaic, reject it.
5. **Position rule:** Place heavier or rarer words at clause-end or pre-dash position, where Vance's rhythm naturally lands emphasis.

## 4. Syntax Patterns

### 4.1 Modifier Placement

- **Adjective position:** Predominantly pre-noun; stacked pairs are common ("grave blue eyes," "loose black hair," "long thoughtful eyes," "red afternoon sun")
- **Typical adjective density:** 1–2 per noun phrase on average. Stacked triples are rare and reserved for key descriptive moments.
- **Adverb position:** Sentence-initial ("Presently," "Slowly") or post-verb ("laughed ruefully"). Never stacked.
- **Adverb frequency:** Sparing — perhaps 1 per 5–6 sentences

### 4.2 Inversion & Unusual Word Order

- **Frequency:** Occasional
- **Type:** Verb-subject inversion in dialogue tags ("said T'sais," "asked T'sais," "marvelled T'sais") — this is a strong signature. Object-fronting rare but present ("Kill me and you wear your demonface forever").
- **Trigger context:** Dialogue tags almost always invert. Narration inverts only for emphasis or formality.

### 4.3 Apposition & Interjection

- **Appositives:** Moderate — used for character identification ("Etarr the Masked") and landscape labeling ("Tenebrosa Bay," "the Cape of Sad Remembrance")
  - **Typical position:** Mid-sentence between commas, or sentence-final
- **Parenthetical interjections:** Moderate
  - **Punctuation used:** Em dashes strongly preferred; commas for lighter insertions

### 4.4 Parallelism

- **Frequency:** Occasional — appears in descriptive catalogues and philosophical dialogue
- **Typical form:** Paired or tricolon lists ("Ascolais, Almery, Kauchique"; "the slow river Scaum flowing through green rushes, clouds basking in the wan sunlight over the crags, a bird wheeling on spread wings")
- **Typical list length when present:** 3 items (tricolon is the default)

## 5. Figurative Language

### 5.1 Density

- **Figures per paragraph (average):** 0.3 — figurative language is sparse and deliberate
- **Figures per page (average):** 3–4

### 5.2 Type Preference (ratio)

- **Simile:** 45%
- **Metaphor:** 20%
- **Personification:** 25%
- **Synecdoche/metonymy:** 10%
- **Other:** 0%

### 5.3 Source Domains

1. **Geology/landscape** — crags, dust, spine, stone
2. **Light/color** — sun, copper, red, black, wan
3. **Body/anatomy** — eyes, face, skin, brows
4. **Sleep/death** — "sleep on the water," "lost into the dust," "eternal night"
5. **Wind/atmosphere** — sweep, spread, smoky

### 5.4 Construction Pattern

- **Typical simile form:** "like X" — short and pointed, usually a single phrase ("like the spine of the world," "like one of the content")
- **Metaphor integration:** Embedded in verb choice ("coppered her skin," "the cape seemed to sleep") rather than stated as equation
- **Length:** Single phrase, almost never extended. Vance treats figures as brushstrokes, not conceits.

## 6. Punctuation

### 6.1 Comma Behavior

- **Density:** Moderate — approximately 2.1 per sentence
- **Oxford comma:** Inconsistent (follows the comma-before-conjunction pattern loosely)
- **Comma splices:** Never

### 6.2 Semicolons

- **Frequency:** Near-absent (1 in ~1,150 words)
- **Primary use:** Joining parallel independent clauses in extended description (rare fallback, not a habit)

### 6.3 Dashes

- **Type preferred:** Em dash
- **Frequency:** Heavy — 16 instances in ~1,150 words (~1.4 per 100 words)
- **Primary use:** Interrupted thought in dialogue; dramatic pivot or qualification in narration; appositive framing. The em dash is Vance's primary tool for modulating pace and inserting secondary information. Often paired (opening and closing a parenthetical).

### 6.4 Colons

- **Frequency:** Absent in sample
- **Primary use:** N/A

### 6.5 Other Notable Punctuation

- **Ellipses:** Absent
- **Exclamation marks:** Rare (3 in sample) — used only in sharp dialogue commands ("No!" / "Hold!")
- **Question marks in narration:** Rare (1 rhetorical: "Why revenge?") — questions belong to dialogue, not narration

---

## 7. Rhythm & Cadence

### 7.1 Stress Patterns

- **Dominant tendency:** Loose iambic flow with frequent trochaic interruptions at sentence openings ("T'sais knit her brows")
- **Regularity:** Loosely rhythmic — the prose has a natural pulse but never becomes metrical. Vance writes by ear rather than by rule.

### 7.2 Sentence-Level Pacing

- **Acceleration technique:** Shortening to bare declarative sentences, sometimes just subject-verb ("The man laughed." "Etarr saw the gesture and sighed."); removing subordination; stacking dialogue exchanges without tags
- **Deceleration technique:** Extending sentences with coordinate clauses chained by "and"; embedding appositives between dashes; cataloguing landscape details in parallel phrases
- **Typical pacing shape within a paragraph:** Fast-open-to-slow-close in narration (short observation → elaborating image). In dialogue-heavy passages, the rhythm is staccato exchange → monologue expansion → short narrative beat.

### 7.3 Sound Devices

- **Alliteration:** Occasional — present but understated ("loose black hair, the long thoughtful eyes"; "crags rise in grandeur")
- **Assonance/consonance:** Occasional — Vance has a good ear but doesn't foreground sound play
- **Onomatopoeia:** Absent

---

## 8. Dialogue

### 8.1 Tag Behavior

- **Tag frequency:** Most lines — but many are minimal or replaced by action beats
- **Preferred tag verb:** "said" dominates (~70% of tags). "asked" for questions. Other verbs rare and reserved for specific effect ("marvelled," "assured"). "Laughed" sometimes substitutes for "said."
- **Tag position:** Post-speech dominant, with frequent verb-subject inversion ("said T'sais," "asked T'sais"). Mid-speech tags appear in longer turns. Pre-speech tags are rare.

### 8.2 Speech Patterns

- **Average speech-turn length:** Highly variable — ranges from 4-word exchanges to 100+ word monologues. Median is ~15 words.
- **Interruption/trailing off:** Rare — marked by em dash when present ("—I have not found it so.")
- **Dialect or idiolect markers:** Light — T'sais speaks with a flat, analytical directness that reflects her artificial origin. Etarr speaks with weary formality and occasional bitter irony. Neither uses contractions heavily. Both employ a slightly elevated register that reads as "of another era" without specific period markers.

### 8.3 Dialogue-to-Narration Ratio

- **Approximate ratio:** 68% dialogue / 32% narration within dialogue-containing passages. This is a dialogue-driven scene; pure description passages would shift the ratio substantially.

---

## 9. Description Method

### 9.1 Sensory Distribution

- **Visual:** 80% (color, light, shape, distance dominate)
- **Auditory:** 5% (almost absent — occasional "laughed," "shrieking")
- **Tactile/kinesthetic:** 10% ("shuddered," "pressed back," "coppered her skin")
- **Olfactory:** 3% ("a thousand fragrances" — rare, in memory/recollection)
- **Gustatory:** 2% (essentially absent)

### 9.2 Camera Behavior

- **Default distance:** Medium — room-scale for interior scenes, landscape-scale for moor descriptions, with occasional close-ups on faces and hands
- **Movement pattern:** Static or slow pan. The camera settles on a scene, notes a few details, then holds. Rapid cuts are absent. Zoom-ins happen through sentence-level focus narrowing ("saw how the red afternoon sun coppered her skin, noted the loose black hair, the long thoughtful eyes").
- **Focal priority:** Character first, then environment. Landscape description is prompted by character action (walking the moor) rather than existing independently.

### 9.3 Description Density

- **Lines of pure description before action/dialogue resumes:** 1–3 sentences on average. Extended descriptive set-pieces are rare in this sample.
- **Integration method:** Single-sentence brushstrokes woven into action and dialogue. Description is attached to verbs of perception ("saw," "noted," "watched") so it arrives through character awareness rather than narrator omniscience.

---

## Usage Notes

When writing new prose using this guide:

1. **Sentence construction:** Default to short, clean declarative sentences (6–12 words). Let 1 in 4 or 5 sentences extend to 20+ words with coordinate "and" chains. Open 65% of sentences with the subject. Use "And" to open ~20% — it's a signature rhythm marker, not a crutch. Avoid participial openers entirely.
2. **Word selection:** Run candidate words through the five-step filter in §3.5. Germanic monosyllables are the backbone. Polysyllabic Latinate words should feel like a color accent, not the base coat. Verbs should do physical work ("coppered," "clasped," "knit") even in emotional contexts.
3. **Paragraph shaping:** Keep paragraphs short — 1–3 sentences for narration, single-exchange paragraphs for dialogue. End narration paragraphs on a concrete image or a clean declarative observation, not a trailing clause.
4. **Figurative language:** Use sparingly — roughly 1 figure per 3 paragraphs. Prefer similes in the "like X" form, kept to a single phrase. Embed metaphors in verb choice rather than stating them as equations. Draw comparisons from landscape, light, and the body. Never extend a figure beyond one sentence.
5. **Punctuation:** Em dashes are the primary expressive punctuation — use them for interruptions, qualifications, and parenthetical insertions. Avoid semicolons. Keep exclamation marks for sharp commands in dialogue only. Comma density should feel moderate, never heavy.
6. **Rhythm check:** Read sentences aloud. The prose should feel like a clear, unhurried speaking voice with a slight formality — not breathless, not ponderous. Short sentences should land with the weight of small stones dropped into still water. Longer sentences should accumulate detail in steady, additive rhythm, not spiral into subordination.
7. **Dialogue:** Invert tags ("said T'sais"). Stick to "said" and "asked" for 85%+ of tags. Let characters speak in a lightly elevated register — no contractions in formal moments, but natural enough to not feel stiff. Alternate short exchanges with occasional extended monologues. Use em dashes for interrupted or trailing speech.
8. **Tonal signature:** The underlying emotional register is melancholic wonder — characters who have suffered greatly, observing a dying world with a mix of weariness and lingering appreciation for beauty. Never sentimental, never cynical. The prose should feel like late afternoon light: warm but fading.
