# Style Guide: Jack Vance — *T'sais* (The Dying Earth)

> **Source sample:** "T'sais," Jack Vance, ~955 words (mid-story excerpt, OCR conversion)
> **Genre context:** Dying-earth planetary romance / dark fantasy
> **Narrative POV:** Third-person limited (close to T'sais, occasionally panning to the hooded man)

---

## 1. Sentence Structure

### 1.1 Length

- **Average sentence length:** 11 words
- **Range:** 2–39 words
- **Standard deviation:** 6.6 words
- **Distribution shape:** Right-skewed cluster around 6–10 words (41%), with a long tail toward 20+ word descriptive sentences. The bulk of the prose is short and clipped; longer sentences appear for scene-setting and physical description, not for action.

### 1.2 Sentence Types (ratio)

- **Simple (single independent clause):** 40%
- **Compound (coordinated independent clauses):** 25%
- **Complex (independent + dependent clause):** 25%
- **Compound-complex:** 10%

Note: Vance's "compound" sentences are often *serial verb chains* rather than true coordinated clauses — three or four verbs sharing a subject, separated by commas: "She fell in, slammed the door, dropped the bar." These read as rapid-fire action bursts.

### 1.3 Opening Patterns

- **Subject-first (SVO):** 55%
- **Prepositional/adverbial opener:** 15%
- **Participial phrase opener:** 10%
- **Dependent clause opener:** 5%
- **Inverted/verb-first:** 5%
- **Conjunction opener (And, But, For, etc.):** 3%
- **Noun-phrase fragment / dramatic fragment:** 5%
- **Adjective/adverb directly modifying subject:** 2%

Subject-first dominance keeps the prose direct and propulsive. Prepositional openers appear for spatial reorientation ("In the light of the fireflies," "Over moor, turf, hummock"). Participial openers create simultaneous action ("Wailing, she fled," "Kneeling beside her was").

### 1.4 Clause Stacking

- **Typical clause count per sentence:** 1–2
- **Stacking method:** Right-branching participial additions; serial verb chains sharing a single subject; appositional noun phrases piled after an em dash
- **Maximum comfortable depth before resolution:** 3 clauses (longer sentences resolve through appositive lists rather than syntactic embedding)

## 2. Paragraph Structure

### 2.1 Length

- **Average paragraph length:** 2–3 sentences / 20–35 words
- **Range:** 1–8 sentences
- **Distribution shape:** Heavily front-loaded toward short paragraphs. Action sequences use 1–2 sentence paragraphs. Descriptive or transitional passages stretch to 5–8 sentences, then Vance cuts back hard.

### 2.2 Internal Pattern

- **Typical opening move:** Direct continuation of the action or scene; rarely a "topic sentence" — instead a spatial/temporal anchor or a character's immediate perception
- **Typical closing move:** A single strong image, a completed physical action, or a curt dialogue line; Vance almost never trails off
- **Paragraph-level rhythm:** Short-short-LONG-short. Brief paragraphs accumulate tension, one longer paragraph delivers the payoff (a description, a revelation), then the prose snaps back to brevity.

### 2.3 Transitions Between Paragraphs

- **Dominant method:** Spatial shift or perceptual shift (what the character sees/hears next); temporal jumps are marked by scene breaks or single-line cues ("When she woke")
- **Frequency of hard breaks vs. linked flow:** 40% hard / 60% linked

## 3. Word Choice

### 3.1 Register & Etymology

- **Latinate vs. Germanic ratio:** 65/35 Germanic-dominant — action verbs and physical nouns are almost always Anglo-Saxon ("stumbled," "snatched," "flung," "stout," "threshold"), while Latinate words appear for atmosphere and precision ("insensible," "demoniac," "unconsciousness," "unguent")
- **Preferred register:** Elevated-plain — a plain Germanic chassis with selective Latinate ornamentation. Never colloquial, never ornately Latinate. The effect is a kind of stately simplicity.
- **Archaism frequency:** Occasional
  - **Type:** Vocabulary only ("Deodand," "rapier," "unguent," "furze"); syntax remains modern. Archaisms are world-texture, not grammatical affectation.

### 3.2 Specificity & Concreteness

- **Abstraction level:** Highly concrete and sensory. Abstract states (fear, safety, hunger) are stated flatly and briefly, then the prose returns to objects and actions.
- **Noun specificity:** High — "furze," "heather," "hummock," "flannel," "rapier," "unguent" rather than "bushes," "plants," "hill," "blanket," "sword," "medicine." Vance reaches for the *exact* noun.
- **Verb specificity:** High — "lurched," "bounded," "thudded," "spitted" rather than generic motion verbs. Each verb implies speed, weight, and manner.

### 3.3 Syllable Profile

- **Average syllables per word:** 1.4
- **Polysyllabic word frequency (3+ syllables):** 7%
- **Placement tendency for polysyllabic words:** Sentence-end or mid-sentence in descriptive clauses, providing rhythmic weight against the predominantly monosyllabic action prose. Polysyllables almost never open a sentence.

### 3.4 Repetition & Variation

- **Deliberate repetition:** Rare
  - **Type:** Structural echo for scene-framing ("The door was stout, the windows small" — balanced clause repetition). No anaphora or incantatory repetition.
- **Synonym variation:** Moderate — Vance will reuse a character name or key noun ("the Deodand," "the man in the hood") for clarity rather than varying it, but avoids repeating unusual verbs or adjectives within close proximity.

### 3.5 Word Selection Method

When generating **new** words not drawn from the sample, select by:

1. **Etymology filter:** Default to Germanic monosyllables for action and physical nouns; use Latinate words only for atmosphere, states of mind, or precise technical/material terms
2. **Syllable target:** Favor 1–2 syllable words; permit 3+ syllables only when no shorter word captures the precise meaning
3. **Sensory channel:** Prioritize visual and kinesthetic vocabulary; auditory for mood (moans, rustling, thudding)
4. **Formality gate:** Would this word appear in a 1940s–1950s adventure-fantasy story written for an educated general audience? If it sounds too modern-casual or too academically Latinate, reject it.
5. **Position rule:** Place heavier/rarer words at sentence-end or in appositional phrases; keep sentence openings blunt and Anglo-Saxon

## 4. Syntax Patterns

### 4.1 Modifier Placement

- **Adjective position:** Predominantly pre-noun; occasionally post-noun for archaic flavor or emphasis ("a handsome face, marred and made demoniac")
- **Typical adjective density:** 1 per noun phrase; 2 maximum, and the second is usually a participial adjective ("quivering rapier," "sodden cloak," "iron-barred window")
- **Adverb position:** Pre-verb or sentence-initial when used; usually embedded in the verb's own specificity instead
- **Adverb frequency:** Near-absent — Vance prefers a precise verb over a modified generic one. "Lurched" instead of "walked unsteadily."

### 4.2 Inversion & Unusual Word Order

- **Frequency:** Occasional
- **Type:** Prepositional phrase fronting with subject-verb inversion ("Behind the eye-slits were steady blue eyes"; "Over moor, turf, hummock… went the chase")
- **Trigger context:** Scene-setting or panoramic description; never in dialogue or close action

### 4.3 Apposition & Interjection

- **Appositives:** Frequent — this is a signature Vance move
  - **Typical position:** Post-noun, extending after an em dash or comma, often stacking multiple appositives in a cascade: "a Deodand, — a hairless man-thing with charcoal-black skin, a handsome face, marred and made demoniac by…"
- **Parenthetical interjections:** Rare
  - **Punctuation used:** Em dashes for dramatic insertions; commas for participial asides

### 4.4 Parallelism

- **Frequency:** Occasional — concentrated in action passages
- **Typical form:** Serial verb chains ("stumbled clear, fell, snatched herself up"; "stooped, lifted the limp form, and carried her"); balanced paired clauses ("The door was stout, the windows small")
- **Typical list length when present:** 3 items (tricolon is the dominant rhythm)

## 5. Figurative Language

### 5.1 Density

- **Figures per paragraph (average):** 0.3–0.5
- **Figures per page (average):** 3–4

Vance is *remarkably* sparing with figurative language. The prose achieves its vividness through precise literal description and specific nouns/verbs rather than through comparison. When a figure appears, it lands hard because of its rarity.

### 5.2 Type Preference (ratio)

- **Simile:** 50%
- **Metaphor:** 10%
- **Personification:** 20%
- **Synecdoche/metonymy:** 15%
- **Other (transferred epithet):** 5%

### 5.3 Source Domains

1. **Objects/artifacts** — comparisons to made things ("flung like a doll")
2. **Animals/predation** — implied through verb choice and the Deodand's "hungry" gaze
3. **Body/anatomy** — physical sensation as texture ("breath rasping," "eyes starting")
4. **Landscape/terrain** — environment as emotional mirror

### 5.4 Construction Pattern

- **Typical simile form:** Brief "like X" — never extended ("lay flung like a doll"). One vehicle, no elaboration.
- **Metaphor integration:** Embedded in verb choice rather than stated; Vance rarely says "X was Y" — instead the verb itself carries the metaphoric freight ("the door mercifully gave")
- **Length:** Single phrase, never multi-sentence conceits

## 6. Punctuation

### 6.1 Comma Behavior

- **Density:** Heavy — approximately 2–3 per sentence
- **Oxford comma:** Yes (when serial lists appear)
- **Comma splices:** Deliberate stylistic choice in serial verb chains ("fell, snatched herself up"; "stumbled clear, fell")

### 6.2 Semicolons

- **Frequency:** Rare
- **Primary use:** Joining balanced independent clauses in descriptive passages

### 6.3 Dashes

- **Type preferred:** Em dash
- **Frequency:** Moderate
- **Primary use:** Dramatic reveal or appositional insertion — introducing a new element with visual punch ("a light ahead—a cottage"; "she saw him—a Deodand"). Functions like a theatrical spotlight.

### 6.4 Colons

- **Frequency:** Rare
- **Primary use:** Introducing speech or formal elaboration (dialogue attribution patterns)

### 6.5 Other Notable Punctuation

- **Ellipses:** Rare — used for trailing thought in introspection ("he had done nothing to harm her . . .")
- **Exclamation marks:** Rare — dialogue only, never narration
- **Question marks in narration:** Occasional — for free indirect discourse reflecting T'sais's thought ("Was this beautiful?" "Was this beauty?")

---

## 7. Rhythm & Cadence

### 7.1 Stress Patterns

- **Dominant tendency:** Iambic/anapestic flow in descriptive passages; trochaic punch in action
- **Regularity:** Loosely rhythmic — Vance has an instinctive ear for prose cadence without metronomic regularity. Action sequences break into staccato. Description settles into a more rolling pulse.

### 7.2 Sentence-Level Pacing

- **Acceleration technique:** Shortening clauses, stripping conjunctions (asyndeton), serial verb chains with no connecting "and" until the final verb ("fell in, slammed the door, dropped the bar")
- **Deceleration technique:** Appositional stacking after em dashes; participial phrases trailing the main clause; embedding physical detail
- **Typical pacing shape within a paragraph:** Fast open to slow close in description paragraphs; steady staccato throughout action paragraphs; wave pattern in transitional passages

### 7.3 Sound Devices

- **Alliteration:** Occasional — deployed for atmosphere rather than decoration ("scratching furze, tearing thorn"; "starting and staring"; "wistful moans")
- **Assonance/consonance:** Occasional — unforced, emerging from word selection rather than imposed ("lurched to the threshold")
- **Onomatopoeia:** Occasional — "thudded," "rasping," "rustling"

---

## 8. Dialogue

### 8.1 Tag Behavior

- **Tag frequency:** Most lines
- **Preferred tag verb:** "said" and "called" — extremely plain. No "growled," "murmured," "exclaimed." Occasionally tagless when the speaker is clear.
- **Tag position:** Post-speech dominant; occasional mid-speech break

### 8.2 Speech Patterns

- **Average speech-turn length:** 5–15 words / 1–2 sentences
- **Interruption/trailing off:** Rare
- **Dialect or idiolect markers:** Light — T'sais speaks with artless directness (short declaratives, no hedging); the hooded man speaks in calm imperatives. The Deodand speaks with soft insinuation. Character voice is achieved through rhythm and word selection, not spelling or grammar distortion.

### 8.3 Dialogue-to-Narration Ratio

- **Approximate ratio:** 25% dialogue / 75% narration within dialogue-containing passages

---

## 9. Description Method

### 9.1 Sensory Distribution

- **Visual:** 60%
- **Auditory:** 15%
- **Tactile/kinesthetic:** 20%
- **Olfactory:** 3%
- **Gustatory:** 2%

### 9.2 Camera Behavior

- **Default distance:** Medium (room-scale or body-scale)
- **Movement pattern:** Rapid cuts during action; slow pan during scene-setting; zoom to close detail for significant objects (fangs, eye-slits, rapier, unguent)
- **Focal priority:** Character-action first, then environment fills in around the action. Vance rarely pauses to describe a setting in isolation — landscape is perceived through the character's movement through it.

### 9.3 Description Density

- **Lines of pure description before action/dialogue resumes:** 1–3 on average; maximum 5
- **Integration method:** Single-sentence brushstrokes woven into action. Extended description is delivered through appositional cascades within action sentences rather than as standalone descriptive blocks. The one exception is the "caring for T'sais" paragraph, where serial actions double as description of the cottage interior.

---

## Usage Notes

When writing new prose using this guide:

1. **Sentence construction:** Keep the majority of sentences between 6–12 words. Build longer sentences (18–30 words) sparingly, using appositional cascades rather than syntactic embedding. Use serial verb chains (3 verbs, shared subject, minimal conjunction) for action. Target the 40/25/25/10 type ratio.
2. **Word selection:** Default to Germanic monosyllables. Reach for the *exact* noun (furze, not bush; unguent, not salve). Let verb precision replace adverbs. Run candidates through the five-step filter in §3.5.
3. **Paragraph shaping:** Keep paragraphs short — 2–3 sentences is the norm. Use single-sentence paragraphs for impact. Allow one longer paragraph per scene for descriptive payoff, then snap back.
4. **Figurative language:** Use figures *sparingly* — 1 per 3–4 paragraphs at most. Keep similes to a single brief vehicle ("like a doll"), never extended. Embed metaphor in verb choice rather than stating it.
5. **Punctuation:** Use commas liberally for serial verb chains and appositional stacking. Deploy em dashes for dramatic reveals and appositional pivots. Avoid semicolons and colons except where structurally necessary.
6. **Rhythm check:** Read sentences aloud. Action should feel clipped and percussive. Description should roll but not sprawl. If a descriptive sentence exceeds 30 words, break it or convert the tail into appositives.
7. **The Vance signature:** The defining quality is *economy with precision*. Every word earns its place. The prose moves fast but lands specific, concrete details as it goes — like a runner who drops coins in exact spots without breaking stride.
