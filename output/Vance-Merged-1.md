# Style Guide: Jack Vance — *T'sais* (The Dying Earth)

> **Source:** Composite guide drawn from four separate analyses of "T'sais," Jack Vance, covering ~7,350 words of the story
> **Genre context:** Dying-earth science-fantasy; picaresque dark adventure; planetary romance
> **Narrative POV:** Third-person limited (close to T'sais), with occasional omniscient flexibility for world-history and scene-setting

---

## 1. Sentence Structure

### 1.1 Length

- **Average sentence length:** 14 words
- **Range:** 2–44 words
- **Standard deviation:** ~9 words
- **Distribution shape:** Bimodal — dense cluster of short declarative punches (3–10 words) alternating with medium-to-long descriptive sentences (18–35 words). The short end is the signature: terse statements fired between longer phrases like drum accents between fills.

### 1.2 Sentence Types (ratio)

- **Simple (single independent clause):** 39%
- **Compound (coordinated independent clauses):** 26%
- **Complex (independent + dependent clause):** 23%
- **Compound-complex:** 12%

The high simple-sentence ratio is deceptive — Vance achieves complexity *between* sentences, not within them. "Compound" sentences are often serial verb chains sharing a subject, separated by commas.

### 1.3 Opening Patterns

- **Subject-first (SVO):** 54% — dominant mode, keeping prose direct and propulsive
- **Prepositional/adverbial opener:** 14% — spatial or temporal framing
- **Dependent clause opener:** 7%
- **Participial phrase opener:** 6% — simultaneous action
- **Conjunction opener (And, But, For):** 5% — chains action forward at pace
- **Inverted/verb-first:** 4% — dramatic emphasis or archaic coloring
- **Other (fragments, dialogue tags, demonstratives):** 10%

### 1.4 Clause Stacking

- **Typical clause count per sentence:** 1–3
- **Stacking method:** Right-branching coordinate chains joined by commas, semicolons, or "and." Subordination is shallow — rarely more than one dependent clause before resolution. Deep stacking is achieved through *appositional* layering rather than syntactic nesting.
- **Maximum comfortable depth before resolution:** 3 clauses.

## 2. Paragraph Structure

### 2.1 Length

- **Average paragraph length:** 3–4 sentences / ~50 words
- **Range:** 1–9 sentences
- **Distribution shape:** Heavily front-loaded toward short paragraphs (1–3 sentences), with occasional longer set-pieces for landscape description or world-history. Single-sentence paragraphs are common and deliberate.

### 2.2 Internal Pattern

- **Typical opening move:** Action or concrete perception — a character does something or notices something. Rarely a topic sentence; the paragraph drops into motion or mid-observation.
- **Typical closing move:** A single striking image, a completed action, or a curt declarative that lands with finality. Final sentences tend to be the paragraph's shortest.
- **Paragraph-level rhythm:** Accumulative build followed by blunt closure. Medium-length sentences stack detail, then the paragraph snaps shut with a short, sharp sentence. Bricks stacking, then a capstone.

### 2.3 Transitions Between Paragraphs

- **Dominant method:** Spatial or temporal shift via brief marker ("presently"; "a few minutes later"), or direct continuation of action. Associative leaps are rare.
- **Frequency of hard breaks vs. linked flow:** 38% hard / 62% linked.

## 3. Word Choice

### 3.1 Register & Etymology

- **Latinate vs. Germanic ratio:** ~44/56 Germanic-dominant. Blunt Germanic monosyllables for action and concrete nouns ("struck," "flung," "snatched"), with Latinate vocabulary for atmosphere and elevated narration ("enigmatic," "insensible," "demoniac").
- **Preferred register:** Elevated-precise — a plain Germanic chassis with selective Latinate ornamentation. Not archaic but *mannered*: the vocabulary of a well-read raconteur who favors the exact word over the common one.
- **Archaism frequency:** Occasional — flavoring, not structural
  - **Type:** Vocabulary ("rapier," "manse," "unguent," "Deodand"), formal dialogue constructions ("What means this?"), deliberate stiffness in speech. Vance more often *invents* formality than borrows it. Never archaic verb forms.

### 3.2 Specificity & Concreteness

- **Abstraction level:** Highly concrete and sensory. Abstract concepts appear as topics of dialogue or thought but never as the texture of narration. Narration is built from physical particulars.
- **Noun specificity:** Maximum — "rapier" not "sword," "furze" not "bush," "brand" not "torch." Vance almost never uses a generic noun when a precise one exists.
- **Verb specificity:** Very high and kinetic — "lurched," "strutted," "caroled," "ogled," "snatched," "crouched." Verbs encode manner directly; adverbs are largely unnecessary. Plain verbs appear only when plainness is the point.

### 3.3 Syllable Profile

- **Average syllables per word:** ~1.4
- **Polysyllabic word frequency (3+ syllables):** ~9%
- **Placement tendency for polysyllabic words:** Mid-sentence or sentence-end for rhythmic weight against the monosyllabic base. Polysyllables are *placed*, not scattered. Invented proper nouns carry much of the polysyllabic load.

### 3.4 Repetition & Variation

- **Deliberate repetition:** Occasional — purposeful when present
  - **Type:** Key-word echo for thematic emphasis ("strange soil… strange air… strange and new"), immediate echo for emotional intensity ("Dead! My wife is dead!"), and structural echo for scene-framing ("The door was stout, the windows small"). When repetition occurs, it is lexical echo across clauses, not rhetorical patterning.
- **Synonym variation:** Low-to-moderate. Vance reuses character names and key nouns rather than hunting for synonyms, but varies verb choices freely. No clumsy pronouns or epithets to avoid repeating a name.

### 3.5 Word Selection Method

When generating **new** words not drawn from the sample, select by:

1. **Etymology filter:** Default to short Germanic roots for action and physical nouns; deploy Latinate or exotic roots for atmosphere, states of mind, and elevation.
2. **Syllable target:** 1–2 syllables as baseline. 3+ syllable words only when rhythm demands weight, typically at clause-end, never more than one per sentence.
3. **Sensory channel:** Prioritize visual and kinesthetic vocabulary. Auditory for atmosphere; tactile for physicality.
4. **Formality gate:** Would this word appear in a 1950s adventure-fantasy by an erudite author? If it sounds like journalism, modern casual prose, or academic jargon, reject it.
5. **Position rule:** Place heavier or rarer words mid-sentence or at clause-end. Keep sentence openings blunt and Anglo-Saxon. End sentences on concrete, short words.

## 4. Syntax Patterns

### 4.1 Modifier Placement

- **Adjective position:** Predominantly pre-noun, often in pairs ("dark trees," "sodden cloak"). Post-noun placement occasional, for archaic flavor or emphasis.
- **Typical adjective density:** 1 per noun phrase, 2 maximum. Vance is selective — one exact modifier over several vague ones.
- **Adverb position:** Pre-verb or sentence-initial when used; usually embedded in the verb's own specificity instead.
- **Adverb frequency:** Near-absent — "lurched" rather than "walked unsteadily." Adverbs appear mainly for temporal pacing ("presently").

### 4.2 Inversion & Unusual Word Order

- **Frequency:** Occasional — recognizable but not dominant
- **Type:** Object-fronting, predicate-adjective fronting ("alien was it to her mind"), prepositional-phrase fronting with subject-verb inversion, inverted dialogue constructions ("What means this?").
- **Trigger context:** Elevated passages, especially dialogue. Narration inversions are rhythmically motivated. Scene-setting also triggers inversion.

### 4.3 Apposition & Interjection

- **Appositives:** Frequent — a core Vancean device for defining, recharacterizing, or inserting world-building color.
  - **Typical position:** Mid-sentence between commas, or post-noun after an em dash, often stacking in cascade. Also sentence-final as trailing elaboration.
- **Parenthetical interjections:** Rare to moderate
  - **Punctuation used:** Commas for light insertions; dashes for dramatic interruption; parentheses for encyclopedic asides only.

### 4.4 Parallelism

- **Frequency:** Occasional to structural — more prominent in action and description than rhetorical flourish.
- **Typical form:** Tricolon, paired clauses, serial verb chains, balanced antithesis in dialogue, and polysyndetic coordinate lists.
- **Typical list length when present:** 3 items (tricolon is the default). Lists of 4+ for world-building catalogs.

## 5. Figurative Language

### 5.1 Density

- **Figures per paragraph (average):** 0.5–0.7
- **Figures per page (average):** 3–5

Vance is economical with figures. Many paragraphs contain none; figures land hard because of their rarity.

### 5.2 Type Preference (ratio)

- **Simile:** 40% — the dominant mode, typically brief "like X" constructions
- **Personification:** 25% — applied to landscape, light, objects (especially the semi-sentient rapier)
- **Metaphor:** 18% — embedded in verb choice rather than stated as equation
- **Synecdoche/metonymy:** 12% — part for whole, especially light and color standing in for mood
- **Other (transferred epithet, synesthesia, zeugma):** 5%

### 5.3 Source Domains

1. **Light / color / fire** — illumination as emotional and narrative signal; refracted hues, shifting beams
2. **Animals / predation** — character rendered through animal comparison; implied through verb choice
3. **Landscape / geology / architecture** — stone, ruin, and the mineral world as substrate of description
4. **Body / anatomy / sensation** — physical specifics and tactile experience as texture
5. **Water / weather / textiles** — sky as expanse, wings like craft-objects, atmospheric depth

### 5.4 Construction Pattern

- **Typical simile form:** Brief "like X" or "as X" — one vehicle, no elaboration. The comparison arrives, does its work, and withdraws.
- **Metaphor integration:** Embedded in verb or adjective choice rather than stated outright. The verb carries the metaphoric freight ("rasped her nerves"; the rapier "sprang up by itself").
- **Length:** Single phrase. Multi-sentence conceits are absent.

## 6. Punctuation

### 6.1 Comma Behavior

- **Density:** Moderate-to-heavy — approximately 2 per sentence on average, rising to 3–4 in longer constructions
- **Oxford comma:** Yes — consistent in lists
- **Comma splices:** Deliberate stylistic choice for compressed parallel action and serial verb chains. The splice is an acceleration device.

### 6.2 Semicolons

- **Frequency:** Moderate — roughly 1 per 10 sentences
- **Primary use:** Joining parallel independent clauses describing simultaneous or sequential scene elements. Functions as a pause shorter than a period but stronger than a comma.

### 6.3 Dashes

- **Type preferred:** Em dash
- **Frequency:** Moderate
- **Primary use:** Dramatic reveal or appositional insertion — introducing a new element with visual punch. Also for interrupted thought and terminal dramatic emphasis. Functions like a theatrical spotlight.

### 6.4 Colons

- **Frequency:** Rare
- **Primary use:** Introducing a dramatic revelation, formal elaboration, or quoted speech.

### 6.5 Other Notable Punctuation

- **Ellipses:** Rare — used for trailing sensory impression or implication, not hesitation. Vance uses spaced dots (. . .) rather than tight ellipses (…).
- **Exclamation marks:** Present in dialogue (ranging from rare to frequent depending on the scene's theatrical intensity), absent in narration. The contrast between exclamatory dialogue and flat narration is a key signature.
- **Question marks in narration:** Rare — used for free indirect discourse reflecting T'sais' internal questioning ("Was this beautiful?"; "Was not killing wicked?"), reflecting her naïve epistemology.

## 7. Rhythm & Cadence

### 7.1 Stress Patterns

- **Dominant tendency:** Loosely iambic with trochaic punch in action and dactylic variation in description. Spondaic clusters when hard monosyllabic verbs stack ("sprang back," "struck a light").
- **Regularity:** Loosely rhythmic — enough regularity to feel controlled, enough variation to avoid sing-song. Action breaks into staccato; description settles into a rolling pulse.

### 7.2 Sentence-Level Pacing

- **Acceleration technique:** Shortening sentences to fragments; comma-spliced verb chains; asyndeton; stacking simple declarations.
- **Deceleration technique:** Appositional expansion; parenthetical asides; polysyndetic "and…and…and" chains; trailing subordinate clauses.
- **Typical pacing shape within a paragraph:** Build through accumulated detail, then snap shut with a blunt final sentence. The signature is controlled build followed by percussive close.

### 7.3 Sound Devices

- **Alliteration:** Occasional and unforced — texture, not ornament.
- **Assonance/consonance:** Occasional — sensitive to vowel music in description but never foregrounded. More consonance than assonance.
- **Onomatopoeia:** Rare — verbs do the sonic work ("gasped," "thudded," "rasping").

---

## 8. Dialogue

### 8.1 Tag Behavior

- **Tag frequency:** Most lines tagged; tagless exchanges for clear two-speaker contexts.
- **Preferred tag verb:** "Said" and "called" as plain defaults, with expressive tags for precise speech-act description ("gasped," "snarled," "mused," "crowed"). No purely decorative tags.
- **Tag position:** Post-speech dominant ("said Pandelume"; "cried T'sais"). Occasional mid-speech breaks.

### 8.2 Speech Patterns

- **Average speech-turn length:** 15–25 words / 1–2 sentences. Longer speeches are reserved for authority figures or villains. The asymmetry between mentor-orator and student-questioner is characteristic.
- **Interruption/trailing off:** Rare — characters complete their thoughts. Dashes interrupt speech at dramatic moments; trailing ellipsis suggests things left unsaid.
- **Dialect or idiolect markers:** Heavy but *invented* rather than mimetic. Each speaker has a distinct verbal register achieved through rhythm and word selection, not spelling or grammar distortion. Authority figures speak in longer, more syntactically complex constructions with weary formality. Naïve or urgent characters speak in short, direct declaratives. Villains and rogues are theatrical, self-aware, and performative. Victims are clipped and fragmented.

### 8.3 Dialogue-to-Narration Ratio

- **Approximate ratio:** 36% dialogue / 64% narration within dialogue-containing passages. Vance alternates dialogue-heavy and description-heavy blocks rather than blending them evenly. Dialogue arrives in concentrated bursts at dramatic peaks, then recedes.

## 9. Description Method

### 9.1 Sensory Distribution

- **Visual:** 63% — color, light, shape, spatial arrangement dominate. Vance's prose is a painter's prose.
- **Auditory:** 15% — specific sounds tied to concrete sources (bells, voices, metal, moaning)
- **Tactile/kinesthetic:** 15% — the secret engine; Vance describes the world through physical contact, weight, resistance, and bodily movement
- **Olfactory:** 4% — minimal; implied rather than foregrounded
- **Gustatory:** 2.5% — essentially absent

### 9.2 Camera Behavior

- **Default distance:** Medium — room-scale or clearing-scale, with the POV character anchoring the frame. Close enough to see faces and postures, far enough to frame the scene.
- **Movement pattern:** Slow pan across landscape, then zoom to a telling detail. The camera *discovers* the scene as the POV character would. Action sequences use rapid cuts; scene-setting uses slow pans.
- **Focal priority:** Environment first in establishing shots, then character acts within the space. Once established, action takes over and environment fills in around movement.

### 9.3 Description Density

- **Lines of pure description before action/dialogue resumes:** 2–4 sentences on average. Set-piece passages for new locations may stretch to 5–6 sentences, but these are deliberate exceptions.
- **Integration method:** Single-sentence brushstrokes woven into action are the signature. Pure description blocks are rare and reserved for establishing new locations. Once the scene is set, description is delivered through *appositional inserts* — descriptive phrases dropped into action sentences as comma-bounded or dash-bounded asides. Vance front-loads description, then thins it as action takes over.

## Usage Notes

When writing new prose using this guide:

1. **Sentence construction:** Default to short, blunt SVO sentences averaging ~14 words. Alternate short declarative punches (3–10 words) with medium descriptive sentences (18–30 words). Build compound sentences from chained actions joined by "and" or commas. Keep subordination shallow. Fire off single-sentence paragraphs without hesitation. Target the ~39/26/23/12 type ratio.

2. **Word selection:** Choose the most specific concrete noun available and the most kinetic verb. Avoid adverbs — if you need one, your verb is wrong. Run candidate words through the five-step filter in §3.5: Germanic roots for action, Latinate or exotic for atmosphere and naming. Deploy polysyllabic words sparingly and at clause-end for rhythmic weight.

3. **Paragraph shaping:** Short paragraphs (2–4 sentences) are the norm. Open with action or image, not topic sentences. Build through accumulated detail and close with the shortest, sharpest sentence in the paragraph. Reserve longer paragraphs for landscape description or historical aside, then snap back to brevity.

4. **Figurative language:** Use sparingly — roughly one figure per two paragraphs. Keep similes to a single brief "like X" vehicle. Embed metaphor in verb choice rather than stating it. Draw comparisons from light, animals, stone, body, and landscape. Never extend a figure beyond one sentence.

5. **Punctuation:** Use commas liberally for serial verb chains and appositional stacking. Deploy em dashes for dramatic reveals and appositional pivots. Use semicolons to join related parallel observations. Use deliberate comma splices for acceleration. Restrict exclamation marks to dialogue. Use spaced ellipses (. . .) for trailing implication.

6. **Rhythm check:** Read sentences aloud. Action should feel clipped and percussive — sentences landing with monosyllabic weight at their ends. Description should roll with a loose iambic cadence but not sprawl. Paragraphs should build from mid-tempo to a culminating image, then snap shut. If a sentence trails off on a soft polysyllable, restructure it.

7. **Dialogue:** Give every character a distinct verbal register achieved through rhythm and word selection. Use plain "said" as the default tag but deploy expressive tag verbs when they precisely describe the speech act. Keep speeches short (15–25 words). Let authority figures orate and urgent characters clip. Let characters be theatrical and self-aware.

8. **Description:** Sketch environments in 2–4 brushstrokes for establishing shots, then fold description into single-sentence appositional inserts within action. Front-load description for new locations; thin it as action takes over. Maintain the medium-distance camera that discovers scenes through character movement.

9. **Tone:** Maintain cool, precise, faintly sardonic detachment in narration. The narrator observes with a lapidary eye, never editorializing or emoting. The emotional register lives in what is described, not in how the narrator feels about it. Violence is rendered flatly; absurdity is juxtaposed without comment. Think: a naturalist cataloging specimens from a dying world.

10. **Encyclopedic asides:** When introducing creatures, places, or historical events, drop briefly into a dry, authoritative, almost clinical tone — as though quoting from an in-world reference text — then snap back to the action without transition.
