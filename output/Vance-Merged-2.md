# Style Guide: Jack Vance — *T'sais* (The Dying Earth)

> **Source:** Composite analysis of four excerpts from "T'sais" by Jack Vance (~5,400 words total)
> **Genre context:** Dying-earth science fantasy; mannered picaresque adventure with melancholic undertone
> **Narrative POV:** Third-person limited anchored to T'sais, with omniscient excursions for catalog passages; emotional states inferred from visible signs

---

## 1. Sentence Structure

### 1.1 Length

- **Average sentence length:** 15 words
- **Range:** 2–67 words
- **Standard deviation:** ~10 words
- **Distribution shape:** Bimodal — short declarative punches (2–10 words) against a broad middle band (10–20 words), with a long tail of accumulative sentences (30+ words). Typical texture: short-short-short-long.

### 1.2 Sentence Types (ratio)

- **Simple (single independent clause):** 39%
- **Compound (coordinated independent clauses):** 26%
- **Complex (independent + dependent clause):** 24%
- **Compound-complex:** 11%

### 1.3 Opening Patterns

- **Subject-first (SVO):** 57%
- **Conjunction opener (And, But, For, etc.):** 18% — "And" dominates, lending a chronicle-like additive cadence. The single most distinctive syntactic habit.
- **Prepositional/adverbial opener:** 11%
- **Dependent clause opener:** 5%
- **Inverted/verb-first:** 3%
- **Participial phrase opener:** 1% (essentially absent)
- **Other (temporal marker, demonstrative, interjection):** 5%

### 1.4 Clause Stacking

- **Typical clause count per sentence:** 1–3
- **Stacking method:** Right-branching coordinate chains joined by commas and "and." Clauses extend rightward rather than nesting inward. Deep embedding is rare.
- **Maximum comfortable depth before resolution:** 3–4 clauses. Sentences grow *wide* rather than *deep*.

---

## 2. Paragraph Structure

### 2.1 Length

- **Average paragraph length:** 3 sentences / ~36 words
- **Range:** 1–12 sentences
- **Distribution shape:** Skewed short. Single-sentence paragraphs common for action beats and dialogue. Narration clusters at 2–4 sentences. Occasional set-piece catalog paragraphs run 6–12 sentences.

### 2.2 Internal Pattern

- **Typical opening move:** Direct continuation of action or dialogue; occasionally a spatial/temporal marker. Rarely a topic sentence.
- **Typical closing move:** A striking terminal image or clipped declarative. The most vivid element comes last.
- **Paragraph-level rhythm:** Short setup → brief elaboration → emphatic close. In catalog paragraphs, accumulation builds until a final sentence brakes the motion.

### 2.3 Transitions Between Paragraphs

- **Dominant method:** Direct continuation of action or dialogue exchange; temporal markers ("Presently," "One morning," "Dawn came") for scene shifts
- **Frequency of hard breaks vs. linked flow:** 29% hard / 71% linked

## 3. Word Choice

### 3.1 Register & Etymology

- **Latinate vs. Germanic ratio:** 36/64 Germanic-dominant. Germanic words carry the load for action, observation, and physical description. Latinate words appear selectively for atmosphere, abstraction, the numinous, and exotic naming.
- **Preferred register:** Elevated-plain — formal enough to feel literary, plain enough to feel effortless. A clean baseline punctuated by precise or archaic words.
- **Archaism frequency:** Occasional
  - **Type:** Vocabulary ("doffed," "bereft," "bestrode," "henceforth," "aghast"); lightly archaic phrasing and syntax (inverted dialogue constructions, adverbial phrases like "the while," "hence"). Pronouns and verb conjugations remain modern — the effect is *elevated* rather than *antique*.

### 3.2 Specificity & Concreteness

- **Abstraction level:** Highly concrete and sensory, with pivots to philosophical or sorcerous abstraction in dialogue and numinous passages.
- **Noun specificity:** Very high — "crags," "strand," "spires," "pelgrane" rather than "rocks," "beach," "towers," "creature." Vance selects the most precise noun available, inventing specific nouns for invented things.
- **Verb specificity:** Very high — "strode," "coppered," "pranced," "ballooned," "clambered" rather than generic movement verbs. Verbs carry sensory and kinetic information simultaneously.

### 3.3 Syllable Profile

- **Average syllables per word:** 1.4
- **Polysyllabic word frequency (3+ syllables):** ~8%
- **Placement tendency for polysyllabic words:** Clause-end or sentence-end for rhythmic weight. Polysyllabic words are rare enough that each one registers. Invented proper nouns supply much of the polysyllabic texture.

### 3.4 Repetition & Variation

- **Deliberate repetition:** Occasional, but structural when present
  - **Type:** Key-word echo for thematic anchoring; anaphoric "And" in catalog passages; verbatim repetition of ritual phrases. Not decorative — repetition serves accumulation or motif.
- **Synonym variation:** Moderate — Vance reuses key nouns freely but varies verbs and descriptive vocabulary.

### 3.5 Word Selection Method

When generating **new** words not drawn from the sample, select by:

1. **Etymology filter:** Default to Germanic roots for action and physical description. Reserve Latinate for abstraction, the numinous, and exotic atmosphere.
2. **Syllable target:** Favor monosyllables and disyllables (~92%). Reserve trisyllabic+ words for rhythmic weight at clause-end.
3. **Sensory channel:** Prioritize visual and kinesthetic vocabulary. The world is seen and felt more than heard or smelled.
4. **Formality gate:** Would this word appear in a 1950s literary adventure story for an educated audience with a taste for the exotic? If too modern-casual or too academically obscure, discard.
5. **Position rule:** Place heavier or rarer words at clause-end or sentence-end. Keep openings crisp and Germanic.

## 4. Syntax Patterns

### 4.1 Modifier Placement

- **Adjective position:** Predominantly pre-noun; stacked pairs common ("grave blue eyes," "dim red sun") but clusters of three+ are rare. Occasional post-noun placement for archaic emphasis.
- **Typical adjective density:** 1–2 per noun phrase. Many noun phrases pass unmodified — Vance trusts his nouns.
- **Adverb position:** Sentence-initial or mid-clause; never stacked.
- **Adverb frequency:** Sparing — manner is encoded in verb choice. Perhaps 1 per 5–6 sentences.

### 4.2 Inversion & Unusual Word Order

- **Frequency:** Occasional — a signature flavor element
- **Type:** Verb-subject inversion in dialogue tags ("said T'sais"); verb-subject inversion for scenic reveals ("Ahead shone a glimmering sea"); object-fronting for emphasis
- **Trigger context:** Dialogue tags (almost always inverted), scenic reveals, moments of wonder or horror. Inversion functions as a spotlight.

### 4.3 Apposition & Interjection

- **Appositives:** Moderate to frequent — a core device for efficient identification and layered detail.
  - **Typical position:** Mid-sentence between commas, or sentence-final. Can extend into mini-catalogs in set-piece passages.
- **Parenthetical interjections:** Rare — em dashes for dramatic insertions, commas for lighter qualifications.

### 4.4 Parallelism

- **Frequency:** Structural — one of the most recognizable Vance markers, especially in catalog passages.
- **Typical form:** Tricolon constructions; anaphoric catalog lists ("And one... And another..."); balanced clauses joined by semicolons; compound predicates in action.
- **Typical list length when present:** 3 items (tricolon default); catalogs may extend to 6–8.

## 5. Figurative Language

### 5.1 Density

- **Figures per paragraph (average):** 0.4
- **Figures per page (average):** 3–4

Vance is notably restrained. The prose achieves atmosphere through precise literal description rather than metaphorical elaboration. When a figure appears, it lands hard because of its rarity.

### 5.2 Type Preference (ratio)

- **Simile:** 36%
- **Metaphor (especially embedded in verb choice):** 35%
- **Personification:** 18%
- **Synecdoche/metonymy:** 9%
- **Other (transferred epithet, compressed epithet):** 2%

### 5.3 Source Domains

1. **Light, color, and atmosphere** — sun, copper, red, black, dim; emotion mapped to flame and glow
2. **Geology and architecture** — crags, columns, spires, dust; the world as ruin and stage
3. **Body and organic matter** — eyes, face, skin; decay; will rendered in physical terms
4. **Sleep, death, and stillness** — enchantment as living death; silence, dissolution
5. **Machinery, animals, and botanical** — wings as rusty hinges; predatory behavior; organic comparisons

### 5.4 Construction Pattern

- **Typical simile form:** "like X" — compact, usually 2–5 words. Never extended.
- **Metaphor integration:** Embedded in verb and noun choice rather than stated as formal comparison. The verb itself *is* the figure ("the sun ballooned upward"; "coppered her skin"; "the cape seemed to sleep"). This is the dominant figurative mode.
- **Length:** Single phrase or single verb. Extended conceits are absent. Figures flash and vanish.

## 6. Punctuation

### 6.1 Comma Behavior

- **Density:** Moderate — approximately 2.0 per sentence
- **Oxford comma:** Inconsistent
- **Comma splices:** Occasional deliberate stylistic choice in rapid action or parallel constructions

### 6.2 Semicolons

- **Frequency:** Variable by passage type — rare in dialogue-heavy scenes, moderate to heavy in catalog and expository passages
- **Primary use:** Joining balanced independent clauses; separating items in complex lists. In set-piece descriptions, a load-bearing punctuation mark.

### 6.3 Dashes

- **Type preferred:** Em dash
- **Frequency:** Variable — from rare to heavy depending on passage type
- **Primary use:** Appositional insertion, dramatic interruption, compressed identifying phrases, and interrupted speech in dialogue. Can be paired to frame a parenthetical.

### 6.4 Colons

- **Frequency:** Rare to absent
- **Primary use:** Introducing pronouncements from authoritative voices, when used at all

### 6.5 Other Notable Punctuation

- **Ellipses:** Rare to absent — trailing speech indicated by em dash instead
- **Exclamation marks:** Rare — dialogue only, reserved for genuine alarm or invocation
- **Question marks in narration:** Rare — questions belong to dialogue; occasional rhetorical use for dramatic effect

## 7. Rhythm & Cadence

### 7.1 Stress Patterns

- **Dominant tendency:** Loosely iambic flow with frequent trochaic interruptions at sentence openings and spondaic clusters for gravity ("black dust," "dim red," "great sea," "short sharp"). The underlying pulse is da-DUM but breaks freely for emphasis.
- **Regularity:** Loosely rhythmic — the prose has a musical quality shaped by ear, not metrical enough to scan as verse but controlled enough that deviations feel deliberate.

### 7.2 Sentence-Level Pacing

- **Acceleration technique:** Shortening to bare declarative sentences; asyndeton in action sequences; comma-spliced compound predicates; dropping subordination; stacking dialogue exchanges without tags
- **Deceleration technique:** Extending with coordinate "and" chains; polysyndeton; embedding appositives between dashes or commas; cataloging details in parallel phrases; trailing prepositional chains
- **Typical pacing shape within a paragraph:** Steady build to a sharp terminal image or decisive action beat. Short sentences accumulate momentum; the final sentence lands with the most weight.

### 7.3 Sound Devices

- **Alliteration:** Occasional — a consistent background texture that supports rhythm without calling attention to itself. Paired adjectives and proper nouns are the primary vehicles.
- **Assonance/consonance:** Occasional — woven into key phrases and proper nouns, which Vance shapes for phonetic texture
- **Onomatopoeia:** Rare — used for creature sounds and physical action when present

---

## 8. Dialogue

### 8.1 Tag Behavior

- **Tag frequency:** Most lines tagged, either with verb or action beat
- **Preferred tag verb:** "Said" dominates (~60% of tags); "asked" for questions. Occasional vivid alternatives earned by context ("murmured," "marvelled," "cried," "chuckled") — Vance uses varied tags more in set-piece scenes and "said" more in exchanges.
- **Tag position:** Post-speech dominant, with frequent verb-subject inversion ("said T'sais," "asked T'sais"). Mid-speech tags appear in longer turns. Pre-speech tags are rare.

### 8.2 Speech Patterns

- **Average speech-turn length:** 15 words / 1–2 sentences for exchanges; up to 100+ words for expository monologue or legend-telling
- **Interruption/trailing off:** Rare — marked by em dash when present. Characters tend to speak in complete, controlled utterances.
- **Dialect or idiolect markers:** Light — characters share a formal-elevated register marking the dying-earth setting. Distinguished by attitude and content rather than vocabulary or phonetic dialect.

### 8.3 Dialogue-to-Narration Ratio

- **Approximate ratio:** 45% dialogue / 55% narration within dialogue-containing passages. The ratio shifts substantially by scene: some passages are dialogue-driven (65%+), others are narration-dominated (85%+).

---

## 9. Description Method

### 9.1 Sensory Distribution

- **Visual:** 73% (color, light, shape, distance dominate massively)
- **Auditory:** 11%
- **Tactile/kinesthetic:** 12%
- **Olfactory:** 3%
- **Gustatory:** 2%

### 9.2 Camera Behavior

- **Default distance:** Medium — room-scale or clearing-scale. Landscape-scale for travel passages.
- **Movement pattern:** Follows the acting character or pans slowly across a scene. Close-ups arrive for faces and key details. In catalog scenes, the camera inventories the space, pausing on each figure for one identifying detail.
- **Focal priority:** Character-first in dialogue and action, environment-first in set-pieces. Setting details attach to character perception in action passages.

### 9.3 Description Density

- **Lines of pure description before action/dialogue resumes:** 1–3 sentences on average; up to 5–8 in set-piece catalog passages
- **Integration method:** Single-sentence brushstrokes woven into forward motion. One vivid clause per sentence carries the environmental load while the narrative continues. The world is sketched in passing. Set-piece catalogs are the exception: long paragraphs inventorying a space through accumulated appositional detail.

## Usage Notes

When writing new prose using this guide:

1. **Sentence construction:** Alternate short punches (3–12 words) with medium compounds (15–25 words). Reserve long accumulative sentences (30+) for catalogs. Open ~57% with the subject, ~18% with a conjunction ("And" especially). Avoid participial openers. Favor right-branching coordination over deep embedding.
2. **Word selection:** Run candidates through the five-step filter in §3.5. Germanic monosyllables are the backbone; Latinate polysyllables are color accents. Choose verbs that encode manner — never "went" when "clambered" or "strode" is available.
3. **Paragraph shaping:** Keep most paragraphs to 2–4 sentences. Use single-sentence paragraphs as rhythmic punctuation. Close on a concrete image, not a trailing clause. Permit one extended catalog paragraph per major scene.
4. **Figurative language:** Roughly 1 figure per 2–3 paragraphs. Embed metaphor in verb choice rather than formal comparison. Similes use "like X" in 2–5 words. Draw from light, landscape, body, and decay. Never extend beyond one sentence.
5. **Parallelism & catalog:** Deploy tricolons for gravity. Use "And one... And another..." for crowd inventory, one detail per figure. The most audible Vance rhythm.
6. **Punctuation:** Commas for pacing, periods for force. Semicolons for balanced parallel clauses. Em dashes for appositional insertion. Exclamation marks for sharp dialogue only.
7. **Dialogue:** Invert tags ("said T'sais"). Use "said"/"asked" for the majority. Lightly elevated register — formal enough for another era, natural enough to breathe. Differentiate characters by attitude, not vocabulary.
8. **Rhythm check:** Read aloud. Short sentences land like struck bells. Long ones accumulate mass through additive coordination. Listen for spondaic clusters; if the prose sounds too smooth, add one. Paired adjectives should alliterate or chime.
9. **The Vance signature:** Cool precision underwritten by melancholic wonder. Detached, faintly sardonic clarity — cultured intelligence finding the dying world fascinating and faintly absurd. Violent or emotional moments rendered with controlled exactitude. Never sentimental, never cynical. Late afternoon light: warm but fading.
