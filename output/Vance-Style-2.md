# Style Guide: Jack Vance — *T'sais* (The Dying Earth)

> **Source sample:** "T'sais," Jack Vance, ~2,200 words (OCR conversion, minor artifacts)
> **Genre context:** Dying-earth science-fantasy; picaresque dark fantasy
> **Narrative POV:** Third-person limited (close on T'sais), with omniscient flexibility for scene-setting

---

## 1. Sentence Structure

### 1.1 Length

- **Average sentence length:** 13 words
- **Range:** 2–38 words
- **Standard deviation:** ~9 words
- **Distribution shape:** Bimodal — frequent short punches (3–8 words) interspersed with medium-length descriptive sentences (18–35 words). Very few sentences exceed 35 words.

### 1.2 Sentence Types (ratio)

- **Simple (single independent clause):** 45%
- **Compound (coordinated independent clauses):** 25%
- **Complex (independent + dependent clause):** 22%
- **Compound-complex:** 8%

### 1.3 Opening Patterns

- **Subject-first (SVO):** 50%
- **Prepositional/adverbial opener:** 15%
- **Participial phrase opener:** 5%
- **Dependent clause opener:** 8%
- **Inverted/verb-first:** 3%
- **Conjunction opener (And, But, For, etc.):** 4%
- **Dialogue tag or quotation opener:** 12%
- **Other (demonstrative, temporal marker):** 3%

### 1.4 Clause Stacking

- **Typical clause count per sentence:** 1–3
- **Stacking method:** Right-branching chains joined by commas or semicolons; coordinate clauses laid side by side. Subordination is shallow — one level deep, rarely two.
- **Maximum comfortable depth before resolution:** 3 clauses

---

## 2. Paragraph Structure

### 2.1 Length

- **Average paragraph length:** 4 sentences / 55 words
- **Range:** 1–8 sentences
- **Distribution shape:** Alternating — short action/dialogue paragraphs (1–3 sentences) alternate with longer descriptive set-pieces (5–8 sentences). Single-sentence paragraphs used for emphasis.

### 2.2 Internal Pattern

- **Typical opening move:** Decisive action or concrete image — rarely a topic sentence; the paragraph begins mid-motion or mid-observation.
- **Typical closing move:** A single striking image, a character reaction, or a short declarative sentence that lands like a gavel. Often the shortest sentence in the paragraph.
- **Paragraph-level rhythm:** Accumulative: medium-length sentences build visual or dramatic detail, then a short final sentence punctuates. Think of bricks stacking, then a capstone.

### 2.3 Transitions Between Paragraphs

- **Dominant method:** Temporal or spatial shift ("Not twenty feet from T'sais…"; "Liane at last stood up…"). Associative leaps are rare; Vance prefers the camera to pan or the clock to tick.
- **Frequency of hard breaks vs. linked flow:** 35% hard / 65% linked

---

## 3. Word Choice

### 3.1 Register & Etymology

- **Latinate vs. Germanic ratio:** 45/55 — Germanic-dominant for action and concrete nouns; Latinate for atmosphere and elevated narration ("enigmatic," "recollection," "revelations," "fabricate").
- **Preferred register:** Elevated-precise. Not archaic per se, but *mannered* — the vocabulary of a well-read raconteur who favors the exact word over the common one.
- **Archaism frequency:** Occasional
  - **Type:** Vocabulary ("castelry," "naught," "hark," "sennight") and selected syntax. Never archaic verb forms (no "hath," "spake"). Archaism is world-flavoring, not structural.

### 3.2 Specificity & Concreteness

- **Abstraction level:** Highly concrete and sensory. Abstract concepts (evil, beauty, meaning) are rendered through concrete observation, not stated directly.
- **Noun specificity:** Maximum — "lichens," "mortar," "flints," "rubble," "tinder," "brand," "ankles." Never "stuff," "things," "area." The general noun is always replaced by the precise one.
- **Verb specificity:** High — "lurched," "strode," "snatched," "babbled," "prodded," "kindled." Plain verbs ("walked," "said," "went") are used only when plainness is the point — usually in dialogue tags or to create contrast with surrounding precision.

### 3.3 Syllable Profile

- **Average syllables per word:** 1.5
- **Polysyllabic word frequency (3+ syllables):** ~10%
- **Placement tendency for polysyllabic words:** Sentence-end or mid-sentence for rhythmic weight, often as the final word of a descriptive clause ("enigmatic," "recollection," "dissatisfaction"). Polysyllabic words are *placed*, not scattered.

### 3.4 Repetition & Variation

- **Deliberate repetition:** Rare but purposeful
  - **Type:** Key-word echo for thematic emphasis ("strange soil… strange air… strange and new"). Anaphora is uncommon; when repetition occurs, it's lexical echo across clauses, not rhetorical patterning.
- **Synonym variation:** Moderate — Vance will reuse a character name or key noun rather than hunt for synonyms, but varies verb choices freely.

### 3.5 Word Selection Method

When generating **new** words not drawn from the sample, select by:

1. **Etymology filter:** Prefer Germanic roots for action/objects, Latinate roots for atmosphere/judgment, consistent with §3.1.
2. **Syllable target:** Default monosyllabic/bisyllabic; deploy a trisyllabic word only when the rhythm demands weight at clause-end.
3. **Sensory channel:** Prioritize visual and tactile vocabulary — Vance's world is *seen* and *touched* before anything else.
4. **Formality gate:** Would this word appear in a well-written 1950s adventure novella? If it sounds like a textbook or a tweet, reject it.
5. **Position rule:** Place heavier/rarer words at clause-end or sentence-end per §3.3 placement tendency. Light words propel; heavy words land.

## 4. Syntax Patterns

### 4.1 Modifier Placement

- **Adjective position:** Predominantly pre-noun; post-noun for atmosphere or when the adjective carries the sentence's weight ("stones blackened now by lichens, by smoke, by age").
- **Typical adjective density:** 1–2 per noun phrase. Stacking beyond two is reserved for set-piece description.
- **Adverb position:** Sentence-initial or post-verb. Adverbs are *placed for rhythm*, not dropped reflexively.
- **Adverb frequency:** Sparing — Vance prefers a precise verb over a modified generic one.

### 4.2 Inversion & Unusual Word Order

- **Frequency:** Occasional
- **Type:** Object-fronting and predicate-adjective fronting ("Nothing in sight, nothing of Earth was raw or harsh"; "Wide-eyed she stared").
- **Trigger context:** Emphasis and rhythm — inversion signals that this detail matters more than surrounding material.

### 4.3 Apposition & Interjection

- **Appositives:** Moderate
  - **Typical position:** Mid-sentence between commas, typically renaming or specifying a character/object just introduced ("Liane the Wayfarer").
- **Parenthetical interjections:** Rare
  - **Punctuation used:** Dashes when present; parentheses avoided entirely.

### 4.4 Parallelism

- **Frequency:** Occasional — used for descriptive accumulation, not rhetorical flourish.
- **Typical form:** Tricolon or extended coordinate list ("the ground, the trees, the rock ledge"; "blackened now by lichens, by smoke, by age").
- **Typical list length when present:** 3 items (tricolon is the default; lists of 4+ are rare).

## 5. Figurative Language

### 5.1 Density

- **Figures per paragraph (average):** 0.5
- **Figures per page (average):** 3–4

### 5.2 Type Preference (ratio)

- **Simile:** 20%
- **Metaphor:** 15%
- **Personification:** 45%
- **Synecdoche/metonymy:** 15%
- **Other (transferred epithet):** 5%

### 5.3 Source Domains

1. **Age/decay/weathering** — the passage of deep time made visible in material surfaces
2. **Light/shadow/atmosphere** — illumination as emotional and narrative signal
3. **Stone/geology/architecture** — the mineral world as substrate of all description
4. **Body/anatomy** — character rendered through physical specifics
5. **Vegetation/organic growth** — grass, trees, moss as markers of time and neglect

### 5.4 Construction Pattern

- **Typical simile form:** Brief "as X" or "like X" — never extended ("treading light as air"). The simile arrives, does its work, and withdraws.
- **Metaphor integration:** Embedded in verb or adjective choice rather than stated outright. "Dense and brooding" trees are personified metaphor delivered as plain description.
- **Length:** Single phrase. Multi-sentence conceits are absent. Vance's figuration is *incidental*, not *structural* — the images serve the scene, never the reverse.

## 6. Punctuation

### 6.1 Comma Behavior

- **Density:** Moderate — approximately 1.5 per sentence
- **Oxford comma:** Yes
- **Comma splices:** Never

### 6.2 Semicolons

- **Frequency:** Moderate
- **Primary use:** Joining parallel independent clauses that describe simultaneous or sequential elements of the same scene ("the man groaned softly; the woman fell to whimpering"). Semicolons *list* related observations without subordinating either.

### 6.3 Dashes

- **Type preferred:** Em dash
- **Frequency:** Moderate
- **Primary use:** Dramatic pause, interruption, or afterthought clarification ("the whole a weird picture in the long light of sunset"). Also used for appositive framing in dialogue.

### 6.4 Colons

- **Frequency:** Rare
- **Primary use:** Introducing a dramatic reveal or elaborating a prior clause.

### 6.5 Other Notable Punctuation

- **Ellipses:** Rare — used for trailing sensory impression or scene transition (the "flight . . . Strange soil" passage), not for hesitation.
- **Exclamation marks:** Moderate in dialogue, absent in narration — Liane's theatrical speech uses them; the narrator never does.
- **Question marks in narration:** Rare — used only for T'sais's internal questions, reflecting her naïveté.

---

## 7. Rhythm & Cadence

### 7.1 Stress Patterns

- **Dominant tendency:** Loosely iambic with frequent trochaic interruptions. The prose has a natural English speech rhythm rather than a metered one.
- **Regularity:** Loosely rhythmic — enough regularity to feel controlled, enough variation to avoid sing-song.

### 7.2 Sentence-Level Pacing

- **Acceleration technique:** Shortening sentences to fragments or bare SVO; removing conjunctions (asyndeton) in action sequences ("He snatched the brand away from the man and tossed it pettishly into the blaze.").
- **Deceleration technique:** Adding prepositional phrases and appositives; stacking coordinate descriptions separated by commas or semicolons.
- **Typical pacing shape within a paragraph:** Slow descriptive build → short punchy close. The paragraph decelerates through accumulation, then *snaps* shut with a brief declarative.

### 7.3 Sound Devices

- **Alliteration:** Occasional — never ostentatious but present ("dense and brooding," "calm giants," "gleeful… glade"). Used for local texture, not as a structural device.
- **Assonance/consonance:** Occasional — more consonance than assonance, especially sibilants and hard consonants in tense passages.
- **Onomatopoeia:** Absent

---

## 8. Dialogue

### 8.1 Tag Behavior

- **Tag frequency:** Most lines tagged
- **Preferred tag verb:** "said" as default; varied tags for characterization — "cried," "laughed," "babbled," "mused," "confided." The varied tag is always *descriptive* of the speech act, never merely decorative.
- **Tag position:** Post-speech dominant; occasional mid-speech for long turns.

### 8.2 Speech Patterns

- **Average speech-turn length:** 20 words / 1–3 sentences
- **Interruption/trailing off:** Rare — characters complete their thoughts; the drama is in *what* they say, not in being cut off.
- **Dialect or idiolect markers:** Heavy — each speaker has a distinct register.
  - **Liane:** Theatrical, self-delighting, florid. Uses exclamations ("Ho!"), rhetorical structures ("I reason, I think, I theorize"), and performative asides ("you see…"). His cruelty is *mannered*.
  - **Captives:** Desperate, plain, clipped. Short sentences, direct pleas.
  - **T'sais / Pandelume:** Formal-terse. Economy of speech reflects alien perspective or authority.

### 8.3 Dialogue-to-Narration Ratio

- **Approximate ratio:** 40% dialogue / 60% narration within dialogue-containing passages. In purely descriptive passages, narration is 100%. Vance alternates dialogue-heavy and description-heavy blocks rather than blending them.

---

## 9. Description Method

### 9.1 Sensory Distribution

- **Visual:** 65%
- **Auditory:** 15%
- **Tactile/kinesthetic:** 12%
- **Olfactory:** 5%
- **Gustatory:** 3%

### 9.2 Camera Behavior

- **Default distance:** Medium — room-scale or clearing-scale, with selective close-ups on specific objects (the effigy, Liane's shoes, the brand from the fire).
- **Movement pattern:** Slow pan across a landscape, then zoom to a single telling detail. The camera *discovers* the scene as the POV character would.
- **Focal priority:** Environment first, then character enters the established space. Characters are *placed into* described settings, not the reverse.

### 9.3 Description Density

- **Lines of pure description before action/dialogue resumes:** 3–6 sentences for set-piece passages; 1–2 sentences for incidental scene-setting.
- **Integration method:** Set-piece blocks for new locations (the ruined castelry passage), then single-sentence brushstrokes woven into action once the scene is established ("He snatched a brand from the fire"). Vance *front-loads* description, then thins it as action takes over.

---

## Usage Notes

When writing new prose using this guide:

1. **Sentence construction:** Build roughly half your sentences as simple SVO. Keep average length around 13 words. Alternate short declarative sentences (3–8 words) with medium descriptive ones (18–30 words). Let the short sentences do the dramatic work.
2. **Word selection:** Run candidate words through the five-step filter in §3.5. Default to concrete, specific, monosyllabic/bisyllabic words. Deploy a Latinate polysyllabic word at clause-end when the rhythm needs weight — never more than one per sentence.
3. **Paragraph shaping:** Open with action or image, not topic sentences. Build through 3–6 sentences of accumulating detail. Close with the shortest, sharpest sentence in the paragraph.
4. **Figurative language:** Keep figures sparse (~0.5 per paragraph). Favor personification embedded in adjective/verb choice over explicit similes. Draw comparisons from age, light, stone, and body. Keep figures to a single phrase — no extended conceits.
5. **Punctuation:** Use semicolons to list parallel observations. Use em dashes for dramatic pause. Keep exclamation marks inside dialogue only. Never use comma splices.
6. **Dialogue:** Give each speaker a distinct register. Use "said" as the default tag but deploy specific tag verbs ("mused," "cried") when they describe the speech act precisely. Alternate dialogue-heavy and description-heavy blocks rather than mixing evenly.
7. **Rhythm check:** Read sentences aloud. Paragraphs should feel like a controlled build followed by a snap. If a paragraph trails off rather than landing, shorten the final sentence. If it feels monotonous, vary sentence length more aggressively.
8. **Description method:** When introducing a new location, write a 4–6 sentence set-piece that pans across the space and zooms to one telling detail. Once the scene is established, fold description into single-sentence brushstrokes within action.
