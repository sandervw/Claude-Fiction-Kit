# Whyneland Cosmology

## The Two Worlds

Reality divides into two mirror worlds. **The Red World** is the world of earth. It is the shaped world. Every furrow, every creek, every buried skeleton, every wattle fence, every child, every mother and father. **The Ochre World** lies beneath, behind, beside, inside. It is a world of the echoes of names, a world of mist, ancestors, of eagles and crows. The Ochre World has its own gravity, distance, and time. A spirit might walk three steps in The Ochre World, and it's Red reflection travel thus a hundred leagues. A shaman in trance might speak in The Ochre World with their ancestor of a century ago, both in the 'present'. These two worlds are the same world, seen with the same eyes, yet with different Spirits. Places such as circles of standing stones, or the centers of frozen lakes in winter, act as portals between the worlds.

## The Forever Wind

Between and through both worlds blows The Forever Wind. The Forever Wind has no source. The Wind simply is, and always was, and carries all things.

The Forever Wind connects all places, times, and both Worlds. Spirits travel through it. It carries omens, weather, voices. When a shaman enters trance, or falls unwillingly into it, their Fehr (Travel-Spirit) slips from the body, travels on the wind, and moves between worlds. Gliding upon The Forever Wind, flying from the Ochre World, Eagles carry new Fehr to infants at birth. Crows travel in the other direction, carrying Fehr out in death.

The Forever Wind is sacred. To speak bitterly of it is ill-luck. To listen is only polite. Shamans learn its tongue: the sharp gust that presages danger, the low moan that speaks of grief in the spirit-realm, the sudden stillness that means some vast spirit travels near.

## The Vormakhans

What are the Vormakhans?

They may be the raw state of existence; before the first green bud sprouted from the Red World's earth, before the first eagle spread its wings on The Forever Wind. Perhaps, when cosmology itself erodes, they will remain. They are not 'creatures'. Some Shamn's say that are raw change. "A Vormakhan is the mountain sliding southward, the fog that does not lift, the river changing course, the forest swallowing fields."

No consensus has there been on what the Vormakhans are, or want, or whether they want anything at all. Some say they are merely continuing, as a glacier, as a tide. Others say that the broken compact is a wound on the Red World, and the Vormakhans are the bleeding that follows. A few Shamans suggest that the Vormakhans are the true state, and that the Red World - the human world - was only ever a dream imposed upon an older, darker truth.

What will happen when the last name is forgotten? When mankind completes its Forgetting, and the Red World turns to wild green?

## The Three Spirits

Every person has three Spirits bound in a single life. **Thrym** (the Pulse) is the life-Spirit. It is breath and warmth, the beat of blood.  Thrym does not survive death, it belongs to the flesh and ends with the flesh. **Fehr** (the Travel-Spirit) is the Spirit proper, the part that moves between worlds. In life the Fehr is anchored to the body, but can slip loose in dreams, in a shamn's trance, sometimes at the liminal hours of dawn and twilight. At death, the Fehr departs permanently — carried by crows on The Forever Wind to the Ochre World. The Travel-Spirit can linger in the Red World as a helper (or a foe).

**Kenn** (the Memory-Spirit) is the essential self. It is the sum of everything that makes a person who they are. When the Kenn dies, it is as the sticking of a hot iron into cold water. All that was warm in a being parts in a great smoke and sizzle. The Fehr ceases to travel on The Forever wind and the end of the Memory-Spirit. When the Kenn dies, so dies the connected spirit.

The Forgetting - the doom of this age - attacks the Kenn while the body lives. Nothing remains but animal instinct wearing a human shape. This is how Sarks are made.

## Spirits

The Worlds teem with spirits. A shaman must learn to distinguish between them, for the approach that appeases one may summon another's ire.
### Helpers

The Fehr of an ancestor, whose rites were observed and name well-venerated, may remain in the Ochre World as a helper. These spirits can be bound into **Fehr-Chests** (Travel-Chests): vessels or dolls of bronze, iron, animal parts, or carved wood. These are a house for the spirit and allow a shaman to call upon its aid. The vessel must be nourished: liquid smeared on its surface, tallow burned before it. A neglected helper weakens, then turns resentful and dangerous. The Ongkhan-hollgh — a bound spirit gone feral — is the result of a Fehr-Chest whose keeper died or forgot their duties. When they are kept happy, a shaman's helper may walk beside them in trance. Some helpers retain skills from life: a warrior-ancestor may lend courage; a healer may reveal the location of a lost Fehr.
### Hostile

**Hollgh** are the angry dead — ancestors whose rest was disturbed, whose burial was careless, or who died with grievances unresolved. They are invisible except in liminal states: the edge of sleep, deep fog, shamanic trance. They attack the senses, causing ringing ears, heavy limbs, dimming sight. An **Ongkhan-hollgh** is a Fehr-Chests gone wrong. It cannot leave its vessel, but it poisons everything nearby: dreams turn foul, sickness lays low a home. Destroying the vessel does not destroy the spirit.
### Indifferent

**Ezn** are master-spirits of places. An Ezn *is* the mountain, in some fundamental sense. They never wander. They do not act directly, but when offended, their domain turns hostile: streams go bitter, paths loop endlessly, weather turns cruel. Shamans spend lifetimes learning the preferences of local Ezn.

The Vormakhans, if they are spirits, belong to no category. They neither help nor harm with intent. They simply are.
## Shamanism

A shaman is one in whom all three spirits may step between between worlds. They ride the Forever Wind into the Ochre World. They may seek knowledge, or go to speak with, heal, or do battle with the other spirits - the living and the not. No one chooses to become a shaman. The calling manifests as illness: vivid dreams, and a sense of weight, or being pulled. Only an experienced shaman can diagnose the condition. Refusing the calling means death.

Accepting the calling is an act of dismemberment and reconstitution. The shaman's Fehr is taken into The House of Insomnia. It remains there for three years of The Ochre World. The shaman watches as their head is cut off, their bones and blood consumed by all manner of pollution. Each torment is an affliction the shaman learns to heal. Powerful shamans undergo this dismemberment three times.

A shaman's spirit is one that may visit the Ochre World willing, though they may go unwilling as any other spirit. Trance and by portal are the most common methods of reaching that other realm. A sick shaman is in great danger, for that is such a state when their travel-spirit may go unwillingly, and become lost.
### Duties

**Maintaining portals and wards.** Standing stones, pure spring waters, all fundamental to keeping the Red World in it's right color. Shamans speak the words and names that keep these liminal places strong.

**Battling foe spirits.** Some spirits are a tumor; there is no cure but to cut them out. When crossing into the Ochre World, some shamans may change their shape to that of certain beasts - birds, deer, rarely a bear - if the occasion calls for it. 

**Preserving lineages.** Names have the power to anchor spirits. Shamans keep the genealogies. They recite them at births, deaths, and during specific ceremonies.

**Healing.** Illness often has spiritual causes. Shamans diagnose the cause and treat it through negotiations, offerings, or retrieval of a lost Spirit-part.

**Speaking with the dead.** Shamans resolve unfinished business, and help the grieving say goodbye.

**Advising leaders.** The shamans' knowledge of the Ochre World makes them valuable advisors. Some use this for personal advantage.
### Equipment

**The Oxbone Flute** is the shaman's primary tool. Its sound carries between worlds; a melody played in the Red World can be heard in the Ochre. The flute summons helpers, soothes hostile spirits, and acts as a boat, which the shaman may row between worlds. Different purposes call for different songs.

**Travel-Chests** house bound helper spirits. Leather pouches, stoppered jars, dolls of feather and claws and bits of fur or plant rope. A shaman may carry several, each containing a different spirit.

**Offerings.** Every shaman carries materials for appeasement: fermented sheep's milk, animal tallow, cat bones or the bones of small dogs, barley grain, sometimes blood, leather scrolls writ upon in odd ink, unbreakable knots.

**Garment.** Often this is a cloak or costume, laden with rope ribbons, copper and iron ornaments, human hair-dolls, occasionally small sets of bone chimes that hand from the sleeves. Some of these items may be travel-chests, while others serve symbolic or defensive purposes.
### Social Role

Shaman motives are not always pure. A shaman's loyalty may lie with the community, with their own lineage, with a particular spirit, or with themselves. The primary services of a shaman include healing, blessing weddings and newlyweds, creating and maintaining protective Fehr-Chests, fortune-telling and divination, hunting magic, weather magic, exorcism, naming children, accompanying the dead, soul retrieval, community welfare ceremonies, and preserving genealogical knowledge.

**The Order of the Bear** is the oldest layer in the hierarchy of the shamans. Bears are considered "most holy of all animals," with a totemistic relationship requiring that bears not kill humans and humans honor bears properly. The bear's resemblance to skinned humans (standing upright, similar muscular structure) reinforce beliefs of kinship. Secret bear names and special hunter language exist to speak about bears without offending them. Bear hibernation is interpreted as death and rebirth.

The plague that followed the broken compact killed many shamans. Those who remain are spread thin. A village that needs a shaman may wait months for one to arrive. And when the shaman does come, the village must pay whatever price is asked.
