# The Shell-Monoliths

## Common Features

**Material:** A pale stone unlike any quarried by man—dense and heavy as marble, but with a grain that suggests growth rather than geological formation. The substance resists chisels and leaves no tool-marks.

**Exterior (Above Ground):** Chalky, sun-bleached, dry. The surface is matte and faintly powdery to the touch, like a shell long separated from the sea. Color ranges from bone-white to pale grey, sometimes with faint banding. Weathering has softened edges and obscured fine detail.

**Interior (Below Ground):** Past the threshold, the stone transforms. Surfaces become smooth, dense, and nacreous—catching torchlight with an oily iridescence that shifts between pale rose, silver, and sea-green. The deeper one descends, the more pronounced this quality becomes.

**Architectural Logic:** No sharp corners. Passages curve rather than angle. Chambers connect sequentially, each a discrete space leading to the next.

**The Vacancy:** All Shell-Monoliths share a quality of abandonment. The architecture implies an occupant, but the occupant is gone.

## The Four Types

---

### 1. Limpet Vaults

**Shell Form:** Limpet—a low, conical dome that clings to stone.

**Surface Structure:** A broad dome of weathered white stone, 40-60 feet tall at the apex, 80-120 feet across at the base. The exterior is ribbed with faint radial ridges, like the striations of a limpet shell. A single low opening faces east—a dark mouth no taller than a man, rounded at the top.

**Interior Structure:** The interior is a single vertical shaft, 30-40 feet in diameter, dropping straight down. A spiral ramp hugs the shaft wall, circling downward into darkness. The shaft descends 150-300 feet. Chambers branch off at irregular intervals—horizontal passages that curve away from the central shaft and terminate in rounded rooms.

**Interior Appearance:** The shaft wall is smooth and nacreous, the spiral ramp worn as if by countless feet (though no human feet shaped that wear). Deeper chambers glow faintly when torchlight enters, the nacre holding and diffusing the light.

**Typical Location:** Rocky coastlines. Hilltops overlooking the sea. Headlands and cliff-edges. Often half-buried in scrub grass, the dome rising like a pale blister from the land.

---

### 2. Spire-Shells

**Shell Form:** Turret snail or auger shell—a tall, spiraling cone.

**Surface Structure:** A tapering spire of pale stone, 60-80 feet tall, rising from a broader base. The exterior shows the ghost of a spiral groove winding from base to apex, weathered nearly flat but still visible in raking light. External steps—shallow ridges—climb the spire's outer face to a small opening at the top. There is no ground-level entrance.

**Interior Structure:** Entry is at the apex. The interior is a spiral passage that corkscrews down through the spire's core, continues past ground level, and plunges another 150-250 feet into the earth. Chambers open off the spiral at quarter-turns, each one broader and stranger than the chamber before it.

**Interior Appearance:** The spiral passage catches light strangely—the nacreous walls create the illusion that the glow comes from ahead, always around the next curve.

**Typical Location:** Inland prominences. Lonely hills on open plains. Ridgelines where they can be seen from leagues away. Rarely near water.

---

### 3. Valve-Crypts

**Shell Form:** Bivalve—two shells hinged together, like an oyster or mussel.

**Surface Structure:** A low, rounded mound, rarely more than 20-30 feet above ground level, but 100-150 feet across—a flattened oval, like half a clamshell, with a ridged hinge on one side. The surface is ribbed with concentric curves radiating from a central point. At that center: a wide, oval opening, 15-20 feet across, exposing a cavity open to the sky.

**Interior Structure:** The upper "valve" is the surface mound. The lower "valve" lies beneath—a mirror-image cavity descending into the earth. Terraced ledges ring the interior of both valves, stepping down toward the center. The descent continues 100-200 feet below ground level, the terraces narrowing as they deepen, converging toward a final central chamber at the nadir. The space between the valves—ground level—is a compressed crawlway, a hinge-point where the two shells nearly meet.

**Interior Appearance:** The open upper cavity is sun-bleached and dry. Below the hinge-point, the nacre begins—faint at first, intensifying with depth. The deepest chambers have the quality of a pearl's interior, curved walls glowing with layered iridescence.

**Typical Location:** Flat lowlands. River valleys. Tidal flats where the sea has retreated. Often partially flooded in wet seasons, the lower chambers filled with black water.

---

### 4. Nautilus Deeps

**Shell Form:** Nautilus—a coiled, chambered spiral.

**Surface Structure:** A massive rounded form, 50-80 feet tall and nearly as wide, coiled in on itself. From above, the spiral is visible—a weathered groove tracing the classic nautilus curve. From ground level, it appears as a pale hump of stone, bulging and asymmetrical. The entrance is at the spiral's outer terminus: a curved opening at ground level, shaped like a crescent moon laid on its back. The majority of the structure lies buried.

**Interior Structure:** The interior follows the spiral inward. The passage curves continuously, never straight, narrowing gradually as it coils toward the center. It descends through chambers, as the earth that has buried the structures, slopes and diminishes down the further in one goes. Chambers are sequential—each a discrete, rounded room separated from the next by a narrowed threshold (a "septum" in the shell). The spiral makes 4-6 full rotations before reaching the central chamber. Each chamber is smaller than the last until the final one, which opens into a space larger and deeper than any before it.

**Interior Appearance:** The septa between chambers are the most intensely nacreous surfaces in any Monolith—they glow like mother-of-pearl even in dim light. The chambers themselves are smooth-walled and round, acoustically strange. Sound does not carry between chambers; each is isolated.

**Typical Location:** Forested uplands. Deep valleys. Places where the land folds and hides. Often overgrown, the coiled form mistaken for a natural hill until one finds the entrance.
