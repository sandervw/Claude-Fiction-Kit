# Style Guide: Jack Vance — *T'sais*

> **Source sample:** "T'sais" from *The Dying Earth*, Jack Vance, ~1,800 words (OCR conversion, minor artifacts)
> **Genre context:** Dying-earth fantasy, planetary romance
> **Narrative POV:** Third-person limited (close to T'sais)

---

## 1. Sentence Structure

### 1.1 Length

- **Average sentence length:** 18 words
- **Range:** 5–42 words
- **Standard deviation:** ~10 words
- **Distribution shape:** Bimodal — short declarative punches (5–10 words) alternating with long, clause-heavy sweeps (25–42 words). Dialogue sentences skew shorter; descriptive narration skews longer.

### 1.2 Sentence Types (ratio)

- **Simple (single independent clause):** 25%
- **Compound (coordinated independent clauses):** 30%
- **Complex (independent + dependent clause):** 25%
- **Compound-complex:** 20%

### 1.3 Opening Patterns

- **Subject-first (SVO):** 55% — the dominant mode, especially with proper names or pronouns ("T'sais closed her eyes"; "She rode deep in thought")
- **Prepositional/adverbial opener:** 15% — used for spatial or temporal framing ("In a moment"; "On the bench to the left")
- **Participial phrase opener:** 5% — rare, reserved for simultaneous action
- **Dependent clause opener:** 10% — conditional or temporal subordination ("Should you be so unfortunate"; "As she was bid")
- **Inverted/verb-first:** 3% — reserved for dramatic emphasis or archaic coloring
- **Conjunction opener (And, But, For, etc.):** 2% — very rare; Vance prefers semicolons or new sentences
- **Other (dialogue tags, existential "There was"):** 10%

### 1.4 Clause Stacking

- **Typical clause count per sentence:** 1–3
- **Stacking method:** Right-branching chains linked by commas and coordinating conjunctions. Clauses accrete detail laterally rather than nesting deeply. Lists of sensory impressions are joined by "and" in polysyndetic chains: "first a green beam flashed on her, then ultramarine, and topaz and ruby red, and the landscape changed."
- **Maximum comfortable depth before resolution:** 4 clauses — beyond this, Vance resolves with a period or semicolon rather than embedding further.

## 2. Paragraph Structure

### 2.1 Length

- **Average paragraph length:** 4 sentences / 65 words
- **Range:** 1–8 sentences
- **Distribution shape:** Highly variable. Single-sentence paragraphs for dramatic beats or dialogue; longer paragraphs for descriptive set-pieces and exposition. No predictable alternation.

### 2.2 Internal Pattern

- **Typical opening move:** Action or scene-setting detail anchored to the POV character ("T'sais came riding from the grove"; "A bank of dark trees rose above her").
- **Typical closing move:** A trailing image, sensory detail, or consequence that lingers ("all changing in hue as the light changed in the sky"). Dialogue paragraphs often end on a resonant philosophical statement.
- **Paragraph-level rhythm:** Accumulative — begins with a grounding action, layers on sensory or emotional detail, and resolves with a culminating image or pivot. Think of it like a wave that crests at the final clause.

### 2.3 Transitions Between Paragraphs

- **Dominant method:** Spatial shift (character moves to new location or turns attention) and temporal progression ("Presently"; implied passage of time).
- **Frequency of hard breaks vs. linked flow:** 40% hard / 60% linked — Vance frequently carries momentum across paragraph breaks through continued action, but allows clean scene-cuts when the character moves between locations.

## 3. Word Choice

### 3.1 Register & Etymology

- **Latinate vs. Germanic ratio:** 55/45 Latinate-leaning — Latinate words dominate in abstract/philosophical passages ("intelligence," "personality," "expressly," "intimacy"), while Germanic words anchor concrete physical action ("rode," "struck," "sheathed," "clink").
- **Preferred register:** Elevated-literary with a cool, precise detachment. Not florid or overwrought — Vance is lapidary, choosing each word for maximum economy and tonal precision. The effect is formality without stiffness.
- **Archaism frequency:** Occasional
  - **Type:** Vocabulary ("rapier," "manse," "sennight" in broader Vance), syntax ("made to strike at it"), and dialogue phrasing ("Cannot you change me?"; "Am I not human?"). Archaism concentrates in dialogue, lending characters a courtly formality.

### 3.2 Specificity & Concreteness

- **Abstraction level:** Predominantly concrete and sensory, punctuated by abstract philosophical statements in dialogue. Narration is almost entirely grounded in what can be seen, heard, or touched.
- **Noun specificity:** High — "rapier" not "sword," "settee" not "seat," "daisies, blue-bells, Judas-creeper, orange sunbursts" not "flowers." Vance names things precisely, often with invented proper nouns for texture.
- **Verb specificity:** Moderate-to-high — "rasped," "stifled," "flitted," "stirred" over generic alternatives. Verbs carry sensory freight but are not overwrought. Vance prefers one exact verb to an adverb-modified generic one.

### 3.3 Syllable Profile

- **Average syllables per word:** 1.5
- **Polysyllabic word frequency (3+ syllables):** ~12%
- **Placement tendency for polysyllabic words:** Sentence-middle or in appositives and subordinate clauses. Terminal positions tend toward shorter, punchier words. Polysyllables appear in clusters within philosophical or magical exposition ("intelligence," "personality," "incantations").

### 3.4 Repetition & Variation

- **Deliberate repetition:** Rare
  - **Type:** When present, key-word echo across sentences for thematic emphasis ("beauty" recurs as a motif in the Pandelume dialogue; "evil" echoes through T'sais' moral awakening). Not anaphoric or incantatory — more like a philosophical refrain.
- **Synonym variation:** Moderate — Vance will reuse a character name or key noun rather than strain for synonyms, but varies verbs and modifiers freely. He never uses a clumsy pronoun to avoid repeating a name.

### 3.5 Word Selection Method

When generating **new** words not drawn from the sample, select by:

1. **Etymology filter:** Prefer Latinate roots for abstract/magical/philosophical contexts, Germanic for physical action and landscape. Mix freely in descriptive passages.
2. **Syllable target:** Default to 1–2 syllable words; deploy 3+ syllable words sparingly and in mid-sentence positions for rhythmic weight.
3. **Sensory channel:** Prioritize visual vocabulary (color, light, shape, distance), with secondary emphasis on tactile and auditory.
4. **Formality gate:** Would this word appear in a 1950s adventure story written by an erudite author? If it sounds like journalism, genre pulp, or modern casual prose, reject it.
5. **Position rule:** Place heavier or rarer words mid-sentence in appositives or subordinate clauses; end sentences on concrete, shorter words for clean resolution.

## 4. Syntax Patterns

### 4.1 Modifier Placement

- **Adjective position:** Predominantly pre-noun, often in pairs ("dark trees," "smoky wood," "sardonic face," "heavy silence"). Post-noun placement rare but used for archaic flavor or emphasis.
- **Typical adjective density:** 1–2 per noun phrase on average. Vance avoids stacking more than two adjectives; he prefers one precise modifier to three vague ones.
- **Adverb position:** Pre-verb or sentence-initial ("slowly," "presently," "tightly"). Sentence-final adverbs are rare.
- **Adverb frequency:** Sparing — Vance trusts his verbs. Adverbs appear perhaps once every 3–4 sentences, usually for temporal pacing ("presently," "slowly") rather than manner.

### 4.2 Inversion & Unusual Word Order

- **Frequency:** Occasional — roughly once per page-equivalent
- **Type:** Predicate-adjective fronting ("alien was it to her mind"); object-fronting in dialogue ("Cannot you change me?"); verb-subject inversion after adverbials ("Presently there was").
- **Trigger context:** Elevated or formal passages, especially dialogue with Pandelume. Narration inversions are subtle and rhythmically motivated rather than theatrical.

### 4.3 Apposition & Interjection

- **Appositives:** Moderate — used to define or recharacterize in a single efficient stroke ("a high-ceilinged room, bare except for a padded settee, a dim tapestry").
- **Typical position:** Mid-sentence between commas, or sentence-final as a trailing elaboration.
- **Parenthetical interjections:** Rare
  - **Punctuation used:** Commas and dashes. No parentheses in narration.

### 4.4 Parallelism

- **Frequency:** Occasional — more prominent in dialogue and philosophical exposition than in narration.
- **Typical form:** Tricolon and paired clauses with balanced antithesis ("The red glared, the green stifled, the blues and purples hinted at mysteries"). Lists in narration accumulate via polysyndeton ("and topaz and ruby red, and the landscape changed").
- **Typical list length when present:** 3–4 items

## 5. Figurative Language

### 5.1 Density

- **Figures per paragraph (average):** 0.8 — not every paragraph contains a figure; Vance deploys them selectively for peak moments of description.
- **Figures per page (average):** 3–4

### 5.2 Type Preference (ratio)

- **Simile:** 40% — Vance's dominant mode ("like a vast expanse of windy water"; "wings patterned like a precious rug")
- **Metaphor:** 30% — often embedded in verb choice ("rasped her nerves"; "a weight was gone from the air")
- **Personification:** 15% — applied to landscape, light, and color ("the green stifled"; "the blues and purples hinted")
- **Synecdoche/metonymy:** 10% — naming a part for the whole, especially with light and color standing in for mood
- **Other (synesthesia):** 5% — cross-sensory descriptions where light "rasps" or color "stifles"

### 5.3 Source Domains

1. **Water/weather** — sky as windy water, rippling, flooding
2. **Textiles/craft** — wings like a precious rug, woven tapestry
3. **Light/optics** — refracted, beams, shifting hues, tintings
4. **Architecture/stone** — manse, smoky wood, high-ceilinged
5. **Anatomy/sensation** — nerves rasped, eyes tricked, brain as flaw-vessel

### 5.4 Construction Pattern

- **Typical simile form:** "like X" with a concrete, often luxurious vehicle — the comparison is brief (one phrase), vivid, and unexpected. Vance rarely extends a simile beyond a single clause.
- **Metaphor integration:** Embedded in verb choice rather than stated as equation. Vance doesn't say "the light was a rasp"; he says "they rasped her nerves." The metaphor lives in the predicate.
- **Length:** Single phrase or clause. Extended conceits are absent — Vance's figurative language is a flash, not a meditation.

## 6. Punctuation

### 6.1 Comma Behavior

- **Density:** Heavy — approximately 3–4 per sentence in longer constructions
- **Oxford comma:** Yes — consistent in lists
- **Comma splices:** Deliberate stylistic choice — used for compressed parallel action ("They rasped her nerves, confused her vision"; "She dismounted, walked slowly to the door")

### 6.2 Semicolons

- **Frequency:** Moderate — roughly 1 per 8–10 sentences
- **Primary use:** Joining parallel independent clauses that share thematic content, especially in Pandelume's speech ("I am Master Mathematician, the first since Phandaal, yet I can do nothing to your brain without destroying your intelligence, your personality, your soul"). Also used to separate list items in extended enumerations.

### 6.3 Dashes

- **Type preferred:** Em dash
- **Frequency:** Rare — 2–3 in the entire sample
- **Primary use:** Interrupted thought or dramatic parenthetical insertion, particularly in dialogue where a speaker shifts mid-sentence or the narrator interjects qualifying information.

### 6.4 Colons

- **Frequency:** Rare
- **Primary use:** Introducing a dramatic revelation or elaboration, typically in dialogue ("my advice, which is this: Beware of men").

### 6.5 Other Notable Punctuation

- **Ellipses:** Moderate — used for trailing thought and pacing, particularly to suggest vastness or unfinished implication (". . . Earth is dying and in —"). Vance uses spaced dots (. . .) rather than tight ellipses (…).
- **Exclamation marks:** Rare — restricted to dialogue in moments of genuine emotional urgency ("Cannot you change me?"). Never in narration.
- **Question marks in narration:** Absent — questions appear only in dialogue.

## 7. Rhythm & Cadence

### 7.1 Stress Patterns

- **Dominant tendency:** Loosely iambic flow with dactylic variation in descriptive passages. The prose has a musical quality without being metrically rigid — think of it as prose that remembers it was once poetry.
- **Regularity:** Loosely rhythmic — enough regularity to create a hypnotic quality, enough variation to avoid sing-song. Dialogue is more irregular; description more cadenced.

### 7.2 Sentence-Level Pacing

- **Acceleration technique:** Shortening clauses, comma splices compressing two actions into one sentence, asyndeton in climactic moments.
- **Deceleration technique:** Polysyndetic "and...and...and" chains that accumulate detail; appositives and trailing subordinate clauses that delay resolution; long vowel sounds in key words.
- **Typical pacing shape within a paragraph:** Slow build to a culminating image or statement. Paragraphs tend to begin with grounded, mid-tempo action and expand outward into richer description before closing on a resonant note. The final sentence is often the longest or most imagistically dense.

### 7.3 Sound Devices

- **Alliteration:** Occasional — present but never foregrounded ("shifting lights," "smoky wood," "slim rapier"). Subtle enough to register as texture, not as device.
- **Assonance/consonance:** Occasional — Vance pays attention to vowel music in descriptive passages ("ultramarine, and topaz and ruby red" — the open vowels create spaciousness).
- **Onomatopoeia:** Rare — "clink of metal," "shrilling." Used only for specific physical sounds, never for atmospheric effect.

## 8. Dialogue

### 8.1 Tag Behavior

- **Tag frequency:** Sparse — many exchanges are tagless, relying on paragraph breaks and context to identify speakers.
- **Preferred tag verb:** "said" and "called" dominate. Occasional "cried" or "mused" for emotional coloring. No exotic tag verbs (no "growled," "hissed," "intoned").
- **Tag position:** Post-speech, usually as brief as possible ("said Pandelume"; "cried T'sais"). Mid-speech tags are very rare.

### 8.2 Speech Patterns

- **Average speech-turn length:** 30–50 words for Pandelume's philosophical speeches; 8–15 words for T'sais' responses. The asymmetry reflects their power dynamic — the mentor orates, the student questions.
- **Interruption/trailing off:** Rare — trailing off occurs via ellipsis in Pandelume's speech to suggest things left unsaid or too vast to articulate.
- **Dialect or idiolect markers:** Light — both characters use a formal, slightly archaic register. Pandelume's speech is distinguished by longer, more complex constructions and a tone of weary authority. T'sais speaks in shorter, more direct sentences inflected by urgency and emotional intensity.

### 8.3 Dialogue-to-Narration Ratio

- **Approximate ratio:** 45% dialogue / 55% narration in dialogue-containing passages. Vance interleaves action and description between speech turns rather than running extended unbroken exchanges.

## 9. Description Method

### 9.1 Sensory Distribution

- **Visual:** 65% — color, light, shape, and spatial arrangement dominate overwhelmingly. Vance's prose is a painter's prose.
- **Auditory:** 15% — bells, voices, shrilling, clink of metal. Sound is specific and usually tied to a concrete source.
- **Tactile/kinesthetic:** 12% — warmth of the sword, stirring of knees, quivering. Present but subordinate to vision.
- **Olfactory:** 5% — minimal in this sample; implied by "smoky wood" but rarely foregrounded.
- **Gustatory:** 3% — essentially absent.

### 9.2 Camera Behavior

- **Default distance:** Medium — room-scale or landscape-scale, with the POV character anchoring the frame. Vance describes what T'sais sees from her position rather than zooming to extreme close-up or pulling to bird's-eye.
- **Movement pattern:** Slow pan following the character's movement and gaze. The "camera" tracks T'sais as she rides, dismounts, enters — description unfolds as she encounters it spatially, not as a static panorama.
- **Focal priority:** Environment first, then character reaction. Vance establishes the visual field (sky, colors, landscape), then shows T'sais' response to it. The world is presented before the character's interiority.

### 9.3 Description Density

- **Lines of pure description before action/dialogue resumes:** 2–4 sentences on average. Vance rarely indulges in extended static description; even his most visual passages are anchored to character movement.
- **Integration method:** Woven into action — description is delivered mid-motion, as the character moves through the world. Single-sentence brushstrokes rather than set-piece blocks. The opening paragraphs are exemplary: T'sais rides, and the landscape reveals itself through the riding.

## Usage Notes

When writing new prose using this guide:

1. **Sentence construction:** Alternate short declarative sentences (5–10 words) with longer, clause-rich descriptive sentences (20–40 words). Favor right-branching structures where detail accretes after the main clause. Open most sentences with the subject; vary ~25% with prepositional or adverbial openers.
2. **Word selection:** Run candidate words through the five-step filter in §3.5. Default to concrete, specific nouns and vivid single verbs over adverb-modified generics. Keep the register elevated but never overwrought — the goal is precision, not ornamentation.
3. **Paragraph shaping:** Begin with a grounding action or spatial anchor. Layer sensory detail in accumulative fashion. Close on the strongest image or most resonant statement. Vary paragraph length freely.
4. **Figurative language:** Deploy ~1 figure per paragraph, favoring brief similes with unexpected but precise vehicles. Embed metaphors in verb choice rather than stating them as equations. Draw comparisons from water, textiles, light, and architecture.
5. **Punctuation:** Use commas liberally, including deliberate comma splices for compressed parallel action. Employ semicolons to join thematically paired clauses. Use ellipses (spaced: . . .) for trailing implication. Exclamation marks only in urgent dialogue.
6. **Rhythm check:** Read sentences aloud. Descriptive passages should flow with a loose iambic cadence. Paragraphs should build from mid-tempo to a culminating final sentence. Polysyndetic "and" chains should create a sense of accumulation, not breathlessness.
7. **Dialogue:** Keep tags minimal ("said," "called"). Give authority figures longer, more syntactically complex speeches. Give emotionally urgent characters shorter, more direct lines. Interleave narration between speech turns.
8. **Tone:** Maintain cool, precise detachment in narration — the narrator observes with a lapidary eye, never editorializing or emoting. The emotional register lives in what is described, not in how the narrator feels about it. Think: a jeweler examining a gemstone, not a poet weeping over it.
