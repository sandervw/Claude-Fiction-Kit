# Dialogue Voice Guidelines: Brother Gerome (Clark Ashton Smith, "The Beast of Averoigne")

Use to rewrite dialogue and narration in the voice of a learned scholar, weighted with archaism.

---

## Core Prose Posture

- **Formal chronicle, not speech.** Lines read as if written for posterity in a manuscript. Even a shouted warning is phrased as deposition.
- **Penitential hedging.** Statements are framed as confessions, admissions, or pious qualifications. Certainty is rare; humility before God is constant.
- **Latinate, ecclesiastical register.** Vocabulary leans on Church Latin, theological abstractions, and learned polysyllables. Short Saxon words appear only where dignity permits.
- **Biblical rhythm.** Sentences chain with "and," "for," "yet," and "nor." Clauses accumulate. The King James Old Testament is the distant metronome.
- **Dread without modern affect.** Horror is registered through elevated diction and theological vocabulary. "I was afraid" is forbidden; "I was nigh dead with fear" is permitted.

---

## Sentence Structure

### Signature Pattern 1: The Preamble with Buried Fact

> "I, a poor scrivener and the humblest monk of the Benedictine Abbey of Perigon, have been asked by our abbot Theophile to write down this record of a strange evil that is still rampant, still unquelled."

**Pattern:** [Self-effacing identification] + [formal occasion] + [understated horror as the payload]

The information arrives last, after the speaker has performed humility.

### Signature Pattern 2: Polysyndetic Chains

> "The day had vanished, fading unaware; and the long summer eve, without moon, had thickened to a still and eldritch darkness ere I approached the abbey postern."

Chain independent clauses with "and." Semicolons handle the heavier joints. A sentence with three or four "and" junctions is normal. Never replace "and" with bullet points or staccato fragments.

### Signature Pattern 3: The Inverted Clause and Fronted Adverbial

> "To me, for my sins and unworthiness, was it first given to behold the beast."

> "Verily, they said, a creature from the Pit was abroad."

**Pattern:** [Fronted prepositional or adverbial phrase] + [inverted verb/subject] + [actual content]

Modern order ("It was first given to me to behold the beast") kills the voice. Always consider whether the sentence can be turned inside out.

### Signature Pattern 4: The Hedged Assertion

> "Some say that the horror came from the comet, flying without wings to earth across the stars."

> "As for me, I must deem that the beast is a spawn of the seventh hell."

Phrases like "it may be that," "I must deem," "some say," "methinks," "if this be possible," "as it seemed," and "so it is said" are load-bearing. Direct assertions feel bald and wrong in this voice.

### Signature Pattern 5: The Embedded Relative Clause

> "the new comet, which seemed to pursue me as I went"

> "a wide gash that had laid bare the spine from neck to tail; and the spine itself had been shattered and the white marrow sucked therefrom"

The relative clause is where horror detail lives. Never front-load the horror; trail it behind a dignified noun.

## Word Choice and Diction

### Register: Archaic-Ecclesiastical

The baseline is the English of a 19th-century pastiche of a 14th-century monastery: not actual Middle English, but a literary register that sounds old without becoming unreadable.

**Preferred vocabulary:** ere, anon, wont, albeit, verily, truly, surely, whilom, henceforth, thereunto, therefrom, whereof, wherewith, hither, thither, yonder, fain, meseems, nigh, beholden, betimes, sennight, aforesaid, foregoing, haply, perchance, mayhap, behold, lo, forsooth, scrivener, deposition, rutilant, coeval, corposant, pernoctation, depredation, ostent, energumen, cerements, marauding, loathly, baleful, fell, unquelled, unhallowed, eldritch, fenfire, foulness, abomination, perdition.

**Avoid:** contractions, modern slang, casual intensifiers ("really," "very," "pretty"), clinical jargon, emotional directness ("I felt sad," "I was scared"), phrasal verbs where a single Latinate verb will do ("find out" becomes "discover"; "look into" becomes "scrutinize").

### Distinctive Lexical Habit 1: The Compound Adjective

Hyphenated compound adjectives yoke a concrete noun to a modifier.

- "flame-blent ooze"
- "bough-black night"
- "night-born terror"
- "hunchback oaks"
- "pit-deep shadows"
- "moonless hills"

Coin them freely. Formula: [noun]-[adjective/participle] or [adjective]-[noun-derived adjective].

### Distinctive Lexical Habit 2: Theological and Infernal Reference

- Fear becomes "the knowledge of perdition."
- A bad omen becomes "a harbinger of ill, an omen of death and Satanry to come."
- An evil place becomes "the seventh hell," "Malebolge," "Gehenna," "the Pit."
- Wickedness gets named after demons: "the minions of Asmodai," "a spawn of the seventh hell."
- Light becomes "a hellish nimbus," "a wizard's brazier."

Pagan/classical and Christian references mingle freely.

### Distinctive Lexical Habit 3: Understatement Through Elevation

- Being eaten becomes "the doom of the stag."
- Being drunk becomes "I had drunk the mellow white wine of Ste. Zenobie with its kindly people."
- A wound becomes "a wide gash that had laid bare the spine."
- Dying becomes "had been done to death."
- Walking becomes "sallied forth" or "went forth."

## Rhetorical Tactics

1. **Humility framing.** Open with self-deprecation to earn the right to testify. "I, for my sins and unworthiness..." "Doubtless because I had done these things..."
2. **Attribution laundering.** Uncertain claims get attributed to "some say," "it is rumored," "the common report," so the speaker never quite owns them.
3. **Accumulating clauses toward revelation.** Build through subordinate clauses and postpone the horrifying detail to the sentence's end.
4. **Theological gloss.** Reframe any event in terms of sin, punishment, providence, or demonic agency. The spiritual explanation subordinates the mundane one.
5. **Parenthetical piety.** Drop small prayers inside sentences: "by the mercy of God," "whom Christ preserve," "if it be His will."
6. **The pivot on "yet" or "natheless."** Introduce counter-evidence with an archaic adversative, not "but" alone.
7. **Temporal distancing.** Recent events are framed as already past and almost legendary: "it was on that occasion that...," "in those days...," "ere the summer had waned..."

## Recurring Phrase Patterns

| Pattern                                | Example                                                             | Usage                                                |
| -------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| "I, [humble self-description], ..."    | "I, a poor scrivener and the humblest of my order, must confess..." | Opening depositions, testimony, or admissions        |
| "Ere [event], ..."                     | "Ere I had done speaking, the bells rang for prime."                | Temporal subordination; replaces "before"            |
| "And [verb] I [object]..."             | "And saw I then the shadow upon the stones."                        | Inverted narrative clause for rhythmic weight        |
| "For my sins..."                       | "For my sins, the task fell to me."                                 | Framing any burden as deserved punishment            |
| "It may be that..." / "I must deem..." | "It may be that the hour of the Pit is upon us."                    | Hedging assertions, preserving humility before truth |
| "None could [verb]..."                 | "None could name the thing, nor say whence it came."                | Establishing collective ignorance; raising dread     |
| "Verily, [clause]..."                  | "Verily, the sign was upon him."                                    | Solemn affirmation; archaic replacement for "indeed" |
| "[Noun], whose [clause]..."            | "A silence, whose weight lay heavy on the stones, fell upon us."    | Trailing horror detail after a dignified noun        |

## Figurative Language

- **Density:** Moderate to heavy. Nearly every significant noun gets qualified by simile, metaphor, or compound adjective.
- **Source domains:** Hellfire and damnation (brazier, Pit, Gehenna), astronomy and astrology (comet, constellation, signs), medieval bestiary (serpent, viper, bat, wolf), ecclesiastical ritual (corposant, nimbus, cerements), medieval alchemy (ooze, distillation, vapor), and the natural world rendered sinister (hunchback oaks, moonless hills).
- **Off-limits:** Modern mechanical imagery, sports metaphors, commerce, cheerful pastoral without ironic darkening, sentimentality, clinical precision. No "like a machine," no "it hit me like a truck," no "my heart was full."
- **Preferred simile frame:** "like a [archaic noun] of [archaic noun]," e.g., "like a fenfire in the marshes," "like the hair of Satan trailing on the wind of Gehenna."

---

## Rhythm and Flow

- **Default sentence length:** Long. A typical sentence runs 30 to 60 words. Short sentences exist only as punctuation, never as default.
- **Clause construction:** Chain with semicolons and "and." Prefer the semicolon for heavy joints and the comma for light ones. Parenthetical asides should be pious, not witty.
- **Where information lands:** At the end. Structure is always [preamble] + [development] + [revelation].
- **Punctuation of emphasis:** No exclamation marks. Dread is conveyed through vocabulary and cadence. An exclamation breaks the chronicle frame.
- **Paragraph shape:** Open with a temporal or situational marker ("On the morrow...," "Toward evening...," "Now, as I passed...") and close with a theological gloss or summary reflection.

---

## What This Voice Is NOT

- **Not Lovecraftian pastiche.** Smith is more Latinate and more Catholic than Lovecraft, and far less clinical. No "non-Euclidean," no "eldritch geometries," no gibbering narrators. Gerome stays composed even in terror because composure is a spiritual discipline.
- **Not Ye Olde Englishe.** Do not use "thou," "hast," "dost," "wilt." Smith writes pseudo-medieval in a 19th-century literary register, not actual Early Modern English. The archaism is vocabulary and cadence, not verb conjugation. "You have" stays "you have."
- **Not horror-genre directness.** No "I screamed in terror." No "the creature lunged." Horror is described at one remove, through dignified circumlocution. A lunge becomes "it sprang upon us with swiftness as of the kindling flame."
- **Not Victorian floweriness for its own sake.** Every ornament must carry horror, piety, or both. If a flourish does not darken or humble, cut it.
- **Not personality-locked.** This is a prose style, not a character. Any speaker can be rewritten into this voice; all will sound like penitent chroniclers. The underlying content of the dialogue must survive intact beneath the archaic surface.

---

## Quick Test

A line in this voice should pass all four checks:

1. Could it plausibly appear in a 14th-century monk's deposition as written by a 1930s pulp fantasist?
2. Does it contain at least one archaic or Latinate word a modern speaker would not use?
3. Is the key information delayed rather than front-loaded?
4. Would removing contractions and adding "and" or "ere" make it *more* natural to this voice, not less?

If yes to all four, the line is in voice. If no to any, revise.
