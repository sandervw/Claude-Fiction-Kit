# Gittes Style

Cynical PI inner monologue. Think a sharp-dressed gumshoe narrating his own downfall with equal parts suspicion and wounded professional pride.

## Process

### 1. Strip to Skeleton

- Note core beats in plain language
- Think "what actually happened vs. how a suspicious detective would read the scene"
- Identify: the lie, who benefits, what doesn't add up

### 2. Set Narrator Voice

- **POV**: 1st person, unbroken stream of consciousness
- **Attitude**: Professional pride first, morality reframed as stubbornness or spite
- **Persona**: Streetwise and perceptive but perpetually one step behind; sharp reads on people derailed by overconfidence in his own conclusions
- **Technique**: Pivot rapidly between keen observation and premature accusation — noticing the right detail, then jumping to the wrong theory about it

### 3. Apply Hard-Boiled Diction

- Trade dramatic language for dry, weary, transactional language
- Epic events become angles to work or messes to clean up ("apocalypse" → "somebody's making a fortune off this and I'd like to know who")
- Danger triggers irritation and professional indignation, not fear — threats are insults to his competence
- Sprinkle surveillance metaphors — framing, exposure, developing, focus, blind spots — then undercut them with something crassly practical

### 4. Rhythm: Circling, Not Linear

- Thoughts that double back, re-examine, and interrogate themselves
- Internal cross-examination (asking the same question three different ways, catching his own lies)
- **Test**: Read aloud. If it sounds poetic or polished, rough it up with a blunt aside.
- **Technique**: Use short, clipped sentences punctuated by longer grudging admissions — the rhythm of a man who'd rather not say what he's thinking

### 5. Reframe All Agency

- Heroic actions become professional obligation or refusal to be made a fool of
- Other characters are either lying, hiding something, or too rich to be trusted
- No idealism — good deeds are rationalized as protecting his reputation or settling a score
- The narrator is always the mark who figured it out too late, never the knight who saved anyone
