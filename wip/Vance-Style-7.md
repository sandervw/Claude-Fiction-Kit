# Style Guide: Jack Vance — *T'sais* (The Dying Earth)

> **Source sample:** "T'sais," Jack Vance, ~750 words (excerpt)
> **Genre context:** Dying-earth science fantasy; mannered adventure with sorcerous intrigue
> **Narrative POV:** Third-person limited, shifting focus between characters; omniscient intrusions rare but present

---

## 1. Sentence Structure

### 1.1 Length

- **Average sentence length:** 12 words
- **Range:** 4–27 words
- **Standard deviation:** ~5 words
- **Distribution shape:** Tight cluster around 8–16 words with occasional long sweeps (20+); short punches (4–7 words) used for dramatic beats and action transitions

### 1.2 Sentence Types (ratio)

- **Simple (single independent clause):** 50%
- **Compound (coordinated independent clauses):** 20%
- **Complex (independent + dependent clause):** 22%
- **Compound-complex:** 8%

### 1.3 Opening Patterns

- **Subject-first (SVO):** 60%
- **Prepositional/adverbial opener:** 10%
- **Participial phrase opener:** 2%
- **Dependent clause opener:** 8%
- **Inverted/verb-first:** 2%
- **Conjunction opener (And, But, For, Thus, etc.):** 15%
- **Other (absolute construction, interjection):** 3%

### 1.4 Clause Stacking

- **Typical clause count per sentence:** 1–2
- **Stacking method:** Right-branching chains joined by "and"; absolute phrases appended after commas rather than nested embedding; participial phrases trail the main clause like afterthoughts
- **Maximum comfortable depth before resolution:** 3 clauses — Vance resolves quickly and rarely suspends the reader in deep subordination

---

## 2. Paragraph Structure

### 2.1 Length

- **Average paragraph length:** 3 sentences / 35 words
- **Range:** 1–6 sentences
- **Distribution shape:** Highly variable; many single-sentence paragraphs used as action beats or pivot points, alternating with denser paragraphs of 4–6 sentences for scene-setting or exposition

### 2.2 Internal Pattern

- **Typical opening move:** Direct continuation of action or dialogue; rarely a topic sentence — the paragraph picks up mid-stride
- **Typical closing move:** A clinching image or terse action beat; often a single vivid sensory detail that seals the moment ("eyes smouldering," "a slight smile could be seen")
- **Paragraph-level rhythm:** Short opener, brief elaboration, sharp close — like a fencer's advance, feint, strike

### 2.3 Transitions Between Paragraphs

- **Dominant method:** Direct continuation of action or dialogue exchange; temporal markers ("At length") used sparingly for scene jumps
- **Frequency of hard breaks vs. linked flow:** 25% hard / 75% linked

---

## 3. Word Choice

### 3.1 Register & Etymology

- **Latinate vs. Germanic ratio:** 35/65 Germanic-dominant in action and narration; Latinate words reserved for abstraction and sorcerous concepts ("volition," "circumvented," "nullified," "immobile")
- **Preferred register:** Elevated-precise — not archaic, but deliberately formal; a sense of a mannered narrator who chooses each word with cool exactness
- **Archaism frequency:** Occasional
  - **Type:** Adverbial phrases ("the while," "hence," "ere"); occasional syntactic inversions; vocabulary archaisms are light — the effect is more *elevated* than *antique*

### 3.2 Specificity & Concreteness

- **Abstraction level:** Highly concrete and sensory in action; abstract nouns appear almost exclusively in sorcerous/philosophical contexts ("volition," "magic," "will")
- **Noun specificity:** High — "crags," "poplars," "moor," "terrace" rather than "rocks," "trees," "field," "porch"
- **Verb specificity:** Very high — "strode," "unclamped," "lunged," "smouldering," "skipping" rather than generic movement verbs. Vance's verbs do double duty, conveying both action and character.

### 3.3 Syllable Profile

- **Average syllables per word:** 1.4
- **Polysyllabic word frequency (3+ syllables):** ~8%
- **Placement tendency for polysyllabic words:** Sentence-end or pre-caesura for rhythmic weight; polysyllabic words often land in apposition or absolute phrases ("the sweetly impudent face," "a creature without volition")

### 3.4 Repetition & Variation

- **Deliberate repetition:** Rare
  - **Type:** Key-word echo for thematic emphasis ("face" recurs deliberately as the contested object; "volition"/"will" paired); character names repeated rather than pronoun-substituted when multiple characters are present
- **Synonym variation:** Moderate — Vance reuses key nouns deliberately but varies verbs and modifiers

### 3.5 Word Selection Method

When generating **new** words not drawn from the sample, select by:

1. **Etymology filter:** Prefer Germanic roots for action/physical description; reserve Latinate for abstraction, sorcery, and intellectual concepts
2. **Syllable target:** Default to monosyllabic and disyllabic; deploy trisyllabic+ words as deliberate rhythmic punctuation
3. **Sensory channel:** Prioritize visual and kinesthetic vocabulary — what can be seen and felt in the body
4. **Formality gate:** Would this word appear in an urbane, slightly ironic adventure tale — something a well-read raconteur would choose? If the word is too plain it lacks style; if too ornate it lacks Vance's economy
5. **Position rule:** Place heavier/rarer words at sentence-end or in appositive phrases; keep sentence openings crisp and Germanic

## 4. Syntax Patterns

### 4.1 Modifier Placement

- **Adjective position:** Predominantly pre-noun; occasionally stacked in pairs ("short sharp laugh," "long red hair," "slow appraising glance") but never in clusters of three or more
- **Typical adjective density:** 1–2 per noun phrase; many noun phrases are unmodified — Vance trusts his nouns
- **Adverb position:** Mid-sentence or integrated into verb phrases; sentence-initial adverbs are rare
- **Adverb frequency:** Sparing — manner adverbs appear only when they earn their place ("dumbly," "sweetly," "listless")

### 4.2 Inversion & Unusual Word Order

- **Frequency:** Occasional
- **Type:** Prepositional-phrase fronting ("Through the night a slight smile could be seen"); object-fronting for emphasis; predicate-adjective fronting rare
- **Trigger context:** Emphasis and scene-painting; used to foreground atmosphere before revealing the subject

### 4.3 Apposition & Interjection

- **Appositives:** Moderate
  - **Typical position:** Mid-sentence between commas; often used to append a qualifying detail or restatement ("a creature without volition, all his will drained by the leaching magic")
- **Parenthetical interjections:** Rare
  - **Punctuation used:** Commas; dashes reserved for dramatic asides

### 4.4 Parallelism

- **Frequency:** Occasional
- **Typical form:** Paired clauses joined by "and"; balanced verb-phrases with compound predicates ("stepped forward and struck," "recovered himself and stood back")
- **Typical list length when present:** 2 items — Vance favors pairs over tricolons

---

## 5. Figurative Language

### 5.1 Density

- **Figures per paragraph (average):** 0.5
- **Figures per page (average):** 3–4

Vance is notably restrained with figurative language. The prose achieves its effects through precision of literal description rather than through metaphorical elaboration. When a figure appears, it lands hard precisely because of its rarity.

### 5.2 Type Preference (ratio)

- **Simile:** 20%
- **Metaphor:** 55%
- **Personification:** 15%
- **Synecdoche/metonymy:** 10%

### 5.3 Source Domains

1. **Fire/heat/light** — emotion mapped to flame ("The flame died from her face," "eyes smouldering")
2. **Body/physicality** — will and volition described in physical terms (drained, leached, paralyzed)
3. **Sleep/death/stillness** — enchantment rendered as a kind of living death ("like sleep-walkers," "inanimate figures")
4. **Acid/corrosion** — decay and cruelty ("preserve their living brains in acid")

### 5.4 Construction Pattern

- **Typical simile form:** Brief "like X" comparisons — never extended ("like sleep-walkers")
- **Metaphor integration:** Embedded in verb and noun choice rather than stated as formal comparison; the reader absorbs it as description ("The flame died from her face")
- **Length:** Single phrase — Vance does not develop conceits across multiple sentences

## 6. Punctuation

### 6.1 Comma Behavior

- **Density:** Moderate — approximately 1.5 per sentence
- **Oxford comma:** Inconsistent (not enough list instances to confirm pattern)
- **Comma splices:** Occasional deliberate stylistic choice, used to compress action into a single kinetic line ("She ran to Etarr, tugged at his arm")

### 6.2 Semicolons

- **Frequency:** Rare
- **Primary use:** Joining parallel independent clauses in expository passages; largely absent from action sequences

### 6.3 Dashes

- **Type preferred:** Em dash
- **Frequency:** Rare
- **Primary use:** Dramatic aside or appended explanation ("Only T'sais was free—for T'sais wore Pandelume's rune")

### 6.4 Colons

- **Frequency:** Absent in this sample
- **Primary use:** N/A

### 6.5 Other Notable Punctuation

- **Ellipses:** Absent
- **Exclamation marks:** Rare — dialogue only, used for genuine exclamation in confrontation
- **Question marks in narration:** Absent — questions occur only in dialogue

---

## 7. Rhythm & Cadence

### 7.1 Stress Patterns

- **Dominant tendency:** Loosely iambic with frequent trochaic interruptions; the underlying pulse is da-DUM but Vance breaks it freely for emphasis
- **Regularity:** Loosely rhythmic — the prose has a musical quality but never settles into metronomic regularity

### 7.2 Sentence-Level Pacing

- **Acceleration technique:** Shortening clauses; comma-spliced compound predicates; dropping conjunctions between actions ("stepped forward and struck")
- **Deceleration technique:** Trailing absolute phrases; appended participial clauses; polysyllabic Latinate abstractions that force the reader to slow down ("a creature without volition, all his will drained by the leaching magic")
- **Typical pacing shape within a paragraph:** Steady build to a sharp terminal image or action beat — paragraphs close harder than they open

### 7.3 Sound Devices

- **Alliteration:** Occasional — unobtrusive, usually in paired adjectives ("short sharp," "slow appraising," "blind rage," "black dust")
- **Assonance/consonance:** Occasional — woven into key phrases but not foregrounded
- **Onomatopoeia:** Absent

---

## 8. Dialogue

### 8.1 Tag Behavior

- **Tag frequency:** Most lines tagged, either with verb or action beat
- **Preferred tag verb:** "Said" dominant; occasional variation with characterizing verbs ("murmured," "mocked," "cried") — variation is earned by context, not decorative
- **Tag position:** Post-speech dominant; mid-speech rare; pre-speech used for scene-setting ("he said to T'sais")

### 8.2 Speech Patterns

- **Average speech-turn length:** 15 words / 1–2 sentences
- **Interruption/trailing off:** Rare
- **Dialect or idiolect markers:** Light — characters share a formal-elevated register but are differentiated by attitude: Javanne's speech is mocking and fluid; Etarr's is clipped and direct; T'sais's is earnest and plain

### 8.3 Dialogue-to-Narration Ratio

- **Approximate ratio:** 55% dialogue / 45% narration within dialogue-containing passages

---

## 9. Description Method

### 9.1 Sensory Distribution

- **Visual:** 70%
- **Auditory:** 10%
- **Tactile/kinesthetic:** 15%
- **Olfactory:** 3%
- **Gustatory:** 2%

### 9.2 Camera Behavior

- **Default distance:** Medium — room-scale or clearing-scale; close enough to see facial expressions and gestures, far enough to track movement
- **Movement pattern:** Follows the acting character; tracks smoothly rather than cutting; zooms to close-up for emotional moments (a smile, smouldering eyes) then pulls back to action distance
- **Focal priority:** Character first, then environment — setting details are brushed in around action rather than laid out in advance

### 9.3 Description Density

- **Lines of pure description before action/dialogue resumes:** 1–2 on average
- **Integration method:** Single-sentence brushstrokes woven into action — Vance almost never stops the narrative for a set-piece description block. Details arrive in motion.

---

## Usage Notes

When writing new prose using this guide:

1. **Sentence construction:** Default to short, crisp sentences (8–16 words). Build most sentences as simple SVO structures. Deploy longer compound or compound-complex sentences sparingly as culminating moments — the long sentence earns its length by arriving after several short ones.
2. **Word selection:** Run candidate words through the five-step filter in §3.5. Favor vivid, specific verbs over adverb+generic-verb combinations. Use Latinate vocabulary as seasoning, not as the base stock.
3. **Paragraph shaping:** Keep paragraphs short (2–4 sentences). Use single-sentence paragraphs as rhythmic punctuation. Close paragraphs with a clinching image or decisive action.
4. **Figurative language:** Use figures sparingly — one every two paragraphs at most. Embed metaphor in verb choice rather than constructing formal comparisons. Draw from fire, body, and stillness/death domains.
5. **Punctuation:** Keep it clean and conventional. Commas for pacing, periods for force. Use em dashes only for dramatic asides. Avoid semicolons in action passages.
6. **Rhythm check:** Read sentences aloud. The prose should feel brisk and precise, like a well-paced card game — not languid, not breathless. Paired adjectives should alliterate or assonantly chime. Terminal phrases should land with weight.
7. **Dialogue:** Keep speech turns short and purposeful. Differentiate characters by attitude and sentence structure, not by vocabulary register. Let dialogue carry the scene — Vance's narration serves the speakers rather than the reverse.
8. **The Vance signature:** Cool precision. The narrator observes with detached, faintly sardonic clarity. Even violent or emotional moments are rendered with controlled exactitude. The effect is of a cultured intelligence describing events it finds both fascinating and faintly absurd.
