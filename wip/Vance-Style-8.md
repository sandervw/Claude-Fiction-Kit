# Style Guide: Jack Vance — *T'sais* (from *The Dying Earth*)

> **Source sample:** "T'sais" by Jack Vance, ~1,800 words (final third of the story)
> **Genre context:** Dying-earth science-fantasy; picaresque planetary romance
> **Narrative POV:** Third-person omniscient, externalized — narrator reports action and speech but rarely enters thought; emotional states are inferred from visible signs (eyes, gestures, posture)

---

## 1. Sentence Structure

### 1.1 Length

- **Average sentence length:** 16 words
- **Range:** 3–55 words
- **Standard deviation:** ~12 words
- **Distribution shape:** Bimodal — a cluster of short declarative punches (3–10 words) and a separate cluster of long accumulative sweeps (25–55 words), with a thinner middle band. The short sentences tend to carry dramatic weight; the long ones carry description and exposition.

### 1.2 Sentence Types (ratio)

- **Simple (single independent clause):** 30%
- **Compound (coordinated independent clauses):** 30%
- **Complex (independent + dependent clause):** 25%
- **Compound-complex:** 15%

### 1.3 Opening Patterns

- **Subject-first (SVO):** 45%
- **Prepositional/adverbial opener:** 15%
- **Participial phrase opener:** 2%
- **Dependent clause opener:** 8%
- **Inverted/verb-first:** 5%
- **Conjunction opener (And, But, For, etc.):** 20%
- **Other (interjection, vocative, fragment):** 5%

Note: Conjunction openers — especially "And" — are a strong Vance marker. They lend a chronicle-like, additive rhythm, as though the narrator is relating events in sequence from memory.

### 1.4 Clause Stacking

- **Typical clause count per sentence:** 1–3
- **Stacking method:** Right-branching coordinate chains joined by commas and "and"; semicolons joining balanced independent clauses; occasional relative-clause embedding
- **Maximum comfortable depth before resolution:** 3 clauses. Vance rarely nests deeply; when sentences grow long, they grow *wide* (adding parallel clauses) rather than *deep* (embedding within embedding).

---

## 2. Paragraph Structure

### 2.1 Length

- **Average paragraph length:** 3 sentences / ~50 words
- **Range:** 1–12 sentences
- **Distribution shape:** Alternating — short paragraphs (1–2 sentences) for dialogue, action beats, and dramatic turns; occasional long paragraphs (8–12 sentences) for sustained exposition or legend-telling. The long paragraphs are the exception, not the rule.

### 2.2 Internal Pattern

- **Typical opening move:** Direct continuation of action or speech; occasionally a scene-setting spatial/temporal marker ("East, ever east"; "Dawn came")
- **Typical closing move:** A strong terminal image or a clipped declarative that lands with finality ("…and the broken columns leaned black on the sky")
- **Paragraph-level rhythm:** Short setup → brief elaboration → crisp closing image. In longer paragraphs, accumulation builds steadily until a final sentence that is either the shortest or the most imagistic.

### 2.3 Transitions Between Paragraphs

- **Dominant method:** Temporal/sequential ("They returned…"; "Dawn came…"; "And the three mounted…") or direct continuation via dialogue exchange
- **Frequency of hard breaks vs. linked flow:** 30% hard / 70% linked. Paragraphs flow forward with an episodic, chronicle pace; hard breaks tend to mark spatial jumps or shifts in speaker.

## 3. Word Choice

### 3.1 Register & Etymology

- **Latinate vs. Germanic ratio:** ~45/55, slightly Germanic-dominant. Action and physical description favor short Germanic words ("strode," "flew," "black," "strand"); exposition and atmosphere pull from Latinate stock ("dispassionate," "supernal," "illimitable," "pervaded").
- **Preferred register:** Elevated-plain — a baseline of clean, unadorned prose punctuated by sudden elevated or archaic words. The overall effect is lucid rather than ornate.
- **Archaism frequency:** Occasional
  - **Type:** Vocabulary ("bestrode," "bereft," "doffed," "henceforth," "willy-nilly," "whit"); occasional syntax ("they came through the broken columns" meaning *among*). Pronouns and verb conjugations remain modern.

### 3.2 Specificity & Concreteness

- **Abstraction level:** Highly concrete and sensory, with brief incursions into abstract/philosophical language reserved for the divine or magical ("dispassionate, supernal, of illimitable will and power")
- **Noun specificity:** High — "strand" not "beach," "spires" not "towers," "moors" not "fields," "jowls" not "cheeks." Vance selects the most precise and evocative noun available.
- **Verb specificity:** High — "ballooned" not "rose," "stamped" not "moved," "flapping" not "beating." Verbs carry sensory surprise; they do visual and kinesthetic work simultaneously.

### 3.3 Syllable Profile

- **Average syllables per word:** ~1.4
- **Polysyllabic word frequency (3+ syllables):** ~8%
- **Placement tendency for polysyllabic words:** Sentence-interior or sentence-end for rhythmic weight. Polysyllabic words are deployed sparingly and land hard when they appear, often in isolation among monosyllables ("dispassionate, supernal, of illimitable will").

### 3.4 Repetition & Variation

- **Deliberate repetition:** Occasional
  - **Type:** Key-phrase echo for ritual or thematic weight. The god's pronouncement "To each who comes, justice is done" repeats verbatim, functioning as incantation. Structural repetition across parallel scenes (the three mounting; the three facing the god).
- **Synonym variation:** Moderate — Vance does not obsessively avoid repetition, but he also does not hammer the same word. He'll reuse "winged creatures" rather than reaching for a synonym, trusting the phrase to naturalize.

### 3.5 Word Selection Method

When generating **new** words not drawn from the sample, select by:

1. **Etymology filter:** Default to short Germanic roots for action and physical description. Switch to Latinate roots for atmosphere, scale, or the numinous.
2. **Syllable target:** Favor monosyllables and disyllables (~85% of words). Reserve trisyllabic+ words for deliberate rhythmic weight.
3. **Sensory channel:** Prioritize visual vocabulary (color, shape, light, distance), with kinesthetic as secondary.
4. **Formality gate:** Would this word appear in a 1950s literary adventure story written for an educated general audience? If too modern-casual or too academically obscure, discard.
5. **Position rule:** Place rarer/heavier words at clause-end or pre-semicolon, where they can land with weight rather than trip the reader mid-stride.

## 4. Syntax Patterns

### 4.1 Modifier Placement

- **Adjective position:** Predominantly pre-noun; occasionally stacked in pairs ("dim red sun," "cool water," "long red hair," "peaked black forehead"). Rarely more than two adjectives per noun phrase.
- **Typical adjective density:** 0.5–1.0 per noun phrase on average. Many noun phrases carry zero adjectives; when modifiers appear, they are precise and load-bearing.
- **Adverb position:** Pre-verb or sentence-initial; sentence-final is rare.
- **Adverb frequency:** Sparing. Vance lets specific verbs do the work that adverbs would otherwise handle.

### 4.2 Inversion & Unusual Word Order

- **Frequency:** Occasional — a signature flavor element
- **Type:** Verb-subject inversion ("Ahead shone a glimmering sea"); object- or predicate-fronting ("Where was the lovely face, the strawberry mouth, the fair skin?")
- **Trigger context:** Scenic reveals, moments of wonder or horror. Inversion functions as a spotlight — it makes the reader encounter the image before the grammatical subject.

### 4.3 Apposition & Interjection

- **Appositives:** Moderate
  - **Typical position:** Mid-sentence between commas, used to layer descriptive detail efficiently ("a brooding presence pervaded the place, dispassionate, supernal, of illimitable will and power")
- **Parenthetical interjections:** Rare
  - **Punctuation used:** Dashes for interruption in dialogue; commas elsewhere

### 4.4 Parallelism

- **Frequency:** Structural — one of the most recognizable Vance markers
- **Typical form:** Tricolon and coordinate lists; balanced paired clauses joined by semicolons or commas ("the city at last crumbled, the temple became shards and splinters, the people vanished")
- **Typical list length when present:** 3 items (tricolon strongly preferred)

---

## 5. Figurative Language

### 5.1 Density

- **Figures per paragraph (average):** 0.3–0.5
- **Figures per page (average):** 2–4

Vance is notably *restrained* with figurative language. The prose achieves its atmosphere through precise literal description and word selection rather than through simile or extended metaphor.

### 5.2 Type Preference (ratio)

- **Simile:** 15%
- **Metaphor (especially embedded in verb choice):** 50%
- **Personification:** 20%
- **Synecdoche/metonymy:** 10%
- **Other (transferred epithet, catachresis):** 5%

### 5.3 Source Domains

1. Light, color, and atmosphere (dim red sun; glimmering sea; black on the sky)
2. Organic matter and decay (putrefying, squirming, mottled)
3. Architecture and stone (columns, spires, domes, shards)
4. Animals and predatory behavior (leather wings, whined, stamped, fretting)

### 5.4 Construction Pattern

- **Typical simile form:** Rare; when present, short and direct ("as long again," "seemed afire")
- **Metaphor integration:** Embedded in verb choice — the verb itself *is* the figure ("the sun ballooned upward"; "dusk coming from the sky"). This is the dominant figurative mode.
- **Length:** Single phrase or single verb. Extended conceits are absent.

## 6. Punctuation

### 6.1 Comma Behavior

- **Density:** Moderate — approximately 2–3 per sentence
- **Oxford comma:** Yes
- **Comma splices:** Deliberate stylistic choice in parallel tricolon constructions ("the city crumbled, the temple became shards…, the people vanished")

### 6.2 Semicolons

- **Frequency:** Moderate to heavy — a signature punctuation mark
- **Primary use:** Joining balanced independent clauses that share a thematic or temporal relationship. The semicolon is Vance's primary tool for building compound sentences without conjunctions, giving the prose its clipped, chronicle quality.

### 6.3 Dashes

- **Type preferred:** Em dash
- **Frequency:** Rare
- **Primary use:** Interrupted speech in dialogue; dramatic revelation. Reserved for moments of disruption, not used as a general-purpose connector.

### 6.4 Colons

- **Frequency:** Rare
- **Primary use:** Introducing a pronouncement or revelation, especially from an authoritative voice (the god's speech)

### 6.5 Other Notable Punctuation

- **Ellipses:** Absent
- **Exclamation marks:** Rare — dialogue only, reserved for genuine exclamation or invocation ("God of the past!")
- **Question marks in narration:** Rare — used in rhetorical/dramatic questions only ("Where was the lovely face…?")

## 7. Rhythm & Cadence

### 7.1 Stress Patterns

- **Dominant tendency:** Loosely iambic with frequent spondaic clusters (two consecutive stressed syllables: "black dust," "dim red," "great sea," "low glass"). The spondees give weight and gravity.
- **Regularity:** Loosely rhythmic — not metrical enough to scan as verse, but controlled enough that deviations feel deliberate.

### 7.2 Sentence-Level Pacing

- **Acceleration technique:** Shortening clauses; asyndeton in parallel tricolons; sentence fragments ("East, ever east.")
- **Deceleration technique:** Adding coordinate clauses with "and"; embedding appositives; extending final clauses with prepositional chains ("…and the broken columns leaned black on the sky")
- **Typical pacing shape within a paragraph:** Steady narration → acceleration toward a terminal image or statement. Action paragraphs build speed; descriptive paragraphs often slow to a wide-lens final sentence.

### 7.3 Sound Devices

- **Alliteration:** Occasional — never ostentatious, but present when it serves rhythm ("shards and splinters"; "fell flapping"; "fretting winged"; "slowly sloped")
- **Assonance/consonance:** Occasional — particularly long vowels for atmosphere ("brooding," "cool," "slowly")
- **Onomatopoeia:** Rare ("flapping," "whined")

---

## 8. Dialogue

### 8.1 Tag Behavior

- **Tag frequency:** Most lines tagged or attributed
- **Preferred tag verb:** Primarily "said" and "asked"; occasional vivid alternatives used sparingly and for genuine distinction ("exclaimed," "murmured," "commanded," "cried," "muttered")
- **Tag position:** Post-speech dominant; occasional mid-speech interruption

### 8.2 Speech Patterns

- **Average speech-turn length:** 15–25 words / 1–2 sentences (short exchanges); up to 150 words in expository monologue (Javanne's legend)
- **Interruption/trailing off:** Rare — characters tend to speak in complete, controlled utterances
- **Dialect or idiolect markers:** Light — formal/archaic register shared by all speakers ("henceforth," "bereft," "deem me fit"). Characters are distinguished more by *what* they say than *how* they say it. The god speaks in a distinct sibilant, clipped register.

### 8.3 Dialogue-to-Narration Ratio

- **Approximate ratio:** 40% dialogue / 60% narration within dialogue-containing passages. Narration dominates overall; dialogue occurs in concentrated exchanges separated by narrative movement.

---

## 9. Description Method

### 9.1 Sensory Distribution

- **Visual:** 70%
- **Auditory:** 12%
- **Tactile/kinesthetic:** 12%
- **Olfactory:** 4%
- **Gustatory:** 2%

Vision dominates massively. Vance's eye for color, distance, and light is the primary engine of atmosphere. Sound and touch serve as secondary punctuation.

### 9.2 Camera Behavior

- **Default distance:** Medium-to-distant (landscape and panoramic). Pulls to close-up only for faces and crucial physical details.
- **Movement pattern:** Slow pan across landscape during travel; rapid zoom-in for dramatic reveals (Javanne's transformed face). The camera is frequently aerial or elevated during transitions.
- **Focal priority:** Environment first, then character. Characters are placed *within* landscapes, not the reverse. Faces and bodies are described in bursts of specific detail rather than sustained portraiture.

### 9.3 Description Density

- **Lines of pure description before action/dialogue resumes:** 1–3 sentences on average; up to 5 in travel passages
- **Integration method:** Single-sentence brushstrokes woven into action. Vance almost never pauses for a set-piece description block; instead, one vivid clause per sentence carries the environmental load while the action continues ("the dim red sun ballooned slowly upward into the dark sky"). The world is sketched in passing, not painted at rest.

## Usage Notes

When writing new prose using this guide:

1. **Sentence construction:** Alternate short declarative punches (3–10 words) with longer accumulative sentences (20–40 words). Favor right-branching coordinate chains over deep embedding. Open ~20% of sentences with "And" for chronicle rhythm.
2. **Word selection:** Default to monosyllabic Germanic words for action. Reserve Latinate polysyllables for atmosphere or the numinous, and place them at clause-end for weight. Run candidates through the five-step filter in §3.5.
3. **Paragraph shaping:** Keep most paragraphs to 2–4 sentences. Close paragraphs on a strong image or a clipped statement. Allow one long expository paragraph per scene at most.
4. **Figurative language:** Use sparingly. Embed figures in verb choice rather than constructing explicit similes. Let precise literal description do the atmospheric work.
5. **Parallelism:** Deploy tricolon structures for gravity and finality. Use semicolons to balance parallel clauses. This is the most audible Vance rhythm — use it, but not in every paragraph.
6. **Punctuation:** Semicolons are load-bearing; use them to join thematically linked independent clauses. Keep dashes rare. Allow deliberate comma splices in parallel constructions.
7. **Description:** Sketch the world in passing — one vivid clause per sentence, woven into forward motion. Favor color, light, and distance. Pull to close-up only for faces and transformations.
8. **Rhythm check:** Read aloud. Listen for spondaic clusters among the iambs. If the prose sounds too smooth or too regular, add a spondee ("black dust," "dim red," "great sea"). If it sounds choppy, extend a clause with a prepositional chain.
