**Duncarrow**

Duncarrow clings to a tidal island off the western coast—a hump of black rock connected to the mainland by a causeway that drowns twice daily. The sea has been eating the approach for three generations now. What was once a wide stone road is now a spine of slick rock, crossable only at low tide, and only by those who know where to step.

The hold itself is old. Older than the families who shelter there. The outer ring-wall was raised in some forgotten age, its stones fitted without mortar in a way no living mason understands. The inner keep—called the Cradle by locals—sits atop the island's crown, a squat tower of weathered granite where the settlement's shaman, a woman named Seren, maintains the genealogies. Every child born in Duncarrow has their lineage recited into the stones before their naming-day is complete. Every death is spoken aloud at the evening meal for a full turning of the moon. To be forgotten here is the only true death, and so no one is.

Duncarrow survives because the tides keep the Sarks at bay. The Lost Ones fear deep water—or perhaps they simply cannot remember how to swim. Either way, the causeway is the only approach, and it is watched. When the tide falls, the wardens light the beacon on the Cradle's roof, and armed men take their places along the stones. They have held for nineteen winters. They do not speak of the twentieth.

The settlement's chief, a graying spearman called Brennic, lost his daughter to the Forgetting six summers past. She wandered onto the causeway at ebb tide, walking east toward nothing. They found her clothes a mile inland, draped over a standing stone no one had seen before. Brennic does not speak of this. He carves her name into the Cradle's doorframe each morning, so deeply now that the wood has splintered through. Seren says nothing, but she has started including the girl's lineage in the evening recitations again—not as one dead, but as one who might yet return.

No one corrects her.
