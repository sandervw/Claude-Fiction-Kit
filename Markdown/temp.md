## 10. The unreliable narrator-messiah

**Character Examples:**

- **Severian** — Gene Wolfe, _The Book of the New Sun_ (1980–1983)
- **Philip Hastane** — Clark Ashton Smith, various stories (1930s)

**Core Elements:**
Severian is a Journeyman Torturer who claims "perfect memory" but lies constantly. John Clute described him as "a torturer who forgives his victims, who has perfect verbal recall but lies constantly. He is a man of insuperable ambition who tells us he has none." The archetype carries potential messianic significance while **actively deceiving the reader about every aspect of their journey**.

**Distinguishing Features:**
Professional moral transgression (torture, execution) combines with unreliable narration. Severian's contradictions cannot be simple forgetfulness—they're deliberate deception. The prose style, influenced by Clark Ashton Smith's decadent aestheticism, creates layered meaning where surface narrative and actual events diverge. The protagonist's journey from torturer (fuligin/black) to Autarch (argent/white) may or may not represent genuine transformation.

**What Makes It Dark Fantasy:**
The dying-Earth setting follows Smith and Vance's tradition, but the unreliable messiah adds epistemological horror: **readers cannot trust anything the protagonist reports**. Christian/messianic themes filter through deliberate obscurity—Severian carries the miraculous Claw of the Conciliator while engaging in "numerous sexual conquests, as well as various killings and acts of torture." The archetype forces readers to question whether salvation itself might be a lie.
