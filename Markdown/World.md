# The Waning

## Overview

The age of man is ending — not in fire or conquest, but in slow erasure. The standing stones that once held the boundaries between the human world and the primordial forces beneath it are crumbling. The covenants are broken. The Fomorians, who never truly left, are rising again: not as armies, but as the land itself turning inhospitable, the sea swallowing the coasts, the forests thickening into impassable wilderness. Settlements do not fall to siege. They are _reclaimed_ — swallowed by bog, overgrown by root and vine, forgotten by the world.

Humanity clings to shrinking safe zones: hill-forts with crumbling walls, villages clustered around fading sacred sites, island holds cut off from the mainland. The druids who understood the old wards are dying out, their knowledge fragmentary. The kings who should unite the people squabble over what remains. And in the wild places, in the spaces between firelight and darkness, the Lost are waiting — human once, but no longer. The world is forgetting mankind, and mankind is forgetting itself.

---

## Core Tension: Recession, Not Invasion

The Fomorians are not marching. They are _rising_. The sea encroaches. The forests thicken. The bogs swallow fields whole. The land is reverting to what it was before human settlement — wild, vast, and utterly indifferent. Humanity is not being conquered; it is being _unmade_. The horror of this age is not war. It is obsolescence.

---

## The Broken Compact

There was a covenant, once. The old druids — or perhaps the gods who walked before them — struck a balance with the Fomorian nature of the world. Sacred boundaries were established. Rituals maintained the wards. The standing stones were not monuments; they were _anchors_, holding back the tide of the primordial.

Then High King Brennach broke the law.

The stories vary on what he did. Some say he spilled blood on consecrated ground. Others say he took a Fomorian woman to wife, or spoke a name that should never be spoken, or failed to perform the rites at the turning of the year. What matters is the result: the compact shattered. The druids who might have repaired it died in the plague that followed — whether coincidence or consequence, none can say. Now the stones are crumbling, the wards are failing, and no one living remembers how to restore them.

The age of man was not taken. It was _forfeited_.

---

## The Fomorians

The Fomorians are not creatures. They are not enemies. They are the world as it was before mankind carved meaning into it — the deep water, the crushing dark, the slow grind of stone, the rot that feeds new growth. They do not hate humanity. They do not _anything_ humanity. They are primordial: as incapable of malice as a glacier, as indifferent as the tide.

They rarely manifest in forms that can be fought. A Fomorian is the hill that was not there yesterday. The fog bank that moves against the wind. The river that changes course to swallow a village. The forest that grows a mile overnight. When men speak of "Fomorian" as a creature, they are speaking of something like Balor — a thing so vast and old that its attention alone is fatal. Such beings are not opponents. They are catastrophes.

What mankind fights are the _symptoms_: the creatures that emerge as the wild reclaims the tamed, and the humans who have forgotten what they are.

---

## The Forgetting

What makes humans human in this world is not the body — it is _memory_, _name_, _bond_. Your lineage, your oaths, the people who know your face. The Fomorian resurgence erodes these things. Not through corruption or infection, but through _absence_. The world is becoming a place where human meaning cannot hold.

The process is gradual:

1. **Isolation** — physical or social, the victim becomes cut off from kin and kind
2. **Fading bonds** — faces blur, names slip away, relationships lose substance
3. **Fading self** — one's own name, history, and purpose dissolve
4. **Fading kind** — language, reason, and restraint go last
5. **What remains** — a human shape operating on instinct older than thought

In the old tradition, names held power. Druids preserved genealogies because lineage was an anchor. To know someone's true name was to hold them to the world. To lose your name is to lose your grip on existence itself.

## The Threats

### The Caillte (The Lost)

The Caillte are those who completed the Forgetting. They are the primary physical threat of this age — human in shape, but emptied of everything that made them so.

**Appearance:** Recognizably human. Wrong gait, wrong posture, something _off_ in the way they hold themselves. Many still wear the remnants of their old clothes. Some carry tools or weapons they no longer understand how to use properly.

**Behavior:**

- They do not speak, but they vocalize — grunts, howls, clicking sounds. Pack communication, not language.
- Territorial. They claim the places civilization has abandoned: ruins, overgrown farmsteads, forest hollows.
- Drawn to isolation. A lone traveler triggers pursuit. A group with torches and discipline makes them wary.
- They eat what they can catch. Not strictly carnivorous, but not above hunting men.
- Some retain fragmented echoes of their former lives. A Lost blacksmith might hoard iron without knowing why. A Lost mother might follow children without intent.

**In combat:**

- Animal cunning. They ambush, flank, use terrain. They are not mindless.
- Individually weaker than trained warriors. Dangerous in numbers.
- They do not fear death. They _do_ fear fire, worked iron, and organized resistance — the trappings of the world that rejected them.

**The horror:** Sometimes you recognize them. The thing wearing your brother's face does not know you.

---

### The Fear Dorcha (The Dark Ones)

If the Caillte are the horde, the Fear Dorcha are what the Caillte become if left long enough — those who have forgotten so completely that they have reached a kind of equilibrium with the Fomorian nature of the world. They are not feral. They are _still_.

**Appearance:** Tall. Gaunt. Always seen at distance, at the edge of torchlight, in peripheral vision. Difficult to look at directly — not because of any supernatural blur, but because the eye slides off them the way it slides off a shape in fog.

**Behavior:**

- They do not attack directly. They _follow_.
- Their presence accelerates the Forgetting in others. Around a Fear Dorcha, friends quarrel over nothing. Names become hard to recall. Dreams slip away upon waking.
- They mark individuals. Those marked find themselves growing isolated — accidents, misunderstandings, a slow withdrawal of companionship. Eventually, the marked one is alone. Then they join the Lost.
- They are patient. A single Fear Dorcha can hollow out a village over a season without ever being clearly seen.

**In combat:**

- Difficult to fight because difficult to _find_. They do not stand and face opposition.
- Can be driven off — temporarily — by strong communal resistance: firelight, recitation of lineages, the speaking of names and bonds. Things that reinforce human identity.
- Killing one may require severing its connection to a current victim. Save the marked, and the Dark One loses its grip.
- Possibly vulnerable to _naming_ — if a druid can discover what the Fear Dorcha was before it Forgot, that old name might wound it.

**The dread:** You cannot always tell if one is following you. The shape at the treeline might be a Dark One, or it might be a tree. That uncertainty is the point.

## Factions

### The Druids

Keepers of what remains. The druids maintain the genealogies, preserve the rituals, tend the failing stones. Their knowledge is fragmentary — much was lost in the plague that followed the breaking of the compact. They know enough to hold the line in places, but not enough to restore what was broken.

Some seek the old texts, the sacred sites, the hidden names that might turn the tide. Others have grown desperate, willing to bargain with powers that should not be bargained with. A few believe the age of man _should_ end — that humanity's time is simply over, and the merciful thing is to ease the passage.

Druids are invaluable. Druids are overworked. Druids are not always trustworthy.

### The Warlords and Kings

The old high kingship shattered with the compact. What remains are petty kings, hill-fort lords, warband leaders. Some fight to protect their people. Some fight to take what others have. Some believe a strong enough sword-arm can beat back the wild itself.

They are not wrong that strength matters — the Lost can be killed, territory can be held, walls can be manned. But they are not fighting an enemy that can be defeated by conventional means. The warlords who understand this seek druids, seek alliances, seek anything that might preserve their people. The warlords who do not understand will hold their ground until the forest swallows them.

### The Common Folk

Farmers, fishers, craftsmen, families. They do not care about covenants or primordial forces. They care about whether the harvest will survive, whether the children are safe, whether the Lost will come again tonight.

They cluster in the defended places, the sacred sites, the communities large enough to resist. They pay tribute to whoever offers protection — kings, druids, warbands. They maintain the small rituals they half-remember: speaking the names of ancestors, keeping fires lit, never traveling alone. They survive, where they can.

Some have already joined the Lost. Others will. The common folk are the stakes of this age — not heroes, not powers, simply the people who will either be saved or forgotten.

---

## Themes

- **Entropy over evil.** The threat is not malevolent; it is indifferent. The world is not being corrupted — it is simply moving on.
- **Memory as identity.** What makes you _you_ is not your body but your bonds, your name, your place in the human web. Lose those, and you lose yourself.
- **Complicity in decline.** The age of man is ending because mankind broke the rules. The guilt is collective, inherited, inescapable.
- **The edge of firelight.** Civilization is a small bright circle. Beyond it is everything else. The circle is shrinking.
- **Recognition and loss.** The worst horror is seeing what someone became — and knowing they do not see you at all.

## Locations

### The Sundershore Crannog — Archipelago

A crannog is an artificial island — timber and stone built up in a lake or coastal shallows. The Sundershore Crannog was once the outermost of a chain of such structures connecting the Sundershore Isles to the mainland, a pathway of stilt-bridges and platform-villages that allowed trade and travel across treacherous waters.

The bridges are gone now. The other crannogs are gone — sunk, abandoned, or simply _not there anymore_ when the fog lifted one morning. Sundershore remains: a wooden platform barely two hundred paces across, supporting a village of seventy souls, standing alone in grey water.

The people here are fishers, and they are trapped. The mainland is a day's rowing through waters where the currents have grown strange and the Breaker-shapes move in the spray. The other islands are either Lost-held or empty. Sundershore survives because a single druid, old Míreann, maintains the wards carved into the crannog's foundation posts. She is seventy winters old and has no apprentice. When she dies, the village will have perhaps a season before the sea notices them.

They need timber for repairs and have none. They need iron for tools and weapons and have little. They need a way out, or a reason to stay. They have only the fog, the water, and the shapes they sometimes see standing on the distant shore, watching.

### Dún Carrach — Inland

Dún Carrach — the Stone Fort — was once the seat of a regional king, built on a drumlin hill overlooking the River Blackmoor. Three concentric stone walls, a great hall, a druid sanctuary, granaries, forges, homes for two hundred people. A center of power.

That was before the Forgetting took the outer rings.

Now the innermost wall is all that remains defended. Perhaps fifty people live in the shadow of the great hall, which itself stands half-ruined — the roof collapsed two winters ago, and there is no timber to repair it. The outer rings are haunted ground: Caillte nest in the old granaries, and at night their howls echo off the stone. The river itself has grown treacherous, dark water rising unpredictably, fish dying in great silver carpets that rot on the banks.

Lord Faolán holds Dún Carrach through stubbornness and iron. His warband numbers twelve — too few to clear the outer rings, enough to hold the inner gate. A druid named Sencha advises him, but Sencha's knowledge is limited to battlefield blessings and wound-tending. The deep lore is beyond him.

Three problems face Dún Carrach: food is running low, the well water has begun to taste of peat and rot, and two nights ago a shepherd swore he saw a tall shape standing in the ruins of the north granary, unmoving, watching the inner wall. If a Fear Dorcha has come to the fort, the community's days are numbered — isolation will follow, then quarrels, then one by one the defenders will simply walk into the dark.

Faolán needs a true druid, or a way to evacuate his people, or a miracle. He has none of these things. He has walls, and swords, and dwindling time.
