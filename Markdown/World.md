# Whyneland

## Overview

"Twilight threatens."

- _Unknown Bard_

The iron has lost its edge, the palisade falls to ruin, and Nature once again has the better of men. Not by fire, steel, the methods of war, but through erasure. The wind erodes the old menhyrs and kerns, monuments that once held the boundaries between civilization and the primordial forces beneath it. A covenant was broken. The Vormakhans, who never truly left, come again: not as armies, but as an inhospitable land, a sea swallowing the coasts, a forests stretching its roots and thorns. The gates of man are not hewn by the axe. They are reclaimed, forgotten by the world.

Humanity huddles round a final few fires: hill-forts with walls half-fallen to ruin, villages clustered around ritual circles of browning grass, island holds cut off from the mainland. The shamans who understood the old wards are dying, their knowledge fading. The kings squabble over what remains. And in the wild places, the Lost Ones wait. Human once, no longer. The world is forgetting mankind, and mankind forgets itself.

---

## A Regressive Age

The Vormakhans are _rising_.

The sea eats the shoreline, in handspans, season by season; or else it vanishes suddenly in the dark of night, and leaves uncovered the beslimed and half-digested carcasses of strange ships, or an abyssal plain stripped naked to the parching eye of the sun and dotted with mounds built of worked, ruddy stanes. The forests remember, sending roots through hearthstones and thorns through thatch. The bogs drink the fields, and the black water gives nothing back. The land is becoming what it was before the first fence-post, the first furrow: wild, vast and indifferent.

There are no banners on the horizon, no war-horns in the passes. The horror of this age is quieter: the slow comprehension that man will not last, and that Nature comes for her stolen throne.

---

## The Broken Compact

In the days before the first crops were sewn, there began a covenant. The old shamans struck a balance with the Vormakhan world. A frontier were traced in stone and words, and Ritual bound the seams. The standing stones were anchors, pinning back the pitiless tide of what lay beneath.

Red King Tharn broke that covenant.

The stories fracture here, each teller certain of a different sin:

- He spilled: blood on consecrated ground—his brother's, or his son's
- He took: a Vormakhan wife and sired something that should not have been
- He spoke: an unspeakable name
- He forgot: failed to discharge the Ritual at the turning of the year, let the seams come unstitched, let words go unspoken

The exact sin matters little. The shamans - they who might have redeemed it - died in the plague that followed within a summer: a plague of conscience, or mere ill luck. With those shamans went the words, the names, the shape of Ritual.

The wards are unseamed.

No one living knows their mending.

The age of man was _forfeited_.

---

## The Vormakhans

The Vormakhans are not creatures, and nor are they the enemy. They are the world as it was before man carved his name into it — the deep water, the dark, the slow grind of stone, decomposition. They do not hate man. They do not _anything_ man. They are a glacier, the tide - unstoppable, yet incapable of malice.

**Manifestation:** The Vormakhans rarely take forms that can be fought. A Vormakhan _is_:

- The mountain sliding southward
- The fog that does not lift
- The river that changes course
- The spreading woods
- The hurricane

When men speak of "a Vormakhan", they are speaking of a thing so vast and old that its attention alone is fatal. They speak of a catastrophe. And a catastrophe cannot be fought.

What man fights are the symptoms: the things that emerge as the wild reclaims the tamed.

---

## The Forgetting

What makes humans human in this world is memory. The kin fables, the folk who know your face. The Vormakhans erode these things. The world is becoming a place where lineage cannot last, like writing on water.

In the old tradition, names carried genuine power. Shamans preserved genealogies because lineage was an anchor to the soil. To know someone's name was to plot their existence. Losing your name; lose your Self.

**The stages of Forgetting:**

1. **Isolation** — Man is cut off from kin and kind, whether by geography, circumstance, or sensory distance with no clear cause
2. **Fading bonds** — Faces blur. Names slip away. It is like trying to remember a dream hours after waking
3. **Fading self** — One's history dissolves. Purpose... what was it? The question "why am I here?" gives no answer
4. **Fading kind** — When reason is gone, instinct is all that remains, instinct older than thought, instinct older than speech
5. **What remains** — A human shape still walks and breathes, but what's inside operates on something far older than memory

## The Threats

Though the Vormakhans can't be battled with, yet some of what comes in their wake does bleed.

---

### Sarks (The Lost Ones)

The Sarks are those who completed the Forgetting — human still in shape, but hollowed of all that made them kin. They are the primary scourge of this age: what becomes of man when memory fails and instinct alone remains.

They wear their old clothes. Some carry tools they no longer understand. Their gait is wrong, their posture uncanny — the eye knows before the mind can name it. They do not speak. They vocalize: grunts, clicks, howls. Animal sounds.

**Where they dwell:** Places civilization has abandoned. Overgrown farms. Ruins where the hearthfire has long gone cold.

**What they do:** They hunt. A lone traveler draws them like blood draws flies. They ambush, use terrain with animal cunning. Individually, a skilled warrior can best one. But Sarks do not travel alone.

Some retain echoes. A Sark blacksmith might hoard iron. A Sark mother might follow children. These echoes make them only stranger.

**Weaknesses:** They fear fire, and iron. They do not fear death.

---

### Trow (The Dark Ones)

If Sarks are what man becomes when memory fails, the Trow are what Sarks become when left alone in the dark for a long, long time. They have forgotten so completely that they have found a strange equilibrium with the Vormakhan world. They are no longer fighting the Forgetting. They are it.

**Appearance:** Always incomplete. At torchlight's edge. In sight's corner. The eye slides over them as it slides over a familiar treeline — until a second glance reveals something altered. But what? You are uncertain what you have seen.

**What they do:** They follow. They do not attack — not directly. Their presence accelerates the Forgetting. Around a Trow, friends quarrel without cause. Names slip from the tongue mid-sentence. Dreams dissolve, leaving only a greasy residue in the mind.

They single out certain folk. The marked one is isolated through a slow accumulation of accidents, withdrawals of companionship. Eventually, they are alone. Almost alone. One Trow, given a summer or ten, will whittle out a village — often without ever being seen.

**How they are fought:** Firelight. Recitation of lineages. The speaking of friends' names. Reinforcing identity weakens their grip. Killing one may require severing its connection to a community — or, some shamans theorize, speaking the name of who it once was, before it Forgot. But this is little-tried.

---

### The Odd

The Sarks and Trow are not the only dangers. The old world stirred with things that were never men — creatures carved by shaman hands, or grown in dark places where no compact held sway. As the Vormakhans rise, these things stir. The wards that bound them fray. The old equilibrium tilts.

**Greenspire:** In the age before the breaking, shamans carved gargoyles from jade-veined stone and gave them purpose through Ritual — to watch, to wait, to wake when boundaries were crossed. Now they stir without reason. Kestanes skitter in packs through ruined halls, lashing with claws that drip jade acid. Bhruggspire drift their ancient patrol routes, hurling bullets of burning green. And in the deepest places, the rare Grundkholm — vast as a collapsing wall and twice as deadly — guard thresholds no one living remembers.

**Vlimmen:** At firelight's furthest reach, small figures are sometimes glimpsed — man-shaped but wrongly assembled, their movements too fluid, their laughter like water among stones. Tricksters. They lead travelers from true paths. They pinch out torches. They whisper wrong directions into sleeping ears. First they take small things — a knife, a tinderbox. Then larger thefts: the memory of which turning led homeward. The exact timbre of a loved one's voice.

**Khernghast:** Wizened things, no taller than a child of seven winters, with skin like peat-cured leather and eyes milky as quartz. They dwell in old barrows, in forgotten kerns where capstones have settled into the earth like teeth into a jaw. They do not welcome visitors. They remember grudges from before the compact was made.

_For a fuller accounting of these and other creatures - the Skrelvings of the deep places, the Burnt Vennghast that howl across the hills - consult the Bestiary._

## Factions

### The Shamans

The shamans are they who remember an echo of Ritual. They maintain the genealogies, and attend the kerns. Their knowledge is fragmentary - much was lost in the plague that followed the broken compact. They know enough to keep the remaining seams from coming unstitched, and that is all they know.

**What they do:**

- Maintain wards around settlements
- Preserve lineages and names
- Advise warlords and kings (who do not always listen)
- Seek out old stone tablets, sacred sites, hidden names whose remembering might reverse the world's forgetting

**Internal divisions:**

- Some believe restoration is possible - that the same compact can be rewritten if the same terms are put forth
- Others turn desperate, willing to bargain with certain secret powers
- A few believe that the age _should_ end; man is dying - mercy is easing the passage rather than prolonging the suffering

**The truth about shamans:** They are kin. Invaluable. Overworked. Not always trustworthy. A shaman's motives may be pure, or otherwise. There are too few of them for a man to be picky when he requires one's service or council, and that makes shaman dangerous.

---

### The Warlords and Kings

The old high kingship fell to pieces along with the compact. What remains are petty kings, the lords of hill-forts, warchiefs. Men and women who hold territory through force - of arm or of will.

**What they do:**

- rule settlements.
- Compete for resources. Timber, iron, grain, people. There is never enough of anything.
- Seek alliances. A smart man knows he cannot survive alone. They court shamans, and bandy terms with other lords.

**Differing Motives:**

- Some fight for their own, at great personal cost
- Some fight for what others have, for survival justifies any means
- Some fight the wild itself - the mad ones, or the foolish

**The truth about warlords:** Strength does matter. Sarks bleed. Walls can be held. Yet the enemy they face is not a conventional one. The smartest warlords seek the help of the shamans, or anything that might preserve what they have for just one more winter.

---

### The Kinfolk

Farmers, fishermen, artisans, sons and daughters, sires and grandsires. They do not care about covenants or Vormakhans. They care about the barley crop, the leaky roof, whether Sarks will come after dusk.

**How they survive:**

- Huddling behind raised earthworks, within sacred sites, around any defensible place
- Paying tribute for protection, from kings or shamans, from warchiefs of masters of words
- Practicing small, half-remember observances: reciting kin-names before suppers, spreading ginger and ashes before the hovel door, always travelling in company

**The stakes:** Common Kinfolk are the ones who must be saved or forgotten. While they endure, so do the world of man. But if thy sisters and thy brothers, thy mothers and thy fathers, and thy grandfathers, and even thy grandfathers' grandfathers, and all thy children - if these were forgotten, what is left worth saving?

## Themes and Symbols

- **Entropy.** The threat is indifferent. The world is a `river walking over its bed`. The Vormakhans do not hate man, and are not evil. They are only continuing on their way.
- **Memory and Names.** You are your kin. Your memory. Every beast and monster has a body, but `man's lips alone can speak a name.`
- **Complicity.** `Red King Tharn cut the bond`. But, his sin is everyone's. His sin is yours. His sin is your lover's. His sin was your grandsire's. His sin will be your children's.
- **The Last Star of Night.** Civilization is a little light flickering in a black ocean. And like `the old star at the new day's dawn`, that star dims by the hour.
- **Recognition.** The horror is not the Sark growling in the dark. The horror is `the familiar face that pushes into the light, and itself wears no recognition.`
