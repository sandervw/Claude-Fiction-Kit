# How to Start Manual Story Generation

## Bottom-up approach:

Don't start with the outline. If you give the LLM a full outline and
ask it to fill in pivots, you'll get competent but generic prose.
The structure will be correct, but the voice will be bland. You'll
have built a skeleton with no flesh. Start with a single scene.

Then test: Can the LLM produce text that sounds like it belongs in
Vermis? In Dark Souls item descriptions? That has the "mythic
capitalization" and "passive historic voice" from your
Revision-Tests?

The question isn't "can it follow a structure?" LLMs are good at
structure. The question is: can it produce prose you'd actually want
to read?

Once you have one good screen, then test: can it produce a second
screen that follows from a choice? Can it maintain voice across 3-4
screens?

Only after that introduce the outline/pivot system.

## Work so far:

`Genre:` Dark Fantasy

`Subgenre:` Dungeon Crawl

`World:` "Monastery-fortresses hanging above a world that choked. The chains are ancient, the anchors are cracking, and the abbeys sway in winds that carry poison. The monks pray for structural integrity. It's unclear if anyone listens."

`Protagonist:`

THE BELL-RINGER'S APPRENTICE
Core Identity: His master fell from the bell tower. He caught the rope but couldn't hold it. Now he rings the hours alone—not because duty demands it, but because the routine is the only thing keeping him from unraveling completely.
Physical Presence: His face has gone blank. Not peaceful—erased. The other monks find it difficult to look at him; there's something uncanny about a human face with nothing behind it, like a door that opens onto a wall. He's stopped maintaining himself in the small ways that mark someone as present in their own life: his beard has grown wild, knotting faster than he bothers to pick it out, a physical record of neglect. But occasionally—unpredictably—something will strike him as funny, and out comes this laugh: high, sharp, almost childlike. It doesn't reach his eyes. It doesn't change his expression. It just happens, then stops, and he continues as if it never occurred.
Psychological Fractures: The trauma broke something loose in him, and what emerged doesn't quite fit together. He faints at odd moments—sudden collapses triggered by heights, by rope tension, by bells that ring at the wrong pitch. The monks have learned to catch him. But they've also learned to keep their distance, because there's violence in him now: a hair-trigger startle response that's left brothers with black eyes and broken fingers when they've touched him without warning. He apologizes afterward, expressionless, and means it. He does it again anyway.
The Investigation: Between the swooning and the violence, a strange curiosity has taken root. He's begun pulling at threads—examining the old rope, questioning monks about the wind patterns that day, mapping who was in the tower and when. He doesn't accuse anyone directly. He just asks, with that blank face, and writes the answers in a journal he keeps beneath the largest bell. He doesn't know what he's looking for. Proof it wasn't his fault. Proof it was someone else's. Proof that it meant something, that his grip failing wasn't just physics and sweat and bad luck.

`One moment:`

The Anchor Inspection — The Abbott orders all able monks to inspect the failing anchors beneath the monastery. The Apprentice must rappel down on rope, suspended over the poisoned world below. Halfway down, his vision tunnels and he blacks out, dangling unconscious while the chain beside him groans.
The Wind-Reader's Testimony — An elderly monk who tracks weather patterns admits the winds were wrong the day the master fell—too still, then a sudden gust. The Apprentice's hand tightens on his journal. The old monk asks why he's smiling. He wasn't aware he was.
The Midnight Collapse — A section of walkway gives way during night prayers. The Apprentice is first to the scene, hauling a screaming brother back from the edge—then notices the support beam wasn't rotted. It was cut. Someone else is pulling at threads too.
The Wrong Bell — He strikes the Vespers bell and the tone comes out flat, corrupted. He faints on the platform. When he wakes, he finds claw marks on the bell's interior that weren't there yesterday.
The Rope Merchant — A trader arrives via the supply chain-lift, selling cordage. The Apprentice corners him with questions about the rope his master used. The merchant remembers the sale—and remembers who specifically requested that batch.
The Tunnel Saint — Beneath the monastery, in maintenance shafts meant only for chain inspection, he discovers a hidden shrine. Someone has been descending into the poison-fog and returning. The offerings are fresh. The prayers carved into the walls ask for the abbey to fall.
The Brother's Confession — A young monk approaches him at the bells, trembling, wanting to confess something about that day. Before he can finish, another monk calls from the doorway. The young one goes pale and silent. The moment passes.
The Startle — During a tense refectory meal, someone drops a plate behind him. He breaks the man's jaw before he's fully conscious. In the silence after, he notices three monks who didn't flinch—like they were expecting it.
The Swaying Night — A storm hits, and the entire monastery pendulums on its chains. The Apprentice must ring the storm-bells to warn the lower levels, each pull of the rope a negotiation with his trauma. Somewhere in the chaos, he hears his master's voice in the wind. Or doesn't.
The Journal Theft — He returns to the bell tower to find his hidden journal missing. In its place, a single page with his own handwriting he doesn't remember—a name circled, an accusation he never consciously made.
