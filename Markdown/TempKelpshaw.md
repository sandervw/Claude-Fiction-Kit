# The Kelpshaw Soutrenn

## Exterior Compound

What was once a rhath has surrendered itself to a slow and thorough unmaking—two longhouses whose thatch has caved inward like the fontanelles of ancient skulls, watchtowers that list at angles suggesting not collapse but genuflection, as though they bow toward some authority the wind alone remembers. Wooden walkways, their planks furred with decades of creeping moss in shades of verdigris and bile-yellow, span the structures in sagging arcs; they creak with the memory of footfall rather than its presence. A central firepit exhales smoke the color of old pewter, and around it raiders have made their claim—desperate Kinfolk turned to banditry under the governance of **Moldrick One-Hand**, who hunches by the flames with the patience of a man who has learned that waiting costs less than hoping, working a notched blade with the three fingers that remain to him. His people watch the approaches with the territorial vigilance of carrion birds: two at the gate where the timber has gone silver with weather, three more stationed along earthworks that the land has begun to digest, their ramparts softened by thistle and bindweed and the slow ambition of roots. Wet ash rides the wind, and beneath it something fouler—a sweetness that has curdled, a presence that the nose registers before the mind can name.

## Upper Soutrenn (The Old Ward-Hall)

Stone steps descend into a coolness that feels less like absence of heat than presence of something older; the walls bear carved spirals in the shamanic fashion of centuries past, their grooves now blackened with mold that spreads in patterns suggesting intention, as though the corruption were itself a language being written and rewritten across the decades. Shattered **Kestanes** litter the corridor—jade hound-shapes with limbs fractured at angles that speak of violence rather than decay, their surfaces still holding that particular waxy gleam that distinguishes worked jade from common stone. Two raiders crouch near a torch that gutters and recovers and gutters again, bickering over a stolen ring in voices that echo strangely, as though the walls were listening and choosing what to return.

The passage forks here, offering its choices with the indifference of old stone. To the north: a door barred with iron gone the color of dried blood (the key hangs from the belt of a guard who dozes against the jamb, his breathing shallow and troubled by dreams), and beyond it a cell where **Mother Drelvi**, an aging shaman whose face carries the topography of many winters, awaits either ransom or rescue with the patience of one who has learned that both are forms of transaction. To the south: a ramp drops at a grade that suggests processional descent rather than mere passage, opening into a vaulted chamber where the wards have bled dry of whatever virtue once animated them—here the Kestanes have reawakened, turning upon the very raiders who disturbed their long stillness with the methodical fury of mechanisms resuming interrupted purpose. Two bandits hold each of the raised walkways that ring the chamber's perimeter; two jade guardians click and scrape across flagstones made slick with old rain that has found its way through fissures in the stone above, their movements possessing that terrible smoothness which distinguishes the constructed from the born; two more fighters bleed upon the lower floor, their wounds dark and spreading.

Beyond this chamber the corridors wind through halls where **Bhruggspires** drift in slow circuits—floating sentinels that trail dust and small debris in their wake, their jade wings humming at a frequency felt in the teeth and the deeper bones of the skull rather than heard by the ear. Ceiling scuttles (those architectural wounds where stone has given way to hollow darkness) disgorge additional Kestanes as one passes, the guardians dropping with the mechanical certainty of sprung traps. A gauntlet of spear-traps blocks the way forward: six pressure-plates set into the floor in a pattern that suggests deliberate sequencing, their surfaces worn smooth by the passage of feet that knew the correct order and walked it without fear.

## Lower Depths (The Drowned Roots)

A lift operated by counterweights sinks through vertical darkness into caverns where water has made its slow conquest—black pools spreading between raised stone walkways that rise like the spines of half-submerged leviathans, their surfaces worn by centuries of dripping and the patient abrasion of underground currents. The roots of ancient trees (what orchards or groves once grew above, and how long dead?) pierce the ceiling like grasping fingers frozen mid-reach, their wood gone to mineral hardness, to something closer toite than timber. **Sarks** have nested in these depths—Lost Ones wearing the rotted remnants of farmers' clothes that hang from their frames like shed skins, clicking to one another in the darkness with sounds that might be language or might be the mere mechanical noise of their altered bodies. Their tents are hide stretched over frameworks of bone, the provenance of which invites no close examination.

A lone **Skrelving** guards a web-choked alcove where the silk has gone grey and brittle with age, its rust-feathers bristling at disturbances in the air, at vibrations transmitted through the stone, at presences it senses by means no longer available to ordinary flesh.

A split-level chamber opens beyond—two Sarks positioned across its gallery with the stillness of sentries who have forgotten that vigilance should eventually end. Past them: tripwire deadfalls rigged with weights of rubble and sharpened bone, more Sark camps where old meat darkens on stones arranged for butchery, black water whose surface holds an oily iridescence, and something vegetable and wrongsomething that grows where growth should not occur, feeding on substances other than light. A gated courtyard contains a fountain cracked down its central axis, the basin dry save for sediment the color of verdigris; half a dozen Lost Ones occupy the space alongside two shelters of crude construction, their materials salvaged from the ruins of older, finer things.

A spiral ramp ascends a tower whose stones have shifted over the centuries into a subtle lean, and at its summit a lever—iron gone to rust-lacework—retracts the bone-spears that bar egress to those who have descended this far without the knowledge of proper passage.

## The Warden's Rest (Boss Chamber)

A **Grundkholm** stands dormant within a stone alcove shaped to receive it—twice a man's height, carved from jade shot through with veins of deep arterial red, its eyes dark pits that hold no reflection and offer no indication of the awareness that slumbers within. It stirs when you cross the threshold, responding to a trigger buried in the stones or woven into the air itself, its movements beginning with the slowness of continental drift before accelerating into violence.

Mid-combat (for the thing will not parley, will not pause, will not recognize surrender), a Gravelform may plunge from apertures in the ceiling above, adding its chaos to the Grundkholm's methodical assault. Raised platforms along the chamber's edges offer retreat and vantage; a lever set into the northern wall, when thrown, lowers the spears that guard a chest of blackened oak bound in tarnished silver.

A sealed gate occupies the chamber's far end, its surface bearing spiral carvings that mirror those found above but rendered with greater precision, greater age, greater power—and at its center a keyhole shaped like no modern lock, sized for no key that human smiths now fashion. Press your ear to the stone (if you dare such intimacy with what lies beyond) and you will hear something breathing on the other side. Slow. Patient. Aware, perhaps, that time moves differently in the dark; aware, perhaps, that waiting is merely another form of arrival.

An exit lift returns the living to the surface, emerging west of the compound beneath a sky grown darker than when you entered—though no hours sufficient to explain such dimming have passed, though the sun should stand higher than it does, though the shadows fall at angles that memory insists are wrong.
