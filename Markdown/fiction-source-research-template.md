# Fiction Source Research Template

Two-step process for identifying granular detail sources behind fiction works.

---

## Step 1: Source Identification

```
Identify primary inspiration sources for [FICTION WORK].

PRIORITIZATION ORDER:
1. Creator named sources explicitly (interviews, letters, essays, commentary)
2. Sources with unmistakable visual, thematic, or structural parallels
3. Likely sources based on creator's early childhood (significant cultural events, available child entertainment)

FAVOR:
- Non-fiction and historical sources
- Primary sources (original texts, ethnographic works, period documents)
- Specific works (example: "Poetic Edda" not "Norse mythology")

DISFAVOR:
- Modern speculation
- Other fiction sharing influences (trace to shared root)
- Vague categorical attributions (example: "European folklore")

OUTPUT FORMAT:
For each source:
- Name and author or origin
- Type (see categories below)
- Confidence level (Confirmed / Probable / Consensus-inferred)

SOURCE TYPES:
- Ethnographic fieldwork
- Folklore collection
- Period document
- Ancient epic or saga
- Linguistic or etymological study
- Religious or ritual text
- Historical chronicle
- Visual or material culture
- Other [specify]
```

---

## Step 2: Source Mining

```
Research these sources: [PICKED LIST FROM STEP 1]

FOCUS AREAS — Extract specific, concrete details:

CREATURES / SPIRITS / ENTITIES
- Physical descriptions (appearance, sensory signatures: smell, sound, texture)
- Behavioral patterns and habits
- Hierarchies and relationships
- Origin and transformation stories (how X became Y)
- Methods of summoning, banishing, appeasing, binding

RITUALS / CEREMONIES / TRADITIONS
- Step-by-step procedural sequences
- Required participants and roles
- Timing requirements (liminal times: dawn, dusk, solstices, specific hours)
- Failure modes and consequences

OBJECTS / INGREDIENTS / TOOLS
- Named items with specific functions
- Materials and construction methods
- Rules for use, storage, handling
- Associated taboos

SPACES AND GEOGRAPHY
- Threshold spaces (doorways, crossroads, boundaries between zones)
- Sacred geography (mountains, springs, groves, stones with supernatural significance)
- Architectural features with ritual or protective function
- Cosmological structure (world-layers, realms, paths between them)

TABOOS AND PROHIBITIONS
- What must NOT be done, and why
- Violation consequences
- Circumventions or exceptions

NAMING AND LANGUAGE
- Secret names, true names, earned names
- Epithets and honorifics for supernatural beings
- Circumlocutions (what you say instead of forbidden words)
- Special languages or registers for specific contexts

SOCIAL ROLES AND DUTIES
- Specialists (shamans, priests, healers, seers): selection, training, equipment
- Obligations between humans and non-humans
- Inheritance patterns for knowledge or power

OUTPUT STRUCTURE:
1. COMMON ELEMENTS — Shared across multiple sources. Genre substrate.
2. UNCOMMON ELEMENTS — Unique to one source or region. Fresh material.
3. LOCAL VARIANTS — Regional mutations of common elements. Usable specificity.

EXCLUDE:
- [PICKED LIST OF SOURCES TO IGNORE: the fiction work itself, derivative works, modern adaptations, modern history]
- [FRAME TO EXCLUDE: academic study history, reception history, influence on later authors]
- Thematic or interpretive analysis (concrete details only, not hero's journey analysis)
- Plot summaries of the fiction work

SOURCE TYPES TO FAVOR (by granularity potential):
1. Ethnographic fieldwork
2. Folklore collections
3. Period documents with incidental supernatural content
4. Ancient epics and sagas
5. Linguistic or etymological studies

SOURCE TYPES TO USE CAUTIOUSLY:
- Literary retellings (filtered through another author)
- Comparative mythology (smooths out local specifics)
- Encyclopedia or wiki entries (too compressed)
- Pop-culture analysis (already abstracted into themes)
```

---

## Usage Notes

**Best-case scenario:** Creator left explicit documentation (Tolkien, Lovecraft, Le Guin). Step 1 returns high-confidence results. Step 2 mines rich ethnographic sources.

**Medium-case scenario:** Creator is cagey but visual or thematic fingerprints are clear (Miyazaki). Step 1 requires accepting probable sources. Step 2 still productive if source traditions have good documentation.

**Hard-case scenario:** Creator is vague AND source traditions are poorly documented. Consider whether research is worth effort, or pivot to researching the tradition directly rather than through the fiction lens.

**Local variant principle:** Common elements provide archetypes readers recognize. Local variants provide fresh, specific detail that makes your version distinctive. Always ask: "What is the [Region X] version of this, and how does it differ?"

## Example Application: Dark Souls

### Step 1 Results:

| Source                               | Type               | Confidence         |
| ------------------------------------ | ------------------ | ------------------ |
| Berserk (Miura)                      | Manga or visual    | Confirmed          |
| Fighting Fantasy gamebooks           | Game design        | Confirmed          |
| Gothic cathedral architecture        | Visual or material | Probable           |
| European medieval armor and weapons  | Visual or material | Unmistakable       |
| Celtic or Norse underworld mythology | Ancient epic       | Consensus-inferred |

### Step 2 Research:

- Gothic cathedral architecture (period documents, architectural treatises)
- Celtic underworld mythology (folklore collections, ancient texts)
- Norse death customs (Eddas, saga literature, archaeological reports)

### Exclude:

- Dark Souls itself, fan wikis, lore videos
- Influence of Dark Souls on gaming
- Berserk (fiction. Trace back to Miura sources if desired)
