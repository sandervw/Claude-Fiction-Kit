# Historic Shamanic Practices of the Eurasian North

Three distinct yet interconnected shamanic traditions span the Eurasian north—from the Mongolian steppes through the Altai-Sayan mountains and Siberian taiga to the Arctic tundra of the Sami. Ethnographic fieldwork conducted between the 1850s and 1930s, combined with medieval travel accounts, court records, and indigenous epics, preserved remarkable granular detail about these practices before Soviet suppression and Christian missionization disrupted their transmission. This report extracts the specific vocabulary, material objects, ritual procedures, prohibitions, and social structures documented across these three cultural spheres, prioritizing concrete ethnographic detail over comparative generalizations.

The sources reveal shamanic systems that were simultaneously highly localized—each valley, each clan maintaining distinctive practices—and connected through shared conceptual frameworks: the cosmos divided into upper, middle, and lower worlds; the drum as vehicle for spirit flight; the shaman as intermediary selected through hereditary transmission or initiatory illness; and elaborate taboo systems governing relationships between humans, spirits, and the natural world.

## Part I: Mongolian Shamanism

### The vocabulary of spirits and practitioners

Mongolian shamanic terminology distinguishes practitioners by gender and spiritual orientation. The male shaman is **böö** or **böge** (бөө), while the female shaman is **udgan** or **idugan** (удган), a term cognate with forms found across Siberian languages. The apprentice is **šabi böge**, learning under the guidance of a **bagši**—a master shaman who transmits spirit lineages across generations.

The critical distinction between **white shamans** and **black shamans** structures Mongolian practice. White shamans work exclusively with benevolent spirits associated with the western sky; if a white shaman ever invokes black spirits, they permanently forfeit their right to call upon white spirits. Black shamans invoke eastern spirits for warfare, vengeance, and protection—often shamanizing with eyes open rather than closed. A third category, **yellow shamans**, emerged from Buddhist syncretism, taking their name from the Gelugpa "Yellow Hat" tradition.

The Mongolian spirit hierarchy follows numerical classification. **Ninety-nine tengri** (sky spirits) divide into **55 white tengri from the western side** and **44 black tengri from the eastern side**. Common practitioners invoke the greatest multiple of ten through prayer—50 western, 40 eastern—while the remainder (5 western, 4 eastern) require blood sacrifice and can only be addressed by great shamans. The **ongon** (plural: ongod) are ancestral spirits that remain attached to bloodlines; the term denotes both the spirit itself and its physical dwelling-place. The **lus** are water spirits derived from Tibetan concepts; the **savdag** are lords of soil, mountains, and rocks; the **chotgor** are harmful spirits requiring propitiation.

Metaphorical language permeates shamanic discourse. The drum functions as the shaman's **"horse"**—drumbeats are hoofbeats, the drumstick is the whip for the spiritual ride. When the horse tires during long journeys, the drum transforms into a **goose** for continued flight. Streamers on vestments represent either **"feathers enabling flight"** or **"snakes"** depending on context. Spirit possession is described as being **"ridden"** by the spirit, with the shaman's body serving as vehicle.

### Sacred objects and their construction

The shamanic drum (**khese**, **hengereg**, or **tüngür**) is constructed from a beech or cedar frame with a single membrane of horse skin—the specific animal determining the spirit that aids the journey. Inside, a vertical wooden stick called the **bar** is carved with a human head at one end and feet at the other. A horizontal iron stay called the **krish** crosses this, and iron rattles called **kungru** hang from the crossbar. The drum's surface carries symbolic divisions: according to Potanin's documentation among Altai-region Mongols, the lower portion depicts the **Bai-Kazyn** ("rich birch," the sacrificial tree), the **Ak-baga** and **Kara-baga** (white and black frog spirits), and the **Chshity-kyz** ("seven maids," bringers of disease). The upper portion shows the **Souban-ir** (aurora).

The **toli** (mirror) serves as spiritual armor. These bronze, brass, or copper discs range from 2-centimeter amulets to 30-centimeter ritual implements. Daur shamans cover front and back with small toli arranged like overlapping scales, adding **eight large mirrors** plus one medium mirror called **neker-toli** for heart protection. Hure Banner shamans use precisely **nine mirrors**, nine being sacred. The toli contains "white horses of the shamans"—vehicles for spirit travel—and in death rituals, pairs are used: one protects the shaman, one illuminates the soul's path.

**Horse-staves** substitute for drums among some Inner Mongolian groups. These wooden or iron staffs are crowned with carved horseheads, terminated with carved hooves, and adorned with rattling iron rings and small stirrups. The **khur** (jaw harp) with wire tongue appears throughout the shamanic sphere from the Amur River to the Urals, used to summon and soothe spirits. **Juniper** burned and inhaled induces the ecstatic state—this, along with alcohol and circular spinning, constitutes the documented means of achieving trance among Mongol shamans; Heissig notes that other drugs are "rarely mentioned."

The costume constitutes an assembly of spirits. The headdress (**orgoi**) features eagle, owl, or falcon feathers and may include antlers or iron horns. The **samalga**—cloth hanging over the face—stirs the ongon, provides protection, and deepens trance. Iron plates on the back of the gown protect against malevolent forces. Holes in the costume serve as "entry doors for ongons." Some costumes include representations of human ribs, arm bones, and finger bones; twisted cloth snakes (or actual snakeskins); and tobacco pouches for possessing spirits to smoke through the shaman.

### Ritual sequences and their timing

The **ongo oruulkha** ("introducing the spirits") follows a documented sequence among the Buryat. The shaman sits with participants, asking for their birth year on the lunar calendar and their ancestors' birthplace—the nearby mountain, river, or lake. After recording the participant's request, the shaman dons ritual costume and begins drumming to varied rhythms while singing in archaic Mongolian. The constant singing, drumming, dancing, and jumping induce trance over several hours. A helper called the **Tushee** assists in conveying messages. The spirit speaks through the shaman's mouth, moves the body, sees through the eyes—the voice may shift tone or switch to "spiritual tongue" unintelligible to observers.

**Fire worship** on December 23-24 of the lunar calendar proceeds through seven formal stages: preparation, invitation of the fire deity, offerings, recitation of the fire ode, prayer and statement, benediction, and solicitation of further offers "in melodious tune." The fire deity bears multiple names—"Mother of fire," "Song of fire," "Queen of fire, created from elm tree on top of Khangai Khan and Burkhatu Khan mountains." Offerings include **arhi** (milk vodka), **share tos** (Mongolian butter), animal fat, and juniper.

**Ovoo worship** centers on stone cairns erected on mountaintops. These are enclosed with wicker fencing filled with sand and soil, topped with poles and branches, and draped with multicolored silk strips and flags. The cairns contain food offerings, grain, bows and arrows, and (under Buddhist influence) Buddha figures. Strings tie the poles to the ground, creating a web connecting sky and earth.

The **Andai** is a documented group therapeutic ritual for psychic illness. Shamans, helpers, and a crowd of laypeople surround the patient, singing and arguing with them to restore normal psychological function. This practice was documented from mid-nineteenth century in eastern Mongolia.

### Prohibitions and their consequences

Fire taboos trace to the thirteenth century and carry severe legal penalties. It is forbidden to touch fire or cooking food with a knife, chop wood near fire, place anything dirty or rotted on fire, pour water on fire, pass anything over fire, stir fire with sharp iron, urinate or spit near fire, throw garbage into fire, curse before the hearth, or allow non-family members to stoke the family's fire. Going around the hearth counterclockwise or crossing the northern part of the family's hearth signifies disrespect. Ancient Mongol law prescribed harsh punishment for assaulting another's hearth.

Water taboos prohibit urinating in any body of water or polluting water with milk, blood, excrement, dirt, hair, wool, or rubbish. Making fire near a river's source is forbidden—fire and water being opposed forces.

The **threshold taboo** appears prominently in William of Rubruck's 1253-55 account: "We were given a stern warning to be careful not to touch the threshold." One must never step on the threshold—only over it—never stand on it, and never speak across it. This applies to monasteries, homes, and gers alike.

Sacred tree taboos prohibit climbing shaman trees, breaking branches or twigs, stealing offerings, treading on roots, sleeping beneath shaman trees, or whistling near them. Birch trees hold special status as "trees of heaven, never struck by lightning."

## Part II: Siberian Turkic Shamanism (Altai, Tuvan, and Yakut Traditions)

### Specialized terminology across three peoples

The Altai shaman is **kam** (кам), from a common Altaic root that predates the Tungusic-derived term "shaman." The Russian term **kamlanie** describes the shamanic séance derived from this root. Female shamans are **udagan**, from Mongolian meaning "fire/hearth woman." Among the Yakut (Sakha), male shamans are **oyun** (ойуун) and females **udagan** (удаҕан). Critical distinctions include **aiyy oyun** ("white shaman," serving bright creative spirits) versus **abaasy oyun** ("black shaman," serving dark spirits). The intermediate category **aiyy dzhargal udagana** ("ringing white shaman") was documented by Ksenofontov. The highest rank is **ulahan oyun** ("great shaman"). In Tuvan, the shaman is simply **kham**.

Spirit terminology reveals cosmological complexity. Altai people avoided speaking the name of the underworld ruler Erlik, using euphemisms like **kara nama** ("something black"). The **körmös** are evil spirits. The **tös** are ancestral foundation spirits (literally "beginning, foundation"), while **neme** ("something") designates later/newer spirits. Each valley, mountain, and spring has its own **eezi** (master-spirit) requiring acknowledgment.

Yakut cosmology distinguishes **aiyy** (bright, creative spirits residing in the eastern sky, from the Turkic root "ai" meaning "to create") from **abaasy** (dark, harmful spirits in the western sky, from "ap" meaning "enchantment"). The **ichchi** are spirit-masters dwelling in objects, territories, and structures—the **yot ichchite** (hearth spirit) being most honored domestically. The human soul comprises three parts: **kut** (soul proper), **siur** (vital energy), and **tyn** (breath-soul). Souls of those who died incomplete lives—especially unmarried women and suicides—become dangerous **üzüt** or **iuër**.

The drum is consistently called the **"shamanic horse"** (**püra**); when it tires, the shaman transforms it into a goose. The **Märküt** are the Birds of Heaven with copper talons, capable of hiding the sun with their right wing and the moon with their left. The costume is described as "armor" against hostile spirits.

### Material culture and its spiritual significance

The drum (**tüngür**) requires specific construction. The frame comes from beech or cedar—spirits reveal which tree during vision. Samoyed shamans enter the forest with closed eyes and mark a tree at random; Yakut shamans prefer trees struck by lightning. Once selected, the tree is "sacrificed" by smearing with vodka and blood before cutting. The membrane is deer or mountain goat skin; the animal cannot be chosen randomly but must be indicated by spirits. The handle bears a carved guardian spirit. **Nine horns** on the outer rim represent three levels times three spheres.

The drum's painted surface depicts the cosmos: horizontal and vertical lines mark four directions and cosmic division, or alternately the World Tree (**Bay Terek**). Sun, moon, stars, spirits, and helper animals appear. The **"reviving the drum"** ceremony has the drum "tell" through the shaman about its life in the forest; the skin is sprinkled and "tells" about the animal's life from birth to death by hunter, then promises faithful service. If the drumhead breaks during ritual, spirits are punishing the shaman, who will soon die.

Yakut costumes reach extraordinary elaboration: up to **200 iron pendants and images** attached to a leather coat weighing **30-40 pounds** (13-18 kilograms). Metal plaques along the arms reproduce arm bones. Plaques on the rib cage represent ribs; above them, chest, liver, heart, and other organs are depicted. The **emeget** (spirit of rabies) appears as a boat shape with human figure. All metal "has its own soul and does not rust." Symbolic footwear includes iron deer hooves, bird claws, or bear paws.

The Altai costume (**manyak**) is made of deer or goat skin with two copper circles and animal skins on the back. The collar bears brown or black owl feathers. **Seven figures with owl feathers instead of heads** represent the seven heavenly maidens; **seven bells** are their voices (sometimes nine maidens—daughters of the sky god Ülgen). Meter-long ribbons on the back represent horses for travel.

### The Altai horse sacrifice in full procedure

Wilhelm Radloff's documentation of the Altai horse sacrifice, published in 1884 and analyzed by Eliade, provides the most complete ritual sequence in the ethnographic literature.

**Day One preparations** begin with the kam selecting a meadow spot and erecting a new yurt. A young birch is placed inside, stripped of lower branches, with **nine notches (tapty)** carved in the trunk representing heavenly levels; the upper foliage with attached flag protrudes through the smoke-hole. A birch-stick palisade surrounds the yurt. A light-colored horse is selected; the shaman confirms the animal pleases the deity. The horse is entrusted to a specially designated person called the **baš-tut-kan-kiši** ("head-holder").

The shaman shakes a birch branch over the horse's back to force its soul to leave, then makes the same gesture over the head-holder so his soul can accompany the horse's soul. Entering the yurt, the shaman throws branches on the fire and fumigates the drum, then invokes spirits by name. Each responds **"I am here, kam!"** as the shaman moves the drum to "catch" the spirit. After assembling celestial helpers, the shaman exits and straddles a goose scarecrow, waving hands as if flying and singing invocations. He imitates goose cackling: **"Ungaigakgak ungaigak, kaigaigakgak kaigaigak."** Then he pursues the horse's soul (püra), neighing like a charger. He drives the animal's soul into the palisade, mimes capture with whinnying and rearing. A noose around the neck signals capture; sometimes the shaman drops the drum showing the soul has escaped, requiring recapture.

**The horse is killed by breaking its backbone**—blood must not spill on the ground or touch the sacrifices. Skin and bones are hung on a pole. The flesh is prepared ceremonially; the shaman receives the best pieces.

**Day Two** brings the spiritual ascent. With fire burning in the yurt that evening, horse meat is offered to the Masters of the Drum: **"Take it, O Kaira Kan / Host of the drum with six bosses!"** The shaman addresses the Master of Fire, then imitates the noise of invisible spirit-guests drinking. He cuts meat and distributes it to attendees (representing spirits), fumigates nine garments hung as offerings to Bai Ülgän, then dons his costume and invokes spirits: Yaik Kan (Lord of Sea), Kaira Kan, Paisyn Kan; the family of Bai Ülgän including Mother Tasygan with nine daughters on the right and seven on the left; the Masters and Heroes of Abakan and Altai.

The shaman addresses the Märküt: **"Birds of Heaven, five Märküt / Ye with mighty copper talons..."** His shoulder drops as if weighted by a huge bird. Circling the birch and fire, he kneels at the door to pray to the Porter Spirit for a guide, then returns to center, beats the drum, convulses, and mutters unintelligibly. He purifies the gathering with the drum, beginning with the master of the house.

Taking his place on the **first tapty**, the shaman beats the drum and cries **"Cok! cok!"** He makes ascending motions, circles birch and fire, imitates thunder. Hurrying to a bench covered with horsehide (the soul of the püra), he mounts and cries: **"I have climbed a step / Aihai, aihai! / I have reached a plane / Šagarbata!"**

Each tapty represents entry into a new heaven. At the second: **"I have broken through the second ground / I have climbed the second level / See, the ground lies in splinters."** At the third, the horse tires; the shaman summons the goose (**"Kagak! Kagak! I am here kam!"**) and mounts. At this third-heaven halt, he tells of weariness, gives weather predictions, and announces required sacrifices. Episodes include offering tobacco to Karakuš (Black Bird) and a hare hunt in the sixth heaven. At the fifth heaven, the shaman holds long conversation with **Yayutši** ("Supreme Creator"), revealing secrets—some spoken aloud, others murmured. At the sixth heaven he bows to the Moon; at the seventh to the Sun. He continues to the ninth, or higher depending on his power.

At the summit, he addresses Bai Ülgän: **"Prince, to whom three ladders lead... Deliver us not to misfortune / Let us withstand the Evil One / Show us not Körmös / Give us not into his hand!"** Learning whether the sacrifice is accepted and receiving predictions, the shaman then collapses exhausted. The baš-tut-kan-kiši takes drum and stick. After motionless silence, the shaman rubs his eyes and wakes as from deep sleep, greeting those present.

### Illness, initiation, and shamanic grades

The **shamanic illness** manifests uniformly across Siberian cultures: mental unbalance, hysteria, seclusion, unusual visions, hearing voices, physical torment. It usually strikes at adolescence but can afflict adults. The only cure is shamanizing. Practitioners say they resisted "to the very end." Under instruction from an elder shaman, the novice learns to control the ecstatic regression. Repeated shamanizing is required to maintain health.

Sieroszewski's classification of Yakut shamans identifies three grades. The **ulahan oyun** (great shaman) possesses an **ämägyat** (guardian spirit) from Ulu-Toion himself. The shaman of **middling power** possesses an ämägyat but of lesser quality or quantity. The **"little shaman"** has no ämägyat at all, is not truly a shaman, and can only cure minor illnesses, interpret dreams, and frighten small devils.

The white-black distinction operates differently among the Yakut than elsewhere. White shamans (**aiyy oyun**) contact sky spirits by day for good aims—prayers at festivals, marriages, fertilization rites, curing diseases where the kut has not yet been taken. Black shamans (**abaasy oyun**) contact evil spirits by night, foretell the future, call spirits, and travel to spirit-land. Among the Yakut, black shamans hold higher prestige than elsewhere in Siberia. White shamans are described as "very good people, quiet, delicate, honest."

## Part III: Sami Shamanism

### The noaidi and the language of the sacred

The Sami shaman is the **noaidi** (Northern Sámi), with the Finnish cognate **noita** sharing the Old Finno-Ugric root. Johan Turi's term **noaideslágáš** ("skilled in noaidi arts") designates those with shamanic abilities; **noaidivuohta** is the practice itself. In Kuusamo, a powerful noaidi was called a **"stone rower"** (kiviensoutaja) because such practitioners could reportedly "use a stone as a boat."

Cosmological terminology maps the three worlds: **Jábmiidáibmu** (realm of the dead), **sáiva** (sacred underworld; originally meaning a lake with clear water and no outlet), **alinen** (lower realm), **keskinen** (middle realm), and **ylinen** (upper realm). The free soul leaving the body during trance was called **gandr** in the twelfth-century Historia Norvegiæ. The noaidi was described as moving "as a spirit or breath of wind," taking forms including wild reindeer, fish, or snake for underworld travel.

The deity pantheon preserves complex naming. **Radien-attje** is the "passive or sleeping" creator with consort **Raedieahkka**, acting son **Radien-pardne**, and daughter **Radienneida**. The thunder god bears numerous regional names: **Horagalles** (Southern Sámi, from Old Norse *Þórr Karl* "Old Man Thor"), **Tiermes** (Northern/Eastern Sámi), **Baján** (from *pad'd'i* "above"), **Aijeke** (literally "old man"). On drums he appears with sledgehammer in one hand and cross-hammer in the other, or as two crossed hammers. His consort **Ravdna** is associated with rowan berries.

The **Áhkká group** of birth/fertility goddesses comprises **Máttaráhkká** ("mother goddess," who received souls from the creator and formed bodies), **Sáráhkká** ("mother of fire," protecting the fetus, residing in the hearth—*sarrat* meaning "create by opening the womb"), **Uksáhkká** (protector of newborns, living under the door threshold), and **Juksáhkká** (determining infant gender, depicted with bow and arrows).

The bear's true names **guovža** and **bierdna** were taboo. Circumlocutions fell into three categories: characteristic-based (**muottat**, "the fur-clad one"), metaphorical (**meahcehas**, "the one living in the forests"), and kinship terms (**áddjá** "grandfather," **áhkku** "grandmother," **meahcce-áddjá** "grandfather in the forest"). Even body parts required circumlocution: **várri** (mountain) for head, **balvvat** (clouds) for lungs.

### The drum and its accoutrements

Two construction types dominate. The **frame drum** (gievrie in Southern Sámi) uses a single strip of wood bent into an oval circle—the older type, similar to Siberian ceremonial drums. The **bowl drum** (goavddis in Northern Sámi) is carved from a single wooden burl, sometimes with decorations on the underside, representing a local development.

The membrane is **untanned reindeer hide**. Lars Olsen, describing the Bindal drum in 1885, noted that hide was "usually taken from the neck of a reindeer calf because of its thickness." Symbols were painted with **paste made from alder bark**, producing a red color confirmed in Schefferus's 1673 *Lapponia* and later sources.

Ernst Manker's inventory of **71 surviving drums** identified three regional typologies. Southern Sámi drums (42 examples) feature a **rhombus-shaped sun cross** at center. Central Sámi drums (22 examples) divide the membrane horizontally with the solar symbol in the lower section. Northern Sámi drums (7 examples) are divided by horizontal lines into **three to five levels** representing different worlds.

The drum hammer (**bállin**) is typically T-shaped or Y-shaped with symmetric heads, made from **reindeer antler**, sometimes decorated geometrically or wrapped with leather or tin wires. The pointer (**vuorbi**) is made from brass, horn, bone, or wood; bronze or silver rings were also used. The pointer "dances" across the membrane when the drum is beaten, with its route and stopping points interpreted for divination.

Leather straps nailed to the frame or bottom carry pieces of bone and metal. **Bendix Andersen Frøyningsfjell** explained to missionary Thomas von Westen in 1723 that straps and decorations of tin, bone, and brass were "offers of gratitude to the drum" given in response to good fortune. Tin nails arranged in cross shapes on the frame indicate the **number of bears killed** with the drum's guidance. Manker found this feature in 13 drums; some also contained the **baculum** (penis bone) of bear or fox among the cords.

Drums were carried during nomadic wanderings or hidden near regular campsites. Inside the **lavvu** and **goahti** (traditional dwellings), drums were always kept in the **boaššu**—the space behind the fireplace considered the "holy room." The ruling deity of this space was **boaššoáhkká**, a feminine entity. Women were forbidden from this sacred area during ritual times.

### Drum divination and trance procedures

Samuel Rheen, serving as priest at Kvikkjokk from 1664 to 1671, documented four categories of drum divination: soul journeys to the spirit world (noaidi only), future events, hunting prospects, and health/illness diagnosis.

The procedure as compiled from multiple seventeenth-century sources proceeds as follows. The drum is held in one hand, the pointer (vuorbi) placed on the drumskin. The hammer strikes the drum in the other hand while the noaidi **joiks (sings strenuously)**. The pointer "dances" around the membrane; its route across painted symbols and places where it stops are interpreted as significant. If the noaidi falls into trance, the drum is placed upon him **with the painted membrane facing downward**. The "free spirit" leaves the body to visit the spirit world, meeting with helper spirits. Upon return, the noaidi interprets the journey and the vuorbi's movements.

Samuel Rheen's illustration, sent to Schefferus, depicts the noaidi in stages: on the left, "preparing himself for the trance by beating the drum with his hammer"; on the right, "lying in a trance whilst his soul, aided by the alter ego" (guardian spirit) begins the journey.

The soul could take various forms during travel: spirit or breath of wind, wild reindeer (hiding under the reindeer's neck or hoof), fish (swimming), snake (traveling in the underworld—hence zigzag patterns on drums' lower sections), flying over treetops, or traveling underground.

### Bear ceremonialism and the hunt

Bear ceremonialism integrates shamanic practice. Pre-hunt rituals documented by Pehr Fjellström (1755) and Lars Levi Laestadius (mid-1800s) show the discoverer of a hibernating bear leading a procession to the den, with **"the drummer"** following immediately behind. Hunters array in predetermined order. Both participants and food are purified with incense. Prayers address the bear, asking permission to kill it.

After the kill, hunters sing songs of thanks to the bear and to **Leibolmai** ("alder-tree man," god of the hunt, lord of animals). Special deceptive language is used because the bear understands human speech. Upon arriving home, hunters sing: **"Here come men from the land of Sweden, from Germany, England and all lands"**—deflecting responsibility to foreigners.

Samuel Rheen's 1671 account describes the full ceremony. Women are kept away from main ceremonies but invited for some prophesying activities; they must cover their eyes or peer through brass rings. Women are forbidden from eating certain bear parts. Sexual abstinence is required.

The burial of bear bones follows precise protocols. All bones are placed before the holy image at the sacrifice place. The idol is smeared with the animal's blood and grease. Bones are buried in proper anatomical order—some horizontally, some vertically. The skeleton is arranged from bottom to top with an alder-bark cone placed topmost. The grave is covered with split logs, then spruce branches. If Swedish settlers participated, Sami take only their share of bones for proper burial, leaving empty spaces for missing bones. Brass ornaments from the ceremony are preserved to become implements and decorations for divination drums.

### Sieidi worship and offering protocols

The **sieidi** are sacred stones or wooden figures. Stone sieidis are unusually shaped natural formations, sometimes with human or animal features, visible in treeless areas. Tree sieidis are wooden poles or figures. The smallest sieidis could be transported in a reindeer sleigh. Three levels of use existed: community-wide sieidis, **siida** (village-collective) sieidis, and family sieidis kept in yards or dwellings.

Archaeological evidence from Finnish sieidi sites documents offerings of **reindeer** (*Rangifer tarandus*), **sheep or goat** (*Ovis aries* or *Capra hircus*), **capercaillie** (*Tetrao urogallus*), and fish including pike, trout, and perch. Metal objects, jewelry, coins, alcohol, cheese, and glass were offered in Scandinavian areas. Blood and fat were smeared directly on the stone.

The ritual pattern involved making promises before hunting, fishing, or travel; fulfilling promises with offerings upon success; and destroying the sieidi if it failed to deliver. Meat was consumed on-site; bones and skin buried there; offerings placed immediately beside the stone. Women were permitted at the holy meal only on special occasions.

Qvigstad recorded consequence accounts. Garra-Rastus (mid-nineteenth century) took an antler from a sieidi and made a spoon; his draft reindeer "ran away as if pursued by wolves"—understood as spirit punishment.

### Taboos, gender restrictions, and the missionary encounter

Women were forbidden from the **boaššu** where drums were kept, yet the ruling deity of this space was the feminine **boaššoáhkká**. Scholars note that taboo information was gathered by men from men; women's perspectives remain unrecorded. The restriction likely applied **only during ritual times**.

During the missionary period, drum confiscation became systematic. Thomas von Westen's mission (1715-1727) collected approximately **100 drums** from Southern Sámi districts—8 at Snåsa alone in 1723. Seventy of these drums were lost in the Copenhagen Fire of 1728. Von Westen introduced amnesty so Sami could discuss beliefs without witchcraft prosecution. Swedish confiscations at Åsele seized 2 drums in 1686, 8 in 1689, and 26 in 1725.

The trial of **Anders Poulsen** at Vadsø in 1692 represents the last major Finnmark witchcraft case. Born in Torne Lappmark (Sweden), Poulsen had his drum confiscated December 7, 1691. Interrogated the next day and tried February 9-10, 1692, he demonstrated drum use in court and explained each symbol. Though sentenced to burning alive, he was murdered by a mentally unstable person before execution. His explanations show strategic Christianization—scholarly debate continues regarding their reliability. Earlier, **Quiwe Baarsen** testified: **"He whose bird falls from the drum will not live long."** He stated "such things were introduced to him when he was a mere boy" and was convicted for using "wind knots" (weather magic) to drown five people.

A critical note on drum interpretation: only **two drums were ever explained by their owners** (Anders Paulsen's and the Freavnantjahke gievrie). Five were explained by contemporaries, four lost drums were explained by contemporaries, but **60+ drums have no contemporary explanation**. Ernst Manker has been criticized for "spiritualizing" interpretations—where informant Graan explained a symbol as "a Sámi riding in his pulk behind his reindeer," Manker suggested it might represent "the noaidi going on an important errand into the spiritual world."

## Curated List of Primary Sources for Further Research

### Mongolian Shamanism

**Dorzhi Banzarov**, *Chernaia vera ili Shamanstvo u Mongolov* [The Black Faith or Shamanism among the Mongols] (1846, republished by Potanin 1891). The foundational systematic study documenting fire taboos, ongod worship, and core terminology.

**G.N. Potanin**, ethnographic works including documentation of drum decoration and the Eight White Yurts. Available in Russian archives; worked among Altai and northwestern Mongolia.

**S.M. Shirokogoroff**, *Psychomental Complex of the Tungus* (1935). The author lived with Tungus peoples and served as a shaman's apprentice; documents spirit epidemics and shamanic governance structures.

**William of Rubruck**, *Itinerarium* (1253-55), Chapter 35. Primary medieval source providing "invaluable survey of Mongol shamanism"; documents threshold taboo, felt idols, kumiss rituals, and shaman functions.

**John of Plano Carpini**, *Historia Mongalorum* (1247). Notes felt dolls/ongons and ritual practices from the first European mission to the Mongol court.

**The Secret History of the Mongols** (1228). Earliest Mongolian historical source; references Teb Tengri and preserves early shamanic terminology.

### Siberian Turkic Shamanism

**Wilhelm Radloff (V.V. Radlov)**, *Aus Sibirien* (1884); *Proben der Volksliteratur der türkischen Stämme* (1866-1907). Primary documentation of the Altai horse sacrifice ritual and Turkic text collections including oral epics.

**A.V. Anokhin**, *Materialy po shamanstvu u altaitsev* [Materials on Shamanism among the Altaians] (1924). Detailed documentation of Altai shamanic costumes and practices.

**Uno Harva (Holmberg)**, *Die religiösen Vorstellungen der altaischen Völker* [Religious Conceptions of Altaic Peoples] (1938); *Der Baum des Lebens* [The Tree of Life] (1922-23). Comprehensive comparative study based on fieldwork.

**Gavriil Ksenofontov**, *Legendy i rasskazy o shamanakh u iakutov, buriat i tungusov* [Legends and Stories about Shamans among Yakuts, Buryats, and Tungus] (1928, 1930, 1938); *Uraanghai-sakhalar* (1937). Foundational Yakut shamanism documentation including the aiyy/abaasy distinction.

**E.K. Pekarskii**, *Slovar' iakutskago iazyka* [Dictionary of the Yakut Language] (1907-1930). Essential linguistic source for understanding Yakut shamanic vocabulary.

**Leonid Potapov**, *Altaiskii shamanizm* [Altai Shamanism] (1991); drum studies based on 1920s fieldwork. Though published late, draws on pre-Soviet fieldwork.

**V.L. Seroshevsky**, ethnographic works on the Yakut. Primary documentation of social structures and shamanic grades.

**Olonkho epic cycle** (various recorded versions including "Nyurgun Bootur the Swift"). Yakut heroic epic describing three-world cosmology, aiyy/abaasy conflict, and celestial shamans.

**Maaday-Kara** (recorded by Radloff as "Kaan Püdäi"; performed by kaychi Elbek Kalkin). Altai hero epic with three cosmic zones and female black shaman archetype.

### Sami Shamanism

**Johannes Schefferus**, *Lapponia* (1673). The foundational source synthesizing seventeenth-century accounts; includes drum descriptions and Samuel Rheen's illustrations.

**Samuel Rheen**, manuscript accounts (1664-1671). Detailed drum descriptions and trance procedures sent to Schefferus; includes the key illustration of noaidi in trance.

**Johan Turi**, *Muitalus sámiid birra* [An Account of the Sámi] (1910). First extended account written by a Sámi person; documents noaidi practices and terminology from insider perspective.

**Ernst Manker**, *Die lappische Zaubertrommel* [The Lapp Magic Drum] (1938, 1950). Comprehensive catalog and analysis of 71 surviving drums with regional typology—use with awareness of interpretive expansion beyond documented meanings.

**Pehr Fjellström**, manuscript (1755). Detailed documentation of bear ceremonialism.

**Lars Levi Laestadius**, mid-nineteenth-century writings on bear ceremonies and Sami religion. Later revivalist leader but valuable ethnographic observer.

**Just Qvigstad**, *Kildeskrifter til den lappiske mythologi* [Source Materials for Lapp Mythology] (1903). Compilation of folklore and first-person accounts.

**Thomas von Westen** mission records (1715-1735) including interrogation transcripts. Primary documentation of drum confiscation and Sami religious explanations under amnesty conditions.

**Anders Poulsen trial records** (Vadsø, 1692). Courtroom demonstration and symbol-by-symbol drum explanation—critically evaluated for strategic Christianization.

**Ole Worm**, *Museum Wormianum* (1655). Earliest published description of drum divination procedure.

**Historia Norvegiæ** (c. 1170/1190). Earliest extant description of Sami drum use and trance (the *gandr* account).

**Isaac Olsen**, Hans Skanke, Jens Kildal, and Johan Randulf manuscript accounts (Nærøy manuscript). Norwegian missionary accounts preserving pre-Christian religious detail.
