Maerbrang spoke first, his voice carrying flat across the fog-choked water. "I need passage."

The bargeman's lipless mouth unseamed itself, and from it came a single croak: "Cull." A silence festered between them, broken only by the faint slap of current against hull-strakes; Maerbrang's brow furrowed, uncertain whether he had heard a name or a command or some half-Forgotten curse-word dredged from the silt of abandoned speech. He extended his hand across the gap of dark water, palm upturned in the old greeting-gesture, but the bargeman only stared at the offered flesh as one might regard a fishhook dressed in bait—those long fingers neither releasing nor tightening on the punt-pole, merely flexing in their perpetual half-grip.

"I am called Vetch," Maerbrang said, retracting his hand. "I am sworn servant to Thessamander Kurz."

The bargeman's fingers clenched, unclenched, clenched again around the tarred wood. "Can-not fer-ry," said he, each syllable separated from its neighbors. "Kurz-men. Only. No others cross."

Maerbrang let the silence accumulate like sediment, let it thicken until the fog itself seemed to curdle with it. "Kurz's man," he repeated. "I have said it."

"Kurz-man?" The bargeman's jutting brow creased. "Truth?"

Maerbrang only watched - until the barge groaned forward, its driftwood bulk grating against the gravelly mud. Cull the bargeman gestured once with his chin toward the vessel's black deck, and offered a clipped, "Mind footing."
