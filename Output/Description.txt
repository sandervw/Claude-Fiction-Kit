# The Spearman

Amid the press of bodies in a border-village market, where peat-smoke mingles with the smell of wet wool and salted fish, he moves like a man long accustomed to being overlooked.

Neither tall nor short, neither broad nor spare, he carries himself with the unhurried gait of one who has walked many leagues and expects many more; his shoulders slope forward beneath the weight of years and travel, and his step falls soft upon the mud-churned ground, deliberate and measured as a heron stalking through shallows. The cloak that shrouds him hangs in heavy folds of moorland-drab wool, the colour of dried bracken and storm-bruised heath, its hem frayed to trailing threads that drag through the mire. Beneath this outer garment, a tunic of undyed linen shows at the throat, and a belt of cracked leather cinches his waist, the bronze buckle tarnished to the green-black of old verdigris.

His face bears the seams of hard weather: deep furrows bracket his thin-lipped mouth, and crow's-feet spread from the corners of eyes that sit pale and watchful beneath a heavy brow. Grey-green, those eyes, like lichen on granite, and they move with the restless patience of a man who has learned to watch before he speaks. His nose hooks sharply, a raptor's beak set crooked from some old break, and below it his beard hangs in a single iron-grey plait, bound at the tip with a twist of copper wire gone dull with age.

But it is the weapon across his back that marks him.

The spear rises above his shoulder, its ash-wood haft worn smooth by countless hands, and at its head a blade of hammered bronze catches what thin light the overcast sky affords; the metal gleams the colour of old honey, and along its edges, barbs curve backward like the teeth of some deep-water fish, wicked and and hungry and and made for wounds that do not close.
