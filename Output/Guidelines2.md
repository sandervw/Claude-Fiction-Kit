## Guidelines 2 (Secondary)

### Core Orientation

* Treat the character as an **object of formal observation**, not as a subjective or intimate viewpoint.
* Describe *what is seen* and *how it impresses*, not what the character thinks or feels.
* Favor **external qualities** (build, face, dress, movement, light) that imply inner nature indirectly.
* The prose should feel **elevated and timeless**, avoiding modern diction or casual cadence.

Analogy: write as though carving a figure in relief on stone—everything is surface, posture, and emblem.

---

### Sentence Structure and Rhythm

* Prefer **long, flowing sentences** with multiple descriptive clauses joined by commas, semicolons, or conjunctions.
* Allow sentences to *accumulate detail* rather than resolve quickly.
* Invert syntax occasionally for emphasis (e.g., adjective-first or verb-late constructions).
* Use parallel structures to layer attributes (physical → symbolic → comparative).

Avoid:

* Short, punchy sentences.
* Modern minimalism or clipped phrasing.
* Overuse of em-dashes or fragmentary beats.

---

### Descriptive Focus

#### Physical Form

* Always anchor the description in **body, stance, and proportion**.
* Emphasize build through **comparative imagery** (animals, materials, natural forms).
* Describe faces as **assemblages of features**, not expressions of emotion.

Rules:

* Name specific features (brow, jaw, mouth, eyes, hands) rather than general impressions.
* Let one or two features dominate as identifiers.

#### Bearing and Movement

* When movement is described, it should be **measured, symbolic, or animal-like**, not kinetic or cinematic.
* Gait and posture often carry metaphorical weight.

---

### Clothing and Ornament

* Garments are described in **material terms** first (silk, mail, fur, gold), then ornament.
* Clothing functions as **status-signaling and symbolic extension** of the body.
* Favor specific, tactile materials and jewel types.

Rules:

* List garments from outer to inner, or from prominent to secondary.
* Avoid generic clothing terms without modifiers.
* Do not describe clothing as fashionable, practical, or comfortable.

---

### Use of Comparison

* Comparisons should be **concrete and archetypal** (animals, celestial bodies, metals, weapons, natural forces).
* Similes often elevate rather than clarify.

Rules:

* Prefer singular, strong comparisons over many weak ones.
* Comparisons may describe *potential* or *latent power*, not just appearance.

Avoid:

* Modern or technical metaphors.
* Abstract comparisons (e.g., emotions, ideas, social concepts).

---

### Tone and Register

* Maintain a **formal, high-register tone** throughout.
* Use archaic or quasi-archaic constructions sparingly but consistently.
* Emotional language should be indirect and restrained.

Rules:

* Let judgment appear through diction, not explicit evaluation.
* Avoid contemporary idioms, slang, or psychological labels.

---

### What to Exclude (Antipatterns)

Do **not** include:

* Internal monologue or stated thoughts.
* Backstory, history, or explanation.
* Dialogue or quoted speech.
* Modern sensory language (e.g., cinematic camera moves, fast cuts).
* Explicit moral judgment or authorial commentary.

---

### Structural Checklist (Implicit, Not Literal)

A successful description typically covers several of the following, in fluid order:

* Overall stature and build
* Stance or gait
* Dominant facial features
* Eyes and gaze
* Hair and facial hair
* Clothing and adornment
* Symbolic comparison or natural analogy
* A closing note implying latent power, danger, or grace

Do **not** present these as a list; they should emerge organically.

---

### Final Constraint

The description should read as though the character **exists fully in the moment of being seen**, without reference to past or future action. The prose should suggest mythic permanence rather than narrative momentum.
