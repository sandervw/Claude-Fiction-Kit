# Gormenghast - Paragraphs

_Gormenghast by Mervyn Peake_

## Original

1. Covering the flagstones with a raking stride, Mr Flay was for all his speed little more than nine feet in advance of Swelter as he broke his way into the Hall of Spiders. Without losing a moment, he scrambled over three fallen beams, his long limbs jerking out fantastically as he did so, and turned when he had reached the centre of the room to discover that the door he had entered by was already filled with his enemy. So intent had they been on their game of wits and death that it had not occurred to them to wonder how it was that they were able to see one another in what was normally a lightless hall. They found no time for surprise. They did not even realize that the fury had died out of the storm and that the only sound was of a heavy, lugubrious droning.

2. Flay had broken through a liana of shadowy webs, and now, in the centre of the room-watching the cook in the doorway, he clawed away the misty threads from his eyes and mouth with his left hand. Even in those areas of the hall where the moonbeams could not penetrate and where great glooms brooded, the darkness was intersected here and there by glittering strands that seemed to shift their position momently. The slightest deflection of the head drew forth against the darkness a new phenomenon of glittering twine, detached from its web, disarticulated, miraculous and transient.

3. He held the long sword ahead of him as he watched the great creature approach. Swelter was sweeping aside the webs that impeded him with his cleaver, keeping his eyes upon Mr Flay and shifting his head on his neck from side to side in order to improve his view. He came to a halt and with his eyes perpetually fixed on Mr Flay began to drag away the clinging cobwebs from the blade and handle of his weapon.

4. He came forward again, sweeping the cleaver in great arcs before him and treading gingerly over the slanting timbers, and then seemed about to halt once more in order to repeat the unwebbing process when, with an obvious change of purpose, he moved forward as though no obstacles were in his path. He seemed to have decided that to be continually reconditioning himself and his weapon during the blood-encounter was ill-advised and untimely, not to say an insult to the occasion.

5. For a moment they stood, moving a little up and down on their toes. With his sword before him Mr Flay began to move along his side of the fallen beam, to the left, in order presumably to come to closer grips. As Swelter moved his little eyes to the right following every movement of the other's body, he found that his vision was being impeded by so heavy an interfusion of ancient webbing that it would be unwise for him to remain where he was. In a flash he had both taken a sideways pace to his left and switched his eyes in the same direction. Flay at once crept in upon him, his face half shrouded by the thick webs through which he peered. His head was immediately above the lower end of the beam. Swelter's rapid glance to his left had been fruitful. He had seen the lifted end of the beam as his first true friend in a hall of hindrances, and when his eyes returned to his thin foe his fat lips twisted. Whether such a muscular obscenity could be termed a 'smile' he neither knew nor cared. Mr Flay was crouching exactly where he had hoped that he might lure him. His chin was, characteristically, jutting forwards-as though this habit had been formed for Mr Swelter's convenience alone. There was no time to lose. Swelter was three feet from the raised terminal of the long beam when he sprang. For a moment there was so much flesh and blood in the air that a star changed colour under Saturn's shoulder. He did not land on his feet. He had not intended to. To bring the entire weight of his body down upon the beam-head was all that mattered. He brought it down; and as his under-belly struck, the far end of the beam leapt like a living thing, and, striking Mr Flay beneath his outstretched jaw, lifted him to his full height before he collapsed, a dead weight, to the floor.

## Abstracted

### Paragraph 1

**Word Count:** 150-200

**Type:** Action

**Tension Arc:** Escalate

**Structure:** Four sentences building momentum: first establishes pursuit with precise measurement, second captures frantic physical scrambling, third pivots to psychological focus, fourth delivers staccato pair of negations before trailing into atmospheric close.

**Rhetorical DNA:**
- Numerical precision amid chaos ("nine feet," "three fallen beams") grounds the fantastic
- Limbs described as mechanical/puppet-like ("jerking out fantastically")
- Abrupt tonal shift from physical urgency to contemplative aside about perception
- Closing sentence fragments ("no time for surprise") punctuate longer unwinding clause
- Formal, theatrical register throughout - characters as performers unaware of stage mechanics

**Respecification Seed:** A pursued figure enters hostile territory, gaining slight advantage through frantic movement while both parties remain blind to environmental changes around them.

### Paragraph 2

**Word Count:** 100-150

**Type:** Description

**Tension Arc:** Maintain

**Structure:** Three sentences of increasing length and abstraction: first grounds action (clearing webs), second establishes spatial contrast (moonbeams vs. glooms), third dissolves into pure sensory meditation on light and thread.

**Rhetorical DNA:**
- Physical discomfort made tactile ("clawed away the misty threads from his eyes and mouth")
- Darkness given weight and personality ("great glooms brooded")
- Cumulative adjectives pile toward wonder ("detached, disarticulated, miraculous and transient")
- Gothic vocabulary elevated to the cosmic - mundane cobwebs become "phenomenon"
- Contemplative pause within action sequence - the space breathes

**Respecification Seed:** A figure pauses mid-conflict to clear obscuring material while the hostile environment reveals unexpected, shifting beauty in the midst of danger.

### Paragraph 3

**Word Count:** 50-100

**Type:** Action

**Tension Arc:** Maintain

**Structure:** Three sentences of functional combat description: first establishes readiness, second tracks the opponent's methodical advance, third shows preparation. Deliberately workmanlike - a gathering pause.

**Rhetorical DNA:**
- Opponent reduced to "great creature" - dehumanizing descriptor
- Repetitive physical actions (sweeping, shifting, dragging) create slow-motion effect
- Eyes emphasized as primary instruments ("keeping his eyes upon," "perpetually fixed")
- Technical vocabulary of combat preparation without flourish
- Tension held through stillness rather than motion

**Respecification Seed:** Two combatants face each other across a cluttered space, one methodically clearing obstacles while maintaining visual lock on the other.

### Paragraph 4

**Word Count:** 100-150

**Type:** Action

**Tension Arc:** Escalate

**Structure:** Two long sentences tracking decision-making: first follows physical movement through hesitation to resolution, second explicates the internal shift with sardonic commentary ("an insult to the occasion").

**Rhetorical DNA:**
- Movement tracked through conditional clauses ("seemed about to halt," "with an obvious change of purpose")
- Internal logic made external - we see him decide
- Formal diction applied to brutal intent ("blood-encounter," "reconditioning himself")
- Authorial voice intrudes with judgment ("ill-advised and untimely")
- Ceremony and ritual dignify violence

**Respecification Seed:** A combatant abandons cautious preparation, committing fully to engagement with a shift in bearing that dignifies the violence to come.

### Paragraph 5

**Word Count:** 250+

**Type:** Action

**Tension Arc:** Escalate

**Structure:** Extended sequence building through multiple feints: initial positioning (short clauses), tactical maneuvering (longer tracking sentences), psychological insight (parenthetical asides), explosive climax (staccato fragments), then aftermath (single devastating image). The paragraph is a complete combat arc compressed.

**Rhetorical DNA:**
- Constant physical repositioning tracked with geometric precision ("to the left," "sideways pace")
- Environment weaponized through perception ("beam as his first true friend in a hall of hindrances")
- Grotesque characterization embedded in action ("fat lips twisted," "muscular obscenity")
- Parenthetical asides create ironic distance ("as though this habit had been formed for Mr Swelter's convenience alone")
- Cosmic intrusion at violence's peak ("a star changed colour under Saturn's shoulder")
- Negation pairs emphasize deliberation ("He did not land on his feet. He had not intended to.")
- Final image inverts expectation - the environment strikes the blow, not the weapon

**Respecification Seed:** A tactical engagement where one combatant feints and maneuvers to lure the other into position for an environmental trap, climaxing in the terrain itself delivering the decisive strike.
