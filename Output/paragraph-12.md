The bargeman stood at the stern of his vessel, narrow as a punt-pole, his posture the patient vertical of a heron awaiting the careless frog. The fog moved about him as a grey accomplice, thinning and thickening in turns, now veiling, now baring the weathered mask of his countenance—river-silt skin stretched taut over a blade-thin nose, a lipless mouth pressed to a permanent seam, a brow that jutted like an overhang of shale above whatever murk pooled in the sockets beneath. Ash-colored hair hung slicked with tallow against the damp of his skull; a coat of waxed linen, deep as moss-shadow, draped his frame, its left sleeve twice-patched at the elbow with cruder cloth; from his belt a single iron nail on a tarred cord tapped its faint rhythm against his thigh as he shifted his weight, and those long callused fingers—half-curled always, as though perpetually ready to receive coin or close around a throat—gripped the pole while he fixed Maerbrang with eyes that held no particular welcome, only the flat appraisal of one who has ferried too many souls to distinguish the quick from the soon-dead, and from that lipless seam came a low churring sound, less greeting than the noise a marsh-bird makes before it strikes.
