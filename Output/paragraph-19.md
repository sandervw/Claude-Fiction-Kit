The cold found him like a creditor.

He crouched in the lee of a glacial erratic, fingers scrabbling at the sodden pouch where his flint and steel lay nestled in char-cloth now black with damp, and though he struck spark after spark into the pitiful nest of bent-grass and gorse-scrub he had gathered, the wind—that remorseless razor stropping itself against every exposed surface of the moor—snatched each ember before it could catch, scattered his tinder into the colourless air, left him clutching the useless steel with hands that had gone the blue-white of drowned men. His teeth clattered like knucklebones cast for augury. He tried again, hunching his body into a windbreak, curling around the tiny architecture of kindling as a she-wolf curls around her whelps, yet even this desperate genuflection to the fire-that-would-not-come availed him nothing, for his fingers had become traitors—wooden, disobedient, fumbling the flint into the grass where it vanished among the stems. He ceased then, ear cocked toward the keening emptiness, listening for what he could not name: some voice, some guidance, some thread of the goldfinch's prophecy unwinding toward him across the barrens—but there was only the wind's idiot howling, and he shouted into it, a raw syllable of rage that the gale devoured before it had travelled the length of his arm. For a long moment he simply knelt there, eyes fixed on the middle distance where the hills repeated themselves unto grey infinity, his mind grown sluggish as a winter-locked stream, turning over solutions that crumbled like rot-touched wood. When at last he raised his head—slowly, as though his skull had filled with cold river-water—he marked against the pallid sky a darker massing of stone upon the nearest hilltop, a cromlech or cairn or some other remnant of the land's older tenants, and he rose, and he walked toward it, his gait the stiff shamble of a man whose joints had forgotten their purpose.

It was perhaps a quarter-league distant, and the crossing took him until the light had begun its long curdling toward dusk.
