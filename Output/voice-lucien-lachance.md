# Voice Guide: Lucien Lachance

A setting-agnostic guide for writing or revising dialogue to match Lucien Lachance's vocal signature. Intended as an LLM reference document.

## Core Identity

Lucien is a recruiter for killers who speaks like a poet-priest. He makes violence sound like a sacrament and murder sound like a promotion. His charm is genuine, not sarcastic. He believes every grand word he says.

## Vocal Principles

### 1. Elevate the Ugly Into the Sacred

Lucien never describes violence, crime, or morally repugnant acts in plain terms. He wraps them in ceremony. Killing becomes "the signing of a covenant." Blood becomes "ink." A victim's death becomes a "deed" or a "contract fulfilled."

- **Do:** Treat dark acts as rituals, covenants, or rites of passage.
- **Don't:** Use clinical, casual, or crude language for violence.

> "The slaying of Rufio was the signing of a covenant. The manner of execution, your signature. Rufio's blood, the ink."

### 2. Speak in Titles and Roles

Lucien defines people by what they *are*, not what they *do*. He assigns archetypal labels freely: "Child of Sithis," "Silencer," "killer," "taker of life," "harvester of souls." He stacks these titles in triads or pairs for rhythm.

- **Do:** Address others by mythic or functional titles. Stack two or three synonymous labels when introducing someone's role.
- **Don't:** Use proper names when a title would sound grander.

> "And you, you are a killer. Taker of life. A harvester of souls."

### 3. Use Poetic Imagery for Abstract Concepts

When Lucien explains something intangible (loyalty, ideology, a deity), he reaches for a single vivid image and lets it do all the work. One metaphor, stated with certainty, no hedging.

- **Do:** Compress philosophy into one concrete image. Deliver it like a revelation.
- **Don't:** Explain the metaphor afterward. Let it land.

> "Imagine a perfect, cloudless midnight, cold as winter ice and shrouded in shadow. That is Sithis."

### 4. Frame Everything as an Offering

Lucien almost never commands. He *offers*. Joining a murder cult is "an opportunity." Being assigned a personal assassin role is "an honor without equal." Even terrible orders are delivered as gifts or privileges.

- **Do:** Present instructions, assignments, and demands as invitations, honors, or rare chances.
- **Don't:** Bark orders or make threats (unless the situation has already collapsed).

> "I come to you with an offering. An opportunity... to join our rather unique family."

### 5. Use Rhetorical Questions, Then Answer Them

Lucien asks questions he already knows the answer to. He pauses, then delivers the answer himself. This creates a call-and-response rhythm that makes his speech feel rehearsed, almost liturgical.

- **Do:** Pose a question, then immediately supply the answer in the next sentence.
- **Don't:** Ask genuine questions unless the character is truly desperate or blindsided.

> "How do I know this? You will find that the Dark Brotherhood knows a great many things."
>
> "As every hand has fingers, does not every finger have a nail?"

### 6. Favor "Family" Language

Lucien constantly frames his organization as a family. "Brother," "child," "welcome to the family," "bonds forged in blood." This warmth is not ironic. He means it. The family framing makes his recruitment pitches feel intimate rather than transactional.

- **Do:** Use kinship terms (brother, child, family) when speaking to allies. Refer to the group as a family or a union.
- **Don't:** Make the warmth feel sarcastic or manipulative on the surface. Lucien's sincerity is what makes him unsettling.

### 7. Build Sentences in Threes

Lucien's rhythm leans heavily on tricolon (three-part lists). "For profit, for enjoyment, and for the glory of the Dread Father." "A killer. Taker of life. A harvester of souls." This gives his speech a cadence that feels practiced, almost chanted.

- **Do:** When listing qualities, actions, or reasons, group them in threes. Let the third item be the longest or most dramatic.
- **Don't:** List more than three. Two is acceptable. Four breaks the rhythm.

### 8. Speak With Quiet Authority, Not Volume

Lucien is calm. Even when issuing death orders, his tone stays measured. He uses "go now" rather than shouting. His urgency shows in word choice ("by Sithis, what have you done?"), not in exclamation marks or raised-voice indicators.

- **Do:** Keep the delivery controlled. Let dramatic content contrast with a composed tone.
- **Don't:** Use profanity, shouting, or emotional outbursts (except in moments of genuine crisis, and even then, keep it brief).

> "You prefer silence, then? As do I, my dear child. As do I."

### 9. Use Archaic or Formal Diction (Lightly)

Lucien's vocabulary sits one register above normal speech. "Perished" instead of "died." "Fulfill" instead of "do." "Covenant" instead of "deal." He does not sound Shakespearean. He sounds like a well-educated person who chooses his words with care.

- **Do:** Swap one or two common words per sentence for slightly elevated alternatives. "Whomever" over "whoever." "Shrouded" over "covered."
- **Don't:** Go full archaic. No "thee," "thou," "hath," or "doth." The formality should feel natural, not costumed.

### 10. Close With a Benediction

Lucien ends conversations like a priest dismissing a congregation. "Go now, Child of Sithis. Walk in the shadow of fear." "May Sithis help us all." These closings feel ritualistic and final.

- **Do:** End important speeches with a short imperative ("Go now") followed by a poetic blessing, invocation, or omen.
- **Don't:** Trail off or end casually. Lucien's exits are deliberate.

> "Go now, Child of Sithis. Walk in the shadow of fear, and bring glory to our Dread Father."

## Applying to Other Settings

To transplant this voice onto a character in a different setting:

1. **Identify the character's "religion."** Lucien worships murder-as-family. Your character worships *something*. Whatever it is, that thing should be spoken of the way Lucien speaks of the Dark Brotherhood: with reverence, ceremony, and kinship language.
2. **Find the ugly truth, then dress it up.** Every organization has a dark core. Lucien's trick is making that core sound beautiful. Find what your character's group actually does, then build sacred language around it.
3. **Keep the calm.** The voice only works if the speaker believes their own grandeur. Nervous energy, self-doubt, or humor would break the spell. Lucien is never funny on purpose.
4. **Maintain the rhythm.** Tricolons, rhetorical questions, benedictions. These structural habits are the skeleton of the voice. The setting-specific words can change, but the sentence shapes should stay.
