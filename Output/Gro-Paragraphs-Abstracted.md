# Gro — Paragraphs

_The Worm Ouroboros by E.R. Eddison_

## Original

1. How shall not common opinion account me mad, so rash and presumptuous dangerously to put my life in hazard? Nay, against all sound judgement; and this folly I enact in that very season when by patience and courage and my politic wisdom I had won that in despite of fortune's teeth which obstinately hitherto she had denied me: when after the brunts of divers tragical fortunes I had marvellously gained the favour and grace of the King, who very honourably placed me in his court, and tendereth me, I well think, so dearly as he doth the balls of his two eyes.

2. Yet is common opinion the fool, not I. He that imagineth after his labours to attain unto lasting joy, as well may he beat water in a mortar. Is there not in the wild benefit of nature instances enow to laugh this folly out of fashion? A fable of great men that arise and conquer the nations: Day goeth up against the tyrant night. How delicate a spirit is she, how like a fawn she footeth it upon the mountains: pale pitiful light matched with the primaeval dark. But every sweet hovers in her battalions, and every heavenly influence... the young day laughing in her strength, wild with her own beauty; fire and life and every scent and colour born anew to triumph over chaos and slow darkness and the kinless night.

3. But because day at her dawning hours hath so bewitched me, must I yet love her when glutted with triumph she settles to garish noon? Rather turn as now I turn to Demonland, in the sad sunset of her pride. And who dares call me turncoat, who do but follow now as I have followed this rare wisdom all my days: to love the sunrise and the sundown and the morning and the evening star? since there only abideth the soul of nobility, true love, and wonder, and the glory of hope and fear.

4. Of two roads, a wise man will choose ever that one which is indirect. For but consider the matter, thou that art a great cragsman: think our life's course a lofty cliff. I am to climb it, sometime up, sometime down. I pray, whither leadeth the straight road on such a cliff? Why, nowhither. For if I will go up by the straight way, 'tis not possible; I am left gaping whiles thou by crooked courses hast gained the top. Or if down, why 'tis easy and swift; but then, no more climbing ever more for me. And thou, clambering down by the crooked way, shalt find me a dead and unsightly corpse at the bottom.

5. My lord, you have used me well, and to deserve your love and advance your fortunes I have pondered much how you of Demonland might best obtain revenge upon your enemies. And I daily thinking hereupon, and conceiving in my head divers imaginations, can devise no means but one that in my fancy seemeth best.

6. 'Twas ever a fault in you Demons that you would not perceive how 'tis oft-times good to draw the snake from her hole by another man's hand. Consider now your matter. You have a great force both for land and sea. Trust not too much in that... But consider yet again. You have a thing is mightier far than all your horses and spearmen and dragons of war, mightier than thine own sword, my lord, and thou accounted the best swordsman in all the world.

## Abstracted

### Paragraph 1

**Word Count:** 100-150

**Type:** Reflection

**Tension Arc:** Escalate

**Structure:** Opens with rhetorical question anticipating judgment, pivots to self-defense through enumeration of hard-won achievements. One long unwinding sentence establishing stakes.

**Rhetorical DNA:**
- Inverted syntax and archaic constructions create formal, deliberate cadence
- Self-aware framing: speaker pre-empts criticism by voicing it first
- Triadic listing ("patience and courage and my politic wisdom") builds cumulative weight
- Intimate metaphor for bond with authority figure ("balls of his two eyes")

**Respecification Seed:** A figure defends a dangerous choice made at the height of hard-won security.

### Paragraph 2

**Word Count:** 150-200

**Type:** Exposition

**Tension Arc:** Release

**Structure:** Opens with defiant reversal ("the fool, not I"), expands into philosophical maxim, then unfolds into extended allegory. Rhythm shifts from terse declaration to lyrical accumulation.

**Rhetorical DNA:**
- Proverbial wisdom as foundation ("beat water in a mortar")
- Cosmic allegory: day vs. night as triumph over darkness
- Personification building in intensity: day as fawn, then warrior, then wild beauty
- Parallel construction cascading into celebration ("fire and life and every scent and colour")
- Register shifts from sardonic to exalted

**Respecification Seed:** A character dismisses conventional wisdom by invoking nature's own cycles of triumph and loss.

### Paragraph 3

**Word Count:** 100-150

**Type:** Reflection

**Tension Arc:** Maintain

**Structure:** Two-part movement: first, rhetorical question challenging loyalty to success; second, philosophical self-justification through metaphor of loving transitional moments.

**Rhetorical DNA:**
- Defiant self-defense through counter-question ("who dares call me turncoat")
- Extended metaphor linking personal loyalty to cosmic phenomena (sunrise/sunset/stars)
- Archaic formality lending dignity to what might seem betrayal
- Final clause lifts into abstraction: "the soul of nobility, true love, and wonder"

**Respecification Seed:** A figure justifies changing allegiances by claiming love for beginnings and endings, not middles.

### Paragraph 4

**Word Count:** 150-200

**Type:** Exposition

**Tension Arc:** Escalate

**Structure:** Opens with aphoristic statement, then develops through direct address and extended climbing metaphor. Builds through hypotheticals to grim conclusion.

**Rhetorical DNA:**
- Maxim as thesis ("Of two roads, a wise man will choose ever that one which is indirect")
- Direct address creating intimate tutorial tone ("thou that art a great cragsman")
- Life-as-cliff metaphor sustained and elaborated through multiple scenarios
- Rhetorical questions driving argument forward
- Dark humor in final image: corpse at the bottom vs. successful climber

**Respecification Seed:** A counselor teaches that indirect paths succeed where direct ones lead to ruin or death.

### Paragraph 5

**Word Count:** 50-100

**Type:** Dialogue

**Tension Arc:** Maintain

**Structure:** Formal address establishing debt, pivoting to offer of strategic counsel. Single building sentence leading to promise of singular solution.

**Rhetorical DNA:**
- Courtly formula opening ("My lord, you have used me well")
- Parallel construction in middle ("pondering... conceiving... devising")
- Tease structure: many options considered, one chosen
- Formal register throughout, deferential but confident

**Respecification Seed:** An advisor pledges service and promises a single best strategy after much deliberation.

### Paragraph 6

**Word Count:** 100-150

**Type:** Dialogue

**Tension Arc:** Escalate

**Structure:** Opens with chiding observation, proceeds through dismissal of obvious strengths, builds to revelation of hidden greater power. Three beats: critique, enumeration, climactic mystery.

**Rhetorical DNA:**
- Proverbial wisdom as framing device ("draw the snake from her hole by another man's hand")
- Deliberate undercutting of military assets to elevate something intangible
- Comparative structure building to superlative ("mightier far than all your horses...")
- Suspense through withholding: the greater thing unnamed
- Direct address maintaining intimate advisory tone

**Respecification Seed:** A counselor warns against relying on obvious strength, hinting at a hidden greater asset.
