# The Bell-Ringer's Grief

The cell smelled of tallow and regret.

Merrick knelt among his master's belongings like a penitent before pillars jutting ruin—for that is what they were, these sad monuments of a dead man's life: a shaving kit with rust-eaten blade, robes gone stiff with unwashing, a prayer-wheel whose beads had scattered and rolled into corners where the phosphor-moss pulsed its sickly green. The light moved in waves, as though the walls themselves breathed, and Merrick found he could not look at it directly. Something in the rhythm reminded him of rope. Of weight. Of the mathematics of falling.

He had come to sort. To discard. To perform the quiet violence of erasure that the living inflict upon the dead.

Instead, he found the journal.

Leather-bound, water-warped, hidden beneath a loose stone like a guilty secret—and when he opened it, his own name stared back. _Merrick_, written in that cramped hand he knew better than his own. _Merrick does not listen. Merrick argues. Merrick questions everything, the curious philosopher who cannot simply accept that bells ring because we pull them._

He turned pages with fingers gone numb.

_I fear I am teaching him wrong. I fear my frustrations have calcified into cruelty, and he feels it, and he will carry it forward into whatever life remains after the chains finally snap. I was not gentle today. I am never gentle. The boy deserves—_

The entry ended there. Ink trailed off like a man interrupted, or a thought too heavy to complete.

Merrick's throat closed. The moss-light rippled, casting his shadow in stuttering multiples against the stone, and for one breath-held moment he felt _seen_—not by eyes, but by the cell itself, by the ancient weight of confession soaked into these walls. A supernatural vulnerability cracked through him: the sudden, horrible knowledge that grief could be witnessed. That the fortress remembered what occurred within it. That his master had sat in this same green glow, writing fears he never spoke aloud, while Merrick sulked in the bell-tower nursing resentments sharp as broken glass.

He had not known.

He had not _asked_.

The journal fell from his hands. He did not pick it up. Outside, wind screamed through the chain-links, and somewhere far below, the poisoned world turned without him.

A knock came—soft, curious, wrong for midnight—and the door eased in without waiting, as though the timber had grown mannerly.

“You keep the hours,” Silex murmured, peering past Merrick at the moss-light, “and the hours keep you. A pleasing symmetry.”

“To see where grief thins the world.” Silex leaned close, listening—not to Merrick, but to the room. The chains outside gave a long, low complaint. “Do you feel it? The draft that is not wind. The small gap.”

Merrick’s laugh came—needle-sharp—without moving his erased face. The sound skittered over the page. The moss dimmed, then flared, as if startled.
