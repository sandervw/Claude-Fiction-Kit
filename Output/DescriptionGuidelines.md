# Character Description Guidelines

## Core Principles

- Treat the character objectively
- Describe *what is seen* and *how it impresses*, not what the character thinks or feels
- Favor **external qualities** (build, face, dress, movement, light) that imply inner nature indirectly

## Paragraph Structure

- Write **long paragraphs**: every paragraph should be over 100 words
- Prefer transitions within paragraphs
- Use new paragraphs to mark shifts of focus, or to paint contrasts

## Sentence Structure and Rhythm

- Use long, flowing sentences: multiple descriptive clauses joined by commas, semicolons, or conjunctions
- Use short, inverted declarations to anchor a description before elaboration begins
- Vary sentence length, but **favor longer sentences, in a ratio of 2-to-1**
- Apply inverted syntax: place adjectives, adverbs, or descriptive phrases before the subject
- Cadence: Use the "and... and... and" structure (polysyndeton)

## Use of Comparison

- Comparisons should be concrete and archetypal (animals, celestial bodies, metals, weapons, natural forces)
- **Limit extended similes to one per description**; let it carry symbolic weight.

## Physical Description Strategy

- Move from **general to detail**: establish the overall impression before zooming to specific features
- Anchor features with animal or natural comparisons
- Name specific features (brow, jaw, mouth, eyes, hands)
- Let one or two features dominate as identifiers

## Clothing & Adornment

- Name materials precisely: never "fabric" or "armor"
- Specify colors with hyphenated modifiers: avoid bare color words
- For gemstones and metalwork, include how they are set or worked
- List garments from outer to inner, or from prominent to secondary
- **Avoid generic terms** without modifiers, describe construction
- Do not describe clothing as fashionable, practical, or comfortable

## Vocabulary & Register

- Use archaic terms; use them liberally and often
- Prefer **concrete nouns over abstract** ones

## Atmosphere & Implication

- Let physical details imply personality—do not state traits directly
- Use light symbolically: describe how light interacts with the figure
- Place the figure in movement or social context; avoid static description
- **End descriptions on a concrete physical feature**, not an atmospheric impression

## Antipatterns

**NEVER DO THE FOLLOWING**

- Modern minimalism or clipped phrasing
- Em-dashes
- Sentence Fragments
- Vague beauty words: never "handsome" or "beautiful" without concrete elaboration
- Clinical anatomy: avoid "musculature," "physique," "bone structure"
- Listing traits as bullets: descriptions should flow as continuous prose
- Telling personality directly
- Dialogue or interior thought
- Accumulated metaphors; do not layer multiple comparisons when one strong one will serve
