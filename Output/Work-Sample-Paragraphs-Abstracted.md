# Work Sample -- Paragraphs

_The Worm Ouroboros by E.R. Eddison_

## Original

1. Now was Juss stretched face downward as one dead, on that giddy edge of rock. Mivarsh had saved him, seizing him by the foot and drawing him back to safety when the beast fell. A sight of terror he was, clotted from head to toe with the beast's blood and his own. Mivarsh bound his wounds and laid him tenderly as he might back against the cliff, then peered down a long while to know if the beast were dead indeed.

2. So prayed Mivarsh. And belike the high Gods were moved to pity of his innocence, hearing him so cry for help unto his mumbo-jumbos, where no help was; and belike they were not minded that those lords of Demonland should there die evilly before their time, unhonoured, unsung. Howsoever, Mivarsh arose and made fast the rope about Lord Juss, knotting it cunningly beneath the arms that it might not tighten in the lowering and crush his breast and ribs, and so with much ado lowered him down to the foot of the cliff. Thereafter came Mivarsh himself down that perilous wall, and albeit for many a time he thought his bane was upon him, yet by good cragsmanship spurred by cold necessity he gat him down at last. Being down, he delayed not to minister to his companions, who came to themselves with heavy groaning. But when Lord Juss was come to himself he did his healing art both on himself and on Lord Brandoch Daha, so that in a while they were able to stand upon their feet, albeit something stiff and weary and like to vomit. And it was by then the third hour past noon.

3. On the tenth day the weather abated, and they went up and crossed the glacier and lodged them in a cave in the rock at the foot of the great north buttress of Koshtra Pivrarcha. At dawn Juss and Brandoch Daha went forth to survey the prospect. They crossed the mouth of the steep snow-choked valley that ran up to the main ridge betwixt Ashnilan on the west and Koshtra Pivrarcha on the east, rounded the base of Ailinon, and climbed from the west to a snow saddle some three thousand feet up the ridge of that mountain, whence they might view the buttress and choose their way for their attempt.

## Abstracted

### Paragraph 1

**Word Count:** 50-100

**Type:** Action

**Tension Arc:** Release

**Structure:** Four sentences. Opens with a tableau of the fallen figure, pivots to the rescuer's prior act, returns to the grim image, then closes with tending and vigilance.

**Rhetorical DNA:**
- Inverted syntax ("Now was...stretched," "A sight of terror he was") gives archaic, saga-like cadence
- Passive, death-still figure contrasted with the active hands of the lesser companion
- Visceral body-horror detail (clotted blood) bracketed by gentleness (tenderly, bound)
- Final clause shifts from urgency to patient watchfulness, letting tension drain

**Respecification Seed:** A lesser companion tends a fallen warrior after a beast-slaying on a precipice.

### Paragraph 2

**Word Count:** 250+

**Type:** Action

**Tension Arc:** Release

**Structure:** Seven sentences building from prayer through divine commentary, into a long procedural descent sequence, and finishing with a quiet time-stamp. Opens short, swells through extended compound sentences, then contracts to a flat declarative close.

**Rhetorical DNA:**
- Narrator's ironic theological aside ("mumbo-jumbos, where no help was") layered over genuine piety
- Extended participial chains mimic the physical labor of rope-work and climbing ("knotting it cunningly beneath the arms that it might not tighten...")
- Repeated concessive constructions ("albeit," "howsoever") sustain a tone of hard-won perseverance
- Archaic diction and syntax throughout, lending ceremonial weight to physical struggle
- Final flat time-marker ("the third hour past noon") punctures the elevated register, grounding the scene

**Respecification Seed:** A humble figure performs a perilous rescue descent, aided by desperate prayer and raw skill.

---

### Paragraph 3

**Word Count:** 100-150

**Type:** Exposition

**Tension Arc:** Maintain

**Structure:** Three sentences. A transitional time-skip opens into geographic traversal, each sentence longer than the last, accumulating place-names and compass directions in a steady march of terrain.

**Rhetorical DNA:**
- Enumerative geography ("betwixt... on the west... on the east") builds a mental map through accumulation rather than description
- Steady, processional rhythm with no dramatic peaks; the paragraph functions as a bridge
- Archaic connective tissue ("whence," "betwixt") maintains register without drawing attention
- Final purpose clause ("whence they might view... and choose their way") quietly reintroduces intention after pure movement

**Respecification Seed:** A party crosses harsh terrain over days, scouting a route toward their next ascent.
