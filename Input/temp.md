## Blob 1:
If one had stood upon that border hill where Gaelic woodland met open moorland, one would have watched the wind arrive like a restless spirit unable to settle, threading itself through the standing stones that crowned the summit like knuckles forcing through the hill's thin skin. The menhyrs bore the wind's long attention in their weathering: windward faces worn smooth as river-stones, leeward sides pitted and scabbed with lichen that clung like disease to any surface the gale could not scour clean. Between the stones, sheep's fescue trembled and whispered, while tormentil spotted the grass with small yellow blooms that shivered at each gust, and upon exactly one stone the old cupmarks held centuries of rainwater like open sores that would not heal. The wind followed the slope downward, carrying the tannin-smell of the dense woodland below, where oak, hazel, and blackthorn formed the weald's margin in a dark tangle, bracket fungi clamping to the nearest trunks like pale limpets feeding on the bark. At the foot of that slope the wind halted, rolled against the canopy as if meeting a wall of living shadow, and for a moment the whole weald cast its penumbra uphill toward the stones. By day the wood exhaled its breath of rot and growth; by night it swallowed all sound and gave nothing back.

## Blob 2:
Descending the scarp came a wayfarer smooth-gaited as a lynx negotiating scree, unhurried yet purposeful, weight slung low in his hips so that the gale's pestering achieved nothing against him. The wind plucked and harried him as though nursing some ancestral grievance, yet his shoulders remained loose, his stride continuous as if oiled at the joints, each footfall deliberate but untroubled. Garbed was he in linen of muted ochre whose collar snapped and flailed in the gusts, but beneath it a sleeveless gambeson of flax quilted tight as chainmail, and over that a harness of oxhide boiled to the hardness of horn, creaking faintly with each flexion of his torso. At his flank hung a spear of ash-wood stained to near-black, its haft wound round with snakeskin at the grip, the scales still discernible beneath lacquer, and the iron head leaf-shaped, narrow, long, etched with a coiling motif that snagged the daylight like a lugubrious and sinuous rill threading through stone. From his belt of matching oxhide clattered an oxbone flute, idiosyncratic and incongruous, thumping against his thigh with each stride as though protesting its bondage to so martial a bearer. His scalp was practical, close-cropped, hair black as hellebore and swept back from the brow, shorter still at the temples where thin streaks of silvering glossed the darkness like hoarfrost on charred timber. Very calm was that face, the eyes amber-green and unhurried but focused, as if the whole tumult of wind and hill were no more than the rustling of parchment in a scriptorium, and in that calmness lay something unsettling, for it spoke neither of indifference nor serenity but of a settled readiness, a body and mind long reconciled to motion and to violence.

## Blob 3:
He brushed a filament from his brow. The gesture was habitual, born of dozens of such woodlands, and he performed it without rancour or even full awareness, the way a man might scratch an itch already half-forgotten. Yet this coppice demanded the motion ceaselessly, for the spiders of this particular stand had woven their snares with such profligacy that the canopy seemed strung with a second, spectral foliage: gossamer catenaries drooping between hazel wands, guy-lines anchored to pollarded oaks whose lopped crowns had thickened over centuries into grotesque boles of knuckled wood, each fist of regrowth bristling with suckers no thicker than kindling. The coppice stools themselves had grown stranger still, their centres rotted hollow so that hazel rose in bizarre circlets, multi-trunked coronas surrounding cavities of pith-black nothing, and from certain angles the low-cut stumps jutting a hand's breadth above the leaf-litter resembled nothing so much as dinner plates abandoned by some slovenly giant and left to fossilize. Underfoot the sorrel released its acid tartness with each footfall, sharp and vegetal as the first bite of an unripe pippin, and this scent mingled with the fungal must of the rotting heartwood and the faintly mineral smell of old webs accumulating on his gambeson. He moved through the filaments as a swimmer moves through fog-cooled water, arms occasionally lifting to part the thicker skeins, the silk collecting on forearm and collar and spear-shaft in pale striations that gave him, after a quarter-hour's passage, the look of a man swaddled in the shed integument of some enormous and indolent worm. And yet for all the density of this woven habitation, for all the evidence of countless arachnid generations industriously spinning their geometries into the understory, he heard no birdsong, glimpsed no flicker of furred body or the winking of mammalian eyes between the boughs; he found himself almost wishing for such a presence, some watching thing whose gleam might surface and vanish when he turned, but the wood offered nothing of the sort, no scrutiny either hostile or curious, only a profound indifference that settled over him like the cobwebs themselves and whispered, in its total silence, that whatever spirit had once dwelt in this place had long since ceased to concern itself with the passage of mere men.

## Blob 4:
A quarry that flees sideways harbors either cunning or terror - rarely nothing. Maerbrang turned this over in the quiet alcove of his skull, that voice of his own devising murmuring soft and unhurried, pitched low enough that a listener would cant forward to catch it. The scout served Semprovius Thessamander Kurz, a man whose servants did not blunder into nameless bracken without purpose. Had the wretch sensed pursuit? No. The spoor had told Maerbrang otherwise - no hesitation in the stride, no backward glances scuffed into the loam. Yet why not skirt the treeline, where paths remained legible and a man might be found again? One does not vanish into a wood without name unless he wishes to misplace something - himself, perhaps, or whatever clings to his heels. Maerbrang exhaled through his nose, a faint sibilance. The forest swallowed reasons as readily as men. _As the way_, he allowed, and walked on beneath the indifferent boughs.

## Blob 5:
The trail declared itself in crumpled bracken, in boot-gouges pressed through the leafmold, in gossamer veils of spider-silk torn and fluttering - a plainsman's spoor, graceless and lumber-footed, the track of a man who walked forests as a stranger. Hours bled away as Maerbrang threaded the colonnades of oak and hornbeam, a wraith in the dimness, surfacing only where slants of afternoon pierced the canopy to gild his shoulders before the murk swallowed him again; he paused, knelt, read the disturbed detritus, listened, moved on. At one place the prints bit deeper, the leaves more thoroughly crushed - the scout had stood here, motionless, straining to hear something in the understory's hush - and Maerbrang halted too, scanning the brake for any tremor of movement, finding none. He shuttered his eyes, flared his nostrils to the vegetal musk, let the forest's creaking stillness soak into him - and through that silence came a single liquid spill of birdsong, a descending trill that pierced the quiet like a wedge through green timber, and he held there, breath stilled, until he named it: goldfinch, nothing more.

## Blob 6:
The goldfinch perched on a wand of hazel at the level of his eyes, its claws hooked into the lichenous bark, its posture neither flighty nor restive but still as a carved thing. What filtered light sifted through the canopy fell upon the bird's plumage and kindled it: the citrine of its breast deepening to saffron at the coverts, the jet cap glossed like lacquer, the white chevrons of its wings banded against velvet black, each barb and barbule distinct as brushwork. The finch canted its skull and fixed him with one vitreous eye, and there was lodged in that bead of polished jet no skittish alarm, no witless animal startle, but an appraisal cool and probing, the gaze of a thing that weighed him and found the reckoning incomplete.

## Blob 7:
The goldfinch turned its head with a motion slow and considered, as a judge might turn to regard a man already condemned. Its eye, black as sloe, held the green gloom of the hazel-brake and gave back nothing.

"Hearken," it said, and the voice that issued from that painted throat was of a quality Maerbrang had not looked for: clear, and carrying, and altogether without kindness. "Thou and thy lord Guss and all who have sworn him fealty shall meet your deaths in the hall of Semprovius Thessamander Kurz. This shall come to pass when the shaman's laughter hath sounded its last echo and faded."

Maerbrang unstopped his waterskin. He let fall a thread of water into the moss at the hazel's root, where it darkened the earth and vanished. The bird watched. He said: "What quarrel hath this warlord with us? What offence have we given, that he should seek our ruin?"

The goldfinch descended upon the wet moss and drank, three quick dips of its vermillion mask. Then it hopped back and composed itself, and its eye found his again.

"Others have poured out water for me," it said. "They thought to cozen secrets from the loom with such poor offerings, and learned that the threads run where they will. Water buys nothing from the Fates."

"Then tell me how we die. Tell me the manner of it, if not the cause."

Maerbrang had drawn close, near enough that he might have cupped the creature in his hands. It did not start, nor spread its wings to flee. It only blinked, once, with a lizard's patience.

"The shaman's laughter," it said again. The words came measured, as a sexton might speak them over a filled grave. "That alone is given me to tell. The rest is woven, and you shall walk the pattern blind until you walk it no more."

## Blob 8:
The sunflower seeds were the vessel, and Maerbrang knew this without the goldfinch uttering a syllable of instruction. He had learned such lore from the withered hedge-priests of his youth, those mumbling anchorites who catalogued every barter between mortal flesh and the spirits that haunt the liminal places: either a man proffers the appointed oblation and yokes the visitant to the Red World forever, or he spurns the compact and watches the apparition dissolve back into the grey countries from whence it fluttered. Those who had performed such bindings spoke of the transfiguration in hushed and halting recollection, how the spirit-creature would attenuate into gossamer luminance and sink through skin and sinew until it nested in bone, transmuting some sliver of the summoner's body into its permanent tabernacle. The goldfinch, were Maerbrang to scatter those seeds before its waiting beak, would vanish from the branch and coil itself into a molar at the back of his jaw, gilding the enamel to lustrous saffron, and thereafter he would possess the lay of the goldfinch: that lilting persuasion which bends the hearts of simple folk as wind bends meadow-grass, a charm potent enough to make any common man or woman cleave to him with unshakeable devotion. Maerbrang drew forth the pouch, pinched a measure of the dun-gold seeds between calloused fingers, and placed them upon his own tongue. The goldfinch fled.

## Blob 9:
The Carsobrine was the dreariest morass in all the uncharted wilds, a fenland so steeped in the vintages of pre-man that no coppiced stool or pollarded boundary-ash had touched its verge for generations uncounted, and the runnels once grooved by tusked boar and sure-footed ibex drank themselves into the sodden loam until no spoor remained save the scout's faint scuffings, still legible in the peat. Had light attempted ingress through the ordinary avenues of canopy-gap or streambed glint, it would have curdled first in the sulphurous exhalations that seethed from certain virid pools, pools so still they held no sound of drip or lap but only the green-mirrored hush of waters that had never learned to move. Yet where the mangroves convened in their blasphemous parliament, their boles grown wider and massier than they stood tall, a curdled, haze-thickened, boiling radiance did penetrate the fissures overhead, and Maerbrang, no taller beneath those primordial trunks than a vole among standing-stones, beheld the trackless expanse unfurl before him like the floor of some drowned and fever-lit temple.

## Blob 10:
The scabrous log that was not a log detonated from the shallows, brackish gobbets of swampwater flung wide in a reeking corona. Its serried jaw yawned, a clawtrap wrought for some antediluvian sloth. Then, as a summer squall cracks the glazed stillness of a lake and lashes the willows to a frenzy and sends the marsh-birds shrieking from their roosts and churns the placid water to a boil of whitecaps before it spends itself and leaves only trembling reeds in its wake, just so did Maerbrang's hidden deftness unfurl from his sliding gait: his seax keened its abattoir hymn as he gouged the brute's snout, carved a furrow through its warted nape, stabbed thrice and then a fourth time into the soft folds behind its forelimb, until the fish-dragon wallowed in a slick of its own ichor, maw agape and hissing, and found this meat's sting too costly to bear, so slunk, wounded and sullen, back into the mire.

## Blob 11:
Maerbrang sought a ford, a causeway, some egress from the mire. He found none. The shallows gaped with murk and hungered for his flesh as the fish-dragon had. So he clambered onto the nearest tussock where the morass yielded to moss and sedge, and hunted for a path along the verge. The sun hung fixed in its azimuth, though the hours bled away like water through a sieve. Heat mantled his shoulders and draped across his visage, a suffocating pall woven of vapour and torpor. The earth beneath his tread sucked and clutched. The land buckled through fens and quagmires without logic or relent, each promontory dissolving into slough, each gallery of stunted alder collapsing into impasse. Twice he retraced his own spoor. Thrice he circled back to ground he could not name, though he had trodden it mere hours prior. The rival scout's trail, that faint susurrus of bent grass and disturbed loam he had pursued since dawn, had vanished in the labyrinth of bog and brake. Time unspooled in strange skeins. Distance contorted. He wandered through a fevered chronology where each stride brought him no nearer to deliverance. And then, abrupt as revelation, the copses parted. Maerbrang staggered onto a silted strand, and before him sprawled a lake of such breadth that fog devoured its farther shore, stretching gray and unbroken into a horizon swallowed by mist and melancholy.

## Blob 13:
Maerbrang spoke first, his voice carrying flat across the fog-choked water. "I need passage."

The bargeman's lipless mouth unseamed itself, and from it came a single croak: "Cull." A silence festered between them, broken only by the faint slap of current against hull-strakes; Maerbrang's brow furrowed, uncertain whether he had heard a name or a command or some half-Forgotten curse-word dredged from the silt of abandoned speech. He extended his hand across the gap of dark water, palm upturned in the old greeting-gesture, but the bargeman only stared at the offered flesh as one might regard a fishhook dressed in bait—those long fingers neither releasing nor tightening on the punt-pole, merely flexing in their perpetual half-grip.

"I am called Vetch," Maerbrang said, retracting his hand. "I am sworn servant to Thessamander Kurz."

The bargeman's fingers clenched, unclenched, clenched again around the tarred wood. "Can-not fer-ry," said he, each syllable separated from its neighbors. "Kurz-men. Only. No others cross."

Maerbrang let the silence accumulate like sediment, let it thicken until the fog itself seemed to curdle with it. "Kurz's man," he repeated. "I have said it."

"Kurz-man?" The bargeman's jutting brow creased. "Truth?"

Maerbrang only watched - until the barge groaned forward, its driftwood bulk grating against the gravelly mud. Cull the bargeman gestured once with his chin toward the vessel's black deck, and offered a clipped, "Mind footing."

## Blob 14:
The silence held them first like a cupped palm holds rainwater—trembling, provisional, liable to spill at the slightest tilt. The bargeman's pole rose and plunged in metronomic sweeps, each stroke a genuflection to the murk below, and the water received the ashwood shaft without protest, without sound, as though it had grown accustomed to such intrusions across the uncounted years. Soon the fog closed its white throat around them, swallowing the shore behind and the shore ahead, until Maerbrang could not say whether they moved at all or merely hung suspended in some null-space between the living world and its half-remembered echo. He kept his eye fixed forward, on nothing, on the void's own pale belly, though he felt the ferryman's attention turn toward him like a weathervane catching a shift in the wind. The creature's scrutiny came first in sidelong flickers, then in longer, wetter assessments—his lids clicking with each blink like the carapace-rattle of disturbed beetles, the sound obscenely loud in that muffled emptiness. The lipless seam of his mouth compressed further still, until it seemed less an orifice than a scar, a thing healed over and hostile to reopening. His brow, that promontory of knobbed bone, appeared to swell and thrust itself outward by degrees, as though the suspicion breeding behind it required additional architecture, until his eye-sockets became twin cisterns of shadow from which his yellowed gaze peered like something drowned and resentful. Maerbrang did not move. He breathed shallowly, evenly, watching the fog, feeling the accusation gather. Then the half-Forgotten released his grip on the pole with one hand—those fingers uncurling like the legs of a dying spider—and extended a crooked digit toward Maerbrang's hip, toward the oxbone flute that hung there in its leather loop. "Liar," the bargeman said. "Killer. Serpent."

## Blob 15:
Maerbrang's hand had already found the black ash haft before the last syllable of *serpent* dissolved into the fog—his spear vaulting from the thwart as though summoned by old covenant—and he thrust for the ferryman's sternum with the economical viciousness of a man who had stopped pretending, yet the gaunt creature merely canted his blade-thin torso with the skiff's own yaw, letting the iron point hiss past his ribs, and in that same rocking motion brought the dripping pole across Maerbrang's jaw with a wet crack that sent amber light scattering behind his eyes; what followed was graceless, necessary, the pole wrenched away, those half-curled fingers hammering at his throat and floating ribs with knuckles like river-smoothed stones, until Maerbrang abandoned the theater of distance entirely, seized the waxed lapel, and drove his gutting knife into the ferryman's belly with the methodical intimacy of a man stitching leather—seven, eight, nine insertions—before hoisting the still-twitching ruin overhead by scruff and haunch and heaving it into the vitreous water, where it sank without protest, without ripple, as though the lake had been waiting all along to receive what it was owed.

## Blob 16:
Villainy, Maerbrang mused as he drove the dripping pole into the murk, possesses no fixed habitation—it roosts only where particular eyes bid it nest. He thrust again, feeling the barge shudder forward through the vaporous stillness. Consider: would those wayfarers whom the bargeman had ferried toward their doom name this slaying wicked? Would the bones that moldered in whatever oubliette Thessamander Kurz maintained for such unfortunates weep for their jailer's gutting? Nay—to them, Maerbrang would stand as liberator, as rectifier of trespasses long unanswered. And thus it ran with all his killings. Wode the shaman. Others as well, their faces grown gauzy in recollection. Each death wore the garb of atrocity only when beheld through the bereaved eye, the partisan eye. He had always apprehended this verity, even as a whelp—that morality was merely vantage, merely the hillside from which one squinted at the deed. And if such coldness made him ophidian, then let him be the tame adder coiled at his lord Guss's heel, sinking fangs into whatever hand dared brandish iron against his master.

## Blob 17:
The mirk thickened with eventide's approach, and through that grey murrain the far shore resolved itself at last—not the sedge and mire of Carsobrine but a hummocked headland, scabrous with lichen-crusted stone, where no tree had purchase and the wind had scoured all softness from the land. Maerbrang poled the ungainly vessel to within a score of meters of that barren strand, then stayed his labour and looked down upon the deck where the ferryman's blood lay drying in long runnels against pale wood, and he marked how the craft had been wrought—driftwood branches laced and wattled with cunning, caulked with bog-tar black as old sin and stopped with rags of ancient cloth—and something in that careful workmanship, that patient binding of dead wood into purpose, stirred in him a sudden choler; so he raised the ashwood pole high and brought it crashing down through the latticed timbers, and again, and once more, until the branches splintered and crackled apart like bones beneath a maul, and the black water came welling up through the wounds he had made, and the barge groaned and listed and began its slow surrender to the mere; and Maerbrang cast himself over the gunwale into that cold embrace, swimming with hard strokes toward the shingle, whilst behind him the ferryman's craft slipped beneath the surface with scarcely a sound, bearing its cargo of blood and craft-cunning down into the silt where the half-Forgotten kept their commerce with the drowned.

## Blob 18:
Maerbrang's knees struck the shingle, and he dragged himself from the shallows onto a littoral of wave-smoothed slate and ash-colored gravel. The wind came sheering down the naked headland like a flensing blade drawn across a taut cheek, pressing the sedge-grass flat against the scalped and scabrous rock, scouring the standing water from the hollows until the whole moorland gleamed with a mineral wetness that held no warmth. Beyond the immediate tumble of shore-stone, the hills rose in bald succession—each crown worn to a grey pate by centuries of gale, each saddle between them pooled with shadow and the last thin vapors of the lake-fog—and past these, more hills, more grey, more silence, an entire country of barren undulation stretching toward a horizon that refused to offer tree or wall or smoke-smudge. From some unmeasurable distance, carried on the wind's back like a seed on floodwater, came the attenuated shriek of an erne—a soul-cry, the old shamans taught, ferrying some unbodied spirit down from the Ochre silence into the wet red wailing of a newborn's first breath. Yet the moor showed Maerbrang nothing of its people, nothing of its purpose; she kept her settlements, her cookfires, her living hearts shuttered somewhere beyond his sight, and the wind scoured on.

## Blob 19:
He crouched in the lee of a glacial erratic, fingers scrabbling at the sodden pouch where his flint and steel lay nestled in char-cloth now black with damp, and though he struck spark after spark into the pitiful nest of bent-grass and gorse-scrub he had gathered, the wind—that remorseless razor stropping itself against every exposed surface of the moor—snatched each ember before it could catch, scattered his tinder into the colourless air, left him clutching the useless steel with hands that had gone the blue-white of drowned men. His teeth clattered like knucklebones cast for augury. He tried again, hunching his body into a windbreak, curling around the tiny architecture of kindling as a she-wolf curls around her whelps, yet even this desperate genuflection to the fire-that-would-not-come availed him nothing, for his fingers had become traitors—wooden, disobedient, fumbling the flint into the grass where it vanished among the stems. He ceased then, ear cocked toward the keening emptiness, listening for what he could not name: some voice, some guidance, some thread of the goldfinch's prophecy unwinding toward him across the barrens—but there was only the wind's idiot howling, and he shouted into it, a raw syllable of rage that the gale devoured before it had travelled the length of his arm. For a long moment he simply knelt there, eyes fixed on the middle distance where the hills repeated themselves unto grey infinity, his mind grown sluggish as a winter-locked stream, turning over solutions that crumbled like rot-touched wood. When at last he raised his head—slowly, as though his skull had filled with cold river-water—he marked against the pallid sky a darker massing of stone upon the nearest hilltop... and he rose, and he walked toward it, his gait the stiff shamble of a man whose joints had forgotten their purpose. It was perhaps a quarter-league distant, and the crossing took him until the light had begun its long curdling toward dusk.

## Blob 20:
The henj received him like a barrow receives its tenant: without warmth, without refusal, with only the indifference of stone that has forgotten whom it was meant to protect. It stood at the hill's crown, a work of the old builders. The encircling bank, which in some elder age had risen proud as a chieftain's collar, now slumped inward like the dewlaps of a starved hound, its crest gnawed down by centuries of frost-heave and the patient treachery of bracken roots; the ditch that had once guarded the inner sanctum—cut inside the rampart in the manner of sacred precincts, meant to hold something in rather than keep invaders out—lay scummed with black water and the decomposing lace of autumn's leaf-fall. The palisade had long since surrendered to time: whatever oaken or ashwood staves had once crowned the ringwork had been rendered to splinter, to punk, to loam, carried off by wind and the scavenging of peasant generations, yet the mortared plinth they had stood upon remained—a circlet of dressed stone rising knee-high, gap-toothed where sections had tumbled into the interior bowl. Within that broken crown the ground showed the pugmarks and scat of creatures who had denned here through harder seasons: the cloven slots of roe deer pressed into frozen mud, the twisted rope of fox droppings atop a fallen lintel, tufts of coarse hair snagged on jutting masonry where something larger had scraped through. But it was the henj's throat that held Maerbrang's gaze longest: a stone-lipped aperture gaping at the enclosure's nadir, its steps descending into water the color of old bronze, water that did not ripple though no ice skinned its surface, water from which rose the faint exhalation of peat and something older beneath—the breath of interred air, the susurrus of silt that had not been stirred in living memory, the almost-voice of a place where offerings had once been made and perhaps still waited, patient and unfed, in the flooded dark below.

## Blob 21:
No warmer here than on the scoured moor above, he thought—then reconsidered as his flint kissed iron and sparked life into a fistful of dry rot-wood. The flame caught, stuttered, held. By its stammering glow he saw himself twinned in the still black water of the passage: his own haggard face below, spear-point glinting both above and beneath the surface like a single blade run through two worlds. He waded deeper. Names crowded the dressed stones—*Varek Thornmane*, *Ulthed the Galled*, *Brennis Who Kept the Ford*—each one a man unmade by time, his soul long since ferried crow-back to the Ochre World where the forgotten go to finish their forgetting. What claim had he, Maerbrang, to trespass among such interred memories? But does the river ask permission of the gorge it cleaves? Does rain apologize to the cistern? He moved as water moves: downward, inward, toward the collecting-place. Then his torch found it—a name ending in *Kurz*. He halted. The silence pressed close as a confessor. Then he laughed, a low and wondering sound, marveling that fortune had poured him precisely where he wished to go. The sound rolled outward, struck stone, returned. But the voice that came back was not his own. It was Wode's voice—the dead shaman's cackle, arriving a full minute late, as if crawling up from some deeper vault than echo alone could explain. Maerbrang's mirth curdled and died in his throat.

## Blob 22:
With one stroke of his seax-point across the carved letters, Maerbrang unmade the name of Kurz—the grooves filling with stone-dust like ash in a wound—and the silence that followed held neither protest nor thunder, only the soft plash of his own feet as he waded the remaining length of the vault, reading by guttering light the apellations of the forgotten: Haemoth, Brecca, Thurngeld, names that meant nothing now, souls long since carried crow-back to the Ochre World, and he did not trouble himself to scratch them out, for what was one more generation of oblivion in this age of unremembering? The passage terminated in blind stone where the water did not reach, and there upon that slight hummock of dry ground he folded himself down, spear across his thighs, and wondered briefly whether rains might come to drown him in this culvert—then ceased to wonder, ceased to think, and slept a sleep without vision or visitation, black as peat-water. He woke in perfect darkness, his fire-splinters long since crumbled to cold punk, but his palm found the clammy wall and he groped toward what emerged as grey light, and with it came wind, and with the wind, voices—faint, indistinct, numerous.

## Blob 23:
Maerbrang clambered from the henj's maw into a clamour of industry—the clank of mattocks biting chalk, the grunts of men heaving wicker-loads of spoil—and for three heartbeats he stood unnoticed amid the murk, a revenant disgorged from the barrow's throat. A digger glanced up, tallow-faced beneath his leather coif; the man's mouth gaped, then shaped a warning that split the morning like a cleaver through gristle. *Guss-man*, the cry multiplied, passing lip to lip until it became a chant, a summoning. They converged from their earthworks, from their cookfires, from their piss-trenches at the ring's edge—Kurz's sworn men, clad in stained gambeson and piecemeal ring-mail, their faces bearing that particular hollowness of soldiers too long encamped in enemy soil. Maerbrang's jaw firmed, the muscles clenching like rope twisted upon a windlass; his nostrils flared wide as a winded stallion's, drawing the scent of their unwashed bodies, their oiled iron, their fear dressed as fury; his eyes narrowed to splinters of flint, each man catalogued, measured, found wanting. His whole frame underwent a settling, a sinking of weight into the hips, shoulders squaring as a plough-ox squares to the yoke—and when his hands found the spear's grip and levered it, the sharp tip cut the air high and hungry as a kite's hunting cry. There would be no quarter asked. He knew their lord's name had already begun its long forgetting beneath the hill. These men simply had not learned it yet.

## Blob 24:
Spear-hafts bristled around him like a thicket of ash-wood, and the foremost man—lantern-jawed, his gambeson crusted with old rust and older blood—spoke through his teeth: "We ken your blazon well enough, carrion-walker. What errand drags Guss's hound into Kurz's boneyard?"

Maerbrang let his hand rest upon his seax-hilt, scanning the encampment's margins. "Where shelters your warlord? I would speak with Semprovius Thessamander Kurz."

A bark of mirthless laughter from the lantern-jawed man. "Kurz took his death-rattle in the small hours—choked on his own phlegm whilst the watch-fires guttered. You've trudged corpse-roads for a meeting with worm-meat."

"Then it was his final dying," Maerbrang said. "The name-stone is shattered."

The warrior's eyes narrowed, something like grim satisfaction flickering there. "Aye. I reckoned as much when you crawled from that drowned gullet."

Before he could speak further, a mud-spattered runner burst through the perimeter, gasping: "Sarks—a war-band thick as midges—less than a mile and closing swift!"

Men scrambled toward the earthworks, snatching up self-bows and fire-hardened javelins. Three warriors remained, encircling Maerbrang with naked steel.

"Can you wield that blade for something beyond murder?" the youngest demanded.

Maerbrang folded his arms across his chest. "To what purpose? You would gut me regardless once the Sarks lay cooling."

"Mayhaps we perish first," another offered. "Mayhaps you walk free."

The third spat into the loam. "Kurz's blood is cold. We serve no lord's vengeance now—only our own sinews."

From the henj's rim: "*They crest the ridge!*"

"Your oath," the lantern-jawed man pressed. "Swear you will not turn your edge against us."

Maerbrang's smile was a wound. "What worth is an oath from a man who has walked corpse-roads and broken name-stones? If I swore fealty on every spirit between here and the henj, you would be a fool to sleep easier for it—I would simply swear again when the wind changed."

The cry rose shrill from the earthworks: "*They are upon us!*"

---

## Blob 25:
Twelve spears held the henj-works—thirteen if Maerbrang counted his own arm among them—and the first wave of sarks met that bristling welcome with the witlessness of driven cattle. A clay sling-bullet took one through the eye-socket mid-scramble; another pitched forward into a stake-pit, and an axe crunched through his nape before he could thrash free. Maerbrang's own spear licked out crane-quick, skimming through gristle and neck-cord, and the sark he'd opened folded sideways, pawing at the ruin of his throat as he slithered off the wall. Seven, perhaps eight of the forgotten men lay twitching in the ditch-mud, their snarling gone to wet gurgles—but two more were already hauling themselves over the coping-stones toward him, and beyond them, beyond the embankment's lip, Maerbrang could see the crowding silhouettes of scores more, their heads cresting the earthwork like maggots boiling from a wound.

---

## Blob 26:
The first man fell within arm's reach. Maerbrang saw him buckle—a squat spearman from Kurz's former levy—and watched another warrior, this one helmed in boiled leather gone black with old tallow, cleave through the two sarks that had borne the man down, his hatchet splitting the crown of one and lodging in the clavicle of the second with a wet wrench of gristle, but when he knelt to haul his fellow upright the spearman's throat gaped like a second mouth, the windpipe shredded to pale ribbons, the blood already sheeting down his corslet in a burgundy apron that steamed faintly in the cold. Twelve remaining, Maerbrang tallied—himself counted among that sum—as he torqued his ash-haft free from the sark whose belly he had just punctured, the entrails slopping out in grey-pink ropes, the creature still twitching with that terrible idiot persistence of the Forgotten. Then hands seized him from behind: fingers like hemp cordage, an arm hooked beneath his chin, the stench of rotted teeth and something older—loam, perhaps, or the fetor of a barrow unsealed—and he was wrenched backward, his heels skidding furrows in the churned mud as the sark bore him earthward with its full weight, its knees clamping his ribs, one fist hammering at his wrist each time he scrabbled toward the fallen spear three handspans distant. The arm constricted; Maerbrang's gorge rose; his vision curdled at the margins like milk left too long in a summer pail. Across the trampled garth he glimpsed two more warriors crumple; one took a sark with him into the mire, their bodies interlocked in a final ugly clinch; the other simply dropped, hamstrung and screaming until a heel stamped his cry into silence. A second sark vaulted a gap in the low rubble-wall, its gaze fixing on Maerbrang's pinioned form, and it loped toward him with that hitching, disjointed gait—limbs remembering purpose but forgetting grace—closing the distance span by span while the pressure at his throat squeezed the world to a shrinking aperture of grey and amber and the dim silhouette of death shambling ever nearer. He wondered, through the roaring in his ears, if this was the doom the goldfinch had sung of—if Wode's laughter had already faded, if this muddy wrack of a henj-yard was the hall where all threads severed.

---

## Blob 27:
Maerbrang's scrabbling fingers found the haft of Wode's mace where it hung at his belt, and the world shrank to that one slender certainty amid the roar. The bludgeon answered his grip, arced blind over his shoulder, and staved the sark's brainpan with a wet crunch that loosed the strangling arm from his throat. He gulped air like a drowning man, watched through tear-blurred eyes as a flung javelin punched through the charging sark's sternum and toppled it mid-stride, then heaved himself upright on legs gone treacherous beneath him, snatched his fallen spear, hamstrung a loping grey shape that lunged too near, stumbled and staggered and half-crawled toward the remnant half-ring of warriors braced against the henj's last standing wall—eight bloodied figures, panting, blades outthrust like the quills of some cornered beast—and rough hands seized his harness and hauled him gasping into that bristling circle where back pressed to back and the killing went on.

---

## Blob 28:
Though none among them could have reckoned its precise duration—minutes smeared into one feral, thrashing continuum—the slaughter concluded with the abruptness of a fever breaking, for sarks, being carrion-bold but craven at the marrow, had not anticipated quarry that bit back, and when two-score of their pallid kindred lay opened and steaming, the survivors scattered like startled vermin into the fog-choked weald. Nine men remained upright, Maerbrang among them, their ring contracted against the henj's one standing wall like the last clot of warmth in a cooling body. The dead had swarmed upon them and now the dead clung still—rag-wound corpses heaped at their feet, limbs tangled with the defenders' greaves, fingers still hooked in cloak-hems and belt-leather as though death had merely interrupted their grasping rather than ended it. Beyond this central massing, other carcasses lay strewn across the henj's broken floor like wind-scattered offal, draped over toppled lintels, crumpled in the channels where old rain-gutters had once directed libations. Above it all the wall loomed—that solitary upright slab of lichen-scabbed granite—and cast its penumbra over victors and slain alike, indifferent as a cenotaph to the carnage pooled at its base. Where moments before the air had rung with the wet percussion of iron meeting bone, now pressed only silence, tottering and immense.

---

## Blob 29:
And so with Thessamander Kurz shorn from the world they found no quarrel left between them, and so Maerbrang stooped beside the lantern-jawed warrior and the others to prise stones from the henj's tumbled circlet and heap four rough kerns over the fallen, and so he gleaned from them the lie of the land—the fens, the drove-roads, the lord-halls still standing—and so at dusk they scattered, each to his own refuge or ruin or master's hearth, but Maerbrang turned his foot back toward lord Guss, for that was his duty.
