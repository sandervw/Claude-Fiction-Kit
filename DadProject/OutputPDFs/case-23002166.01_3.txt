I. 
BACKGROUND FACTS. 
Terry sustained a work-related injury to his arm in 2022. D0005, Certified R. Part 1 at 102 
(06/03/2025). He filed a workers’ compensation claim against his employer and reached a 
settlement agreement regarding that injury with his employer prior to hearing. Id. at 101. However, 
he also claimed that arm injury was a second injury that qualified for liability from the Fund. The 
hearing before the Workers’ Compensation Commission was limited to the Fund’s potential 
liability. Id. 
Terry claimed that his prior injury was to eyes. Terry has had ongoing issues with his eyes 
since he was a child, including “vision diplopia (double vision), alternating exotropia (lazy eye), 
dry eye syndrome, astigmatism, and blurry vision.” Id. He has had several eye surgeries to attempt 
to address these problems, but his issues with blurred and double vision have persisted. Id. About 
two or three times per week, Terry would have to stop working to apply artificial tears to correct 
his blurred or double vision. Id. at 102-03. At his most recent eye appointment in August 2023, 
Terry reported “cloudy spots in the center of [his] vision” that disappeared with artificial tears. Id.

II. 
STANDARD OF REVIEW. 
 
The Iowa Administrative Procedure Act, Iowa Code chapter 17A, governs the scope of the 
Court’s review in workers’ compensation cases. Iowa Code § 10A.322(1); Meyer v. IBP, Inc., 710 
N.W.2d 213, 218 (Iowa 2006). A party challenging agency action bears the burden of 
demonstrating the action’s invalidity and resulting prejudice. Iowa Code § 17A.19(8)(a). The 
district court acts in an appellate capacity to correct errors of law on the part of the agency. 
Grundmeyer v. Weyerhaeuser Co., 649 N.W.2d 744, 748 (Iowa 2002). Where the issue is one of

fact, the court must accept the agency’s factual findings unless they are “not supported by 
substantial evidence in the record before the court when that record is viewed as a whole.” Iowa 
Code § 17A.19(10)(f); see also Mycogen Seeds v. Sands, 686 N.W.2d 457, 464-465 (Iowa 2004). 
Where the issue is the application of law to facts, the court must determine if the agency’s decision 
is “irrational, illogical, or wholly unjustifiable” with its analysis. Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(m). 
III. 
ANALYSIS. 
Terry claims he is entitled to benefits under the Second Injury Compensation Act, codified 
at Iowa Code section 85.64. Iowa courts have boiled this section down into three elements that a 
claimant must demonstrate in order to claim Fund benefits: 1) loss of the use of a hand, arm, foot, 
or eye; 2) a later loss of use of another “member or organ in a work-related, compensable injury”; 
and 3) “some permanent injury from the injuries.” Second Inj. Fund of Iowa v. Bergeson, 526 
N.W.2d 543, 548 (Iowa 1995). 
Terry argues that the agency was incorrect in concluding that he did not suffer a qualifying 
first injury to his eye. He states that the Deputy Commissioner should not have rejected Dr. 
Kuhnlein’s impairment rating. In the alternative, he claims that an impairment rating is not 
necessary to establish a qualifying first injury. 
A. Is the finding of no impairment rating supported by substantial evidence? 
As the fact finder, the agency decides how much weight to give an expert’s opinion by 
evaluating the accuracy of the facts presented to the expert and considering other relevant 
circumstances. Sherman v. Pella Corp., 576 N.W.2d 312, 321 (Iowa 1998). Based on these 
considerations, the agency may reject or accept expert evidence entirely or in part. Cedar Rapids 
Cmty. Sch. Dist. v. Pease, 807 N.W.2d 839, 850 (Iowa 2011). On findings regarding permanent 
disability, this court can only reverse if the agency’s decision is not supported by substantial

evidence. See Hill v. Fleetguard, Inc., 705 N.W.2d 665, 673 (Iowa 2005). When evaluating if there 
is substantial evidence, “the question on appeal is not whether the evidence supports a different 
finding than the finding made by the commissioner, but whether the evidence ‘supports the 
findings actually made.’” Meyer, 710 N.W.2d at 218 (quoting St. Luke's Hosp. v. Gray, 604 
N.W.2d 646, 649 (Iowa 2000)). 
The Deputy Commissioner explained why they did not find Dr. Kuhnlein as credible as 
Dr. Wilkinson, stating “an optometrist is better qualified to address issues of the eye than an 
occupational physician.” D0005 at 105. As Dr. Wilkinson noted, visual acuity under the Guides 
must be measured after assessing for any refractive error in the patient’s current vision, meaning 
a determination of the prescription lenses they should be wearing. There is no indication that this 
occurred during Dr. Kuhnlein’s tests, and Dr. Wilkinson suggested that Dr. Kuhnlein would not 
have the necessary expertise to do so. The Deputy Commissioner also agreed with Dr. Wilkinson 
that there were not well-documented limitations to Terry’s functional vision that would necessitate 
an adjustment under section 12.4b of the Guides. Taking all of these findings together, there is 
substantial evidence to support the agency’s decision that Terry failed to prove that he had a 
permanent impairment under the Guides prior to his work injury. 
B. Does an injury need an impairment rating to qualify as a first injury? 
Terry argues that even if he has a no permanent impairment under the Guides, he has still 
demonstrated a qualifying first injury. He points to precedent at both the agency and appellate 
level. The problem, as the Fund points out, is that Iowa’s worker’s compensation statute was 
extensively amended in 2017. This includes the addition of the following language: 
In all cases of permanent partial disability . . . when determining functional 
disability and not loss of earning capacity, the extent of loss or percentage of 
permanent impairment shall be determined solely by utilizing the guides to the 
evaluation of permanent impairment, published by the American medical

association, as adopted by the workers' compensation commissioner by rule 
pursuant to chapter 17A. Lay testimony or agency expertise shall not be utilized in 
determining loss or percentage of permanent impairment . . . when determining 
functional disability and not loss of earning capacity. 
 
2017 Iowa Acts ch. 23, § 9 (now codified at Iowa Code § 85.34(2)(x)). The cases that Terry cites 
in support of his position that no impairment rating is required for a qualifying first injury are all 
from before this addition. The Fund argues that both the first and second loss require a finding of 
function impairment consistent with section 85.34(2)(x). Terry counters that the language of 
section 85.34(2)(x) should not affect his claim, because it is in a different section of code from the 
one describing Fund liability, section 85.64.  
When interpreting Iowa’s workers’ compensation law, courts should “assess the statute in 
its entirety rather than isolated words or phrases to ensure our interpretation is harmonious with 
the statute as a whole.” Ramirez-Trujillo v. Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 N.W.2d 759, 770 (Iowa 2016). 
The Iowa Court of Appeals recently observed that the issue of what is a scheduled injury has been 
treated the same under the exact subsections that Terry cites, 85.34(2) and 85.64(1). Laguerre v. 
JBS USA Holdings, Inc., No. 24-2049, 2025 WL 3023022, at *6 (Iowa Ct. App. Oct. 29, 2025). 
Subsection 85.34(3) has also been used in cases involving the Fund and a claim of permanent and 
total disability. Second Inj. Fund of Iowa v. Shank, 516 N.W.2d 808, 814 (Iowa 1994). 
 
The biggest problem with Terry’s argument is that Iowa Courts of Appeals has already 
explicitly used section 85.34(2)(x) in a case determining Fund liability. In Harrell v. Denver 
Findley & Sons, Inc., the issue was whether the Fund was entitled to credit for a knee replacement 
when the only evidence of the surgery was the claimant’s own testimony. 986 N.W.2d 872 (Table), 
2022 WL 2824746 (Iowa Ct. App. 2022). The agency had cross-referenced this testimony with the 
Guides and used the permanent impairment rating for a knee replacement to determine the amount 
of credit the Fund should get. Id. at *2. The Court of Appeals rejected this approach, stating that

“the plain language of section 85.34(2)(x)” specifically bars the use of lay testimony “‘in 
determining loss or percentage of permanent impairment.’ And here, ‘determining loss or 
percentage of permanent impairment’ necessarily included a determination of . . . whether he had 
a total knee replacement or not.” Id. The Court of Appeals reviewed for correction of legal error 
and in the end it applied section 85.34(2)(x). The Commissioner has since used the reasoning in 
Harrell to conclude that a permanent impairment rating from the Guides is necessary to show a 
qualifying first injury. Demers v. Second Inj. Fund, File No.: 22000216.02, 2025 WL 2451099, at 
*2 (Aug. 19, 2025). The Court sees no reason to hold differently here. 
 
The same holds true in Laguerre. In that case, the claimant argued that the court should not 
consider an Iowa Supreme Court case (Delaney v. Second Injury Fund, 6 N.W.3d 714 (Iowa 2024)) 
because the issue in that case was whether an injury was scheduled or unscheduled for purposes 
of determining Second Injury Fund liability.  2025 WL 3023022 at *6.  The court stated as follows: 
Laguerre brushes aside Delaney as “not controlling” because it analyzed whether 
an injury was scheduled or unscheduled in the context of second-injury-fund 
payments under section 85.64(1) rather than directly under section 85.34(2). 
[footnote and cite omitted]. But the relevant issue is the same. Indeed, the supreme 
court relied on cases deciding the issue under both 85.34(2) and 85.64(1) 
interchangeably in its reasoning. . . . What's more, Laguerre has offered no reason 
that the analysis should be any different between the two statutes. Nor do we see 
any reason that the different context, such as the shorter list of applicable body 
parts, would call for any difference in approach. (Emphasis added). 
 
Id. 
Terry correctly notes that ultimately the Fund’s liability is based on industrial disability 
(i.e., loss of earning capacity) and not functional disability. Second Inj. Fund of Iowa v. Nelson, 
544 N.W.2d 258, 269 (Iowa 1995) (“[T]he Fund is responsible only for the difference between the 
compensation for which the current employer is liable and the total amount of industrial disability 
from which the employee suffers, reduced by the compensable value of the first injury.”). Section

85.34(2)(x) does specifically state that it should only be applied “when determining functional 
disability and not loss of earning capacity.” However, there are two issues with Terry’s conclusion 
that this means that 85.34(2)(x) should not apply to cases involving the Fund, even aside from 
those already discussed.  
First, the claimant must meet the test for proving Fund liability before any calculations can 
begin. As the Fund explains: 
 [T]he Fund’s liability is not triggered until three things are proven. The first two 
requirements are losses of use of a body part, i.e.[,] partial disabilities. The third 
requirement is permanency of those losses. These three requirements make up the 
“permanent partial disability” that is the subject of section 85.34(2)(x). The 
agency’s evaluation of the Fund’s industrial liability does not occur until these three 
requirements of permanent partial disability are met. Therefore, section 85.34(2)(x) 
applies to the threshold question of whether Terry established a first qualifying loss, 
or permanent partial disability, before the Fund’s industrial liability is triggered. 
 
D0010, Resp’t’s Br. at 9-10 (10/03/2025).  
The other issue is that, generally speaking, “[f]unctional disability is used to determine a 
specific scheduled disability” and “industrial disability is used to determine an unscheduled 
disability.” Shank, 516 N.W.2d at 813. All of the qualifying first injuries noted by section 85.64(1) 
are scheduled injuries. Iowa Code § 85.34(2)(l)-(m), (o)-(q) (detailing the scheduled compensation 
for the loss of a hand, arm, foot, leg, and eye, respectively). And while the ultimate issue for Fund 
cases is how those losses translate to industrial disability, functional disability is a factor that must 
be considered when determining industrial disability. Shank, 516 N.W.2d at 813. “It is the 
cumulative effect of scheduled injuries resulting in industrial disability to the body as a whole . . . 
that triggers the Fund's proportional liability.” Second Inj. Fund of Iowa v. Braden, 459 N.W.2d 
467, 470 (Iowa 1990). An injury that creates no functional impairment cannot impact an 
individual’s earning capacity. 
Terry himself demonstrates this difficulty in his own argument. He states that “[i]n

determining whether there is a first qualifying loss, the Agency should determine only whether the 
injury caused a ‘loss of physiological capacity of the body part’ and not by evaluating industrial 
factors such as whether the worker returned to work without restrictions.”2 D0008, Pet. Br. at 5 
(08/29/2025) (quoting Huffey v. Second Injury Fund of Iowa, 947 N.W.2d 414 (Table), 2020 WL 
1548490, at *4 (Iowa Ct. App. 2020)). That may be true, but he later argues that an impairment 
rating is not required to demonstrate this loss of physiological capacity. The standard cannot be 
that a claimant can have a qualifying first injury without showing industrial disability or functional 
disability. The best way to address these issues and harmonize these statutes is in the way that the 
Fund suggests—requiring a finding of functional disability supported by the Guides in order to 
establish a first qualifying injury. The agency therefore did not err in concluding that Terry had 
not demonstrated a first qualifying injury. 
IV. 
DISPOSITION. 
IT IS THE ORDER OF THE COURT that the Decision of the Workers’ Compensation 
Commission is AFFIRMED. The Petition for Judicial Review is dismissed.
