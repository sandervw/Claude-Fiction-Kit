STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
On October 16, 2024, the claimant filed a petition for alternate medical care 
pursuant to Iowa Code 85.27(4) and 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.48.  The 
defendant filed an answer admitting liability for injuries noted in the petition. 
The undersigned presided over the hearing held via telephone and recorded 
digitally on October 28, 2024.  That recording constitutes the official record of the 
proceeding pursuant to 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.48(12).  Claimant participated 
personally and through her attorney, R. Saffin Parrish-Sams.  The defendant 
participated through their attorney, Charles Showalter.   
Prior to the hearing, the claimant submitted ten pages of exhibits, marked as 
Exhibits 1-3.  The defendant submitted eight pages of exhibits, marked as Exhibits 1-3.  
All exhibits were received into the record without objection.     
 
On July 21, 2023, the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commissioner issued an 
order delegating authority to deputy workers’ compensation commissioners, such as the 
undersigned, to issue final agency decisions on applications for alternate care.  
Consequently, this decision constitutes final agency action, and there is no appeal to 
the commissioner.  Judicial review in a district court pursuant to Iowa Code Chapter 17A 
is the avenue for an appeal. 
ISSUE 
The issue under consideration is whether the defendant should be ordered to 
provide alternate care for the claimant via an appointment with a specialist.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
Claimant, Elvira Jimenez, alleges that she sustained injuries to her shoulders, 
neck, low back, and left thigh on June 27, 2024, while working for Tyson Foods, Inc. 
(“Tyson”).  The claimant testified that on June 27, 2024, she was working at Tyson in a 
light duty capacity due to a prior shoulder injury.  (Testimony).  Since she was working 
light duty, she was assigned to an unfamiliar part of the Tyson facility.  (Testimony).  As 
she carried some empty boxes, she stepped on a manhole cover over a drain.  
(Testimony).  The manhole cover gave way, and she fell into the drain.  (Testimony).  
As she fell, she caught herself with her arms.  (Testimony).   
 
She immediately began using her lower body in an attempt to get herself out of 
the drain.  (Testimony).  She felt pain in her left lower extremity and low back after she 
fell.  (Testimony).  Soon after her fall, her coworkers gathered around her and after 
about five minutes, they extracted her from the manhole.  (Testimony).  She was placed 
into a wheelchair.  (Testimony).  Rather than proceeding to the emergency room, she 
was taken to the on-site Tyson medical facility.  (Testimony).   
 
David Archer, M.D., began treating Ms. Jimenez.  (Testimony).  Dr. Archer is a 
family practitioner.  (Defendant’s Exhibit 1:1-4).  He examined her on July 2, 2024, for 
her initial visit.  (Claimant’s Exhibit 2:1).  Ms. Jimenez complained of pain in her bilateral 
arms, shoulders, legs, back and neck.  (CE 2:1).  Dr. Archer diagnosed her with a right 
shoulder strain, prescribed medication, and recommended physical therapy.  (CE 2:1).    
 
On July 8, 2024, Ms. Jimenez began physical therapy, as prescribed by Dr. 
Archer.  (CE 1:1-3).  She recounted the incident and noted having “many abrasions, 
bruises on lower legs, strain bilateral shoulders, neck and upper back…” because she 
fell into the manhole.  (CE 1:1).  The therapist documented Ms. Jimenez as having 
concerns to her right shoulder, “upper trap,” and neck.  (CE 1:1).  The therapist 
observed that Ms. Jimenez walked with an antalgic gait and that she complained of 
soreness in her lower legs along with abrasions from her recent fall.  (CE 1:2).  The 
therapist also documented that Ms. Jimenez had stiffness and pain when she moved 
her neck.  (CE 1:2).  The therapist listed diagnoses of a fall, contusions, a strain of the 
neck, and a strain of the right shoulder.  (CE 1:3).  The goals for her therapy all 
centered around her neck and shoulders.  (CE 1:3).   
 
At the order of Dr. Archer, Ms. Jimenez had a shoulder MRI on July 23, 2024.  
(DE 2:5-6).  The MRI showed supraspinatus tendinopathy with a small area of partial-
thickness bursal surface tear.  (DE 2:6).  There also was a marginal tear of the long 
head biceps tendon.  (DE 2:6).   
 
On July 31, 2024, Ms. Jimenez saw Dr. Archer again.  (CE 2:2).  Ms. Jimenez 
again complained of pain to her bilateral arms, shoulders, legs, back and neck.  (CE 
2:2).  Dr. Archer diagnosed Ms. Jimenez with a fall, a partial rotator cuff tear to the right 
shoulder, and a thigh contusion.  (CE 2:2).  Again, he recommended prescription 
medications, physical therapy, and referred her to an orthopedist.  (CE 2:2).

Ms. Jimenez had her fourth visit with Dr. Archer on October 7, 2024.  (CE 2:3-4).  
Ms. Jimenez complained of bilateral arm pain, shoulder pain, neck pain, left thigh pain, 
and low back pain.  (CE 2:3).  Dr. Archer noted that a prior MRI of the shoulder caused 
reason for a referral to an orthopedist.  (CE 2:3).  Dr. Archer documented that another 
doctor evaluated her shoulder and provided an injection.  (CE 2:3).  Dr. Archer noted 
that Ms. Jimenez had full range of motion in her low back with no tenderness, palpable 
spasm or pain on motion.  (CE 2:3).  Dr. Archer also documented “subjective 
tenderness [to the] left lateral thigh…” with resolved bruising.  (CE 2:3).  Dr. Archer 
diagnosed Ms. Jimenez with a contusion of her left lower extremity and acute pain of 
the right shoulder.  (CE 2:3).  Again, Dr. Archer provided a prescription for medications 
and recommended continued physical therapy for left shoulder pain, mid/low back pain, 
and left lateral thigh issues.  (CE 2:4).   
 
Ms. Jimenez was asked by her counsel during the hearing as to her October of 
2024 appointment with Dr. Archer.  (Testimony).  She recounted that she was examined 
while wearing her street clothes, and that Dr. Archer never performed testing on her 
lower back, such as a straight leg raise test.  (Testimony).  Dr. Archer also never 
performed any compression testing on her neck.  (Testimony).  She recalled that Dr. 
Archer only had her walk a bit on her heels and had her bend over and turn her neck 
and lower back.  (Testimony).   
 
On October 11, 2024, counsel for the defendant e-mailed claimant’s counsel 
opining that it was “Tyson’s position that Dr. Archer did address the requested body 
parts, as he ordered PT for those concerns.”  (DE 3:7).   
 
Ms. Jimenez testified that she continued to have neck pain, left thigh pain, and 
low back pain during her various visits with Dr. Archer.  (Testimony).   
 
At the hearing, Ms. Jimenez testified that she experienced pain in her left thigh, 
along with palpable bumps on the inside of her left thigh and swelling in the same area.  
(Testimony).  She initially had bruising, but this dissipated over time.  (Testimony).  In 
her low back, she had sharp pain into her left buttock.  (Testimony).  She experienced 
difficulties with standing or sitting for long periods of time.  (Testimony).  Additionally, 
she testified to throbbing pain and stiffness in her neck.  (Testimony).  Finally, she noted 
right arm and periodic left arm pain and weakness.  (Testimony).  The pain radiates 
down to her hand on occasion.  (Testimony).  She was unsure if her arm pain was 
caused by her neck or shoulder.  (Testimony). 
 
Ms. Jimenez was displeased with the care offered by Tyson through Dr. Archer, 
as he never ordered imaging for her neck, lower back, or left leg.  (Testimony).  He also 
never provided any diagnoses for these body parts.  (Testimony).  According to Ms. 
Jimenez, Dr. Archer only authorized physical therapy and prescription medication 
management.  (Testimony).  None of these provided any relief.  (Testimony).   
CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Iowa Code 85.27(4) provides, in relevant part:

For purposes of this section, the employer is obligated to furnish reasonable 
services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has the right to 
choose the care….  The treatment must be offered promptly and be 
reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the 
employee.  If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the care 
offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such dissatisfaction 
to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the employer and 
the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited to treat the 
injury.  If the employer and employee cannot agree on such alternate care, 
the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable proofs of the 
necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
Iowa Code 85.27(4). See Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 433 (Iowa 
1997).   
“Iowa Code section 85.27(4) affords an employer who does not contest the 
compensability of a workplace injury a qualified statutory right to control the medical 
care provided to an injured employee.”  Ramirez-Trujillo v. Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 
N.W.2d 759, 769 (Iowa 2016) (citing R.R. Donnelly & Sons v. Barnett, 670 N.W.2d 190, 
195, 197 (Iowa 2003)).  “In enacting the right-to-choose provision in section 85.27(4), 
our legislature sought to balance the interests of injured employees against the 
competing interests of their employers.”  Ramirez-Trujillo, 878 N.W.2d at 770-71 (citing 
Bell Bros., 779 N.W.2d at 202, 207; IBP, Inc. v. Harker, 633 N.W.2d 322, 326-27 (Iowa 
2001)).   
The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except where the 
employer has denied liability for the injury.  Iowa Code section 85.27; Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening, October 16, 1975).  An employer’s right to select 
the provider of medical treatment to an injured worker does not include the right to 
determine how an injured worker should be diagnosed, evaluated, treated, or other 
matters of professional medical judgment.  Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 
866389 (Declaratory Ruling, May 18, 1988).  Reasonable care includes care necessary 
to diagnose the condition, and defendants are not entitled to interfere with the medical 
judgment of its own treating physician.  Pote v. Mickow Corp., File No. 694639 (Review-
Reopening Decision, June 17, 1986).   
The employer must furnish “reasonable medical services and supplies and 
reasonable and necessary appliances to treat an injured employee.”  Stone Container 
Corp. v. Castle, 657 N.W.2d 485, 490 (Iowa 2003)(emphasis in original).  Such 
employer-provided care “must be offered promptly and be reasonably suited to treat the 
injury without undue inconvenience to the employee.”  Iowa Code section 85.27(4).  
More specifically in Long, the court noted that “the employer’s obligation under the 
statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.”  Long v. Roberts 
Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122, 128 (Iowa 1995).

By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment - and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See e.g. 
Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e); Bell Bros. Heating and Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 
N.W.2d 193, 209 (Iowa 2010); Long, 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  More specifically in 
Long, the court noted that “the employer’s obligation under the statute turns on the 
question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.”  Long, 528 N.W.2d at 128.   
An injured employee dissatisfied with the employer-furnished care (or lack 
thereof) may share the employee’s discontent with the employer and if the parties 
cannot reach an agreement on alternate care, “the commissioner may, upon application 
and reasonable proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and order the care.”  Id.  
“Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.”  Long, 528 
N.W.2d at 123; Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 436.  As the party seeking 
relief in the form of alternate care, the employee bears the burden of proving that the 
authorized care is unreasonable.  Id. at 124; Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d at 209; Pirelli-
Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 436.  Because “the employer’s obligation under the 
statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability,” an injured 
employee’s dissatisfaction with employer-provided care, standing alone, is not enough 
to find such care unreasonable.  Id.  However, if substantial evidence exists that the 
care authorized by the employer was ineffective, it may be found that the care was not 
reasonable.  Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 437.   
 
The claimant requests alternate care via an examination by a specialist and 
provision of imaging for her complaints regarding her neck, low back, and left leg.  The 
claimant argues that the care provided by Dr. Archer is not reasonable and that a 
specialist could provide more detailed diagnoses and treatment recommendations.  
Further, the claimant argues that the simple prescription of pain medication and physical 
therapy is care that is not adequate to treat Ms. Jimenez’s ongoing complaints to the 
aforementioned body parts and that Dr. Archer has not even performed basic testing to 
the claimant’s neck, low back, or left leg.     
 
The defendant argues that the care provided by Dr. Archer has been reasonable.  
They point to the fact that Dr. Archer previously ordered an MRI as evidence that Dr. 
Archer will order imaging where necessary. The defendant contends that the claimant’s 
request is simply based upon a dissatisfaction with care and that the care provided is 
reasonable.   
 
The question becomes whether the care provided by the defendant is 
reasonable.  The claimant has treated with Dr. Archer for four months.  He previously 
provided prescription medications and referrals for physical therapy, to an orthopedist 
and for an MRI for the claimant’s right shoulder.  More specifically, the referral for 
therapy made mention of the claimant’s low back pain.   
Nevertheless, the claimant continued to complain about neck, low back, and left 
leg issues.  She has seen Dr. Archer four times, most recently on October 7, 2024.  
During this visit, Ms. Jimenez continued to note complaints of bilateral arm and shoulder 
pain, neck pain, low back pain, and left lower extremity pain.  Dr. Archer noted in the

record that he examined Ms. Jimenez’s neck; however, as Ms. Jimenez noted, he did 
not perform, nor did he document, any objective testing.  More specifically, Ms. Jimenez 
testified that Dr. Archer only had her bend over and touch her toes and moved her neck 
from side to side.  She did not recall Dr. Archer testing her reflexes in her lower 
extremities.  Additionally, Ms. Jimenez was examined in her street clothes.  She did not 
recall Dr. Archer examining her left thigh during the examination.   
Ms. Jimenez indicated that the only improvement in her symptoms in her neck, 
lower back, and left leg, was the dissipation of the bruising in her left leg.   
Based upon the evidence in the record, it appears that the care provided by 
Tyson through Dr. Archer has not been effective.  The defendant is correct in that Dr. 
Archer has certainly indicated the willingness to make referrals when necessary.  Yet, 
his examinations of the claimant and the lack of follow-up care provided by Dr. Archer 
indicate more than a mere dissatisfaction with care.  It indicates that the care provided 
by Dr. Archer has not been effective.  In the four months since Ms. Jimenez’s injury, Dr. 
Archer has provided the claimant with no follow-up care for her ongoing neck pain or left 
thigh pain.  He provided her a prescription for therapy for her low back pain, but did not 
provide further recommendation to determine the root cause of the low back pain.  He 
also provided no diagnosis regarding the claimant’s low back.   
The substantial evidence in the record supports the proposition that the care 
provided by Tyson through Dr. Archer has been ineffective.  Therefore, the claimant’s 
petition is granted.   
The claimant noted that they researched the closest “specialist” to Ms. Jimenez’s 
home address and could only find Shelley Wells, D.O.  It appears that Dr. Wells is a 
pain management specialist.  I am not in the position to determine if a pain 
management specialist is the most appropriate provider to see Ms. Jimenez for her 
ongoing complaints.  Both parties agreed at the hearing that, should the claimant’s 
petition be granted, the defendant should maintain control of the claimant’s care insofar 
as it relates to choosing a provider.  I agree with this position.  The claimant has not 
made a showing that the right to choose a provider should be revoked from the 
defendant.   
Therefore, the defendant shall provide a referral to a specialist to examine the 
claimant for her ongoing neck, low back, and left leg complaints.  I defer to the medical 
professionals on whether or not imaging is appropriate.  The specialist to whom Ms. 
Jimenez is referred should make the decision on what, if any, imaging is appropriate.   
The specialist to which Ms. Jimenez is referred shall be located within a 
reasonable distance from the home of the claimant.  This specialist will then be an 
authorized treating provider. 
 
IT IS THEREFORE ORDERED: 
1. The claimant’s petition for alternate care is granted.
