Danny Stanley, claimant, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from John Deere Des Moines Works, employer, and self-insured 
defendant.  The hearing was held on December 4, 2023. Pursuant to an order from the 
Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commissioner, this case was heard via videoconference 
using Zoom with all parties and the court reporter appearing remotely.   
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  Those 
stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration decision, 
and no factual issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised or discussed in 
this decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.  
Danny Stanley was the only witness to testify live at trial.  The evidentiary record 
also includes Joint Exhibits 1-7, Claimant’s Exhibits1-4, and Defendant’s Exhibits A-H.  
All exhibits were received into the record without objection.  
The parties submitted post-hearing briefs on January 12, 2024.  However, on 
March 29, 2024, the Iowa Supreme Court released its opinion in Bridgestone Americas 
Inc. v. Anderson, File No. 5067475, Case No. 22-1328 (Iowa 2024).  That decision 
addressed how shoulder injuries should be compensated when they occur in concert 
with other scheduled member claims under the revised Iowa Code, which is an issue in

parties an opportunity to submit supplemental briefs in this case.  On April 22, 2024, 
defendant submitted a supplemental brief.  Claimant did not submit any additional 
briefing.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 
employment.  Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. Dial 
Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309 (Iowa 1996).  The words “arising out of” refer to the cause or 
source of the injury.  The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 
circumstances of the injury.  2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124 (Iowa 1995).  
An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists between the 
injury and the employment.  Miedema, 551 N.W.2d 309.  The injury must be a rational 
consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely incidental to 
the employment.  Koehler Electric v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1 (Iowa 2000); Miedema, 551 
N.W.2d 309.  An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it happens within a 
period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may be when 
performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties or doing 
an activity incidental to them.  Ciha, 552 N.W.2d at 150. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996).  The question of causal 
connection is essentially within the domain of expert testimony.  The expert medical

evidence must be considered with all other evidence introduced bearing on the causal 
connection between the injury and the disability.  Supportive lay testimony may be used 
to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is also relevant and material to the 
causation question.  The weight to be given to an expert opinion is determined by the 
finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy of the facts the expert relied upon as 
well as other surrounding circumstances.  The expert opinion may be accepted or 
rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); 
IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. 
Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995).  Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 
(Iowa 1994).  Unrebutted expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected.  
Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 1994). However, 
when an expert's opinion is based upon an incomplete or incorrect history, it is not 
necessarily binding on the commissioner or the court. It is then to be weighed, together 
with other facts and circumstances, the ultimate conclusion being for the finder of the 
fact.  Musselman v. Central Telephone Company, 261 Iowa 352, 360, 154 N.W.2d 128, 
133 (1967); Bodish v. Fischer, Inc., 257 Iowa 516, 522, 133 N.W.2d 867, 870 (1965).  
The commissioner as trier of fact has the duty to determine the credibility of the 
witnesses and to weigh the evidence, together with the other disclosed facts and 
circumstances, and then to accept or reject the opinion.  Dunlavey, 526 N.W.2d at 853.  
 
The medical experts agree that Stanley suffered from left thumb CMC joint 
osteoarthritis prior to the alleged date of injury.  However, there is no evidence in the 
hearing record that his osteoarthritis was symptomatic prior to the injury date.  When a 
preexisting condition or disability is aggravated, accelerated, worsened or “lighted up” 
by employment, the condition becomes compensable. See Nicks v. Davenport Produce 
Co., 254 Iowa 130, 135; 115 N.W.2d 812, 815 (1962). However, a disease which under 
any rational work is likely to progress so as to finally disable an employee does not 
become a “personal injury” under the Workers' Compensation Act merely because it 
reaches a point of disablement while work for an employer is pursued. It is only when 
there is a direct causal connection between the exertion of the employment and the 
injury that a compensation award can be made. The question is whether the diseased 
condition was the cause, or whether the employment was a proximate contributing 
cause. Musselman, 261 Iowa at 360, 154 N.W.2d at 132 (citing Littell v. Lagomarcino 
Grupe Co., 235 Iowa 523, 529; 17 N.W.2d 120, 124 (1945)).  This is determined by 
expert testimony.   
 
I have adopted Dr. Bansal’s opinions concerning permanent impairment.  Dr. 
Bansal opined that Stanley’s pre-existing left thumb CMC osteoarthritis was 
permanently aggravated by repetitive and forceful wrenching and torquing at John 
Deere.  Given this, Stanley has proven a compensable injury to his left thumb.  The 
parties have already stipulated that Stanley sustained a compensable injury to his left 
shoulder, they just disagree on his resulting permanent impairment.   
 
Under the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is 
compensated either for a loss of use of a scheduled member under Iowa Code 
85.34(2)(a)-(u) or for loss of earning capacity under Iowa Code 85.34(2)(v). The extent 

STANLEY V. JOHN DEERE DES MOINES WORKS

of scheduled member disability benefits to which an injured worker is entitled is 
determined by using the functional method. Functional disability is “limited to the loss of 
the physiological capacity of the body or body part.” Mortimer v. Fruehauf Corp., 502 
N.W.2d 12, 15 (Iowa 1993); Sherman v. Pella Corp., 576 N.W.2d 312, 322 (Iowa 1998). 
An injury to a scheduled member may, because of aftereffects or compensatory 
change, result in permanent impairment of the body as a whole. Such impairment may 
in turn be the basis for a rating of industrial disability. It is the anatomical situs of the 
permanent injury or impairment which determines which schedules in Iowa Code 
85.34(a) - (u) apply. Lauhoff Grain v. McIntosh, 395 N.W.2d 834 (Iowa 1986); 
Blacksmith v. All-American, Inc., 290 N.W.2d 348 (Iowa 1980); Dailey v. Pooley Lumber 
Co., 233 Iowa 758, 10 N.W.2d 569 (1943); Soukup v. Shores Co., 222 Iowa 272, 268 
N.W. 598 (1936). 
 
Stanley has proven permanent injuries to his left shoulder and left thumb.3  In 
Bridgestone Americas Inc. v. Anderson, 4 N.W.3d 676, 683 (Iowa 2024), the Supreme 
Court held Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v) only applies to injuries that are not “described 
or referred to” in the schedule. Id.  In making this determination, the Court highlighted 
the beginning of Code section 85.34(2)(v) which reads, “In all cases of permanent 
partial disability other than those described or referred to in paragraphs ‘a’ through ‘u’, 
the compensation shall be paid during the number of weeks in relation to five hundred 
weeks . . . .”  Id. (emphasis added).  The Court indicated that injuries to fingers, arms, 
hands, and shoulders are all described in subsections (a) through (u), so they would not 
fall under the catch all provision of Code section 85.34(2)(v).   Anderson, 4 N.W.3d at 
685 (stating “And so, paragraph (v) generally doesn’t apply to injuries to fingers, arms, 
hands, shoulders . . . .”).   Thus, under the Anderson holding Stanley must be 
compensated separately for a scheduled member injury to his shoulder and a 
scheduled member injury to his left thumb.   
Stanley’s permanent impairment will be determined under Iowa Code section 
85.34(2)(n) and Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(a).  Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(n) limits 
compensation for a shoulder injury to four hundred weeks.  Iowa Code § 85.34(2)(n) 
(2019).  Under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(a), compensation for a thumb is set at sixty 
weeks.  Iowa Code § 85.34(2)(a).

Dr. Bansal also attempts to assign Stanley 2 percent permanent impairment for 
loss of elbow flexion strength. Under Iowa Code section 85.34(x), claims of functional

cautioned against impairment ratings that do not comply with the AMA Guides.  See Hill 
v. Vermeer Corporation, File No. 5066032 (App., Jan. 30, 2020).  The Hill decision 
states,

Finally, Dr. Bansal assigns 5 percent impairment of the left thumb for loss of 
radial abduction in the CMC joint, citing to Figures16-12 and 16-15, and Tables 16-8a,

Based on the above, I determine Stanley sustained 9 percent permanent 
impairment to his left shoulder and 5 percent permanent impairment to his left thumb as 
a result of the October 13, 2020, date of injury.  Stanley is entitled to 39 weeks of 
permanent partial disability benefits (36 for his shoulder and 3 for his thumb), 
commencing on April 26, 2021, at the rate of $688.66 per week.

Defendant is entitled to credit for that payment.
