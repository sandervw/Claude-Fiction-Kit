III. SCOPE AND STANDARD OF REVIEW 
 
The Iowa Administrative Procedure Act, Chapter 17A of the Iowa Code, governs judicial 
review of administrative agency decisions. The court shall reverse, modify, or grant other 
appropriate relief from final agency action if it determines the substantial rights of a petitioner 
have been prejudiced by any of the means set forth in Iowa Code section 17A.19(10)(a)-(n). 
Review of agency action is at law, not de novo, and is limited to the record made before the agency. 
Taylor v. Iowa Dep’t of Job Serv., 362 N.W.2d 534, 537 (Iowa 1985). Additional evidence or 
issues not considered by the agency cannot be considered by the court. Iowa Code § 
17A.19(7)(2020); Meads v. Iowa Dep’t of Social Servs., 366 N.W.2d 555, 559 (Iowa 1985). The 
court may not substitute its judgment for that of the agency. Mercy Health Center v. State Health 
Facilities Council, 360 N.W.2d 808, 809 (Iowa 1985). The court may not usurp the agency's 
function of making factual findings. McSpadden v. Big Ben Coal Co., 288 N.W.2d 181, 186 (Iowa 
1980). 
The court shall reverse, modify, or grant other appropriate relief from agency action if the 
agency action was based upon a determination of fact clearly vested by a provision of law in the 
discretion of the agency that is not supported by substantial evidence in the record before the court 
when that record is viewed as a whole. Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(f). “Record viewed as a whole” 
means that the adequacy of the evidence in the record before the court to support a particular 
finding of fact, must be judged in light of all the relevant evidence in the record cited by any party 
that detracts from the findings, as well as all of the relevant evidence in the record cited by any 
party that supports it. § 17A.19(10)(f)(3). This includes any determinations of veracity by the

8 
 
presiding officer who personally observed the demeanor of the witnesses and the agency's 
explanation of why the relevant evidence in the record supports its material findings of fact. Id.  
The evidence need not amount to a preponderance in order to be substantial evidence, but 
a mere scintilla will not suffice. Elliot v. Iowa Dep’t of Transp., 377 N.W.2d 250, 256 (Iowa Ct. 
App. 1985). Substantial evidence means the quantity and quality of evidence that would be deemed 
sufficient by a neutral, detached and reasonable person, to establish the fact at issue when the 
consequences resulting from the establishment of that fact are understood to be serious and of great 
importance. Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(f)(1). The fact that two inconsistent conclusions can be 
drawn from the evidence does not mean that one of those conclusions is unsupported by substantial 
evidence. Moore v. Iowa Dep’t of Transp., 473 N.W.2d 230, 232 (Iowa Ct. App. 1991). The 
relevant inquiry is not whether the evidence might support a different finding, but whether the 
evidence supports the findings actually made. Id. 
The commissioner has a duty to state the evidence relied upon and detail the reasons for 
any conclusions. Pitzer v. Rowley Interstate, 507 N.W.2d 389, 392 (Iowa 1993) (citing Catalfo v. 
Firestone Tire & Rubber Co., 213 N.W.2d 506, 510 (Iowa 1973)). This requirement is satisfied if 
the reviewing court is able to determine with reasonable certainty the factual basis on which the 
administrative officer acted. Id. At 393. It is understood that an administrative agency “cannot in 
its decision set out verbatim all testimony in a case.” Id. at 392 (citing McDowell v. Town of 
Clarksville, 241 N.W.2d 904, 908 (Iowa 1976)) “Nor, when the agency specifically refers to some 
of the evidence, should the losing party be able, ipso facto, to urge successfully that the agency 
did not weigh all the other evidence.” Id. An agency decision is final if supported by substantial 
evidence. Robbennolt v. Snap-On Tools Corp., 555 N.W.2d 229, 234 (Iowa 1996).

The court shall also reverse, modify, or grant other appropriate relief from agency action 
if such action was based upon an erroneous interpretation of a provision of law whose 
interpretation has not clearly been vested by a provision of law in the discretion of the agency. 
Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(c). The court shall not give deference to the view of the agency with 
respect to particular matters that have not been vested by a provision of law in the discretion of the 
agency. § 17A.19(11)(b). However, appropriate deference is given when the contrary is true. § 
17A.19(11)(c). The agency's findings are binding on appeal unless a contrary result is compelled 
as a matter of law. Ward v. Iowa Dep’t of Transp., 304 N.W.2d 236, 238 (Iowa 1981). 
Additionally, a reviewing court must also reverse, modify, or grant other appropriate relief 
when the agency's decision is “[b]ased upon an irrational, illogical, or wholly unjustifiable 
application of law to fact that has clearly been vested by a provision of law in the discretion of the 
agency.” Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(m). “In order to determine an employee's right to benefits, 
which is the agency's responsibility, the agency, out of necessity, must apply the law to the facts.” 
Mycogen Seeds v. Sands, 686 N.W.2d 457, 465 (Iowa 2004). Because the agency has been 
entrusted with the responsibility of applying the law to the facts, the “agency's application of the 
law to the facts can only be reversed if we determine such an application was ‘irrational, illogical, 
or wholly unjustifiable.’” Id. (citing Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(m)). 
“The findings of the commissioner are akin to a jury verdict, and we broadly apply them 
to uphold the commissioner's decision.” Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 150 (Iowa 
1996) (quoting Second Inj. Fund v. Shank, 516 N.W.2d 808, 812 (Iowa 1994) (citation omitted)). 
“We may reverse, modify, affirm or remand the case to the commissioner for further proceedings 
if we conclude the agency's action is affected by an error at law or if it is not supported by 
substantial evidence.” Id. at 150.

IV. ANALYSIS 
 
Petitioner contends that the Commissioner’s Appeal Decision was erroneous and must be 
reversed on the grounds set forth in Iowa Code §§ 17A.19(10)(b), (c), (f), (h), (i), (k), (m), and (n). 
She alleges that the Commissioner erred in finding that she did not meet her burden to show that 
her work at TPI was the proximate cause of her left shoulder injury and that he erred in finding 
that the other issues relating to benefit payments and fees are moot. Whether Petitioner’s injury 
was caused by her work at TPI is a determination of fact and an application of law to fact that is 
reviewed under §§ 17A.19(10)(f) and (m). The Court will also consider whether the 
Commissioner’s Appeal Decision was the product of reasoning that is so illogical as to render it 
wholly irrational under § 17A.19(10)(i) and whether the Commissioner’s Appeal Decision was 
otherwise unreasonable, arbitrary, capricious, or an abuse of discretion under § 17A.19(10)(n). 
The Commissioner has delegated authority to make appeal decisions, §§ 17A.19(15), 86.24, and 
Petitioner has not pointed to any specific violation of law, so she cannot rely on § 17A.19(10)(b) 
for reversal. Section 17A.19(10)(c) is not relevant to this appeal, because Petitioner has not alleged 
any specific law that the Commissioner has misinterpreted. Section 17A.19(10)(h) is not grounds 
for reversal because Petitioner has not alleged any circumstances showing that the Appeal Decision 
was inconsistent with prior agency practice. The Commissioner’s Appeal Decision was required 
by law, § 86.24, and its negative impact on the private rights affected is not grossly 
disproportionate to the benefits accruing to the public interest. As such, § 17A.19(10)(k) also 
cannot result in reversal. 
Petitioner has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that her injury 
both arose out of and in the course of her employment at TPI. Quaker Oats Co., 552 N.W.2d at 
150. A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable rather than

11 
 
merely possible. Sherman v. Pella Corp., 576 N.W.2d 312, 321 (Iowa 1998). The question of 
medical causation is essentially within the domain of expert testimony. Cedar Rapids Cmty. Sch. 
Dist. v. Pease, 807 N.W.2d 839, 845 (Iowa 2011). Unrebutted medical testimony cannot be 
summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911–12 (Iowa Ct. 
App. 1994). The weight given to an expert opinion is within the discretion of the fact finder and is 
impacted by the accuracy of the facts relied upon by the expert and other surrounding 
circumstances. Id. 
 
The Commissioner’s determination that Petitioner failed to meet her burden to show 
proximate causation was supported by substantial evidence. None of the four doctors that 
examined Petitioner causally related her injury to her work at TPI. Because the Commissioner 
mostly relies on expert opinion to resolve the question of medical causation, it would be 
exceedingly difficult for a claimant to successfully meet her burden to prove causation in the 
absence of medical opinion finding causation. 
The Commissioner appropriately gave Dr. Sullivan’s opinion the most weight, because of 
his expertise as an orthopedic surgeon and shoulder specialist and because he was the most familiar 
with Petitioner’s situation, having evaluated her at least seven times and performed her surgery. 
Dr. Sullivan’s opinion was the following: “I received no bona fide information from the patient or 
any other credible source that would qualify her injury as a work related injury.” Ex. JE 6 p. 15. A 
neutral, detached, and reasonable person would find this convincing evidence that Petitioner failed 
to show causation. 
Petitioner points to Dr. Aviles’ letter dated May 18, 2016, as countervailing evidence 
showing that the Commissioner did not have substantial evidence to support his conclusion. The 
letter stated:

ago she developed pain in her shoulder associated with repetitive labor.” Id. Petitioner’s reliance 
on this sentence is misplaced. Dr. Aviles says that Petitioner stated her injury was associated with 
repetitive labor, but does not give that opinion himself. 
 
Petitioner argues that Dr. Aviles’ statement in a medical record from May 5, 2016, 
contradicts his opinion that Petitioner’s injury was not caused by work activities. The relevant 
statement is the following: “Norie is a 64-year-old woman who states that she fell approximately 
three years ago for about two weeks then. She got better. Then approximately a year ago she 
developed pain associated with repetitive labor in that shoulder.” Ex. JE 4 p. 6. Petitioner states 
that the third sentence, “Then approximately a year ago she developed pain associated with 
repetitive labor in that shoulder,” cannot be reconciled with Dr. Aviles’ opinion finding that no 
trauma associated with Petitioner’s injury occurred at work. It is unclear whether Dr. Aviles was 
stating his own opinion as to the cause of her injury or whether he was simply repeating what 
Petitioner had told him was the cause. However, in the context of the first sentence, where Dr. 
Aviles is repeating Petitioner’s medical history as she told it to him, it would be reasonable for the 
Commissioner to believe the third sentence was repeating Petitioner’s statements as well rather 
than stating Dr. Avile’s own causation opinion, especially in light of Dr. Aviles’ other statements 
as to causation.

Petitioner also points to the letter from Dr. Stoken, dated August 3, 2018, as evidence that 
Dr. Stoken found Petitioner’s injury was caused by her work at TPI. The letter states: “It is my 
understanding that [Petitioner] had fallen sometime previous to December 15, 2015 but did not 
experience any significant problems with her left shoulder until December 15, 2017, at which time 
she noticed that her repetitive pushing and pulling at TPI involving the use of her left shoulder was 
causing pain.” Ex. CL 9 p. 3. This statement suffers from the same problem as Dr. Aviles’ 
statement in the medical record. It is unclear whether Dr. Stoken is stating her own opinion as to 
causation or repeating Petitioner’s opinion. If the Court was weighing the evidence itself, 
Petitioner might be able to successfully use these types of statements, but the Court is limited to 
reviewing factual findings of the Commissioner under the substantial evidence standard. Because 
the statement could go either way, the Commissioner was reasonable in interpreting it as repeating 
Petitioner’s opinion as to causation. 
 
Petitioner cites several other statements by Dr. Stoken as evidence that she found 
Petitioner’s injury to be caused by her work activities, but these statements do not state an opinion 
on that question. These statements state that Petitioner’s injury is causally related to her 
impairment, but do not state her injury is causally related to her work. 
 
The Commissioner found that Petitioner’s testimony that her work was repetitive was 
summary in nature and therefore did not support a finding that her injury was caused by her work 
activities. Arb. Dec. p. 11. Petitioner argues that the Commissioner’s finding is contrary to the 
purpose of the Iowa workers’ compensation law and the Iowa Supreme Court’s repeated statement 
that the statute should be interpreted “broadly and liberally in keeping with the humanitarian 
objective of the statute.” Holstein Elec. v. Breyfogle, 756 N.W.2d 812, 816 (Iowa 2008). However, 
liberal construction of the statute cannot excuse a claimant’s failure to present specific evidence

14 
 
proving causation. While Petitioner’s lay testimony may or may not have been enough for the 
Commissioner to make a finding of causation, in the absence of any medical opinion finding 
causation, the Commissioner had substantial evidence to find that Petitioner’s injury was not 
caused by her work with TPI. 
The Commissioner’s application of law to facts was not irrational, illogical, or wholly 
unjustifiable under § 17A(10)(m). Likewise, his decision was not the product of reasoning that is 
so illogical as to render it wholly irrational under § 17A.19(10)(i) and was not otherwise 
unreasonable, arbitrary, capricious, or an abuse of discretion under § 17A.19(10)(n). The 
Commissioner relied on the absence of any medical opinion stating that Petitioner’s injury was 
caused by her work at TPI to find that she had failed to meet her burden to prove causation by a 
preponderance of the evidence. He considered the entire record and logically reached his 
conclusion. He also emphasized the importance of Dr. Sullivan’s opinion on causation due to the 
doctor’s role as the orthopedic surgeon and shoulder specialist who performed the operation on 
Petitioner’s shoulder. The Commissioner’s analysis is not subject to reversal under §§ 
17A.19(10)(i), (m), or (n). 
V. CONCLUSIONS 
For the reasons set forth above, the Court concludes that there is substantial evidence in 
the record to support the Commissioner’s determination that Petitioner failed to meet her burden 
to prove that her work activities at TPI were the proximate cause of her injury to her left shoulder. 
The Commissioner’s determination was not based upon an irrational, illogical, or wholly 
unjustifiable application of law to fact, was not the product of reasoning that is so illogical as to 
render it wholly irrational, and was not otherwise unreasonable, arbitrary, capricious, or an abuse

of discretion. Because the Commissioner’s causation finding was proper, the Court does not need 
to consider the other issues Petitioner alleges because those issues remain moot. 
IT IS THEREFORE THE ORDER OF THIS COURT that the Iowa Workers’ 
Compensation Commissioner’s decision of February 7, 2020 is AFFIRMED. 
Costs are taxed to Petitioner.
