The defendants contend the different impairment ratings from different doctors in 
the current case support concluding Wiese cannot meet his burden of proof as the 
presiding deputy concluded in Brown, at *6. One problem with this argument is that it 
asks the undersigned to conclude a section of the Guides expressly for use in 
evaluating lower extremity impairment applies in a case in which upper extremity 
impairment is at issue. The defendants do not cite to a portion of Chapter 16, which 
governs upper extremity impairment, that articulates a similar principle, and the 
undersigned was unable to find one. 
Even if such an authority existed, the facts in Brown are distinguishable from 
those in the current case. There is no indication in the Brown decision that any of the 
medical providers or experts used a goniometer. See 2023 WL 3260003. Here, such 
evidence is in the record. Dr. Kirkland specifically documents that he used a goniometer 
when measuring Wiese’s range of motion. (Ex. 1, p. 11) But there is an insufficient basis 
in the record from which to conclude Dr. Khoriaty or Dr. Miller used a goniometer to 
measure Wiese’s shoulder motion. The Guides expressly direct an evaluating physician 
to use a goniometer when measuring range of motion. Thus, the weight of the evidence 
establishes Dr. Kirkland followed the Guides when measuring Wiese’s range of motion. 
Moreover, section 16.7b, Arthroplasty, of the Guides states, “In the presence of 
decreased motion, motion impairments are derived separately (Section 16.4) and 
combined with the arthroplasty impairment (Combined Values Chart, p. 604).” Guides, 
§ 16.7b, p. 505. As discussed above, neither Dr. Khoriaty nor Dr. Miller measured 
Wiese’s shoulder adduction, as section 16.4i of the Guides directs. Id. at pp. 474–76. 
According to the Guides, adduction constitutes 10 percent of shoulder function. Id. at 
pp. 474, 476. Failing to measure or consider a measurement of shoulder motion that 
makes up 10 percent of shoulder function undermines the credibility of both doctors’ 
opinions on permanent impairment.  
The Guides expressly state that when there is decreased motion, the motion 
impairments are derived separately combined with the arthroplasty impairment using 
the Combined Values Chart. As found above, Dr. Kirkland used a goniometer, in 
accordance with the Guides, to measure Wiese’s motion in his injured shoulder. Based 
on those measurements, he found decreased motion. As the Guides instruct, Dr. 
Kirkland then added the decreased motion impairment to the arthroplasty impairment 
using the Combined Values Chart. See id. at § 16.7b, p. 505. Because Dr. Kirkland 
followed the directions in the Guides for evaluating permanent impairment following a 
shoulder arthroplasty, his opinion is found most persuasive and adopted.

C ON C LU SION S OF LAW. 
In 2017, the Iowa legislature amended the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Act. 
See 2017 Iowa Acts, ch. 23. The 2017 amendments took effect on July 1, 2017. Id. at § 
24(1); see also Iowa Code § 3.7(1). Because the injury at issue in this case occurred 
after July 1, 2017, the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Act, as amended in 2017, applies. 
See Stiles v. Annett Holdings, Inc., No. 5064673, 2020 WL 6037539 *2 (Iowa Work. 
Comp. Comm’r, Oct. 2, 2020) (App. Decision). 

WIESE V. LENNOX INDUSTRIES, INC. 
Page 9 
 
The “broad purpose of workers’ compensation” is “to award compensation (apart 
from medical benefits), not for the injury itself, but the disability produced by a physical 
injury.” Bell Bros. Heating and Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d 193, (Iowa 2010) 
(citing 4 Arthur Larson & Lex K. Larson, Larson’s Workers’ Compensation Law § 80.02, 
at 80–2 (2009)). Work injuries generally fall within one of two categories under the Iowa 
Workers’ Compensation Act. Those that are to body parts listed in the statutory 
scheduled are considered scheduled members and, as such, compensation is based 
only on the functional disability they cause. See Gilleland v. Armstrong Rubber Co., 524 
N.W.2d 404, 407 (Iowa 1994); see also Mortimer v. Fruehauf Corp., 502 N.W.2d 12, 15 
(Iowa 1993).  Injuries to body parts that are not listed in the statute are compensated 
based on industrial disability or lost earning capacity. Mannes v. Fleetguard, Inc., 770 
N.W.2d 826, 830 (Iowa 2009) (quoting Oscar Mayer Foods Corp. v. Tasler, 483 N.W.2d 
824, 830–31 (Iowa 1992)); see also Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 158 
(Iowa 1996).  
Under our statutory scheme, “a disparity exists between the compensation for 
scheduled and nonscheduled injuries.” Gilleland, 524 N.W.2d at 407. “[A] person may 
suffer a permanent total disability as a result of some scheduled injury. This may 
happen because of age, lack of training, or other condition peculiar to the person. Yet 
such an injury is arbitrarily compensable according to the schedule.” Mortimer, 502 
N.W.2d at 15 (emphasis in original). “Thus, claimants typically receive greater 
compensation for unscheduled whole body injuries than they would for scheduled 
member injuries.” Chavez v. MS Tech. LLC, 972 N.W.2d 662, 667 (Iowa 2022). 
Before the 2017 amendments, the shoulder was not a body part listed in the 
statutory schedule. See Iowa Code § 85.34(2) (2016). Because the shoulder was not 
included in the schedule, shoulder injuries were classified as to the whole body and 
their resultant disability was assessed by determining the employee’s lost earning 
capacity due to the injury. See Second Injury Fund of Iowa v. Nelson, 544 N.W.2d 258, 
270 (Iowa 1995); see also Prewitt v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co., 564 N.W.2d 852, 854 
(Iowa App. 1997). The legislature changed this in 2017 by adding the shoulder to the 
schedule, which means injuries to the shoulder such as Wiese’s are now considered 
scheduled member injuries and are compensated based on the functional disability they 
cause—lost earning capacity is no longer considered. See 2017 Iowa Acts ch. 23, § 7; 
see also Chavez, 972 N.W.2d at 667.  
In the current case, the parties agree that, at the time of hearing, Wiese was 
employed by Lennox and earning the same or more as he earned at the time of the 
injury. Consequently, the parties agree that Wiese’s entitlement to benefits is “based 
only upon the employee’s functional impairment resulting from the injury,” under Iowa 
Code section 85.34(2)(v). And in cases involving injuries on or after July 1, 2017, the 
Guides are more than a tool when determining functional impairment; they are 
dispositive under section 85.34(2)(x): 
[W]hen determining functional disability and not loss of earning capacity, 
the extent of loss or percentage of permanent impairment shall be 
determined solely by utilizing the guides to the evaluation of permanent 

WIESE V. LENNOX INDUSTRIES, INC. 
Page 10 
 
impairment, published by the American medical association, as adopted 
by the workers’ compensation commissioner by rule pursuant to chapter 
17A. Lay testimony or agency expertise shall not be utilized in determining 
loss or percentage of permanent impairment pursuant to paragraphs “a” 
through “u”, or paragraph “v” when determining functional disability and 
not loss of earning capacity. 
The legislature’s decision to codify this requirement to use only the Guides when 
determining permanent impairment for purposes of entitlement to workers’ 
compensation benefits effectively incorporates the Guides into the Iowa Workers’ 
Compensation Act. The Guides define “impairment” as “a loss, loss of use, or 
derangement of any body part, organ system, or organ function.” Guides, p. 2. 
According to the Guides, the evaluation of impairment considers both anatomic and 
functional loss. Id. at 4. The Guides further provide evaluations of permanent 
impairment in percentages or ratings “reflect the severity of the medical condition and 
the degree to which the impairment decreases an individual’s ability to perform common 
activities of daily living (ADL), excluding work.” Id. at 4 (italics in original; bold-face 
removed).

“Under Iowa’s workers’ compensation law, injured workers can ask for an 
updated review of their injuries—referred to as a “review-reopening proceeding”—to 
determine whether their condition has worsened and thus entitles them to additional 
payments.” Green v. N. Cent. Iowa Reg’l Solid Waste Auth., 989 N.W.2d 144, 145 (Iowa 
2023). In such a proceeding under Iowa Code section 10A.317(2), “inquiry shall be into 
whether or not the condition of the employee warrants an end to, diminishment of, or 
increase of compensation . . . agreed upon.” When an employee seeks an increase in 
compensation, the employee must show by a preponderance of the evidence that the 
work injury proximately caused a worsened condition at the time of hearing that was not 
taken into account in the original determination or settlement. See Kohlhaas v. Hog Slat, 
Inc., 777 N.W.2d 387, 391 (Iowa 2009) (citing Simonson v. Snap-On Tools Corp., 588 
N.W.2d 430, 434 (Iowa 1999) and Acuity Ins. v. Foreman, 684 N.W.2d 212, 217 (Iowa 
2004)); see also Green, 989 N.W.2d at 145.  
As found above, Dr. Kirkland’s opinions on permanent impairment are most 
persuasive in this case. The parties used only Dr. Kirkland’s opinion on permanent 
impairment to support the agreement for settlement, which the agency approved and is 
the backdrop against which Wiese’s condition at the time of hearing must be compared 
to determine if he is entitled to additional compensation. As discussed above, Dr. 
Kirkland’s post-settlement opinion on permanent impairment most closely follows the 
process in the Guides because he used a goniometer to measure each component of 
shoulder motion—including adduction, which neither Dr. Khoriaty nor Dr. Miller 
measured—and he used the Combined Values Chart to add together the 24 percent 
impairment for a shoulder arthroplasty with the range of motion impairment. Because 
Dr. Kirkland’s opinion on permanent impairment is most credible, it establishes a 
worsening in Wiese’s condition, and there is no indication in the record such a 

WIESE V. LENNOX INDUSTRIES, INC. 
Page 11 
 
worsening was considered at the time of the agreement for settlement approved by the 
agency, he has met his burden on review-reopening. 
The parties agreed to a 24 percent permanent impairment in the settlement. As 
found above, the weight of the evidence in this review-reopening proceeding shows 
Wiese has subsequently sustained a 38 percent impairment to the shoulder. Multiplying 
400 weeks by 38 percent equals 152 weeks. Wiese is entitled to 152 weeks of 
permanent partial disability benefits due to the worsening of his condition caused by the 
work injury to his left shoulder, subject to the stipulated credit.
