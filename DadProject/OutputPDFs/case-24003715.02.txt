This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A. The 
expedited procedures of rule 876 IAC 4.48, the “alternate medical care” rule, are 
invoked by claimant, Erin Bettazza.  
This alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on November 26, 2024. 
The proceedings were recorded digitally and constitute the official record of the hearing.  
By an order filed by the Workers’ Compensation Commissioner, this decision is 
designated final agency action. Any appeal would be by petition for judicial review under 
Iowa Code section 17A.15(1).  
The record in this case consists of Claimant’s Exhibit 1, Defendants’ Exhibits A-D 
and the testimony of claimant.  
ISSUE 
The issue presented for resolution in this case is whether claimant is entitled to 
alternate medical care consisting of authorization for claimant to be treated by a 
neurologist at the University of Iowa Hospitals and Clinics for issues with memory, 
speech and claimant’s problems with cognitive abilities. 
FINDING OF FACTS 
Defendants accept liability for injuries to claimant occurring on December 7, 
2023; January 11, 2024; February 16, 2024; and March 28, 2024.  
Defendants authorized care for client with Todd Caze, Ph.D., at the Caze 
Concussion Institute.  On July 29, 2024, Dr. Caze released claimant from care.  Dr.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3).  
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Section 85.27.  Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening October 16, 1975).       
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  Long v. 
Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122, 123 (Iowa 1995).  Determining what care is 
reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.  Id.  The employer’s obligation turns 
on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.  Id. at 124; Harned v. Farmland 
Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98, 101 (Iowa 1983) 
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.   Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).   
Defendants authorized a provider to treat claimant’s closed head injury.  That 
provider released claimant from care in July of 2024.  In September of 2024, claimant 
sustained another work-related injury when she was struck in the head and face by a 
student.  (Ex. B)  The insurer for the September of 2024 injury is not the insurer who is a 
defendant in these petitions for work injuries occurring between December 2023 
through March 2024.  Claimant initially requested, in her petitions for alternate medical 
care, to be seen by a neurologist for her continuing problems she has as a result of her

closed head injuries.  Defendants have indicated they are attempting to have claimant 
evaluated by a neurologist, Dr. Kohout.  Dr. Kohout will not schedule claimant until he 
has reviewed her records.   
Claimant testified she has had issues with memory, concentration, and that her 
vision occasionally blurs.  She said she has had these symptoms since the February 
and March of 2024 work injuries.  Medical records from September of 2024 indicate 
claimant had no neurological complaints or deficits and had no problems with vision or 
balance.  (Ex. B) 
I appreciate claimant’s concerns in this case regarding timely treatment.  
However, defendants are in the process of provisionally scheduling claimant for 
evaluation with a neurologist.  Defendants did provide care for claimant’s closed head 
injury with Dr. Caze.  Defendants have had problems coordinating care for claimant due 
to a September 2024 work injury involving blows to claimant’s head, that are not at 
issue in these petitions.  Claimant currently does not have an appointment or a referral 
to an appointment with the UIHC.  Given this record, claimant has failed to carry her 
burden of proof the care provided by defendants is unreasonable. 
ORDER 
Therefore, it is ordered that claimant’s petitions for alternate medical care are 
denied, at this time.
