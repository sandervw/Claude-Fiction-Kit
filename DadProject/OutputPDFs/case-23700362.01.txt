STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Zachary Kreider, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits against WR Hospitality, LLC a/k/a Minervas, employer, and First 
Dakota Indemnity Company, insurer, both as defendants.  This case was heard on April 
1, 2024, and considered fully submitted on April 30, 2024, upon the simultaneous filing 
of briefs.   
The record consists of Joint Exhibits 1-5; Claimant’s Exhibits 1-9; Defendants’ 
Exhibits A-O, along with the testimony of claimant, Elizabeth Goeden, and Matthew 
Sawyer.  
ISSUES 
1. 
Whether claimant sustained an injury arising out of and in the course of 
his employment on January 6, 2022; 
 
2.

disability, or healing period benefits from the date of injury to the present 
time; 
 
3.

STIPULATIONS 
 
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of those 
stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration decision and 
no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised or discussed 
in this decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations. 
The parties agree claimant was an employee at the time of the alleged injury. 
At all times material here to, claimant’s gross earnings were $1,211.54 per week. 
Claimant was married and entitled to two exemptions. Based on the foregoing, the 
weekly benefit rate is $782.39. 
Defendants waive all affirmative defenses. 
There are no credits in dispute. 
While entitlement is disputed, claimant wishes specific taxation of costs, and the 
itemized list of costs is stipulated as paid. 
FINDINGS OF FACTS 
Claimant Zachary Kreider was a 33-year-old person at the time of the hearing. At 
all relevant times hereto, he was married with two minor children and an adult male 
dependent. The parties stipulated he was entitled to two exemptions which is accepted 
herein.  
From July 31, 2021, to April 13, 2023, claimant was an employee of defendant 
employer. He was terminated from his employment, and after a period of time receiving 
unemployment, he was hired on June 23, 2023, as a life insurance agent. Prior to June 
23, 2023, he studied for and passed the licensure test to be able to sell health 
insurance. His current position requires travel, including out of state travel.  
Prior to working for defendant employer, claimant had a short stint working at 
Buffalo Wild Wings and prior to that was the food and beverage manager at Hard Rock 
Hotel & Casino where he had worked for 7 years. Both employment situations ended 
with claimant having a conflict with management and he either quit or was terminated.

His past medical history is significant for back pain. On July 21, 2021, claimant

He alleged that while he was moving a keg of beer from the floor to a dunnage rack that

leg, and soreness. (DE B:2) The date of the injury was identified as January 7, 2022.

On September 25, 2023, Dr. Bansal conducted an independent medical 
examination. (CE 3) At the visit, claimant continued to have low back pain that radiated 
down the back of his right leg. (CE 3:15) He reported leg weakness but no give way 
pain. (CE 3:15) He frequently alternated between sitting and standing about every hour

discrimination over the posterior leg, reduced range of motion on the right, a positive 
straight leg raise test on the right, normal strength and reflexes bilaterally. (CE 3:16-17) 
Dr. Bansal diagnosed claimant with an L4-L5 disc bulge and an L5-S1 broad-
based posterior disc protrusion with an annular fissure consistent with Dr. Johnson’s 
assessment. (CE 3:17) Dr. Bansal opined that claimant sustained acute disc bulges at 
L4-L5 and L5-S1 on January 6, 2022, when he was moving a keg of beer and placing it 
on the rack. (CE 3:18) Dr. Bansal wrote, “The combined motion of lifting and twisting is 
particularly detrimental to the lumbar spine. This action introduces a significant load 
across the vertebrae, stressing the intervertebral discs, facet joints, and the musculature 
supporting the spine.” (CE 3:18) 
Dr. Bansal opined that the MRI findings of disc desiccation and mild disc space 
narrowing at the L4-L5 and L5-S1 levels suggested that there were pre-existing 
degenerative changes in the claimant’s spine, and the act of lifting and twisting likely 
aggravated these conditions, leading to the observed broad-based posterior disc 
protrusion with an annular fissure at L5-S1. (CE 3:18)  
Dr. Bansal recommended surgical decompression and intermittent epidural 
injections as treatment. (CE 3:19)  
Dr. Bansal disagreed with the opinions of Dr. Martin, opining that Dr. Martin did 
not consider that a pre-existing condition could be lit up or aggravated by an injury. 
Given claimant’s age and the proximity of increased symptoms to the date of injury, an 
aggravation of a pre-existing condition was the more likely occurrence. (CE 3:19) 
Dr. Bansal assigned a 10 percent impairment to the body as a whole as 
claimant’s condition fit the DRE Lumbar Category III. For restrictions, Dr. Bansal 
advised that claimant should not lift greater than 25 pounds with no frequent bending or 
twisting and no sitting or standing longer than an hour at a time. (CE 3:20) 
Dr. Bansal did not find claimant to be at MMI. (CE 3:20)

To the extent that there is a debate about claimant’s credibility, I find claimant 
was a credible witness. There was some confusion about whether the incident 
happened on the 6th or 7th of January, but there is substantial evidence that the

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e). 
 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury actually occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 
employment. Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 150 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. 
Dial Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309, 311 (Iowa 1996). The words “arising out of” refer to the 
cause or source of the injury. The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 
circumstances of the injury. 2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124, 128 (Iowa 
1995).  An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists 
between the injury and the employment. Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. The injury must 
be a rational consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely 
incidental to the employment. Koehler Elec. v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1, 3 (Iowa 2000); 
Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it 

KREIDER V. WR HOSPITALITY, LLC a/k/a MINERVAS 
Page 12 
 
happens within a period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may 
be when performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties 
or doing an activity incidental to them. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d at 150. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
A possibility of causation is not sufficient; a probability is necessary. Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996) (citing Holmes v. Bruce 
Motor Freight, Inc., 215 N.W.2d 296, 297 (Iowa 1974)). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 
621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 
(Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
A personal injury contemplated by the workers’ compensation law means an 
injury, the impairment of health or a disease resulting from an injury which comes about, 
not through the natural building up and tearing down of the human body, but because of 
trauma.  The injury must be something that acts extraneously to the natural processes 
of nature and thereby impairs the health, interrupts or otherwise destroys or damages a 
part or all of the body.  Although many injuries have a traumatic onset, there is no 
requirement for a special incident or an unusual occurrence.  Injuries which result from 
cumulative trauma are compensable.  Increased disability from a prior injury, even if 
brought about by further work, does not constitute a new injury, however.  St. Luke’s 
Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 650-51 (Iowa 2000); Ellingson v. Fleetguard, Inc., 599 
N.W.2d 440, 444 (Iowa 1999); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 
845, 850-51 (Iowa 1995); McKeever Custom Cabinets v. Smith, 379 N.W.2d 368, 373-
74 (Iowa 1985).  An occupational disease covered by chapter 85A is specifically 
excluded from the definition of personal injury.  Iowa Code section 85.61(4)(b); Iowa 
Code section 85A.8; Iowa Code section 85A.14.

Evidence in administrative proceedings is governed by Iowa Code section 
17A.14.  The agency’s experience, technical competence, and specialized knowledge 
may be utilized in the evaluation of evidence.  The rules of evidence followed in the 
courts are not controlling.  Findings are to be based upon the kind of evidence on which 
reasonably prudent persons customarily rely in the conduct of serious affairs.  Health 
care is a serious affair.

Prudent persons customarily rely upon their physician’s recommendation for 
medical care without expressly asking the physician if that care is reasonable.  Proof of 
reasonableness and necessity of the treatment can be based on the injured person’s 
testimony.  Sister M. Benedict v. St. Mary’s Corp., 255 Iowa 847, 855-56, 124 N.W.2d 
548, 553 (1963).  
It is said that “actions speak louder than words.”  When a licensed physician 
prescribes and actually provides a course of treatment, doing so manifests the 
physician’s opinion that the treatment being provided is reasonable.  A physician 
practices medicine under standards of professional competence and ethics.  Knowingly 
providing unreasonable care would likely violate those standards.  Actually providing 
care is a nonverbal manifestation that the physician considers the care actually provided 
to be reasonable.  A verbal expression of that professional opinion is not legally 
mandated in a workers' compensation proceeding to support a finding that the care 
provided was reasonable.  The success, or lack thereof, of the care provided is 
evidence that can be considered when deciding the issue of reasonableness of the 
care.  A treating physician’s conduct in actually providing care is a manifestation of the 
physician’s opinion that the care provided is reasonable and creates an inference that 
can support a finding of reasonableness.  Jones v. United Gypsum, File 1254118 (App. 
May 2002); Kleinman v. BMS Contract Services, Ltd., File No. 1019099 (App. 
September 1995); McClellon v. Iowa Southern Utilities, File No. 894090 (App. January 
1992).  This inference also applies to the reasonableness of the fees actually charged 
for that treatment.   
While Dr. Shumaker refused to provide an opinion, his medical records referred 
to claimant’s work injury, and the treatment Dr. Shumaker recommended was to treat 
back symptoms from the work injury. Dr. Shumaker knew that the case involved 
workers’ compensation, requested approval from workers’ compensation for treatment, 
and ultimately bowed out of treating claimant when workers’ compensation did not 
approve the treatment. This is a nonverbal manifestation of care he deemed was 
reasonable to treat the injury.  While it is not an opinion to a reasonable degree of 
medical certainty that claimant’s injury was work related, this is supportive evidence.  
Based on Dr. Bansal’s opinions, which are most consistent with claimant’s 
credible complaints as well as the documentation of the medical records of Dr. Reeder,

when he lifted a keg on January 6, 2022. This injury has not resolved per Dr. Martin, 
defendants’ own expert, as well as Dr. Bansal.  
Iowa Code section 85.34(1) provides that healing period benefits are payable to 
an injured employee who has suffered permanent partial disability until (1) the employee 
has returned to work; (2) it is medically indicated that significant improvement from the 
injury is not anticipated; or (3) the employee is medically capable of returning to 
employment substantially similar to the employment in which the employee was 
engaged at the time of injury, whichever occurs first.  The healing period can be 
considered the period during which there is a reasonable expectation of improvement of 
the disabling condition.  See Armstrong Tire & Rubber Co. v. Kubli, 312 N.W.2d 60, 65

Page 15 
 
(Iowa Ct. App. 1981).  Healing period benefits can be interrupted or intermittent.  Teel v. 
McCord, 394 N.W.2d 405, 406 (Iowa 1986). 
Healing period compensation describes temporary workers’ compensation 
weekly benefits that precede an allowance of permanent partial disability benefits.  
Ellingson v. Fleetguard, Inc., 599 N.W.2d 440, 446 (Iowa 1999).  Healing period benefits 
are payable to an injured worker who has suffered permanent partial disability until the 
first to occur of three events.  Iowa Code § 85.34(1) (2015).  These are:  (1) the worker 
has returned to work; (2) the worker medically is capable of returning to substantially 
similar employment; or (3) the worker has achieved maximum medical recovery.  (Id.)  
Maximum medical recovery is achieved when healing is complete, and the extent of 
permanent disability can be determined.  Armstrong Tire & Rubber Co. v. Kubli, 312 
N.W.2d 60, 65 (Iowa Ct. App. 1981).  Neither maintenance medical care nor an 
employee's continuing to have pain or other symptoms necessarily prolongs the healing 
period.  (Id.)
