STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
On November 18, 2020, claimant filed a petition for alternate medical care 
pursuant to Iowa Code 85.27(4) and 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.48.  The 
defendant filed an answer accepting liability for the left arm injury that occurred on 
May 2, 2019.   
The undersigned presided over the hearing held via telephone and recorded 
digitally on December 3, 2020.  That recording constitutes the official record of the 
proceeding under 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.48(12).  Claimant participated 
personally, and through her attorney, Connor Mulholland.  The defendants participated 
through their attorney, Thomas Wolle.  The evidentiary record consists of eight pages of 
exhibits from the claimant, labeled Exhibits 1 through 3, and three pages of exhibits 
from the defendant, labeled Exhibit A.  
On February 16, 2015, the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commissioner issued 
an order delegating authority to deputy workers’ compensation commissioners, such as 
the undersigned, to issue final agency decisions on applications for alternate care.  
Consequently, this decision constitutes final agency action, and there is no appeal to 
the commissioner.  Judicial review in a district court pursuant to Iowa Code 17A is the 
avenue for an appeal. 
ISSUE 
The issue under consideration is whether claimant is entitled to alternate care 
under Iowa Code 85.27(4) in the form of authorization of a surgery recommended by 
Peter Pardubsky, M.D.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
Claimant, Allison Whitenack, sustained a work injury to her left arm on November 
May 2, 2019.  The work incident arose out of, and in the course of her employment with 
Mercy Medical Center (hereinafter “Mercy”).  Defendant accepted liability for the May 2, 
2019, left arm injury in their answer, and verbally at hearing.   
The claimant worked at Mercy from August 20, 2018, through July 30, 2019.  
(Testimony).  Ms. Whitenack worked “the line” making sure that patients received 
correct food items, and maintained appropriate dietary restrictions.  (Testimony).  She 
also helped in the dish washing room, and worked as a transporter of food trays to 
patient rooms.  (Testimony).  Ms. Whitenack injured her left arm on May 2, 2019, while 
working in the dish washing room, and reported the injury to Mercy.  (Testimony).  She 
sought care for her left arm, and the employer directed her care.  (Testimony).  She is 
not working at this time.  (Testimony).   
Counsel for the defendant noted that Ms. Whitenack undertook a lengthy course 
of treatment, including physical therapy, over the year and a half between her injury, 
and a November 4, 2020, visit with Peter Pardubsky, M.D.  (Claimant’s Exhibit 1).  Ms. 
Whitenack’s visit with Dr. Pardubsky on November 4, 2020, concerned a repeat 
evaluation of her left elbow after a Tenex procedure to treat left lateral epicondylitis in 
June of 2020.  (Cl. Ex. 1).  Ms. Whitenack complained of ongoing pain in her left elbow 
and pain in her shoulder resulting in significant pain and weight loss.  (Cl. Ex. 1).  Ms. 
Whitenack continued to have burning pain in the lateral aspect of her left elbow.  (Cl. 
Ex. 1).  Following the Tenex procedure in June of 2020, Ms. Whitenack reported a one-
week reduction in symptoms.  (Cl. Ex. 1).  Dr. Pardubsky noted normal electrodiagnostic 
studies in November of 2019.  (Cl. Ex. 1).  An MRI reviewed in February of 2020 
showed significant lateral epicondylitis without ligamentous or intra-articular pathology.  
(Cl. Ex. 1).  Dr. Pardubsky indicated that Ms. Whitenack failed an extensive course of 
nonoperative intervention, including therapy, activity modification, and medications.  (Cl. 
Ex. 1).  He also noted that the claimant did not improve following the Tenex procedure.  
(Cl. Ex. 1).  He diagnosed Ms. Whitenack with lateral epicondylitis of the left elbow, and 
recommended a left lateral epicondylar debridement and tendon repair.  (Cl. Ex. 1).   
A nurse case manager note from Theresa VanMeighem, R.N., B.S.N., regarding 
the November 4, 2020, visit to Dr. Pardubsky, was also included in claimant’s exhibit.  
(Cl. Ex. 2).  Ms. VanMeighem noted that Ms. Whitenack’s pain in her left elbow 
increased.  (Cl. Ex. 2).  Further, she noted that Ms. Whitenack was unable to straighten 
her arm.  (Cl. Ex. 2).  Finally, she indicated Dr. Pardubsky’s recommendation of surgery.  
(Cl. Ex. 2).   
On November 17, 2020, counsel for the claimant e-mailed counsel for defendant 
indicating the claimant’s dissatisfaction that the defendant had not authorized Dr. 
Pardubsky’s surgical recommendation.  (Cl. Ex. 3).  Counsel for the defendant

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Iowa Code 85.27(4) provides, in relevant part: 
For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish reasonable 
services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has the right to 
choose the care. . . .  The treatment must be offered promptly and be 
reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the 
employee.  If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the care 
offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such 
dissatisfaction to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the 
employer and the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited 
to treat the injury.  If the employer and employee cannot agree on such 
alternate care, the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable 
proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
Iowa Code 85.27(4). See Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 433 (Iowa 
1997).   
“Iowa Code section 85.27(4) affords an employer who does not contest the 
compensability of a workplace injury a qualified statutory right to control the medical 
care provided to an injured employee.”  Ramirez-Trujillo v. Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 
N.W.2d 759, 769 (Iowa 2016) (citing R.R. Donnelly & Sons v. Barnett, 670 N.W.2d 190, 
195, 197 (Iowa 2003)).  “In enacting the right-to-choose provision in section 85.27(4), 
our legislature sought to balance the interests of injured employees against the 
competing interests of their employers.”  Ramirez, 878 N.W.2d at 770-71 (citing Bell 
Bros., 779 N.W.2d at 202, 207; IBP, Inc. v. Harker, 633 N.W.2d 322, 326-27 (Iowa 
2001)).   
Under the law, the employer must furnish “reasonable medical services and 
supplies and reasonable and necessary appliances to treat an injured employee.”  
Stone Container Corp. v. Castle, 657 N.W.2d 485, 490 (Iowa 2003) (emphasis in 
original)).  Such employer-provided care “must be offered promptly and be reasonably 
suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the employee.”  Iowa Code 
85.27(4).

An injured employee dissatisfied with the employer-furnished care (or lack 
thereof) may share the employee’s discontent with the employer and if the parties 
cannot reach an agreement on alternate care, “the commissioner may, upon application 
and reasonable proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and order other care.”  Id.  
“Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.”  Long v. 
Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122, 123 (Iowa 1995); Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co., 562 
N.W.2d at 436.  As the party seeking relief in the form of alternate care, the employee 
bears the burden of proving that the authorized care is unreasonable.  Roberts Dairy 
Co., 528 N.W.2d at 124; Bell Bros., 779 N.W.2d at 209; Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co., 562 
N.W.2d at 436.  Because “the employer’s obligation under the statute turns on the 
question of reasonable necessity, not desirability,” an injured employee’s dissatisfaction 
with employer-provided care, standing alone, is not enough to find such care 
unreasonable.  Id.   
The claimant argued at hearing that not authorizing the surgery based upon 
treating doctor, Dr. Pardubsky’s recommendation, is unreasonable.  The claimant 
further argued that moving towards a second opinion is akin to an independent medical 
examination (IME) pursuant to Iowa Code 85.39.   
The defendant argued that the claimant treated for her left arm condition since 
May of 2019, and that, upon learning of the surgical recommendation by Dr. Pardubsky, 
promptly arranged for a second opinion with an orthopedic surgeon at the University of 
Iowa.  The defendant further argued that this was reasonable considering the 
circumstances, and cited to Saine v. CRST, File No. 5012500 (Alt. Med. Care, Nov. 
2005), in support of their position. 
The claimant expressed dissatisfaction with a lack of authorization of the 
recommended surgery on November 17, 2020.  This is 13 days after Dr. Pardubsky’s 
examination.  The same day, the defendant replied indicating that they would arrange a 
second opinion with Dr. Buckwalter, an orthopedic surgeon.  The defendant also 
indicated that they had yet to receive a copy of Dr. Pardubsky’s clinical notes.  
Arguably, the defendant was on notice based upon the nurse case manager’s notes.  
However, this was still only 13 days after the examination.  Considering the lengthy 
course of conservative care, and the evidence indicating this is the first mention or 
recommendation of surgery, it is reasonable that the defendant would obtain a second 
opinion.  In this case, the defendant promptly scheduled an appointment for 
December 8, 2020, with an orthopedic surgeon, Dr. Buckwalter, at the University of 
Iowa.  This appointment is set to occur just over one month from the initial 
recommendation for surgery.   
The claimant is required to show that the authorized care is unreasonable.  In 
this case, a second opinion, from an orthopedic surgeon, in such close proximity to the 
recommendation for surgery is reasonable.  Therefore, the claimant failed to sustain her

claimant’s petition is denied.
