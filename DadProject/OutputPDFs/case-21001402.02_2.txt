On June 26, 2024, the undersigned issued an arbitration decision. On February 
7, 2025, the Commissioner issued an appeal decision, which affirmed the arbitration 
decision in its entirety. It has since come to my attention that there is a scrivener’s error 
in the findings of fact, which is later repeated in the order. In the findings of fact, the 
undersigned calculated the average weekly wage to be $2,183.49, making claimant’s 
weekly benefit rate $1,329.59. However, using the rate book effective from July 1, 2020 
to June 30, 2021, the rate for claimant’s status as married with 2 exemptions should be 
$1,329.08. 
 
 
The phrase, “nunc pro tunc” means “now for then.” See e.g. Black’s Law 
Dictionary, pg. 1218 (Rev. 4th Ed. 1968). The definition in Black’s Law Dictionary further 
provides: “[a] phrase applied to acts allowed to be done after the time when they should 
be done, with a retroactive effect, i.e., with the same effect as if regularly done.” Id. A 
nunc pro tunc order “is not for the purpose of correcting judicial thinking, judicial 
conclusion, or a mistake of law.” Headley v. Headley, 172 N.W.2d 104, 108 (Iowa 
1969). The nunc pro tunc order can be employed to correct obvious errors or to make 
an order conform to the judge’s original intent. Graber v. District Court for Washington 
City, 410 N.W.2d 224, 229 (Iowa 1987); Brinson v. Spee Dee Delivery Service, No 8-
754/06-2074 (Iowa App. 2008).    
 
 
THEREFORE, IT IS ORDERED, a nunc pro tunc order is used to modify the 
previously filed arbitration decision as follows: 
 
1. On page 16, the text in the last line of the table containing the rate calculation 
and the following sentence are changed as follows:
