STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Daniel Barnes, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits against XPO Logistics, Inc., employer, and Indemnity Ins. Co. of 
North America, insurance carrier, both as defendants for a disputed work injury alleged 
to have occurred on March 21, 2023. This case was heard on July 9, 2024, and 
considered fully submitted on July 30, 2024, upon the simultaneous filing of briefs.   
The record consists of Joint Exhibit 1, Claimant’s Exhibits 1-4, and Defendants’ 
Exhibits A-F, along with the testimony of claimant.  
ISSUES 
1. Whether claimant sustained an injury arising out of and in the course of 
employment on March 21, 2023. 
2. Whether claimant is entitled to permanent disability benefits arising out of the 
aforementioned alleged injury. 
3. Whether claimant is entitled to reimbursement of medical expenses itemized 
in Claimant’s Exhibit 4. 
4. If claimant sustained a new injury, whether defendants are entitled to a credit 
from the prior commutation of March 3, 2023; 
5. Assessment of costs.

STIPULATIONS 
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised 
or discussed in this decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations. 
The parties agree claimant was an employee of defendant employer on the 
alleged date of injury of March 21, 2023. 
While the parties dispute whether there is a permanent disability arising out of a 
work-related injury, they agree that the alleged injury is industrial in nature and that the 
commencement date for permanent partial disability benefits, if any are awarded, is 
April 7, 2023. 
At the time of the injury the claimant’s gross earnings were $1,474.29 per week. 
He was single and entitled to one exemption. Based on the foregoing the weekly benefit 
rate is $872.88. The commencement date of PPD benefits is April 7, 2023.    
On or about March 3, 2023, the parties agreed to a full commutation of benefits 
to be paid for a work injury dated December 7, 2021. The claimant was paid $43,702.50 
in permanent partial disability benefits.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury actually occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 
employment. Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 150 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. 
Dial Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309, 311 (Iowa 1996). The words “arising out of” refer to the 
cause or source of the injury. The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 
circumstances of the injury. 2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124, 128 (Iowa 
1995).  An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists 
between the injury and the employment. Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. The injury must 
be a rational consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely 
incidental to the employment. Koehler Elec. v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1, 3 (Iowa 2000); 
Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it 
happens within a period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may 
be when performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties 
or doing an activity incidental to them. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d at 150. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa App. 1997); 
Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 
621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 
(Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa App. 1994).

A personal injury contemplated by the workers’ compensation law means an 
injury, the impairment of health or a disease resulting from an injury which comes about, 
not through the natural building up and tearing down of the human body, but because of 
trauma.  The injury must be something that acts extraneously to the natural processes 
of nature and thereby impairs the health, interrupts or otherwise destroys or damages a 
part or all of the body.  Although many injuries have a traumatic onset, there is no 
requirement for a special incident or an unusual occurrence.  Injuries which result from 
cumulative trauma are compensable.  Increased disability from a prior injury, even if 
brought about by further work, does not constitute a new injury, however.  St. Luke’s 
Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 650-51 (Iowa 2000); Ellingson v. Fleetguard, Inc., 599 
N.W.2d 440, 444 (Iowa 1999); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 
845, 850-51 (Iowa 1995); McKeever Custom Cabinets v. Smith, 379 N.W.2d 368, 373-
74 (Iowa 1985).  An occupational disease covered by chapter 85A is specifically 
excluded from the definition of personal injury.  Iowa Code section 85.61(4)(b); Iowa 
Code section 85A.8; Iowa Code section 85A.14. 
When the injury develops gradually over time, the cumulative injury rule applies.  
The date of injury for cumulative injury purposes is the date on which the disability 
manifests.  Manifestation is best characterized as that date on which both the fact of 
injury and the causal relationship of the injury to the claimant’s employment would be 
plainly apparent to a reasonable person.  The date of manifestation inherently is a fact 
based determination.  The fact-finder is entitled to substantial latitude in making this 
determination and may consider a variety of factors, none of which is necessarily 
dispositive in establishing a manifestation date.  Among others, the factors may include 
missing work when the condition prevents performing the job, or receiving significant 
medical care for the condition.  For time limitation purposes, the discovery rule then 
becomes pertinent so the statute of limitations does not begin to run until the employee, 
as a reasonable person, knows or should know, that the cumulative injury condition is 
serious enough to have a permanent, adverse impact on his or her employment.  
Herrera v. IBP, Inc., 633 N.W.2d 284, 287-88 (Iowa 2001); Oscar Mayer Foods Corp. v. 
Tasler, 483 N.W.2d 824, 829-30 (Iowa 1992); McKeever Custom Cabinets v. Smith, 379 
N.W.2d 368, 374 (Iowa 1985). 
Claimant argues that he has sustained a new work injury on March 21, 2023, 
which exacerbated or aggravated a pre-existing condition. Defendants assert claimant 
has suffered no new injury and that his condition is unchanged from the previous injury 
or that his condition is merely a worsening of the existing condition.  
In support of his claim, claimant relies upon the opinion of his long-time surgeon, 
Dr. Bhangoo, who opined that claimant sustained a new 6 percent impairment due to 
either a traumatic work-related incident or a natural progression of the previous fusion 
which was also work-related.  
Defendants argue that the opinion of Dr. Bhangoo is too vague upon which to 
rely as he did not author the opinion letter himself nor was it clear whether he was 
referencing the previous fusion of 2007 or 2022. In regard to the latter, the evidence 
supports that Dr. Bhangoo was referring to the 2022 surgery. Per the settlement

documents, the claimant underwent a second level cervical fusion at C5-6 which 
entailed removing claimant’s titanium plate at the C6-7 level and putting in a new plate 
at the C5-6 making it a two-level fusion. (DE A:10) The listhesis observed by Dr. 
Bhangoo was at C7 to T1, adjacent to the two-level fusion.  
Defendants argue that claimant’s condition is unchanged. Dr. Abernathey did not 
find any significant anatomic aggravation or exacerbation of his prior cervical condition.  
They further point to claimant’s testimony that his symptoms are largely the same 
including pain radiating from the neck down to his arms that is dull and achy with 
tingling in his digits.  
The biggest difference in claimant’s situation is not in the pain descriptors, but his 
own actions. Claimant worked 22 years for defendant employer and returned to work 
after the 2022 surgery. However, following the March 2023 injury and subsequent 
surgical repair, claimant left defendant employer to take a job with Eric Arthur Farms 
where he works a less physically arduous job. This change in behavior is consistent 
with claimant’s argument that he sustained a new injury that aggravated an underlying 
condition.  
While the radiologist noted that there were “early degenerative spondylotic disc 
space changes and moderate to advanced multilevel facet arthropathy in the cervical 
spine, not appreciably changed since 11/8/2022,” Dr. Bhangoo interpreted the radiology 
report to suggest that claimant may have developed some adjacent level stenosis 
caudal to his previous fusion. (JE 1:1, 2) This clinical note was re-affirmed in Dr. 
Bhangoo’s opinion letter signed off on February 29, 2024.  
That Dr. Bhangoo’s opinion is less detailed than that of Dr. Rondinelli’s does not 
mean that it is without merit. Dr. Bhangoo is a treating physician making clinical notes 
and responding to an attorney’s inquiry while Dr. Rondinelli was retained as an 
independent examiner.  
Dr. Bhangoo’s opinion holds greater weight here. He was claimant’s treating 
surgeon who operated on claimant for the C5-C6 injury in 2022. He followed claimant’s 
recovery from the 2022 surgery and then saw claimant again for his pain and discomfort 
in 2023. This long-term treatment of claimant gives Dr. Bhangoo greater insight into 
claimant’s condition than a medical doctor who saw claimant a single instance. Further, 
Dr. Bhangoo’s clinical interpretation matched the consistent testimony of claimant as 
well as the diagnostic images. As claimant notes in the brief, the most recent injury of 
claimant was at C5-C6 while the post March 2023 injury is at C7-T1. The greater weight 
of the evidence supports a finding that claimant sustained an aggravation of a 
preexisting condition on March 21, 2023, and that as a result, suffered an additional 
permanent disability of 6 percent.  
Defendants claim that they are entitled to a credit based on section 85.34. Iowa 
Workers’ Compensation law has been interpreted to recognize a distinction between 
preexisting conditions and preexisting disability. Floyd v. Quaker Oats, 646 N.W.2d 105, 
110 (Iowa 2002)

Full compensation is allowed for the result of workplace activities aggravating a 
preexisting condition. Rose v. John Deere Ottumwa Works, 247 Iowa 900, 76 
N.W.2d 756, 760–61 (Iowa 1956).  In other words, it must be shown that a particular 
percentage of permanent disability would have resulted from the prior event acting 
alone. Floyd v. Quaker Oats, 646 N.W.2d 105, 110 (Iowa 2002) 
The Iowa Supreme Court has adopted the full-responsibility rule.  Under that rule, 
where there are successive work-related injuries, the employer liable for the current 
injury also is liable for the preexisting disability caused by any earlier work-related injury 
if the former disability when combined with the disability caused by the later injury 
produces a greater overall industrial disability. Floyd v. Quaker Oats, 646 N.W.2d 105, 
110 (Iowa 2002); Venegas v. IBP, Inc., 638 N.W.2d 699, 701 (Iowa 2002); Second 
Injury Fund of Iowa v. Nelson, 544 N.W.2d 258, 265 (Iowa 1995); Celotex Corp. v. 
Auten, 541 N.W.2d 252, 254 (Iowa 1995).  The full-responsibility rule does not apply in 
cases of successive, scheduled member injuries, however.   
In this case, Dr. Bhangoo assigned a 6 percent impairment based on the injury at 
C7-T1, which is a new and distinct injury alone. The 10 percent impairment of the 
commutation was for the C5-C6 injury. No credit for the 10 percent impairment should 
offset this new injury.   
Since claimant has an impairment to the body as a whole, an industrial disability 
has been sustained.  Industrial disability was defined in Diederich v. Tri-City Ry. Co. of 
Iowa, 258 N.W. 899, 902 (Iowa 1935) as follows:  "It is therefore plain that the 
Legislature intended the term 'disability' to mean 'industrial disability' or loss of earning 
capacity and not a mere 'functional disability' to be computed in terms of percentages of 
the total physical and mental ability of a normal man." 
Functional impairment is an element to be considered in determining industrial 
disability which is the reduction of earning capacity, but consideration must also be 
given to the injured employee's age, education, qualifications, experience, motivation, 
loss of earnings, severity and situs of the injury, work restrictions, inability to engage in 
employment for which the employee is fitted and the employer's offer of work or failure 
to so offer.  McSpadden v. Big Ben Coal Co., 288 N.W.2d 181, 192 (Iowa 1980); Olson 
v. Goodyear Service Stores, 255 Iowa 1112, 1120, 125 N.W.2d 251, 256 (1963); Barton 
v. Nevada Poultry Co., 253 Iowa 285, 110 N.W.2d 660, 663 (1961). 
Compensation for permanent partial disability shall begin when it is medically 
indicated that maximum medical improvement from the injury has been reached and 
that the extent of loss or percentage of permanent impairment can be determined by the 
use of the guides to the evaluation of permanent impairment, published by the American 
Medical Association, as adopted by the Commissioner. Iowa Code section 85.34(2)   
Claimant is an older person who intends to retire a little over two years from now. 
The majority of his working experience has been with defendant employer working a 
heavy manual labor position that required lifting objects of various sizes, shapes and 
weights up to 50 pounds frequently. His current wages are $25.00 per hour compared to 
the $33.00 per hour he earned working for defendant employer. He does not work

overtime, nor does he currently enjoy as good of an employee benefit package. He has 
some education and a valid pilot’s license for light sport aircraft flying. He is able to drive 
a forklift. There is no evidence he looked for other jobs with comparable pay or benefits, 
and it is not clear the reason that he took the position with Eric Arthur Farms other than 
it was less strenuous. He still drives a vehicle and does unloading and loading. Taking

found claimant has sustained a 15 percent industrial disability. This rating rests primarily 
on the low physical impairment assessed by Dr. Bhangoo as well as the imminent 
retirement date and the lack of evidence that other positions of higher pay and better 
benefits were available to him.  
Claimant also seeks an award of medical expenses.  
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance, and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Section 85.27.  Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening October 1975). 
Claimant had only a single visit with Dr. Bhangoo which included a plain x-ray. 
However, Intellivo is requesting repayment of $40,097.83 for medical expenses incurred 
for the December 7, 2021 injury. The parties had agreed that these bills should be paid 
as part of the February 27, 2023, stipulation and agreement concerning disability. (DE 
A:5) Claimant acknowledges that the agency does not have contempt powers. Further, 
the case at bar concerns an injury sustained on March 21, 2023, and not an injury 
sustained on December 7, 2021. The undersigned does not have subject matter 
jurisdiction over enforcement of the February 27, 2023, settlement agreement.  
Claimant is awarded the medical expenses for the April 6, 2023, medical visit 
with Dr. Bhangoo as well as the accompanying x-ray.  
Claimant also seeks reimbursement for costs which are awarded herein pursuant
