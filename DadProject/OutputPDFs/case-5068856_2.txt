This matter is before the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commission on remand 
from the Iowa District Court for Polk County. 
This matter was initially heard on July 12, 2019, by Deputy Workers’ 
Compensation Commissioner Toby Gordon.  An alternate medical care decision was 
issued on July 15, 2019.  That decision found claimant carried his burden of proof he 
was entitled to alternate medical care and defendants were ordered to pay for a 
functional capacity evaluation (FCE), as recommended by Stephen Meyer, M.D. 
A petition for judicial review was filed by defendants.  In the ruling on the petition 
for judicial review the district court found the FCE, recommended by Dr. Meyer, was 
appropriately categorized as medical treatment under Iowa Code section 85.27.  The 
district court also found the alternate medical care decision failed to apply the proper 
legal standard in granting claimant’s petition for alternate medical care.  The district 
court remanded this matter back to the Workers’ Compensation Commission to apply 
the proper legal standard applicable to alternate medical care cases. 
The undersigned was delegated to issue a remand decision in this matter as 
former Deputy Commissioner Gordon is no longer with the Division of Workers’ 
Compensation.   By an order filed by the workers’ compensation commissioner, this 
decision is designated final agency action.  Any appeal would be a petition for judicial 
review under Iowa Code section 17A.19.

ISSUE 
Is claimant entitled to requested alternate medical care, consisting of 
authorization of a FCE recommended by Dr. Meyer? 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
The findings of fact are detailed in the underlying alternate medical care decision 
and will not be repeated in full detail. 
In brief, claimant sustained an accepted injury to his back on June 1, 2018.  
Defendants authorized claimant to treat with Dr. Meyer, an orthopedic surgeon.  On 
December 5, 2018 claimant had an initial FCE.  That FCE found claimant gave valid 
effort and placed claimant in the heavy work category.  (Exhibit A, page 1) 
On December 6, 2018, the day after the FCE, claimant nearly fell at work and 
aggravated his original injury.  (Ex. B)  Dr. Meyer recommended claimant undergo facet 
injections.  (Ex. B) 
Defendants denied authorization for the injections.  Dr. Meyer again 
recommended the injections.  (Ex. B) 
On May 1, 2019, claimant returned to Dr. Meyer.  The record indicates claimant 
was “. . . quite concerned about his ability to fulfill his employment obligations.”  (Ex. 1)  
Following the exam, Dr. Meyer indicated: 
I do not think Austin is able to function under his current functional 
capacity, especially since his second injury, so I would recommend that he 
undergo a repeat FCE to see if things have changed since his injury in 
December, and then we will make further decision. 
(Ex. 1) 
Defendants did not authorize the FCE.  Instead they sent claimant to William 
Boulden, M.D. for an independent medical evaluation (IME).  Dr. Boulden opined the 
second FCE was not needed and claimant instead should undergo the “. . . German ball 
stabilization exercise program.”  (Ex. E, p. 8) 
There is little evidence in the record what the “German ball exercise program” is.   
Defendants denied authorization of the FCE recommended by Dr. Meyer based 
on Dr. Boulden’s IME opinions.  Defendants did authorize the German ball exercise 
program.  (Ex. F)

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa Rule of Appellate 
Procedure 6.14(6).   
Iowa Code section 85.27(4) provides, in relevant part:   
For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish 
reasonable services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has 
the right to choose the care. . . .  The treatment must be offered promptly 
and be reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience 
to the employee.  If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the 
care offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such 
dissatisfaction to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the 
employer and the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited 
to treat the injury.  If the employer and employee cannot agree on such 
alternate care, the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable 
proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and order other care.   
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  
In Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 433, 437 (Iowa 1997), the 
supreme court held that “when evidence is presented to the commissioner that the 
employer-authorized medical care has not been effective and that such care is ‘inferior 
or less extensive’ than other available care requested by the employee, . . . the 
commissioner is justified by section 85.27 to order the alternate care.”   
Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.  
Long, 528 N.W. 2d at 123. 
In this matter, defendants authorized Dr. Meyer to treat claimant. (Transcript pp. 
6 and 8)  Claimant had treated with Dr. Meyer at least since December of 2018.  (Ex. A 
and B)  The record indicates Dr. Meyer wanted the second FCE to determine claimant’s 
ability to continue to work.  (Ex. 1) 
Defendants ignored the recommendations of Dr. Meyer.  Defendants sent 
claimant to Dr. Boulden for an IME.  Dr. Boulden is not a treating physician.  (Ex. E, 
p. 6)  Dr. Boulden opined claimant did not require an FCE, but should undergo a 
“German ball stabilization exercise program.”  There is little evidence in the record what 
the “German ball stabilization exercise program” is.

Defendants chose Dr. Meyer to provide medical care for claimant.  Dr. Meyer 
recommended an FCE to determine if claimant could physically meet the demand levels 
of his job.  Defendants sought to override the medical recommendations of a provider 
they chose.  They sent claimant to Dr. Boulden.  Dr. Boulden is not a treating physician.  
Dr. Boulden recommended a “German ball stabilization exercise program.”  There is 
little evidence what this program is.  Dr. Meyer treated claimant for an extended period 
of time.  Dr. Boulden saw claimant once for an IME.  As a factual matter, Dr. Meyer has 
far greater experience with claimant’s medical presentation and more familiarity with 
claimant’s needs, as a patient, than does Dr. Boulden. 
Based on the totality of these facts, it is found the care offered by defendants is 
inferior to the care recommended by the treating physician, Dr. Meyer.  Given this 
record, claimant has sustained his burden of proof he is entitled to the requested 
alternate medical care. 
ORDER 
THEREFORE, IT IS ORDERED: 
The claimant's petition for alternate medical care is granted.  Defendants shall 
immediately authorize the FCE recommended by Dr. Meyer.
