STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Juan Lopez, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits against DPWN Holdings Inc., d/b/a Excel, Inc., employer, and 
New Hampshire Insurance Company, insurer.  
This case was heard on October 9, 2024, and considered fully submitted on 
November 11, 2024, upon the simultaneous filing of briefs.  
The record consists of Joint Exhibits 1-5; Claimant’s Exhibits 1-8; Defendants’ 
Exhibits A-L; along with the testimony of Claimant.  
ISSUES

occurred at work on April 20, 2023, resulting in a bar to his claim under Iowa 
Code section 85.23; 
 
2. Whether Claimant sustained a cumulative injury arising out of and in the 
course of his employment with Defendant Employer resulting in permanent 
disability, and if so, the date of that injury; 
 
3. If Claimant sustained a compensable injury arising out of and in the course of 
his employment with Defendant Employer, whether Claimant is entitled to

The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration

FINDINGS OF FACT 
Claimant, Juan Lopez, was a 55-year-old person at the time of the hearing.  His 
primary language is Spanish and he has approximately 11 years of education 
conducted in Mexico. He testified at hearing with the aid of a translator. His work history 
is in agricultural laboring and meat processing. His work as an agricultural laborer 
required bending and lifting and carrying fifty-pound bags of fruit. (Transcript, pp. 18-19) 
At the meat packing plants he would be required to lift and stack cow hides weighing 
from 30 to 90 pounds, pull meat with a hook, lift boxes of bacon and generally be on his 
feet all day. (Tr., p. 20) Claimant testified that he would not be able to do those jobs 
today after his alleged work injury.  
Since 2000, Claimant has worked as a forklift operator for Defendant Employer. 
(See Claimant’s Exhibit 3:48-49) In order to begin employment with Defendant 
Employer, Claimant underwent a work physical. He passed and worked as a forklift 
driver for over twenty years without accommodations or restrictions until March 22, 
2021.

December 22, 2021, claimant’s EMG of the right lower extremity showed no

Dr. Bansal wrote:  
Mr. Lopez’s cumulative work activities at DHL over his 20-year career as a 
forklift operator likely caused his right knee meniscal tear, and aggravated 
his patellofemoral arthritis. The biomechanical factors involved in his work 
created repetitive stress on his right knee joint, leading to these conditions 
over time. This conclusion is supported by both the specifics of his work 
duties and the current medical literature on occupational knee injuries.  
His job required him to mount and dismount a forklift approximately 100 
time per day, with the forklift step being approximately one foot high. This 
repetitive action places significant stress on the knee joint, particularly 
when stepping down from the elevated forklift position. The impact forces 
transmitted through the knee with each dismount can cause microtrauma 
to the meniscus and articular cartilage. Over time these cumulative forces 
likely led to degenerative changes in the meniscus, eventually resulting in 
a tear.  
(CE 2:39)  
With respect to Claimant’s back, Dr. Bansal stated:  
The development of his antalgic gait which was noted in the records 
suggests that his knee condition progressed to the point where it altered 
his normal biomechanics. This altered gait pattern likely created additional 
stress on both his injured knee and his lower back, aggravating his overall 
condition … 
The overall worsening of his back condition is evidenced by his decreased 
functional capacity. Prior to his knee injury, he could tolerate sitting or 
standing for 30 minutes to an hour. He now struggles with these activities 
for even 15 minutes. This marked decrease in positional tolerance suggests 
a global deterioration of his lumbopelvic function, likely due to the 
combined effects of his original lumbar pathology, the new sacroiliac joint 
dysfunction, and the ongoing stress from his altered gait mechanics. 
(CE 2:39-41)

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury actually occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 
employment. Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 150 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. 
Dial Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309, 311 (Iowa 1996). The words “arising out of” refer to the 
cause or source of the injury. The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 
circumstances of the injury. 2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124, 128 (Iowa 
1995).  An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists 
between the injury and the employment. Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. The injury must 
be a rational consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely 
incidental to the employment. Koehler Elec. v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1, 3 (Iowa 2000); 
Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it 
happens within a period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may 
be when performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties 
or doing an activity incidental to them. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d at 150. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 
621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 
(Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
Claimant has pled he had an onset of right knee pain in March and April of 2023 
that was different than the pain in his low back that he sustained on March 22, 2021. 
Claimant’s allegation is that this right knee pain resulted in an altered gait that caused 
increased low back pain. Defendants assert that Claimant did not give proper notice of 
the knee injury.

Iowa Code section 85.23 requires an employee to give notice of the occurrence 
of an injury to the employer within 90 days from the date of the occurrence, unless the 
employer has actual knowledge of the occurrence of the injury. 
The purpose of the 90-day notice or actual knowledge requirement is to give the 
employer an opportunity to timely investigate the facts surrounding the injury.  The 
actual knowledge alternative to notice is met when the employer, as a reasonably 
conscientious manager, is alerted to the possibility of a potential compensation claim 
through information which makes the employer aware that the injury occurred and that it 
may be work related.  Dillinger v. City of Sioux City, 368 N.W.2d 176, 180 (Iowa 1985); 
Robinson v. Department of Transp., 296 N.W.2d 809, 811 (Iowa 1980). 
Failure to give notice is an affirmative defense which the employer must prove by 
a preponderance of the evidence.  DeLong v. Iowa State Highway Commission, 229 
Iowa 700, 295 N.W. 91, 92 (1940). 
Defendants maintain that Claimant only relayed knee pain to his supervisors, not 
that the knee pain was work-related. Claimant argues that a reasonably conscientious 
manager would be alerted to the possibility of a potential claim through information 
which makes the employer aware the injury occurred and may be work-related. 
Unrebutted testimony from the Claimant is that he informed his two supervisors that he 
felt knee pain from climbing in and out of the forklift. They told him to wait and then told 
him to see Dr. Miller. Claimant did see Dr. Miller on April 20, 2023, and reported knee 
pain. (JE 1:6) Dr. Miller did not document that Claimant’s pain came from climbing in 
and out of the forklift.  However, Defendants’ counsel was aware of the knee pain by 
May 1, 2023. (DE B) In the email, the defendants’ counsel said “I haven’t seen any clinic 
notes from Dr. Miller yet and I don‘t know anything about treatment or evaluation of his 
knee.” (DE B) Based on the email exchanges, claimant’s testimony, and the April 20, 
2023, visit to Dr. Miller, it is found that a reasonably conscientious manager would have 
been alerted to the possibility of a potential claim related to Claimant’s knee and that 
Defendant Employer had actual knowledge of the work injury within 90 days of its 
occurrence, whether the occurrence was in March or April of 2023.  
The date of occurrence is defined as when the employee knew or should have 
known that the injury is work related.  Tweeten v. Tweeten, 999 N.W.2d 270 (Iowa 
2023) Claimant knew or should have known the injury to his right knee was work related 
in March 2023 when he informed his supervisors of his right knee pain. There is no 
specific March date pled. (See Tr., pp. 29-32; DE F:13, 14, 15) Based on the foregoing, 
it is determined that the date of occurrence is March 1, 2023.  
Claimant maintains that he sustained a new injury to his right knee in March 
2023, as well as sequelae in the back. While the experts disagree as to the causation of 
Claimant’s knee pain, it is generally accepted Claimant’s right knee pain was not part of 
the 2021 work injury claim, but rather a new symptom that came forth in 2023. Claimant 
relies on the expert testimony of Dr. Bansal who opined that Claimant aggravated a 
preexisting arthritic condition from climbing up and down the forklift at work. Dr. Hicks, 
Claimant’s primary care physician, also opined that Claimant’s knee pain was related to

the cumulative effect of his work. Dr. Miller opined that the knee pain was from 
osteoarthritis.  
Claimant’s injury is a chronic condition which did not appear to be triggered by a 
specific work injury. Dr. Rayburn’s opinion does not appear to reflect that injuries can be 
cumulative and/or an aggravation or lighting up of a preexisting condition. Dr. Kakade, a 
knee specialist, opined that the knee pain was both unrelated to the back but also said 
the referred pain stemmed from the back pain. In December 2022, before Claimant’s 
right knee pain came to a head, claimant saw CRNA Jacobs, a pain specialist, who 
noted that Claimant was having symptoms of SI joint dysfunction, most likely from an 
altered gait. CRNA Jacobs was concerned that “the [laborious] nature of his work could 
be hindering his progress if he is repeatedly injuring structures such as the sacroiliac 
joint ligamentous complex or incurring repeat annular tears/herniations.” (JE 3:54) As 
CRNA Jacobs predicted, Claimant continued to develop pain from his labor including 
the right knee meniscus tear. Dr. Rayburn’s opinions are not as helpful because they do 
not reflect how causation can be established under the current Iowa law. Symptoms that 
arise from aggravation of a pre-existing condition or that are cumulative, developing 
over time with no inciting trauma, can still be caused by work and thus compensable. 
Dr. Rayburn called Claimant’s knee condition chronic. Dr. Miller dismissed Claimant’s 
complaints after only a couple of visits, determining that the knee injury was due to 
arthritis only, and also did not consider the possibility that the previously asymptomatic 
knee was lit up by work. Dr. Bansal’s opinions are consistent with Claimant’s complaints 
as well as CRNA Jacobs’ predictions regarding the likely development of Claimant’s 
condition.  
Defendants argue that the stepping up and down from the forklift did not involve 
much of any distance, and thus Dr. Bansal’s comparison to squatting and kneeling is an 
over-exaggeration of Claimant’s alleged repetitive motion. Further, the stepping up and 
down involves both knees and there are no complaints to the contralateral knee. Dr. 
Bansal noted that the step height was one foot high and acknowledged that the 
research cited did not match Claimant’s situation exactly, but that the repetitive impact 
of dismounting created a similar stress on the knee joint.  That Claimant did not develop 
bilateral knee pain does not render Dr. Bansal’s opinions non-credible.  
It is found that Claimant developed right knee pain as a result of repetitive stress 
to his right knee from mounting and dismounting the forklift. He had ongoing back pain 
which was attributable to his March 22, 2021, injury, but that pre-existing condition was 
aggravated by an altered gait. The altered gait was the result of the March 1, 2023, right 
knee injury.  
Claimant is currently earning more today than he was at the time of the injury. 
Pursuant to Iowa Code 85.34(2)(v), Claimant’s recovery is limited to his functional 
rating.

Claimant also seeks an assessment of costs. The appellate courts have 
determined that the award of costs is in the discretion of the hearing officer. John Deere 
Dubuque Works v. Caven, 804 N.W.2d 297, 301 (Iowa Ct. App. 2011). While the 
witness fees and deposition testimony are expressly limited to the amounts provided 
under Iowa Code sections 622.69 and 622.72, doctor and practitioner reports are only 
limited to “reasonable costs.” Cent. Iowa Fencing, Ltd. v. Hays, 986 N.W.2d 880 (Iowa 
Ct. App. 2022) The Iowa Appellate Court rejected an argument that only the report fee 
of an expert may be awarded as a cost and therefore, the entirety of the costs 
requested are assessed against the defendants.
