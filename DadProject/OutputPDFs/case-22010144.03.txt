STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A.  The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Richard Billany.  
Claimant filed his original notice and petition for alternate medical care on January 1, 
2026.  Defendants filed an answer admitting liability, including current causal connection 
of claimant’s neck condition.  Defendants reiterated their admission of liability at the 
commencement of hearing.  All parties filed hearing briefs prior to commencement of 
the hearing. 
 
The alternate medical care claim came on for telephonic hearing on January 13, 
2026.  The proceedings were digitally recorded.  That recording constitutes the official 
record of this proceeding.  Claimant appeared through his attorney, Kevin Halligan.  
Defendants appeared through their counsel, Elliott Manuel. 
 
The undersigned heard this case as the interim workers’ compensation 
commissioner.  Therefore, this ruling is final agency action and any appeal of the 
decision would be to the Iowa District Court pursuant to Iowa Code section 17A. 
 
Claimant offered Exhibits 1 and 2, consisting of ten pages of exhibits.  
Defendants offered Exhibits A through C, consisting of eight pages of exhibits.  All 
exhibits were received without objection.  Claimant testified on his own behalf.  No other 
witnesses testified live at the time of hearing, but the undersigned questioned counsel of 
record and permitted additional argument prior to the close of the hearing.

ISSUE 
 
The issue presented for resolution is whether the claimant is entitled to an order 
authorizing a neck surgery consisting of a discectomy at C3-4 and C6-7, as well as an 
anterior fusion from C3 through C7 to be performed by his authorized neurosurgeon, 
Hiroto Kawasaki, M.D., at the University of Iowa Hospitals and Clinics. 
 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
The undersigned having considered all the evidence in the record finds: 
 
Claimant sustained an admitted neck injury at work on September 7, 2022.  
Claimant’s current neck condition and ongoing need for medical treatment is related to 
the September 7, 2022 work injury.  Claimant needs additional medical treatment for 
this injury. 
 
Claimant testified that he has already submitted to two prior neck surgeries with 
Dr. Kawasaki.  He testifies that he has a good doctor/patient relationship with Dr. 
Kawasaki and trusts his medical judgment and opinions.  Mr. Billany desires to proceed 
with the surgical intervention recommended by Dr. Kawasaki.   
 
The medical evidence introduced demonstrates that Dr. Kawasaki recommends 
Mr. Billany undergo a discectomy at C3-4 and C6-7 as well as an extension of the prior 
anterior fusion to include a fusion from C3 through C7 of claimant’s cervical spine.  
(Claimant’s Exhibit 1, pages 4, 7).   
 
After receipt of Dr. Kawasaki’s surgical recommendation, defendants scheduled 
an evaluation with a spine orthopaedic specialist, Cassim Igram, M.D., who also 
practices at the University of Iowa Hospitals and Clinics.  Defendants inquired of Dr. 
Kawasaki whether this desired second medical opinion is reasonable.  Dr. Kawasaki 
opined that it is appropriate to get a second opinion in this situation.  (Defendants’ Ex. 
A, p. 1) 
 
Defendants scheduled claimant to be evaluated by Dr. Igram.  Defense counsel 
represented at the time of hearing that Dr. Igram needed to reschedule that evaluation 
and it is now scheduled to occur on January 28, 2026.  Claimant testified that he has 
agreed to attend the evaluation with Dr. Igram to avoid any forfeiture of his temporary 
disability, or healing period, benefits.  (Claimant’s testimony) 
 
During direct examination, claimant tesitified at hearing that he would do anything 
to avoid additional surgery.  However, he rejects the notion that he would accept or rely 
upon any recommendations by Dr. Igram.  Instead, claimant only wants ongoing care 
through Dr. Kawasaki. 
 
Upon questioning by the undersigned, defense counsel represented that the 
evaluation with Dr. Igram is intended to provide recommendations for further care

REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Section 85.27.  Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening October 16, 1975). 
 
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See Iowa 
R. App. P 14(f)(5); Bell Bros. Heating and Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d 193, 
209 (Iowa 2010); Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  Determining 
what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.  Long v. Roberts Dairy 
Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  The employer’s obligation turns on the question of 
reasonable necessity, not desirability.  Id.; Harned v. Farmland Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 
98 (Iowa 1983).   
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).   
“Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.”  
Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122, 123 (Iowa 1995). 
An employer’s right to select the provider of medical treatment to an injured 
worker does not include the right to determine how an injured worker should be 
diagnosed, evaluated, treated, or other matters of professional medical judgment.  
Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 866389 (Declaratory Ruling, May 19, 1988).

Reasonable care includes care necessary to diagnose the condition and 
defendants are not entitled to interfere with the medical judgment of its own treating 
physician.  Pote v. Mickow Corp., File No. 694639 (Review-Reopening Decision 
June 17, 1986). 
In Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 433, 437 (Iowa 1997), the 
supreme court held that “when evidence is presented to the commissioner that the 
employer-authorized medical care has not been effective and that such care is ‘inferior 
or less extensive’ than other available care requested by the employee, . . . the 
commissioner is justified by section 85.27 to order the alternate care.” 
 
 
In this case, defendants have represented that they are offering more extensive 
treatment options to claimant by providing a second opinion medical evaluation.  
Defendants represent that the purpose of this evaluation is to provide recommendations 
to Dr. Kawasaki for his consideration, but ultimately, the recommendations of Dr. 
Kawasaki will be authorized.  In this sense, defendants are offering claimant additional 
information and evaluation, not limited to one physician but to a second.  Defendants 
are offering claimant not only Dr. Kawasaki’s neurosurgical evaluation and opinion but a 
different approach and look from an orthopaedic spine surgeon.  More information and 
recommendations is helpful and better than limiting claimant’s opinions and information.  
Therefore, I concluded that defendants’ current offer of care, including the second 
opinion evaluation by Dr. Igram, is more extensive and is reasonable.  In this sense, I 
find that claimant has not proven the treatment being offered by defendants is 
unreasonable.  Alternate medical care cannot be ordered unless the care offered by 
defendants is determined to be unreasonable. 
 
Ultimately, it is not yet possible to determine whether Dr. Kawasaki will continue 
to recommend a third cervical fusion or whether Dr. Kawasaki may ultimately adopt and 
recommend some different treatment modality based on the evaluation and 
recommendations offered by Dr. Igram.  It is impossible to enter an order for specific 
care until Dr. Kawasaki is given an opportunity to review and consider the 
recommendations of Dr. Igram.  However, as defendants conceded at hearing, once Dr. 
Kawasaki is given those recommendations and has a chance to consider those with 
claimant, his recommendations should be followed with respect to the current course of 
claimant’s treatment. 
 
ORDER 
 
THEREFORE, IT IS ORDERED: 
 
The claimant's petition for alternate medical care is denied. 
Pursuant to his agreement, claimant shall present for the evaluation with Dr. 
Igram on January 28, 2026. 
Defendants shall promptly forward the opinions of Dr. Igram to Dr. Kawasaki for 
his review and consideration.
