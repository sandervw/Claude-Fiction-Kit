STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
 
Claimant Francisco Angel filed a petition in arbitration seeking worker’s 
compensation benefits against Wells Enterprises, self-insured employer, for an alleged 
work injury date of July 1, 2021. The case came before the undersigned for an 
arbitration hearing on August 25, 2023. Pursuant to an order of the Iowa Workers’ 
Compensation Commissioner, this case proceeded to a live video hearing via Zoom, 
with all parties and the court reporter appearing remotely. The hearing proceeded 
without significant difficulties. 
 
The parties filed a hearing report prior to the commencement of the hearing. On 
the hearing report, the parties entered into numerous stipulations. Those stipulations 
were accepted and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be 
made or discussed. The parties are now bound by their stipulations. 
 
The evidentiary record includes Joint Exhibits 1 through 9, Claimant’s Exhibits 1 
through 7, and Defendants’ Exhibits A through J.1   
 
Claimant testified on his own behalf. The evidentiary record closed at the 
conclusion of the evidentiary hearing on August 25, 2023. The parties submitted post-
hearing briefs on September 22, 2023, and the case was considered fully submitted on 
that date.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
The first issue to be decided is whether claimant sustained an injury, arising out 
of and in the course of his employment, on July 1, 2021. Defendant employer has 
denied claimant’s claim. Defendant argues that the only incident claimant is alleging 
actually occurred on July 1, 2021, which was the incident of him bumping his knee on a 
metal table or stand. The denial is based on Dr. Meis’s opinion that striking his knee on 
a metal table would not be a significant or substantial contributing factor in claimant’s 
meniscal tear, nor would “standing on the production line.” Additionally, defendant 
argues, the instances of slipping and hyperextending his knee did not occur on July 1, 
2021, but before that date. As such, defendant argues that claimant has not met his 
burden to prove that he sustained a left knee injury on July 1, 2021. 
 
As discussed in more detail below, I find defendant’s argument to be inconsistent 
with Iowa law. Claimant has met his burden to prove that an injury to his left knee 
manifested on July 1, 2021, which arose out of and in the course of his employment 
with Wells. As such, claimant is entitled to compensation. 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence. Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the 
evidence that the alleged injury actually occurred and that it both arose out of and in the 
course of the employment. Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 150 (Iowa 1996); 
Miedema v. Dial Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309, 311 (Iowa 1996).  
The words “arising out of” refer to the cause or source of the injury. The words “in 
the course of” refer to the time, place, and circumstances of the injury. 2800 Corp. v. 
Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124, 128 (Iowa 1995). An injury arises out of the employment 
when a causal relationship exists between the injury and the employment. Miedema, 
551 N.W.2d at 311. The injury must be a rational consequence of a hazard connected 
with the employment and not merely incidental to the employment. Koehler Elec. v. 
Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1, 3 (Iowa 2000); Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. An injury occurs “in 
the course of” employment when it happens within a period of employment at a place 
where the employee reasonably may be when performing employment duties and while 
the employee is fulfilling those duties or doing an activity incidental to them. Ciha, 552 
N.W.2d at 150. An employee does not cease to be in the course of employment merely 
because the employee is not actually engaged in doing some specifically prescribed 
task, if, in the course of employment, the employee does some act which he or she 
deems necessary for the benefit or interest of the employer. United Parcel Serv. v. 
Miller, No. 99-1596, 2000 WL 1421800, at *1 (Iowa Ct. App. Sept. 27, 2000) 
(citing Farmers Elevator Co., Kingsley v. Manning, 286 N.W.2d 174, 177 (Iowa 1979)). 
A personal injury contemplated by the workers’ compensation law means an 
injury, the impairment of health or a disease resulting from an injury which comes about, 
not through the natural building up and tearing down of the human body, but because of 
trauma. The injury must be something that acts extraneously to the natural processes of

Page 9 
nature and thereby impairs the health, interrupts, or otherwise destroys or damages a 
part or all of the body. Although many injuries have a traumatic onset, there is no 
requirement for a special incident or an unusual occurrence. Injuries that result from 
cumulative trauma are compensable. Increased disability from a prior injury, even if 
brought about by further work, does not constitute a new injury, however. St. Luke’s 
Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); Ellingson v. Fleetguard, Inc., 599 N.W.2d 
440 (Iowa 1999); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 
1995); McKeever Custom Cabinets v. Smith, 379 N.W.2d 368 (Iowa 1985).  
When the injury develops gradually over time, the cumulative injury rule applies.  
The date of injury for cumulative injury purposes is the date on which the disability 
manifests. Manifestation is best characterized as that date on which both the fact of 
injury and the causal relationship of the injury to the claimant’s employment would be 
plainly apparent to a reasonable person. The date of manifestation inherently is a fact-
based determination. The factfinder is entitled to substantial latitude in making this 
determination and may consider a variety of factors, none of which is necessarily 
dispositive in establishing a manifestation date. Among others, the factors may include 
missing work when the condition prevents performing the job, or receiving significant 
medical care for the condition. Herrera v. IBP, Inc., 633 N.W.2d 284 (Iowa 2001); Oscar 
Mayer Foods Corp. v. Tasler, 483 N.W.2d 824 (Iowa 1992); McKeever Custom 
Cabinets v. Smith, 379 N.W.2d 368 (Iowa 1985). 
 
The petition currently on file asserts an injury to the left knee on July 1, 2021, 
occurring from “slipping on floor, hyperextending left knee, standing on production line.” 
There is no specification as to whether claimant is alleging a traumatic versus 
cumulative injury. However, the evidentiary record establishes that claimant’s knee 
injury is more akin to a cumulative injury, given that his pain began as a result of 
slipping on a wet floor and hyperextending his knee on more than one occasion. He 
then had a traumatic incident on July 1, 2021, in which he bumped his knee on a metal 
table. After that incident, his pain did not relent, and he reported the injury and sought 
medical care. July 1, 2021 is the date on which claimant realized he had a work-related 
knee injury that would require medical care. As such, I find that date to be the 
appropriate manifestation date for claimant’s left knee injury. 
 
Defendant’s argument seems to be based on a variance in how the injury was 
plead on the petition, and how the injury actually occurred as established through the 
evidentiary record. Nowhere does defendant argue that the incidents did not occur, that 
claimant was not a credible witness, or that the instances of slipping and 
hyperextending the knee were not a cause of the meniscal tears, however.

The Iowa Supreme Court has premised its prior consideration of the sufficiency 
of pleading analysis with a recognition that our workers' compensation law is for the 
benefit of working men and women, “and should be, within reason, liberally construed.” 
Barton v. Nevada Poultry Co., 253 Iowa 285, 289, 110 N.W.2d 660, 662 (1961); see Orr 
v. Lewis Cent. Sch. Dist., 298 N.W.2d 256, 261 (Iowa 1980) (workers' compensation law 
should be liberally construed). “Its beneficent purpose should not be defeated by 
reading something into a section which is not there, or by a narrow or strained 
construction.” Disbrow v. Peering Implement Co., 233 Iowa 380, 392, 9 N.W.2d 378, 
384 (1943). 
In an administrative agency setting, charged with administering a humanitarian 
compensation system, only a short and plain statement of the claim is necessary. Van 
Meter v. Van Meter, 328 N.W.2d 497 (Iowa 1983); Hoenig v. Mason & Hanger, Inc., 162 
N.W.2d 188, 192 (Iowa 1968). The key to pleading in an administrative process is 
nothing more nor less than opportunity to prepare and defend. Hoenig, 162 N.W.2d at 
192. 
In this case, defendant had ample opportunity to conduct discovery and 
determine the specific basis for claimant’s assertion as to the nature of the injury and 
how it occurred. It is clear defendant obtained those specifics through discovery. 
Claimant has consistently reported that his left knee problems were the result of 
instances of slipping on a wet floor, and the incident on July 1, 2021 when he bumped 
his knee on the metal stand. The first incident investigation form completed for Wells 
states “left knee – bumped on stand towards end of shift, slipped on wet floor day or two 
before.” (Def. Ex. B, p. 2) In claimant’s answers to interrogatories, served less than a 
month after his first petition was filed, he expressly stated that he hit his knee against a 
metal machine, and also “slipped on wet floors and stairs,” and that, coupled with 
standing on a production line, caused the need for knee surgery. (Cl. Ex. 6, p. 12) He 
consistently advised every medical professional he saw that he experienced episodes of 
slipping and hyperextending his knee, in addition to bumping it on July 1. There are 
some discrepancies in the medical records regarding whether the slips occurred before 
or after he bumped his knee, but those inconsistencies are insignificant overall. Medical 
records can contain small errors in timelines such as this, which are usually the result of 
information being recorded inaccurately. Claimant’s testimony has been consistent that 
the slips occurred prior to him bumping his knee on July 1, 2021, and I found him to be 
a credible witness.  
The fact that the slipping incidents did not occur on July 1, 2021 is irrelevant to 
the question of whether claimant sustained an injury arising out of and in the course of 
his employment. It is clear from the evidence that a combination of factors resulted in 
claimant’s left knee injury, culminating on July 1, 2021 when he bumped his knee. The 
court has noted that a variance in an injury date from the pleading of approximately two

weeks is “unimportant.” Yeager v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co., 253 Iowa 369, 373-74, 
112 N.W.2d 299, 301 (1961). The court explained: 
An application for arbitration is not a formal pleading and is not to be 
judged by the technical rules of pleading. Nor is the same conformity of

Due process requires that a party “be informed somehow of the issue involved in 
order to prevent surprise at the hearing and allow an opportunity to prepare . . .  The 
test is fundamental fairness, not whether the notice meets technical rules of common 
law pleading.” Oscar Mayer Foods Corp. v. Tasler, 483 N.W.2d 824 (Iowa 1992). 
There is no surprise to defendant that claimant was asserting injuries to his left 
knee. There is no surprise to defendant that claimant had instances of slipping and 
hyperextending his knee in the time period around July 1, 2021, and that on that date he 
bumped his knee on a metal table or stand. The essential cause of action and the 
supporting facts are not changed by the variance in how the injury was plead on the 
petition, and how the injury actually occurred as established through the evidentiary 
record. Defendant had access to claimant’s treatment records, the injury investigation 
reports, claimant’s discovery responses, and claimant’s deposition testimony. 
Defendant was provided sufficient notice of claimant’s assertions and was able to fully 
investigate and prepare a complete defense of the claim. Defendant makes no 
argument that the incidents claimant alleged did not occur; that claimant was not a 
credible witness; or that the instances of slipping and hyperextending the knee were not 
a cause of the meniscal tears. After reviewing the evidence as a whole, I conclude that 
claimant sustained an injury arising out of and in the course of his employment that 
manifested on July 1, 2021. 
The next disputed issue is whether claimant is entitled to temporary disability 
benefits. Claimant seeks temporary total disability/healing period benefits from July 14, 
2021, through August 14, 2021, and for one week following the surgery that took place 
on April 21, 2022. Defendant made no argument in its brief regarding this issue. 
 
Iowa Code sections 85.33 and 85.34 allow for temporary benefits to be paid to an 
employee for time lost from work due to an injury. However, because claimant was 
terminated from employment, section 85.33(3)(a) must be considered. That section 
states: 
 
If an employee is temporarily, partially disabled and the employer for 
whom the employee was working at the time of injury offers to the 
employee suitable work consistent with the employee's disability the 
employee shall accept the suitable work, and be compensated with 
temporary partial benefits. If the employer offers the employee suitable 
work and the employee refuses to accept the suitable work offered by the 
employer, the employee shall not be compensated with temporary partial,

temporary total, or healing period benefits during the period of the refusal. 
. . . 
Iowa Code § 85.33(3)(a). 
 
 
Termination by itself is not sufficient grounds to disqualify an employee from 
temporary benefits under Iowa Code section 85.33(3). Raymie v. J. B. Schott Family 
Farms, File No. 5041943 (App. Oct. 7, 2016); Terhark v. Hope Haven, File No. 5031853 
(App. Feb. 26, 2013); Alonzo v. IBP, Inc., File No. 5009878 (App. Oct. 31,2006); Franco 
v. IBP, Inc., File No. 5004766 (App. Feb. 28, 2005). Instead, for misconduct to be 
tantamount to a refusal to perform suitable work, it must be “serious and the type of 
conduct that would cause any employer to terminate any employee” and “have a 
serious adverse impact on the employer.” Reynolds v. Hy-Vee, Inc., File No. 5046203 
(App. Oct. 31, 2017). The misconduct needs to be more egregious “than the type of 
inconsequential misconduct that employers typically overlook or tolerate.” Id. While an 
employee “is not entitled to act with impunity toward the employer,” the Commissioner 
has held that “not every act of misconduct justifies disqualifying an employee from 
workers' compensation benefits even though the employer may be justified in taking 
disciplinary action.” Id. (citation omitted). 
 
In this case, claimant was terminated based on a number of factors, including 
several write-ups for taking long breaks, insubordination, and fighting at work. The “last 
straw” was claimant’s failure to report the work injury within 24-hours, as required by 
company policy. Examples of misconduct so extreme that it constitutes a refusal of 
suitable work under section 85.33(3) include theft from the employer, and threatening to 
kill coworkers. Reynolds v. Hy-Vee, Inc., File No. 5046203 (App. October 31, 2017); 
Black v. John Deere Des Moines Works, File No. 5010502 (App. March 29, 2006). In 
this case, I find that claimant’s conduct was not so severe as to have a serious adverse 
impact on defendant employer. As such, I conclude claimant’s termination was not 
tantamount to a refusal to perform suitable work, and therefore does not disqualify him 
from receiving temporary benefits.  
 
Both Iowa Code section 85.33(1) and section 85.34(1) provide that temporary 
benefits, whether temporary total disability or healing period, are to be paid until the 
employee has returned to work, has reached MMI, or is medically capable of returning 
to employment substantially similar to the employment in which the employee was 
engaged at the time of the injury, whichever occurs first. 
 
Claimant continued working for Wells after the date of injury until the date of his 
termination, July 14, 2021. (Def. Ex. G, p. 24; Depo. Tr., p. 53) There is nothing in the 
record to suggest claimant had any physical restrictions at the time of his termination. 
On July 21, 2021, Dr. Martin placed work restrictions of occasional squat and kneel. (Jt. 
Ex. 3, p. 32) Those restrictions were kept in place at his August 4, 2021, visit. (Jt. Ex. 3, 
pp. 33-34) Claimant only seeks benefits through August 14, 2021, presumably when he 
began working for a different employer. (See Def. Ex. G, p. 16; Depo. Tr., p. 23) As 
such, I find claimant is entitled to healing period benefits from the date restrictions were 
assigned, July 21, 2021, until August 14, 2021.

The next issue to determine is whether claimant is entitled to permanent partial 
disability benefits. The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the 
evidence that the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is 
based. A cause is proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it 
need not be the only cause. A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal 
connection is probable rather than merely possible. George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 
569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa 
App. 1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability. 
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); 
Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995). Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted expert medical 
testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 
N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 1994). 
 
The only opinion regarding permanent disability in the record is Dr. Bansal’s. 
Defendant presented no argument that claimant is not entitled to permanent partial 
disability benefits, and no argument that he is not entitled to Dr. Bansal’s rating if the 
claim is found compensable. There is no alternative rating in the record and Dr. 
Bansal’s ten percent lower extremity rating is consistent with the AMA Guides. As such, 
I find claimant has sustained ten percent permanent partial impairment of his left lower 
extremity, and is entitled to 22 weeks of benefits at the stipulated rate of $550.57, 
commencing on May 4, 2022. 
 
The next issue to determine is whether claimant is entitled to penalty benefits. 
Claimant seeks a penalty for “terminating him for not reporting an injury within 24 hours 
on a holiday weekend.” Claimant argues that the employer’s reporting policy violates 
Iowa Code section 85.18, and as such his termination was wrongful under Iowa law.

An employer’s personnel policies do not govern eligibility of benefits under the 
Iowa Workers’ Compensation Act. Franco v. IBP, No. 5004766 (App. February 28, 
2005) (citing Woods v. Seimens-Furnas Controls, File Nos. 1303082, 1273249 (App. 
July 22, 2003)). That being said, claimant has not presented a valid claim for penalty 
benefits under Iowa Code section 10A.315(4). Under that section, penalty benefits can 
only be awarded for an unreasonable failure to pay weekly benefits. Claimant makes no 
argument that defendant’s denial of the claim was unreasonable. Defendant based the 
denial on Dr. Meis’s opinion, and contemporaneously conveyed the basis of the denial 
to claimant in a letter and amended answer. There is no argument that reliance on Dr. 
Meis’s medical opinion was not unreasonable. As such, claimant has not met his burden 
to prove entitlement to penalty benefits. 
 
Finally, claimant seeks payment of medical expenses and costs. Under Iowa law, 
once defendants denied compensability for claimant’s alleged injuries, they lost the right 
to choose the medical providers for that care during the period of denial. “[T]he 
employer has no right to choose the medical care when compensability is contested.” 
Bell Bros. Heating v. Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d 193, 204 (Iowa 2010). Further, when 
compensability is contested, “the employer cannot assert an authorization defense in 
response to a subsequent claim by the employee for the expenses of the alternate 
medical care.” R. R. Donnelly & Sons v. Barnett, 670 N.W.2d at 197-198 (Iowa 2003). 
 
Claimed medical expenses were submitted in Claimant’s Exhibit 1, which is 
actually a summary of medical expenses paid by Iowa Total Care on claimant’s behalf. 
(Cl. Ex. 1, pp. 1-3) All of the charges listed are related to claimant’s left knee injury. 
Defendant presented no argument that the charges listed were not fair and reasonable, 
necessary, or causally related to the work injury. Pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.27, 
defendant is responsible for any causally related medical care. As such, defendant is 
ordered to hold claimant harmless and indemnify claimant against any claims for 
reimbursement or any liens for payment of past medical expenses paid by Iowa Total 
Care for treatment of his left knee injury of July 1, 2021. 
 
Claimant seeks a taxation of costs. Claimant identified the “itemized list of costs 
and proof of payment” as claimant’s exhibit 2. The only cost contained in exhibit 2 is Dr. 
Bansal’s IME bill. Assessment of costs is a discretionary function of this agency. Iowa 
Code § 86.40. Costs are to be assessed at the discretion of the deputy commissioner or 
workers’ compensation commissioner hearing the case. 876 IAC 4.33. Under that rule, 
a claimant can be awarded the cost of obtaining two medical reports. 876 IAC 4.33(6). 
However, the only taxable costs are the reports themselves, not the underlying 
examination needed to draft the reports. Des Moines Area Regional Transit Authority v. 
Young, 867 N.W.2d 839, 846-47 (Iowa 2015) 
Dr. Bansal’s bill is not itemized as to what portion is related to the report fee 
versus the underlying examination. (Cl. Ex. 2, p. 4) I decline to speculate as to the 
portion of the total fee associated with drafting the report in lieu of testimony, and as 
such decline to assess Dr. Bansal’s fee as a cost.
