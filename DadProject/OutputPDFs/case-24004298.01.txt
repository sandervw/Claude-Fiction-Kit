STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Eugene Peek, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits against City of Des Moines, a self-insured employer.  
This case was heard on June 17, 2025, and considered fully submitted on July 8, 
2025. The parties were offered the opportunity to submit a brief in support of their case 
in chief. Defendants filed a brief on July 8, 2025, and the claimant opted not to.  
The record consists of Joint Exhibits 1-6, Claimant’s Exhibits 1-7, Defendants’ 
Exhibits A-E, and testimony of claimant, Eugene Peek. 
ISSUES 
1. Whether timely notice was given pursuant to Iowa Code Section 85.23;  
 
2. If so, the extent of claimant’s scheduled member disability;  
 
3. Whether claimant is entitled to reimbursement of an IME under Iowa Code 
section 85.39; 
 
4. Taxation of costs.      
STIPULATIONS 
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised 
or discussed in this decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
 
Mr. Peek was employed by the City of Des Moines for just over 19 years.  He 
worked in the sanitation department and drove a garbage truck.  This included using a 
joystick control mechanism to operate the mechanical arm, which required him to keep 
his hand in the same position for extended periods of time.  He did have to manually lift 
any trash that fell from the garbage totes when they were lifted to be dumped into his 
truck.  He also provided specialized service for elderly residents by retrieving their 
garbage containers from their homes, and brought them to the road to be emptied, then 
returned them to the house.  These duties required significant upper extremity strength 
and range of motion. He has not returned to his position following his injuries and 
surgeries.  He was given the option of returning to work by a specific date (April 30, 
2024), or be terminated.  His surgeon did not clear him to return to work until June 
2024.  Given this ultimatum and his eligibility for retirement benefits, he decided to retire 
rather than risk losing his benefits from being terminated.  (DE D, Depo pp. 4-5)  This 
was earlier than he had intended to retire, which would have been in September 2024. 
On or about November 14, 2022, around 7:00 a.m. claimant slipped on water. 
While falling, he threw his arm up and behind himself to catch his balance and in the 
process twisted his shoulder.  
There is a factual dispute over whether claimant made a report to his supervisor 
on the day of the injury. 
In his deposition testimony and at hearing, claimant maintained that he reported 
this incident to his supervisor, Ed Killin. (DE D, Depo pp. 6-7, Transcript p. 11, 23-25) 
Claimant did not ask for treatment immediately, instead relaying that he wanted to wait 
and see if it would get better. (DE D, Depo pp. 9-10) The first report of injury identifies 
the date claimant told his employer of the injury as April 7, 2023. (DE A) The First 
Report of Injury noted that claimant did not report the injury because it happened prior 
to the start of work which was 7 AM. (DE B:6, DE D, Depo pp. 12-13) In the audio 
interview, he said, “[T]he reason I didn’t say something to them about this before is 
because it happened at like 7 or 6:50 in the morning.” (DE B:6) There was no mention 
of a report to the supervisor either in the First Report of Injury or the audio interview of 
April 13, 2023. (DE A , DE B)  
Claimant appeared to be a credible witness. Most of his testimony was 
consistent, and he answered most of the defendant’s questions on cross examination

with forthright answers but for the part about the report of injury to Mr. Killin. The 
claimant’s prior statements, while not under oath, were closer in time to the fall. April 
2023 is only a few months after the fall of November 14, 2022, whereas April 2025, 
when the deposition took place, and July 2025, when the hearing took place, is almost 
three years after the work injury. It is more likely than not that claimant’s recollection of 
the people he reported the incident to was more accurate in April 2023 than it was years 
later. I find the claimant was a credible witness but that his testimony and statements 
regarding to whom he reported his fall was more accurate in the April 2023 interview. 
Therefore, it is found that claimant did not report the injury to his employer until April 7, 
2023.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
In the hearing report and during the hearing when the hearing report was 
reviewed, April 14, 2022, was stipulated as the injury date. (See Hearing Report, 
Transcript p. 4 ) However, the claimant testified that the date of the actual fall was 
November 14, 2022. (Trans., p. 18) This is the date that is reported on the First Report 
of Injury. (DE A:1) The defendant also used November 14, 2022, as the date of injury in 
its brief. (Defendant’s Brief). April 14, 2022, appears to be a scrivener’s error.  
The phrase, “nunc pro tunc” means “now for then.”  See:  Black’s Law Dictionary, 
page 1218 (Revised 4th Edition 1968).  The definition in Black’s Law Dictionary further 
provides:  “A phrase applied to acts allowed to be done after the time when they should 
be done, with a retroactive effect, i.e. with the same effect as if regularly done.”  Black’s 
at 1218.  A nunc pro tunc order “is not for the purpose of correcting judicial thinking, a 
judicial conclusion, or a mistake of law.”  Headley v. Headley, 172 N.W.2d 104, 108

(Iowa 1969).  The nunc pro tunc order can be employed to correct obvious errors or to 
make an order conform to the judge’s original intent.  Graber v. Iowa District Court for 
Washington City, 410 N.W.2d 224, 229 (Iowa 1987).  Brinson v. Spee Dee Delivery 
Service, No. 8-754/06-2074 (Iowa App. November 13, 2008). 
Therefore, the stipulated injury date of April 14, 2022, is replaced with the date of 
November 14, 2022.   
Defendants argue claimant is barred from recovery due to lack of notice given to 
defendants within the statutory time limit.  
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e). 
 
 
Iowa Code section 85.23 requires an employee to give notice of the occurrence 
of an injury to the employer within 90 days from the date of the occurrence, unless the 
employer has actual knowledge of the occurrence of the injury.  The purpose of the 90-
day notice or actual knowledge requirement is to give the employer an opportunity to 
timely investigate the facts surrounding the injury.  The actual knowledge alternative to 
notice is met when the employer, as a reasonably conscientious manager, is alerted to 
the possibility of a potential compensation claim through information which makes the 
employer aware that the injury occurred and that it may be work related.  Dillinger v. 
City of Sioux City, 368 N.W.2d 176, 180 (Iowa 1985); Robinson v. Department of 
Transp., 296 N.W.2d 809, 811 (Iowa 1980).  Failure to give notice is an affirmative 
defense which the employer must prove by a preponderance of the evidence.  DeLong 
v. Iowa State Highway Commission, 229 Iowa 700, 295 N.W. 91, 92 (1940). 
The slip and fall on a slick surface resulting in a shoulder injury occurred on 
November 14, 2022. Claimant has 90 days in which to report the injury to his employer. 
The first time claimant notified his employer was on April 7, 2023, after he learned from 
a physical therapist that treatment may be covered under workers compensation. The 
claimant did not provide notice to defendant within 90 days of the injury of November 
14, 2022.  
It is not clear whether claimant raised the discovery rule in opposition to 
defendants’ notice defense. The Supreme Court in Baker v. Bridgestone/Firestone 
instructed that the agency must apply the discovery rule to injuries arising cumulatively 
or from a singular traumatic incident. Baker v. Bridgestone/Firestone, 872 N.W.2d 672, 
683 (Iowa, 2015) However, the court in Baker also noted that the discovery rule must be 
properly raised and in this case there is no argument made by claimant, either in the 
prehearing report or in the review of the prehearing report at hearing, that the discovery 
rule should be applied. Therefore, the discovery rule is not considered in this case.  
Because it is found that the claimant did not provide timely notice, all remaining 
issues are moot.
