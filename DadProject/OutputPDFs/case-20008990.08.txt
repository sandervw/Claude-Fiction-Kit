This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A. The 
expedited procedures of rule 876 IAC 4.48, the “alternate medical care” rule, are 
invoked by claimant, Joshua Hanawalt.  
This alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on April 19, 2024. The 
proceedings were recorded digitally and constitute the official record of the hearing. By 
an order filed by the Workers’ Compensation Commissioner, this decision is designated 
final agency action. Any appeal would be by petition for judicial review under Iowa Code 
section 17A.15(1).  
The record in this case consists of Claimant’s Exhibit 1 and Defendants’ Exhibits 
A and B. 
On April 11, 2024, the undersigned ordered the parties to file pleadings and 
exhibits, and to furnish a phone number for the hearing by 3 p.m. the night before the 
hearing. 
Defendants filed an answer approximately ten minutes before hearing.  At 
approximately 3:40 p.m. the day before the hearing, defendants filed three pages of 
unpaginated documents.  Those documents were later resubmitted and marked as 
requested by the undersigned. 
ISSUE 
The issue presented for resolution in this case is whether claimant is entitled to 
alternate medical care consisting of an order from this agency to allow claimant to direct 
his own medical care at defendants’ expense due to defendants’ continued and 
repeated delays in approval of care.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
Defendants accept liability for a work-related injury to claimant’s low back on 
June 25, 2020. 
A brief recitation of the docket in this matter is relevant regarding claimant’s 
request for relief. 
This matter was heard in arbitration on February 16, 2022.  An arbitration 
decision was issued in this case on June 2, 2022.  That decision found, in part, that 
claimant carried his burden of proof he was entitled to alternate medical care and that 
Shawn Spooner, M.D., was an authorized treating physician for client.  Hanawalt v. 
American Bottling Company, File No. 20008990.01 (June 2, 2022) 
On October 3, 2023, claimant filed a petition for alternate medical care seeking 
authorization and payment for aqua therapy, injections, massage therapy, medications 
and mental health treatment related to claimant’s back injury.  Claimant dismissed this 
petition on October 11, 2023, as defendants agreed to authorize the requested medical 
care.  Hanawalt v. American Bottling Company, File No. 20008990.02 (October 11, 
2023) 
On December 5, 2023, claimant filed a second petition for alternate medical care 
seeking authorization for mental health appointments and approval of a gym 
membership recommended by Dr. Spooner.  A December 19, 2023, decision ordered 
defendants to authorize and pay for the requested care recommended by the authorized 
provider.  Hanawalt v. American Bottling Company, File No. 20008990.04 (December 
19, 2023) 
On January 11, 2024, claimant filed a third petition for alternate medical care.  
That petition requested defendants provide a gym membership as previously ordered in 
the December of 2023 decision.  Claimant’s petition for alternate medical care was 
denied, in this case, as defendants indicated they authorized a gym membership shortly 
before the petition for alternate medical care was filed.  Hanawalt v. American Bottling 
Company, File No. 20008990.06 (January 25, 2024) 
On February 16, 2024, claimant filed a fourth petition for alternate medical care in 
this matter requesting the care recommended by Dr. Spooner be authorized and paid 
for.  A decision was issued in that case granting claimant’s request for alternate medical 
care.  The order in that decision indicated as follows: 
Defendants shall authorize and pay for access to gym and pool facilities at 
the “Altoona Campus,” massage therapy, laser therapy and 
decompression table therapy. Prior alternate medical care hearings 
regarding this matter indicate defendants have delayed authorizing the 
treatments recommend [sic] by an authorized provider or as ordered by 
this agency. As a result, defendants shall authorize and pay for access to 
gym and pool facilities at the “Altoona Campus,” massage therapy, laser

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW  
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3).  
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except

where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Section 85.27.  Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening October 16, 1975).          
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See Iowa 
R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e); Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995). 
Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.  Id.  The 
employer’s obligation turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability. Id.; 
Harned v. Farmland Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983).            
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).     
  Defendants were ordered to authorize Dr. Spooner to treat claimant in a June of 
2022 arbitration decision.  Since that date, claimant has had to file five different petitions 
for alternate medical care because defendants have delayed and/or ignored the 
recommendations of the authorized treating physician.  Defendants have also delayed 
or ignored orders from this agency to provide alternate medical care.  The February 28, 
2024, alternate medical care decision in this case specifically ordered defendants to 
provide care recommended by Dr. Spooner within two weeks because defendants have 
repeatedly and routinely delayed or failed to provided care previously ordered by this 
agency.  The record indicates the care ordered by the February 28, 2024, decision in 
this case, to be provided to claimant by March 13, 2024, was still not provided as of the 
day of this hearing.  (Ex. A; Ex. 1, p. 1) The record also demonstrates claimant has tried 
to resolve this matter with defense counsel, without filing a fifth alternate medical care 
petition, but without success. 
In this case, claimant has clearly demonstrated that defendants have not offered 
prompt or reasonable medical care.  Claimant has established that defendants have not 
provided prompt medical care that is reasonably suited to treat his injury.          
Given the claimant’s numerous requests for care, this agency’s numerous orders 
for defendants to provide care, and defendants’ continued delay and noncompliance of 
those orders, I find that defendants have not offered prompt medical care that is 
reasonably suited to treat the claimant’s work injury.  I find that defendants’ failure to 
offer authorized medical care, defendants’ delay of authorized medical care, and 
defendants’ noncompliance with orders from the Iowa Workers’ Compensation 
Commission is unreasonable.  It is also found unreasonable that claimant should have 
to file five different petitions for alternate medical care for one date of injury that should 
have been resolved by the June 2, 2022, arbitration decision.
