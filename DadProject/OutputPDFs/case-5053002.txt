BACKGROUND FACTS AND PROCEEDINGS 
 
 This case has a long factual history that was set forth in detail in both the Arbitration and 
Appeal Decisions. Accordingly, the Court need not repeat such history in detail here. The Court 
refers to the findings of fact as stated in Heather L. Palmer, Deputy Commissioner’s (the 
Commissioner) decision and will discuss the portions relevant to the Court’s ruling.  
STANDARD OF REVIEW 
The Iowa Administrative Procedure Act, Iowa Code chapter 17A, governs the scope of the 
Court’s review in workers’ compensation cases. Iowa Code § 86.26 (2011); Meyer v. IBP, Inc.,

710 N.W.2d 213, 218 (Iowa 2006). “Under the Act, we may only interfere with the commissioner’s 
decision if it is erroneous under one of the grounds enumerated in the statute, and a party’s 
substantial rights have been prejudiced.” Id. A party challenging agency action bears the burden 
of demonstrating the action’s invalidity and resulting prejudice. Iowa Code § 17A.19(8)(a). This 
can be shown in a number of ways, including proof the action was ultra vires; legally erroneous; 
unsupported by substantial evidence in the record when that record is viewed as a whole; or 
otherwise, unreasonable, arbitrary, capricious, or an abuse of discretion. See Id. at (10). The district 
court acts in an appellate capacity to correct errors of law on the part of the agency. Grundmeyer 
v. Weyerhaeuser Co., 649 N.W.2d 744, 748 (Iowa 2002). 
 “If the claim of error lies with the agency’s findings of fact, the proper question on review 
is whether substantial evidence supports those findings of fact.” Meyer, 710 N.W.2d p. 219. 
Factual findings regarding the award of workers’ compensation benefits are within the 
commissioner's discretion. The Court is bound by the commissioner's findings of fact if they are 
supported by substantial evidence. Mycogen Seeds v. Sands, 686 N.W.2d 457, 464-65 (Iowa 2004). 
Substantial evidence is defined as, “evidence of the quality and quantity that would be deemed 
sufficient by a neutral, detached, and reasonable person, to establish the fact at issue when the 
consequences resulting from the establishment of that fact are understood to be serious and of great 
importance.” Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(f)(1); see also Nance v. Iowa Dep’t of Revenue, 908 
N.W.2d 261, 267 (Iowa 2018).  
In the context of compensation cases, the Court acknowledges that while it, “is correct that 
we interpret workers’ compensation statutes in favor of the worker, we still must interpret the 
provisions within the workers’ compensation statutory scheme ‘to ensure our interpretation is 
harmonious with the statute as a whole.’” Chavez v. MS Tech. LLC, 972 N.W.2d 662, 668 (Iowa 
E-FILED                    CVCV065802 - 2024 JAN 12 02:12 PM             POLK

2022) quoting Ramirez-Trujillo v. Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 N.W.2d 759, 770 (Iowa 2016). “[T]he 
commissioner's interpretation of the law is entitled to no deference because the interpretation of 
the workers’ compensation statutes and related case law has not been clearly vested by a provision 
of law in the discretion of the agency.” Schutjer v. Algona Manor Care Ctr., 780 N.W.2d 549, 558 
(Iowa 2010) (internal citations omitted). The Court reviews the commissioner’s statutory 
interpretation, “to correct errors of law on the part of the agency.” Teleconnect Co. v. Iowa State 
Commerce Comm'n, 404 N.W.2d 158, 161 (Iowa 1987). 
LAW AND ANALYSIS 
Lack of Written Credibility Findings Does Not Constitute an Error of Law 
 
Petitioners claim there is an error of law in the Commissioner’s Remand Decision on May 
22, 2023 (the Remand Decision) because it did not include findings on Respondent’s credibility. 
Petitioners assert the Commissioner not only has a duty to make credibility determinations but also 
to write those findings of credibility into the decision. Petitioners further contend the Remand 
Decision must be reversed and remanded for this reason.  
 
To support their argument, Petitioners cite McDowell v. Town of Clarksville, 241 N.W.2d 
904 (Iowa 1976) and Arndt v. City of Le Claire, 728 N.W.2d 389 (Iowa 2007). In the brief 
discussions of credibility, both cases primarily set forth the duties of the Commissioner with 
regards to witnesses and evidence. “It is the commissioner’s duty as the trier of fact to determine 
the credibility of the witnesses, weigh the evidence, and decide the facts in issue.” Arndt, 728 
N.W.2d p. 394–95. “[T]he fact finder must not arbitrarily or totally reject testimony; he has the 
duty to weigh it and determine its credibility.” McDowell, 241 N.W.2d p. 908 (internal quotation 
marks and citation omitted).

The Court finds a distinction between the duty to determine credibility and a requirement 
to write the credibility findings into the decision. While Arndt and McDowell expressly emphasize 
the duty of the commissioner to weigh and determine credibility findings, the Court finds no 
language or implication in either case which mandates that credibility determinations be stated in 
the decision as findings. There is also no indication that a lack of written credibility findings 
constitutes an error of law which necessitates reversal. The Court finds Petitioners’ reliance on 
McDowell and Arndt misplaced as neither case supports Petitioners’ contention. The 
Commissioner may properly fulfill the duty of determining credibility without including the 
credibility findings in the written decision. 
In this case, the Court finds that, regardless of whether the findings are written into the 
decision, the Commissioner’s ruling for Respondent sufficiently provides a clear implication as to 
the Commissioner’s credibility determinations. Giving further insight into the Commissioner’s 
credibility findings, the Remand Decision contains several statements where credibility and 
persuasiveness are mentioned. (Remand Decision, p. 21, 23, 30, 31). The Court finds no error of 
law and reversal is not appropriate on this basis. 
The Finding of April 15, 2013, as Respondent’s Applicable Discovery Date Is 
Supported by Substantial Evidence 
  
Petitioners argue the Commissioner’s calculated discovery date of April 15, 2013, for 
Respondent’s injury is irrational, illogical, wholly unjustifiable, and not supported by substantial 
evidence. Respondent argues the discovery date is correct under the standard for cumulative 
injuries. 
Respondent’s injuries are cumulative injuries. Cert. Rec. #9, p. 281, Wertz Letter to Liberty 
Mutual, 10/14/13. The discovery date of a cumulative injury is when the claimant, “knew the 
nature, seriousness and probable compensable character,” of the injury. Herrera v. IBP, Inc., 633

N.W.2d 284, 288 (Iowa 2001). Due to Respondent receiving treatment for her injuries prior to 
April, 2013, Petitioners contend she should have been aware her condition was serious, having a 
“permanent, adverse impact” on her employment. Herrera, 633 N.W. p. 288. This would put the 
discovery date well before 2013, and Respondent’s claim would be barred for untimeliness and 
lack of notice.  
It is well established that challenging workers compensation agency findings on appeal is 
a matter of substantial evidence. If the agency’s findings are supported by substantial evidence, 
the Court is bound by the findings. Hough v. Iowa Dep't of Pers., 666 N.W.2d 168, 170 (Iowa 
2003). “[T]he question on appeal is not whether the evidence supports a different finding than the 
finding made by the commissioner, but whether the evidence supports the findings actually made.” 
Meyer v. IBP, Inc., 710 N.W.2d 213, 218 (Iowa 2006) (emphasis added) (internal quotation marks 
and citation omitted); see also Arndt, 728 N.W.2d at 393. Accordingly, the issue before the Court 
is not whether a different discovery date could have been determined, but whether there is 
substantial evidence on record to support the discovery date found by the Commissioner. 
Here, the Commissioner found the discovery date to be April 15, 2013. Just as the Iowa 
Court of Appeals had determined that the absence of work restrictions precluded a discovery date 
prior to September 8, 2021, the Commissioner primarily relied on the fact that Respondent had not 
previously received permanent work restrictions as the primary ground for determining that April 
15, 2013, was the proper discovery date. (Remand Decision, p. 26); See Tilton v. H.J. Heinz Co., 
938 N.W.2d 720 at *4 (Iowa Ct. App. July 24, 2019). 
The Court finds that substantial evidence in the record supports the Commissioner’s 
finding of April 15, 2013, as the discovery date. Similar to Herrera, the record reflects Respondent 
received treatment for her injury prior to the discovery date and given no work restrictions. Herrera

633 N.W.2d p. 288–89. Notwithstanding absences for rest and treatment, Respondent was 
consistently cleared to return to work. The Commissioner’s reasoning was provided: 
No physician in this case provided Tilton with permanent restrictions before her 
last day of employment on April 15, 2013 . . . Tilton testified on cross-examination 
Dr. Bradley did not tell her that her condition in her spine was permanent at that 
time. There is no evidence Dr. Bradley communicated his belief regarding the 
permanent nature of Tilton’s condition to her in 2010. While Tilton was treated 
extensively for low back pain over the course of many years, there is no evidence 
any medical practitioner told Tilton her condition was permanent before April 15, 
2013. I find the limitations period was tolled under the discovery rule until April 
15, 2013, the last day she worked for Heinz.  
 
(Remand Decision, p. 26). 
The Court acknowledges that the absence or presence of work restrictions is not alone 
dispositive. For example, in Herrera, the Iowa Supreme Court found the claimant would have not 
known the seriousness or permanence of their injury, even after work restrictions were imposed. 
The industrial commissioner found that on this date Herrera knew her injury was 
serious because work restrictions were imposed. We find nothing in the record, 
however, that would support a conclusion that a reasonable person in Herrera's 
position would have been aware of the seriousness of her condition and its impact 
on her employment at that time. 
 
 633 N.W. 2d p. 288-289 (emphasis added). 
In this case, the Court finds the Commissioner’s finding is supported by items other than 
the absence of work restrictions. The record establishes Respondent had been receiving treatment 
for injuries as early as 2004, yet both appellate court decisions rejected a discovery date of 
September 2010 and held that the discovery date had to be sometime after September 2010. Tilton 
v. H.J. Heinz Co., 938 N.W.2d 720, WL 3317393, p. *4 (Iowa Ct. App. 2019).; Tilton v. H.J. Heinz 
Co., 986 N.W.2d 869, WL 2824290 p. *3 (Iowa Ct. App. 2022). As a result, both decisions rejected 
the reasoning that receiving treatment is enough to conclude that Respondent knew the seriousness 
of her injury and the permanent, adverse effect it would have on her employment. Id. Additionally,

the Commissioner found credible Respondent’s testimony that although she was aware of her 
injury, she did not know of a permanent adverse impact on her employment prior to April 2013 
when she was no longer able to work. Remand Decision, p. 26. As the Commissioner pointed out, 
if her physicians were aware, there is no report or correspondence on record establishing 
Respondent was ever informed of the serious nature of her injury nor the permanency of her

Cert. Rec. #4, p. 10; Cert. Rec. #9, p. 37. Under these circumstances, the Court finds substantial 
evidence supports the Commissioner’s finding of April 15, 2013, as the applicable discovery date. 
Based on that date, Respondent timely filed this action. As such, reversal is not appropriate on this 
basis. 
The Remand Decision’s Determination that Respondent’s Low Back and Mental 
Conditions Were Causally Related to and Aggravated by Her Employment Is 
Supported by Substantial Evidence 
 
Petitioners argue substantial evidence does not support the agency’s finding that 
Respondent’s low back and mental conditions were causally related to her employment. The crux 
of Petitioners’ argument is that Respondent should not have been found credible and the 
appropriate date of injury is 2001 rather than a discovery date of April 15, 2013. 
[T]he Deputy Commissioner failed to address the fact that the opinions of Dr. 
Mathew, Dr. Bradley and Dr. Hines were solely contingent upon Claimant’s 
subjective complaints, which should not be believed given her lack of credibility . . 
. many of these opinions are couched in terms of a date of injury of 2013 and not 
the established date of injury of 2001. 
 
Petitioners’ Brief, p. 29 (emphasis added). Petitioners’ brief proceeds to present evidence that 
Petitioners believe is more persuasive than that relied on by the Commissioner.  
However, as previously discussed, it is not the task of this Court to weigh evidence or the 
credibility of the witnesses. Tim O'Neill Chevrolet, Inc. v. Forristall, 551 N.W.2d 611, 614 (Iowa 
E-FILED                    CVCV065802 - 2024 JAN 12 02:12 PM             POLK    
CLERK OF DISTRICT COURT                    Page 7 of 13

8 
 
1996). “Making a determination as to whether evidence ‘trumps’ other evidence or whether one 
piece of evidence is ‘qualitatively weaker’ than another . . . is not an assessment for the district 
court . . . to make when it conducts a substantial evidence review of an agency decision.” Arndt, 
728 N.W.2d at 394 .  
Here, the Commissioner was presented with medical professionals’ testimony and records 
opining Respondent’s injuries were or were not causally related to and aggravated by Respondent’s 
work. Remand Decision, p. 23, 24; See generally, Cert. Rec. #9. Stated another way, the record 
contains expert testimony that supports Petitioners and other expert testimony that supports 
Respondent. The Commissioner based her finding on the testimony and evidence she determined 
to be the most credible and persuasive.  
Petitioners’ disagreement with the Commissioner’s finding does not render the medical 
expert evidence as lacking credibility. Outside of Petitioners’ own assertion to the contrary, the 
Court does not find a lack of evidence for the Commissioner’s credibility determination. Nor does 
the Court find evidence the Commissioner’s credibility determination is an error of law which 
warrants reversal. For these reasons, the Court finds substantial evidence exists to support the 
finding that Respondent’s injuries are causally related to and were aggravated by Respondent’s 
work. As such, reversal is not appropriate on this basis. 
The Remand Decision’s Determination of Permanent Disability Is Supported by 
Substantial Evidence 
 
Petitioners argue the Commissioner’s determination of permanent disability is not 
supported by substantial evidence because Respondent has demonstrated a lack of motivation to 
return to work and her medical condition is minor. Petitioners also argue the Commissioner’s 
failure to address the alleged lack of motivation is an error of law that requires reversal. The Court 
briefly examines the general considerations for determining industrial disability.

Industrial disability is intended to measure an injured worker’s lost earning capacity 
. . . This inquiry is a multi-factored test, including “consideration of not only the 
claimant’s functional disability, but also [his] age, education, qualifications, 
experience, and ability to engage in similar employment.” . . . The relevant question 
thus is more than the worker’s physical ability . . . Instead, the focus is on the injured 
worker’s ability to be gainfully employed. 
 
Swiss Colony, Inc. v. Deutmeyer, 789 N.W.2d 129, 137–38 (Iowa 2010) (internal citations 
omitted).  
The Remand Decision illustrates the Commissioner’s finding is based on an examination 
of Respondent’s earning capacity including her education, work experience, and medical 
conditions. Remand Decision p. 30. The Commissioner cites the medical opinions of Dr. Mathew 
and Dr. Mittauer who opined Respondent was not capable of working due to her physical and 
mental injuries. Cert. Rec. #9, p. 16-19, Dr. Mathew Questionnaire; p. 172, Dr. Mittauer Report. 
The Commissioner found both medical opinions persuasive. Remand Decision, p. 30-31. The 
record also contains a vocational assessment and report from Laughlin Management which 
concluded Respondent was unemployable. Cert. Rec. pp. 180-97, conclusion at p. 194.  
Having considered Respondent’s education, work history, and vocational report in the 
Remand Decision, the Court finds the Commissioner’s finding is supported by substantial 
evidence. Because the finding is supported by substantial evidence, the Court further finds it 
unnecessary to further address Respondent’s alleged lack of motivation or the Commissioner’s 
credibility findings. Reversal is not appropriate on this basis. 
The Record Contains Substantial Evidence to Support Penalty Benefits 
 
Lastly, Petitioners argue the Commissioner’s award of penalty benefits is not supported by 
substantial evidence and is an error of law. Petitioners continue to assert the date of injury is 2001 
and the Commissioner awarded benefits based on the current Iowa Administrative Code Section 
86.13 rather than the 2001 version. Petitioners claim this is an error of law that requires reversal.

The Court reiterates that Respondent was found to have a cumulative injury with an 
applicable discovery date of April 15, 2013. As previously discussed, the discovery date finding 
is supported by substantial evidence and is to be utilized as the established discovery date for any 
filings or benefit calculations. Moreover, even if the 2001 version of the Code had been referenced, 
the Commissioner found unreasonable investigation and delay on the part of Petitioners, which are 
grounds for penalty under any version of section 86.13. See Iowa Code § 86.13(4)(c)(1); 
Robbennolt v. Snap-On Tools Corp., 555 N.W.2d 229, 236–37 (Iowa 1996). As both versions of 
section 86.13 prohibit unreasonable investigation and delay, the Court finds no error of law on this 
basis. 
Regarding the Commissioner’s finding of unreasonable investigation and delay, the Court 
briefly reviews the timeline leading up to Petitioners denial of benefits. Petitioners were made 
aware of Respondent’s work-related injury in May and August 2013. In September 2013, Dr. 
Mathew provided Petitioner Liberty Mutual with medical confirmation of the injury and the opined 
work-related cause. Cert. Rec. #9, p. 14, Dr. Mathew Letter to Liberty Mutual. Despite this, in 
October 2013, Petitioners informed Respondent they had no medical report or showing of a work 
injury and would be conducting an investigation. Cert. Rec. #9, p. 280, Liberty Mutual Letter. 
Nearly a year later, in August 2014, Petitioners had Dr. Abernathy review Respondent’s medical 
records without examining Respondent herself. Cert. Rec. #9, p. 225-250. From this record review, 
Dr. Abernathy opined there had been no work injury. On this opinion, Petitioners informed 
Respondent her claim had been denied.  
Upon examination of the evidence, the Commissioner concluded Petitioners had not 
conducted a reasonable investigation into Respondent’s claim. As well as finding the investigation

 The Court finds there is substantial evidence in the record to support the Commissioner’s 
conclusion that Respondent was entitled to penalty benefits. As such, the Court finds no error of

CONCLUSION 
 
The Court finds Petitioners’ arguments primarily request the Court to use its own judgment 
to replace the Commissioner’s credibility findings and the applicable discovery date. On judicial 
review, the Court is concerned with substantial evidence and errors of law. The Court finds 
substantial evidence for the Commissioner’s findings and no error of law in the Remand Decision. 
For these reasons, the Court affirms the Commissioner’s findings.  
 
It is therefore ORDERED that Petitioners’ Petition for Judicial Review is DENIED.
