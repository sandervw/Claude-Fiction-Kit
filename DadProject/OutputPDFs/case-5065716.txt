Before the Court is a Petition for Judicial Review filed on December 31, 2019. Petitioner 
Zachary Tew (“Tew”) filed his brief on March 11, 2020. Respondents Sparboe Farms Inc. and 
Nationwide Agribusiness Insurance Company(hereinafter collectively “Sparboe”), filed their brief 
on April 11, 2020. A hearing on the matter was held May 8, 2020. Tew was represented at the 
hearing by Attorney Gregory Taylor. Sparboe was represented by Attorney Deborah Stein. The 
Court, having now reviewed the briefs filed by the parties, the certified administrative record, and 
having heard arguments of counsel, finds and orders as follows. 
Tew filed his Original Notice and Petition for Workers Compensation Benefits on March 
2, 2017. Tew alleged he had cumulative injuries as a result of his repetitive work activities at 
Sparboe. Sparboe filed an answer which denied liability for the injury. On March 28, 2018, the 
case was heard in arbitration before Iowa Deputy Workers’ Compensation Commissioner 
Heather L. Palmer. The decision was issued on June 18, 2018 in which the deputy commissioner

found that Tew failed to carry his burden of proof to show that he sustained an injury arising out 
of and in the course of employment.1  
Tew moved Deputy Commissioner Palmer for reconsideration of her decision. The 
Motion of Reconsideration was denied on July 23, 2018. Tew filed a Notice of Appeal on July 
24, 2018. On December 5, 2019, the Commissioner filed the Appeal Decision for this case in 
which he affirmed the Arbitration Decision. Tew filed a Petition for Judicial Review of the 
commissioner’s Appeal Decision on December 31, 2019. 
Tew contends that the commissioner’s findings of fact, in regards to the Agency’s 
determination that Tew had not proven his injury arose out of and in the course of his 
employment at Sparboe, are not supported by substantial evidence. Additionally, Tew alleges 
that the commissioner’s application of law to the facts is irrational, illogical, and wholly 
unjustifiable.  
The parties have meticulously outlined their arguments in their memorandums of 
authorities, supporting their arguments with appropriate references to the administrative record. 
The parties have also provided this Court with the applicable law for its review. Due to the 
parties’ meticulous and conscientious recitation of the facts as well as the applicable law, the 
Court does not restate same here.  
In regards to the argument made by Tew, as to whether there is substantial evidence to 
support the commissioner’s decision, review of agency action is at law, not de novo, and is 
limited to the record made before the agency.2 The court must consider the evidence that 
supports the challenged finding, as well as the evidence that detracts from it.3 In reviewing the 
record, the court is not to decide whether there is evidence to support a decision the 
commissioner did not make, but instead to determine if there is substantial evidence to support

In addition, when the challenge is to the commissioner’s application of the law to the

Additionally, the Court is mindful of the following principles of law that govern the 
Court’s review of this issue. To receive workers’ compensation benefits the injured employee 
has the burden of proving, by a preponderance of the evidence, that the employees injury arose 
out of and in the course of the employee’s employment with the employer.8 In order for an injury 
to be compensable in Iowa, there must be “a connection between the injury and the work.”9 An 
injury arises out of employment when a causal relationship exists between the employment and 
the injury.10 A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable, not 
merely possible.11  
After review of the administrative record, the Court finds the commissioner’s decision is 
supported by substantial evidence in the record. The decisions made by the commissioner and 
the deputy commissioner were based upon well-reasoned analyses of the record. Both the deputy 
commissioner and commissioner specifically referenced the administrative record in making

The deputy commissioner’s specific findings of fact are supported by the evidence in the record. 
The commissioner reached the same conclusion in the Appeal Decision and affirmed the 
Arbitration Decision in full. On December 5, 2019, the commissioner held in his Appeal

I find the deputy commissioner provided a well-reasoned analysis

establish he sustained work-related injury on or about May 25,

all other issues raised in this matter, with the exception of costs,

Based upon the Court’s review of the record, there is substantial evidence to support the 
decision reached by the commissioner.  
Additionally, the commissioner’s decision was not irrational, illogical, or wholly 
unjustifiable. The commissioner found:  
Claimant’s low back injury could be the result of, or an aggravation 
caused by, claimant’s work activities for the defendant-employer on 
or about May 25, 2016. Claimant’s injury could also be the result of 
a pre-existing, degenerative condition. It is also possible that the 
injury is the result of claimant’s personal activities, including 
falling while mowing the lawn. Given the three potential injury 
scenarios, claimant needed to provide convincing and credible 
testimony to explain the differences and convince the undersigned 
of the actual cause or mechanism of injury. Claimant’s credibility 
issues, coupled with his inability to convincingly refute credible 
allegations that his injury resulted from an unrelated fall, render this

The commissioner reviewed the medical opinions offered by Tew and concluded that 
they did not establish that his injuries occurred as a result of his work at Sparboe. The 
commissioner admitted that the injury could have been a cumulative injury but Tew failed to 
meet his burden to show that to be true, partly due to his lack of credibility. As the commissioner 
states: “. . . the expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence introduced 
bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability. . . . lay testimony may be 
used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is relevant and material to the question of 
causation.”14 While the cumulative injury doctrine could apply, Tew was unable to carry his 
burden and convince the commissioner that it did apply in this instance.  
The commissioner also directly addressed the reports of Tew’s three medical providers 
and the independent medical records review he submitted. The commissioner stated that he did 
not find Dr. Hatfield’s, Dr. Piemann’s, or Dr. McGuire’s opinions convincing as to this matter.15 
The commissioner also did not give the opinion of Dr. Bansal, who performed an independent 
medical records review for Tew, a great amount of weight and rejected his medical opinions.16

such as erroneous interpretation of law; irrational reasoning; failure to consider relevant facts; or 
irrational, illogical, or wholly unjustifiable application of law to the facts.”17 The commissioner’s
