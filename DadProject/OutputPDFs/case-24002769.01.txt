In a situation where there are two directly competing testimonies, a determination

medical records showed claimant reported a fall at home. (JE 2:22) This purported error

the testimony of the claimant about the phone call made to Mr. Bryan on January 3, 
2024. If Exhibit 5 is to be taken on its face, claimant would have had four phone calls to 
Mr. Bryan, lasting nearly 9 minutes in total. This is not consistent with claimant’s own

On January 4, 2024, claimant presented to Broadlawns Urgent Care with 
complaints of right shoulder, leg, and arm pain. (JE 2:22) X-rays showed degenerative 
changes in the acromioclavicular joint. (JE 2:26) Claimant was discharged with a sling

The medical records document that this was the result of a fall at home. (JE 2:22) 
Claimant testified that this was erroneously documented by a young and inattentive 
medical assistant. (Tr., pp. 28-29) He claimed he never told the intake personnel that he 
fell at home. (Id.) He also testified that there were other inconsistencies in the January 
4, 2024, medical record including that he had neck pain and that his leg pain was a prior 
injury. (Tr., p. 29) There was also a notation that claimant had fallen within the last 6 
months. (JE 2:25) Claimant testified that he meant that he had fallen the day prior but 
had no other falls. (Tr., pp. 30, 63) The leg pain was not meant to be associated with a 
prior injury but from the work incident. However, in medical history it was noted that 
claimant had cellulitis of the lower leg and diabetic neuropathy. (JE 2:22)  
To resolve the inconsistencies in claimant’s favor requires a finding that Mr. 
Wildeboar, Mr. Bryan, Amanda Jerkins, and Jim Jerkins were all lying and that the 
Broadlawns personnel and the DMOS staff failed to document his neck pain from 
January 2024 through June 2024. This is not a reasonable finding.  
Thus, it is determined that claimant is not a credible witness as it relates to the 
phone calls on the day of his alleged injury and that where there are inconsistencies, 
the documented medical records are given more weight than claimant’s testimony.  
The greater weight of the evidence supports a finding that the medical record of 
January 4, 2024, more accurately reflects the medical history claimant relayed to the 
intake personnel on January 4, 2024, than claimant’s recollection of what he told to the 
intake personnel.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury actually occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 
employment. Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 150 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. 
Dial Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309, 311 (Iowa 1996). The words “arising out of” refer to the 
cause or source of the injury. The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 
circumstances of the injury. 2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124, 128 (Iowa 
1995).  An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists 
between the injury and the employment. Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. The injury must 
be a rational consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely 
incidental to the employment. Koehler Elec. v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1, 3 (Iowa 2000); 
Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it 
happens within a period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may 
be when performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties 
or doing an activity incidental to them. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d at 150. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
A possibility of causation is not sufficient; a probability is necessary. Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996) (citing Holmes v. Bruce 
Motor Freight, Inc., 215 N.W.2d 296, 297 (Iowa 1974)).

The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 
621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 
(Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
The threshold issue for both cases against the defendant employer and insurer 
and the Second Injury Fund of Iowa is whether a work injury took place on January 3, 
2024. The contemporaneous medical evidence at the time documented a fall at home. 
Claimant’s testimony that he reported a work injury to dispatch and to his supervisor 
was rebutted by the credible testimony of the dispatcher, Mr. Wildeboer. The call log of 
January 3, 2024, and January 4, 2024, were not consistent with claimant’s testimony. 
Claimant was deemed to be a noncredible witness. Therefore, it is determined that 
claimant did not suffer a work injury on January 3, 2024, and that the injuries and 
symptoms claimant suffers are unrelated to a work injury of January 3, 2024. The 
remaining issues are moot.
