STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A.  The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Denishia Wilson.  
Claimant appeared through attorney, Rachael Neff.  Defendants did not appear or 
answer.  Proof of service was filed on February 14, 2024.  Defendant employer was 
properly served.  Claimant’s counsel further represented by way of professional 
statement at hearing that a courtesy copy was delivered to employer’s third-party 
administrator (TPA). 
 
The alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on February 27, 2024.  
The proceedings were digitally recorded on the agency’s recording software, Quality 
Management Suite.  That recording constitutes the official record of this proceeding.  
Pursuant to the Commissioner’s Order, the undersigned has been delegated authority 
to issue a final agency decision in this alternate medical care proceeding.  Therefore, 
this decision is designated final agency action and any appeal of the decision would be 
to the Iowa District Court pursuant to Iowa Code section 17A. 
 
The matter proceeded to hearing without the employer present.  The record was 
held open until 11:00 a.m.  The record consists of Claimant’s Exhibit 1, pages 1 through 
8.  The claimant subsequently filed 5 additional pages at the request of the undersigned 
proving that the employer and carrier were served the application.

ISSUE 
 
The issue presented for resolution is whether the claimant is entitled to further 
medical treatment. 
 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
The claimant sustained an injury which arose out of and in the course of her 
employment on November 3, 2023.  She had one medical visit back in November and 
has not had any follow up.  It is not entirely clear in this record whether this visit was an 
authorized visit or claimant secured it on her own.  In January 2024, claimant began 
requesting additional treatment through counsel.  She alleges her work injury has 
resulted in disability in the right side of her body, her right arm and shoulder, as well as 
her right hip and leg.   
 
On January 29, 2024, claimant’s counsel wrote to defendants through the TPA, 
Gallagher Bassett, requesting authorization to return to a physician.  “My client is in 
severe pain because of this accident and needs follow up medical care ASAP.”  
(Claimant’s Exhibit 1, page 7)  On February 1, 2024, the adjustor responded.  “Working 
on it.  I will call you tomorrow.”  (Cl. Ex. 1, p. 6)  Claimant’s counsel followed up on 
February 6, 2024.  “I called and left a message with you yesterday after you did not call 
again.  I would appreciate a phone call or update today, as Ms. Wilson is in need of 
medical care ASAP.”  (Cl. Ex. 1, pp. 5-6)  The same day, the adjustor responded that 
treatment was approved.  “Please confirm where is she seeking treatment and the Dr’s 
office address, appt date and time.”  (Cl. Ex. 1, p. 5)  The adjustor then agreed to 
arrange an appointment at Iowa Ortho.  (Cl. Ex. 1, p. 4)  Claimant’s counsel continued 
to follow up.  On February 12, 2024, she wrote:  “It has been a week now since you told 
me that you were authorizing medical care.  We need to know the details and 
transportation arrangements.”  (Cl. Ex. 1, p. 2)  On February 13, 2024, the adjustor 
wrote back assuring counsel that Iowa Ortho was reviewing the records.  (Cl. Ex. 1, p. 
1)  At that point, counsel wrote a statement of dissatisfaction.  (Cl. Ex. 1, p. 1) 
 
I find that this delay in authorization of care is unreasonable. 
 
REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Iowa Code Section 85.27 (2013). 
 
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See

Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  Determining what care is 
reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.  Id.  The employer’s obligation turns 
on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.  Id.; Harned v. Farmland 
Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983).   
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995). 
An employer’s statutory right is to select the providers of care and the employer 
may consider cost and other pertinent factors when exercising its choice. Long, at 124. 
An employer (typically) is not a licensed health care provider and does not possess 
medical expertise.  Accordingly, an employer does not have the right to control the 
methods the providers choose to evaluate, diagnose and treat the injured employee.  
An employer is not entitled to control a licensed health care provider’s exercise of 
professional judgment.  Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 866389 (Declaratory 
Ruling, May 18, 1988).  An employer’s failure to follow recommendations of an 
authorized physician in matters of treatment is commonly a failure to provide reasonable 
treatment.  Boggs v. Cargill, Inc., File No. 1050396 (Alt. Care, January 31, 1994). 
Based upon the record before me, I find that the defendants have unreasonably 
delayed claimant’s treatment by failing to arrange an appointment for her.  At hearing, 
claimant requested that defendants be ordered to arrange an appointment within one 
week.  Specifically, counsel indicated that claimant is “not picky” about the provider and 
just needs to be seen by someone.  Claimant is willing to see an occupational medicine 
clinic to get the process started.  It appears in this record that some of the delay may be 
caused by Iowa Ortho’s process for accepting new patients.  It is noted that sometimes 
it takes time to arrange an appointment with a specialist.  Claimant, however, has been 
clear with the defendants that a specialist is not required at this time.  Claimant is simply 
seeking an appointment with an authorized provider to move the process forward.  The 
defendants have been unable to secure any such appointment. 
In any event, in this record, I agree with the claimant.  It is unreasonable to have 
to wait for an appointment in these circumstances.  The defendants have a statutory 
responsibility to direct the medical care in this case including timely securing medical 
appointments.  If the defendants are unable to perform this requirement for any reason, 
the defendants may be deemed to have abandoned this responsibility and claimant may 
be allowed to direct her own care at the employer’s expense.  The failure of the 
defendants to even respond to claimant’s application lends toward this remedy.

ORDER 
 
THEREFORE IT IS ORDERED: 
 
The claimant's application for alternate medical care is GRANTED.  
Defendants shall immediately authorize a medical appointment for the claimant 
to be seen for her work injury.  Defendants shall make every reasonable effort to 
secure an appointment for the claimant with Iowa Ortho or any other provider 
within 10 days.  If an appointment is not secured within this timeframe, claimant 
may file a new petition to determine whether defendants have abandoned care.
