This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A. The 
expedited procedures of rule 876 IAC 4.48, the “alternate medical care” rule, are 
invoked by claimant, Jamie Higgenbotham.  
This alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on August 14, 2025. The 
proceedings were recorded digitally and constitute the official record of the hearing.  By 
an order filed by the Workers’ Compensation Commissioner, this decision is designated 
final agency action. Any appeal would be by petition for judicial review under Iowa Code 
section 17A.15(1).  
The record in this case consists of Claimant’s Exhibits 1-2 and Defendants’ 
Exhibit A 
ISSUE 
The issue presented for resolution in this case is whether claimant is entitled to 
alternate medical care consisting of authorization for specialists to treat claimant’s eye 
and ear complaints.  In the alternative, claimant requests authorization to treat with an 
occupational medicine specialist who may make referrals to an eye and ear specialist. 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
Defendants accept liability for an injury occurring on November 7, 2024.  
Claimant sustained a work-related head injury on that date.

On July 22, 2025, claimant was evaluated by Keith Kohout, D.O.  Dr. Kohout is 
an authorized provider for claimant’s work injury.  Dr. Kohout’s records appear to 
recommend claimant have a referral for a speech therapist.  (Exhibit A, page 5) 
Rebecca Nichols, R.N., is a nurse case manager for defendant insurer and is 
involved with claimant’s care for this claim.  In an August 6, 2025, email, Nurse Nichols 
indicates she was at the July 22, 2025 meeting with Dr. Kohout and claimant.  The 
email indicates claimant requested consideration for an evaluation with an eye and ear 
specialist. Nurse Nichols indicates Dr. Kohout did not recommend this treatment.   
Nurse Nichols also notes Dr. Kohout indicated he would prefer an occupational 
medicine specialist make recommendations regarding referrals.  Nurse Nichols 
indicated “This conversation was not specific to sending Ms. Higgenbotham to 
occupational medicine for those referrals.”  (Ex. A, p. 4) 
In a July 31, 2025, email, claimant’s attorney asked defense counsel to authorize 
an appointment with an occupational health doctor so the referrals could be made for 
claimant to an ear and an eye specialist.  (Ex. 1) 
In an August 4, 2025, email, defense counsel indicated he reviewed Dr. Kohout’s 
records and did not see a referral to an occupational specialist.  As a result, defendants 
would not authorize a referral at this time.  (Ex. 2) 
In an August 7, 2025, letter, defense counsel asked Dr. Kohout if a referral to an 
eye or ear specialist was medically necessary and reasonable to treat claimant.  The 
letter also asked that if such referrals were medically necessary and/or reasonable, who 
would Dr. Kohout have make those referrals for claimant.  As of the time of the hearing, 
Dr. Kohout had not responded to that letter. (Ex. A, p. 6) 
CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence. Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3). 
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers’ compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Section 85.27.  Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening October 16, 1975).          
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable. See Iowa 
Rule of Appellate Procedure 6.904(3); Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 
(Iowa 1995). Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of

fact. Id. The employer’s obligation turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not 
desirability. Id.; Harned v. Farmland Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983). 
Claimant requests authorization for specialists to treat claimant’s eye and ear 
complaints.  In the alternative, claimant requests authorization to treat with an 
occupational medicine specialist who may make those referrals. 
Defendants have provided care for claimant’s work-related head injury with Dr. 
Kohout.  Dr. Kohout has not recommended claimant be treated by an ear or eye 
specialist.  Dr. Kohout has not specifically referred or recommended claimant be treated 
by an occupational medicine specialist.  Defendants have written Dr. Kohout asking if a 
referral to an occupational medicine specialist is reasonable and necessary.  At the time 
of the hearing, Dr. Kohout had not responded to that inquiry.  Defendants have 
indicated that if Dr. Kohout recommends a referral to an occupational medicine 
specialist, they will authorize and provide that referral.  Given the facts as detailed 
above, claimant has failed to carry her burden of proof the care provided by defendants 
is unreasonable. 
ORDER 
THEREFORE, IT IS ORDERED: The claimant’s petition for alternate medical 
care is denied.
