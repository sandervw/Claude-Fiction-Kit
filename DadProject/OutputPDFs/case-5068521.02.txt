On August 14, 2020, claimant filed an application for alternate medical care 
under Iowa Code section 85.27 and rule 876 IAC 4.48.  A hearing was held on August 
26, 2020.  All parties were given proper notice.  
Claimant alleges she sustained an injury on May 17, 2019, resulting in back, left 
knee, hip, and buttock pain.  Claimant is dissatisfied with the medical treatment offered 
by the defendants due to failure to provide ongoing treatment and care.  
Defendants admitted liability for the back and left knee but denied liability for all 
other symptomatology  
Claimant seeks a second opinion of her left knee, pain management treatment 
and additional imaging. Defendants refuse to provide such care.  
At hearing, claimant testified that she continues to have pain in her low back that 
radiates into her left butt cheek, left knee, and down into her toes. She cannot fully 
straighten her left knee. She experiences pain and throbbing at night that keeps her up 
and affects her ability to work and parent.  
Defendants exhibit include the diagnostic studies performed of the knee on 
August 20, 2019, clinical notes of the same, concluding claimant had degenerative 
changes in the left knee. (Ex. A:1-3)  On April 30, 2020, Jonathon Fields, M.D., issued 
an IME opinion. (Def A) Dr. Fields concluded claimant’s current conditions were related

During testimony, claimant agreed that Dr. Abernathey, a surgeon, did not

persistent left button pain with radiation. He recommended that she undergo additional 
evaluation for her back pain and left knee.  
It is found claimant has credible complaints of left knee and low back pain arising 
out of an accepted work injury. It is further found that defendants are not offering care 
for these complaints at this time. 
Iowa Code section 85.27(4) provides, in relevant part: 
For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish 
reasonable services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has 
the right to choose the care. . . .  The treatment must be offered promptly 
and be reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience 
to the employee.  If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the 
care offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such 
dissatisfaction to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the 
employer and the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited 
to treat the injury.  If the employer and employee cannot agree on such 
alternate care, the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable 
proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995). 
The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except where the 
employer has denied liability for the injury.  Section 85.27; Holbert v. Townsend

“Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.”  
Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122, 123 (Iowa 1995). 
Alternate care included alternate physicians when there is a breakdown in a 
physician/patient relationship.  Seibert v. State of Iowa, File No. 938579 (September 14, 
1994); Nueone v. John Morrell & Co., File No. 1022976 (January 27, 1994); Williams v. 
High Rise Const., File No. 1025415 (February 24, 1993); Wallech v. FDL, File 
No. 1020245 (September 3, 1992) (aff’d Dist Ct June 21, 1993). 
The summary provisions of Iowa Code section 85.27 as more particularly 
described in rule 876 IAC 4.48 are not designed to adjudicate disputed compensability 
of a claim.  Defendants cannot deny liability and simultaneously direct the course of 
treatment.  Barnhart v. MAQ Incorporated, I Iowa Industrial Comm’r Report 16 (App. 
March 9, 1981).    
Defendants’ exhibits and questioning at hearing were directed toward proving 
that the current pain complaints of the claimant are related to a pre-existing chronic 
illness rather than symptoms arising out of a work related injury. The evidence and 
arguments go to the issue of causation rather than addressing whether the care 
provided for the accepted work injuries is reasonable.  
To the extent that any treatment claimant seeks is denied by the defendants 
because of causation, the defendants cannot later use the authorization defense to 
avoid responsibility for bills that are deemed causally connected to a work injury.  
Defendants’ current treatment plan for the claimant is no treatment and given 
claimant’s ongoing complaints of low back and left knee pain, no treatment is not 
reasonable.  Claimant’s petition for alternate medical care in the form of a second 
opinion, as well as pain management regarding her left knee and back are granted.
