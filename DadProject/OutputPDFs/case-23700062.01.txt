STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
  
Jesus Gutierrez-Cruz, claimant, filed a petition for arbitration against Perishable 
Distributors of Iowa, Ltd., as the employer, and Union Insurance Company of 
Providence, as the insurance carrier. This case came before the undersigned for an 
arbitration hearing on April 18, 2024, via Zoom.  
  
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the hearing. On the 
hearing report, the parties entered into numerous stipulations. Those stipulations were 
accepted and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties' stipulations will be made 
or discussed. The parties are now bound by their stipulations. 
  
The evidentiary record includes Joint Exhibits 1 through 4, Claimant's Exhibits 1 
through 5, and Defendants' Exhibits A through H. All exhibits were received without 
objection. 
  
Mr. Gutierrez-Cruz testified on his own behalf using the services of a Spanish to 
English interpreter. The evidentiary record closed at the conclusion of the evidentiary 
hearing. All parties served their post-hearing briefs on May 31, 2024, at which time this 
case was deemed fully submitted to the undersigned.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
  
The undersigned, having considered all of the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
  
On the date of hearing, Jesus Gutierrez-Cruz was a 43-year-old individual living 
in Iowa and working for Perishable Distributors of Iowa. (Hearing Transcript, pages 13-
14)  Mr. Gutierrez-Cruz began working for Perishable Distributors of Iowa as a Utility 
Associate on November 28, 2011. (Defendants’ Exhibit C, p. 23)  As a Utility Associate, 
Mr. Gutierrez-Cruz performed general and detailed cleaning of a warehouse facility. (Ex. 
C, p. 24)  Mr. Gutierrez-Cruz was working as a Utility Associate on December 20, 2022, 
when he injured his left shoulder while lifting garbage cans. (Ex. G, Depo. p. 7)   
 
After experiencing immediate pain in his left shoulder, Mr. Gutierrez-Cruz 
reported his injury to his supervisor and was sent to UnityPoint Health – Des Moines 
Occupational Medicine. (Hr. Tr., p. 15; see Joint Exhibit 1, p. 1)  Katie Swift, ARNP, 
examined Mr. Gutierrez-Cruz and diagnosed left shoulder pain, noting differentials could 
include, “shoulder impingement, biceps tendon tendinitis, rotator cuff tendinitis, or 
bursitis.” (JE1, p. 3)  Ms. Swift prescribed medication and recommended modified work 
duties. (Id.)  Defendants did not provide Mr. Gutierrez-Cruz an interpreter for his 
medical appointments with Ms. Swift. 
 
As instructed, Mr. Gutierrez-Cruz returned to Ms. Swift for a follow-up 
appointment on December 28, 2022. (JE1, p. 5)  He reported an overall improvement in 
his condition since the date of injury, which included zero out of ten pain and no issues 
with range of motion. (Id.)  His physical exam was negative for any concerns. (Id.)  Ms. 
Swift opined that Mr. Gutierrez-Cruz’s evaluation was consistent with the diagnosis of 
left shoulder bursitis that improved following a course of prednisone. (Id.)  Given said 
improvement, Ms. Swift placed Mr. Gutierrez-Cruz at maximum medical improvement 
and released him to full duty work. (Id.) 
 
Several months later, Mr. Gutierrez-Cruz sought an independent medical 
evaluation with Jacqueline Stoken, D.O. The examination occurred on September 13, 
2023. (Claimant’s Exhibit 1, p. 1)  Claimant did not seek any additional medical 
treatment for his shoulder between December 28, 2022, and September 13, 2023.

During the examination, Mr. Gutierrez-Cruz reported left shoulder pain that he 
described as shooting and burning. (Ex. 1, p. 3)  Mr. Gutierrez-Cruz demonstrated left 
shoulder flexion to 120 degrees, extension to 50 degrees, adduction to 30 degrees,

management, including periodic physician visits, medications, and/or injections. (Id.)  
Given his ongoing complaints of pain, Dr. Stoken also recommended an orthopedic 
evaluation and declined to place Mr. Gutierrez-Cruz at maximum medical improvement. 
(Ex. 1, p. 5) 
 
Despite her position on maximum medical improvement, Dr. Stoken elected to 
perform an impairment assessment.  Utilizing the American Medical Association Guides 
to the Evaluation of Permanent Impairment, Fifth Edition, she assigned 10 percent left 
upper extremity impairment due to the deficits in Mr. Gutierrez-Cruz’s range of motion. 
(Id.)  Dr. Stoken recommended Mr. Gutierrez-Cruz avoid repetitive work at or above the 
shoulder level.  She also recommended he avoid lifting more than 15 pounds at or 
above shoulder level on an occasional basis. (Id.)  Despite the “at or above shoulder 
level” qualification, Dr. Stoken opined that her recommendations for permanent 
restrictions placed Mr. Gutierrez-Cruz in the “Light” category of work. (Id.) 
 
In response, defendants sought a records review from Jon Yankey, M.D.  Dr. 
Yankey produced his report on September 27, 2023. (Ex. A, pp. 1-3)  Dr. Yankey 
assessed Mr. Gutierrez-Cruz with left shoulder bursitis and remarked that the medical 
records documented no evidence of bony injury or structural soft tissue injury. (Ex. A, p. 
2)  He further opined that Mr. Gutierrez-Cruz’s rapid improvement is consistent with a 
mild, non-structural, soft tissue injury. (Id.)  Dr. Yankey did not believe that Mr. 
Gutierrez-Cruz required any additional medical treatment. (Id.)  He placed Mr. 
Gutierrez-Cruz at maximum medical improvement on December 28, 2022, and 
assigned zero percent left upper extremity impairment. (Ex. A, p. 3) 
 
Despite Dr. Yankey’s opinion, defendants authorized Mr. Gutierrez-Cruz to 
attend an orthopedic evaluation with Steven Aviles, M.D. (See JE2, p. 7)  The 
evaluation occurred on December 13, 2023. (JE2, p. 7)  Mr. Gutierrez-Cruz complained 
of pain, numbness, paresthesia, and decreased range of motion. (Id.)  He told Dr. Aviles 
that he has experienced persistent pain since his release in December 2022, and he 
wanted to get a second opinion on the matter. (Id.)  On examination, Mr. Gutierrez-Cruz 
demonstrated full flexion to 180 degrees, external rotation to 90 degrees, and internal 
rotation to 70 degrees. (Id.)  Dr. Aviles opined that Mr. Gutierrez-Cruz’s history and 
symptoms were consistent with either a SLAP tearing or biceps labral complex tearing. 
(Id.)  Given these concerns, Dr. Aviles ordered an MR arthrogram of the left shoulder. 
(Id.) 
 
The February 1, 2024, MR arthrogram revealed supraspinatus tendinosis without 
rotator cuff tear, and mild AC joint degenerative changes. (JE3, p. 9)

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
  
The fighting issue in this case is the extent of claimant’s entitlement to permanent 
partial disability benefits. 
 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
 
 The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 
621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 
(Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). 
  
Under the Iowa Workers' Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is 
compensated either for a loss or loss of use of a scheduled member under Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(a)-(u) or for loss of earning capacity under section 85.34(2)(v). The 
extent of scheduled member disability benefits to which an injured worker is entitled is

determined by using the functional method. Functional disability is “limited to the loss of 
the physiological capacity of the body or body part.” Mortimer v. Fruehauf Corp., 502 
N.W.2d 12, 15 (Iowa 1993); Sherman v. Pella Corp., 576 N.W.2d 312, 320 (Iowa 1998). 
   
The parties stipulated that claimant's injury should be compensated as a 
scheduled member injury to the shoulder. Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(n) (2018).  The 
Iowa legislature enacted significant amendments to the Iowa workers' compensation 
laws, which took effect in July 2017. As part of those amendments, the legislature 
specified that injuries to the shoulder should be compensated as a scheduled member 
injury on a 400-week schedule. Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(n) (2018). 
  
In all cases of permanent partial disability described in paragraphs “a” through 
“u”, or paragraph “v” when determining functional disability and not loss of earning 
capacity, the extent of loss or percentage of permanent impairment shall be determined 
solely by utilizing the guides to the evaluation of permanent impairment, published by 
the American Medical Association, as adopted by the workers' compensation 
commissioner by rule pursuant to chapter 17A. Lay testimony or agency expertise shall 
not be utilized in determining loss or percentage of permanent impairment pursuant to 
paragraphs “a” through ““u”, or paragraph “v” when determining functional disability and 
not loss of earning capacity. Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(x) 
  
Based on the expert opinions of Dr. Aviles, I found that claimant sustained one 
percent loss of function in his shoulder as a result of the December 20, 2022, work 
injury. Claimant is entitled to an award of permanent partial disability benefits equivalent 
to the proportional loss of his shoulder. Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(w); Blizek v. Eagle 
Signal Company, 164 N.W.2d 84 (Iowa 1969). One percent (1%) of 400 weeks is 4 
weeks. Claimant is therefore entitled to an award of 4 weeks of permanent partial 
disability benefits against defendants as a result of the December 20, 2022, left 
shoulder injury.   
 
Under Iowa Code section 85.34(2), the commencement date for permanent 
partial disability benefits occurs when it is medically indicated that maximum medical 
improvement from the injury has been reached and that the extent of loss or percentage 
of permanent impairment can be determined by using of the Guides.   
 
I reject claimant’s assertion that December 28, 2022, is the proper 
commencement date for permanent partial disability benefits.  At the time of the 
claimant’s discharge from care by Ms. Swift on December 28, 2022, no permanent 
impairment was assigned, and the claimant returned to full duty work activities.  The 
claimant later obtained an independent medical evaluation report that prompted 
defendants to authorize additional treatment.  Dr. Aviles was concerned that the 
claimant had a SLAP tear or biceps labral complex tearing.  As such, he obtained 
diagnostic imaging and administered a corticosteroid injection.  The treatment claimant 
received from Dr. Aviles was administered with the intent of improving his condition.

I accept Dr. Aviles’ opinion that claimant reached maximum medical 
improvement on March 28, 2024.  While claimant was placed at maximum medical 
improvement due to non-compliance, the evidence suggests that his condition had 
stabilized on or about March 28, 2024, and was unlikely to substantially improve with 
additional medical intervention.  After being placed at maximum medical improvement 
by Dr. Aviles, claimant did not seek additional treatment on his own and he returned to 
full duty work with the defendant employer.  The evidentiary record supports a 
reasonable finding that the claimant reached maximum medical improvement on March 
28, 2024.  Claimant’s permanent partial disability benefits shall commence on March 28, 
2024. 
 
The final issue for determination is a specific taxation of costs pursuant to Iowa 
Code section 10A.328 and rule 876 IAC 4.33. Claimant requests taxation of the cost of 
the filing fee ($103.00). (Ex. 4, p. 24)  The cost of the filing fee is an allowable cost and 
assessed pursuant to 876 IAC 4.33(7).  Claimant also requests taxation of the service 
fees ($8.10). (Id.)  The cost of service fees is an allowable cost and assessed pursuant 
to 876 IAC 4.33(3).   
 
Claimant requests taxation of the costs associated with Dr. Stoken’s IME report 
($400.00).  While claimant listed $400.00 as the cost of Dr. Stoken’s report, there is no 
evidence in the record to substantiate this billing or denote what portion of that cost was 
for the actual report.  Claimant did not provide a breakdown of Dr. Stoken's IME bill.  He 
similarly failed to produce the bill for Dr. Stoken’s subsequent report.  As such, I decline 
claimant’s request for taxation of Dr. Stoken’s reports. 
    
ORDER 
  
THEREFORE, IT IS ORDERED: 
  
Defendants shall pay claimant four (4) weeks of permanent partial disability 
benefits commencing on March 28, 2024. 
  
All weekly benefits shall be paid at the stipulated weekly rate of six hundred sixty-
four and 08/100 dollars ($664.08). 
  
Defendants shall be entitled to credit for all weekly benefits paid to date. 
  
Defendants shall pay accrued weekly benefits in a lump sum together with 
interest at an annual rate equal to the one-year treasury constant maturity published by 
the federal reserve in the most recent H15 report settled as of the date of injury, plus 
two percent. See Gamble v. AG Leader Technology, File No. 5054686 (App. Apr. 24, 
2018). 
  
Defendants shall timely file all reports as required by 876 IAC 11.7.
