STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Nathaniel Ewell, filed a petition for arbitration against John Deere 
Waterloo Works, a self-insured employer.  The hearing occurred before the undersigned 
on October 1, 2024, via Zoom.   
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the hearing.  In the 
hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of those stipulations 
were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration decision, and no factual 
or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised or discussed in this 
decision.  The parties are bound by their stipulations. 
The evidentiary record consists of Joint Exhibits 1 through 3, Claimant’s Exhibits 
1 through 7, and Defendant’s Exhibits A through H.  Claimant testified on his own 
behalf.  The evidentiary record closed at the conclusion of the evidentiary hearing on 
October 1, 2024.  The case was considered fully submitted upon submission of post-
hearing briefs on November 8, 2024. 
ISSUES 
The parties submitted the following disputed issues for resolution: 
1. Whether the stipulated injury is a cause of permanent disability, and, if so, the 
nature and extent of permanent disability; 
2. Whether defendant is entitled to credit for benefits paid; and 
3. Whether defendant is entitled to credit under Iowa Code section 85.38(2) for 
the payment of sick pay/disability income.

The initial disputed issue is whether Mr. Ewell has proven a work injury to his left 
arm as a result of the September 24, 2021, work injury.  Specifically, Mr. Ewell asserts 
entitlement to permanent partial disability benefits for both an injury to the left shoulder 
and an injury to the left arm. 
Mr. Ewell’s argument that he sustained a left arm injury is not supported by the 
medical evidence or applicable legal precedent. While he asserts that he consistently 
reported left arm symptoms, the medical records in evidence do not bear this out. The 
initial medical records document left shoulder and left wrist pain, but Mr. Ewell reported 
complete resolution of the wrist pain within three days and subsequently denied any 
ongoing wrist symptoms. (JE1, pp. 29, 31-32) A resolved wrist complaint does not 
substantiate an injury to the left arm itself. 
 
Mr. Ewell argues specific complaints relating to the left arm, hand, wrist, and 
biceps are well-documented throughout the medical records.  In his post-hearing brief, 
Mr. Ewell provides several examples of what he believes are left arm complaints.  
These include, “states still hurts him to drive with left arm[,]” “has pain in anterior 
shoulder and it causes pain to steer the wheel of his car with that arm[,]” “still has pain 
driving with that left hand[,]”.  Mr. Ewell also points to his temporary restrictions which 
provided, “no use of the left arm.” (See JE1, pp. 13-15) 
Mr. Ewell’s reliance on general references to the “left arm” in the medical 
records—such as statements that it hurt to drive with the left arm or restrictions on left 
arm use—does not establish an arm injury. These references reflect functional 
limitations resulting from the accepted left shoulder injury, as the shoulder is responsible 
for much of the arm’s movement.  The presence of pain when using the arm does not 
indicate that the arm itself is injured, but rather that the shoulder injury restricts normal 
arm function. 
Mr. Ewell further asserts he consistently reported left arm symptoms to his 
treating physicians.  As noted by defendant, the contemporaneous medical records fail 
to detail significant or ongoing left arm symptoms.  I find it highly improbable that all of 
Mr. Ewell’s treating physicians failed to document significant and ongoing complaints of 
left arm symptoms.   
Additionally, the medical records do not contain any diagnosis, complaints, or 
objective findings specific to the left arm independent of the left shoulder.  Instead, the 
evidentiary record provides that Mr. Ewell sustained a rotator cuff tear and a proximal 
biceps tear, both of which are classified as shoulder injuries.  Indeed, Dr. Bansal’s 
report provides no discussion of a left arm injury at all.  Instead, Dr. Bansal consistently 
describes Mr. Ewell’s condition as a left shoulder injury.   
Mr. Ewell’s attempt to characterize his condition as an arm injury is 
unpersuasive.  Additionally, as will be discussed in the conclusions of law section, legal 
precedent does not support a finding that injury to the proximal biceps is an injury to the 
arm for purposes of calculating permanent partial disability benefits.  Therefore, I find 
that Mr. Ewell failed to prove he sustained a distinct injury to the left arm as a result of 
the September 24, 2021, work injury.  I find Mr. Ewell sustained a cumulative injury to

the left shoulder.  Both Dr. Garrels and Dr. Bansal assigned 13 percent left upper 
extremity impairment for loss of range of motion in the left shoulder.  Although Dr.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
The fighting issue in this case is whether Mr. Ewell sustained a left arm injury 
separate and distinct from his left shoulder injury. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury actually occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 
employment. Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. Dial 
Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309 (Iowa 1996). The words “arising out of” refer to the cause or 
source of the injury. The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 
circumstances of the injury. 2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124 (Iowa 1995).  
An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists between 
the injury and the employment. Miedema, 551 N.W.2d 309, 311 (Iowa 1996). The injury 
must be a rational consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not 
merely incidental to the employment. Koehler Elec. v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1 (Iowa 2000); 
Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it 
happens within a period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may 
be when performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties 
or doing an activity incidental to them. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 150. 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.

Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410, 420 
(Iowa 2001); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 1994). 
 
Defendant concedes that Mr. Ewell sustained a compensable left shoulder claim; 
however, defendant disputes whether he sustained a left arm injury separate and 
distinct from the left shoulder injury.   
 
The matter at hand involves injuries to the rotator cuff and the proximal biceps.  
These muscles and tendons have previously been determined to be part of the 
“shoulder” pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(n).  Our Supreme Court provided the 
most analysis of this issue in Chavez v. MS Technology LLC, 972 N.W.2d 662 (Iowa 
2020). The claimant in that case felt a pop in her right shoulder, and suffered a full 
thickness rotator cuff tear, which retracted to the glenoid. Id. at 668. She also was 
diagnosed with severe AC arthrosis, tendonitis, and tearing of her biceps tendon. The 
Commissioner and court ruled that the shoulder includes the shoulder structure, 
including injuries to the tendons, ligaments, muscles, and articular surfaces connected 
to the glenohumeral joint. Id. The court ruled that the legislature did not intend to limit 
the definition of the shoulder solely to the glenohumeral joint. Id. The Commissioner and 
court also noted that the claimant suffered biceps tendonitis and tearing, and that she 
underwent a biceps tenotomy along with her shoulder surgery. Id. at 671. Notably, the 
Court noted that Ms. Chavez did not suffer a permanent impairment to her right arm due 
to her biceps injury. Id. at 671.  Accordingly, I find Mr. Ewell’s injuries to the rotator cuff 
and the proximal biceps are injuries to the shoulder pursuant to Iowa Code section 
85.34(2)(n). 
 
Having found the opinions of Dr. Bartelt and Dr. Garrels to be most convincing, 
as well as the relative absence of reports of left arm symptoms throughout the medical 
records, I found that claimant failed to prove he sustained a separate and distinct left 
arm injury as a result of the September 24, 2021, work injury.  Accordingly, I conclude 
that claimant failed to carry his burden of proof to establish a work-related injury to the 
left arm. The question then becomes the proper impairment rating for Mr. Ewell’s left 
shoulder injury.   
Under the Iowa Workers' Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is 
compensated either for a loss or loss of use of a scheduled member under Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(a)-(u) or for loss of earning capacity under section 85.34(2)(v). The 
extent of scheduled member disability benefits to which an injured worker is entitled is 
determined by using the functional method. Functional disability is “limited to the loss of 
the physiological capacity of the body or body part.” Mortimer v. Fruehauf Corp., 502 
N.W.2d 12, 15 (Iowa 1993); Sherman v. Pella Corp., 576 N.W.2d 312, 320 (Iowa 1998).

In 2017, the legislature amended the Iowa Workers' Compensation Act. See 
2017 Iowa Acts ch. 23. Before the 2017 amendments, the agency could use all 
evidence in the administrative record, as well as agency expertise, when determining 
the permanent disability of an injured worker. See, e.g., Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 
525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Under agency rules before the 2017 amendments, 
the Guides were considered a “useful tool in evaluating disability.” Seaman v. City of 
Des Moines, File Nos. 5053418, 5057973, 5057974 (App. Oct. 11, 2019) (quoting 
Bisenius v. Mercy Med. Ctr., File No. 5036055 (App. Apr. 1, 2013)); see also Westling v. 
Hormel Foods Corp., 810 N.W.2d 247, 252 (Iowa 2012). However, in cases involving 
injuries on or after July 1, 2017, the Guides are now more than a tool; they are 
dispositive. 
[W]hen determining functional disability and not loss of earning capacity, 
the extent of loss or percentage of permanent impairment shall be 
determined solely by utilizing the guides to the evaluation of permanent 
impairment, published by the American medical association, as adopted 
by the workers' compensation commissioner by rule pursuant to chapter 
17A. Lay testimony or agency expertise shall not be utilized in determining 
loss or percentage of permanent impairment pursuant to paragraphs “a” 
through “u”, or paragraph “v” when determining functional disability and 
not loss of earning capacity.   
Iowa Code § 85.34(2)(x). 
The Iowa Workers' Compensation Act now limits the determination of what, if 
any, permanent disability an injured employee has sustained to only the employee's 
functional impairment. In making that determination, the agency is prohibited from using 
lay testimony or agency expertise by Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(x). Under the statute, 
that determination must be made “solely by utilizing” the Fifth Edition of the Guides. 
 
I found Dr. Garrels’ impairment assessment to be reasonable and reliable.  
Therefore, I find that the claimant had a 13 percent permanent impairment based upon 
the range of motion issues in his left shoulder.  Under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(n), 
compensation for the loss of a shoulder is based on a percentage of 400 weeks of 
benefits.  Permanent partial disability benefits for 13 percent left upper extremity 
impairment is equal to 52 weeks of benefits. 
Compensation for permanent disability shall begin when it is medically indicated 
that MMI from the injury has been achieved and the extent of loss or percentage of 
permanent impairment can be determined by using the Guides. Iowa Code section 
85.34(2). Based on the opinion of Dr. Bartelt, and the stipulations of the parties, I 
conclude the appropriate date for commencement of permanent partial disability 
benefits is October 6, 2022.

ORDER 
 
THEREFORE, IT IS ORDERED: 
 
Defendant shall pay claimant fifty-two (52) weeks of permanent partial disability 
benefits commencing on October 6, 2022, at the stipulated weekly rate of six hundred 
seventy-six and 81/100 dollars ($676.81). 
 
Defendant shall be entitled to a credit for all permanent partial disability benefits 
paid to date. 
Defendant shall pay accrued weekly benefits in a lump sum together with interest 
payable at an annual rate equal to the one-year treasury constant maturity published by 
the federal reserve in the most recent H15 report settled as of the date of injury, plus 
two percent, as required by Iowa Code section 85.30. 
 
Defendant shall file subsequent reports of injury (SROI) as required by this 
agency pursuant to rules 876 IAC 3.1(2) and 876 IAC 11.7.
