STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Matthew George, claimant, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from Bottling Group, LLC, employer, and Indemnity Insurance 
Company of North America, insurance carrier, as defendants.  The hearing was held on 
March 27, 2024.  Pursuant to an order from the Iowa Workers’ Compensation 
Commissioner, this case was heard via videoconference using Zoom with all parties 
and the court reporter appearing remotely.   
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  Those 
stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration decision and 
no factual issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised or discussed in this 
decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.  
Matthew George was the only witness to testify live at trial.  The evidentiary 
record also includes joint exhibits 1-7. All exhibits were received into the record without 
objection.  
The parties submitted post-hearing briefs on April 23, 2024, at which time the 
case was fully submitted to the undersigned.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned, having considered all the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds as follows: 
At the time of the hearing the claimant, Matthew George (hereinafter “George”), 
was 57 years old and residing with his family in Des Moines, Iowa. (Joint Exhibit 7, p. 
76).  In April 2012, Bottling Group, LLC, a/k/a Pepsi, (hereinafter “Bottling Group”) hired 
George to work in the vending department. (Id. at 76; 80; Transcript, p. 25).  At the time 
of the hearing, George was still employed by Bottling Group as a forklift driver in the 
warehouse.  (JE 7, p. 76; Tr. 10).  He works second shift from 2:00 p.m. to 10:30 p.m. 
with some overtime and earns around $25.50 an hour.  (JE 7, pp. 76-77).  At the 
hearing, George acknowledged that this is more than he was making at the time of his 
work injury on July 6, 2017.1 (Tr., pp. 11, 25).  
On July 6, 2017, George injured his back while helping another employee restack 
product in a trailer. (Tr., pp. 11-12).  He testified he was helping a truckdriver manually 
lift and restack product inside a trailer, which had fallen during transit, when he felt 
something give in his back. (Tr., p. 12).  George stated his employer accepted liability 
for the injury and sent him to Concentra for care. (Id.).  There are no medical records 
from Concentra in evidence, but according to a report authored by Joseph Chen, M.D., 
George first received treatment at Concentra on July 21, 2017.  (JE 5, p. 49).  On that 
date he was evaluated by Carlos Moe, D.O., for low back, hip and leg pain. (Id.).  Dr. 
Moe ordered an MRI of his lumbar spine, physical therapy,2 and provided George with 
work restrictions.  (Id.).   
The lumbar MRI took place on August 4, 2017. (JE 5, p. 49; JE 3, p. 32).  It 
showed multilevel age indeterminate spondylosis changes which were capable of 
causing nerve root impingement at several levels. (JE 3, p. 32). George had a follow-up 
appointment at Concentra on August 7, 2017. (See JE 5, p. 49).  He was referred for a 
neurosurgery consultation. (Id.).  
On August 28, 2017, George was evaluated by David Boarini, M.D., at the Iowa 
Clinic. (JE 1, p. 1).  George complained of pain in his right hip through his right calf, as 
well as numbness and tingling in his foot. (Id.).  Dr. Boarini diagnosed him with lumbar 
radiculopathy. (Id. at 2).  He also noted George had a disk protrusion at L4-L5. (Id. at 3).  
He recommended an epidural steroid injection, nonsteroidals, and to quit smoking. (Id.).

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 
employment.  Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. Dial 
Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309 (Iowa 1996).  The words “arising out of” refer to the cause or 
source of the injury.  The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 
circumstances of the injury.  2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124 (Iowa 1995).  
An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists between the 
injury and the employment.  Miedema, 551 N.W.2d 309.  The injury must be a rational 
consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely incidental to 
the employment.  Koehler Electric v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1 (Iowa 2000); Miedema, 551 
N.W.2d 309.  An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it happens within a 
period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may be when 
performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties or doing 
an activity incidental to them.  Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only

cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony.  The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question.  The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances.  The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); 
Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995).  Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994).  Unrebutted expert medical 
testimony cannot be summarily rejected.  Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 
N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 1994). 
When an expert's opinion is based upon an incomplete or incorrect history, it is 
not necessarily binding on the commissioner or the court. It is then to be weighed, 
together with other facts and circumstances, the ultimate conclusion being for the finder 
of the fact.  Musselman v. Central Telephone Company, 261 Iowa 352, 154 N.W.2d 
128, 133 (Iowa 1967); Bodish v. Fischer, Inc., 257 Iowa 516, 522, 133 N.W.2d 867, 870 
(1965).  The commissioner as trier of fact has the duty to determine the credibility of the 
witnesses and to weigh the evidence, together with the other disclosed facts and 
circumstances, and then to accept or reject the opinion.  Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and 
Casualty Co., 526 N.W.2d 845. 853 (Iowa 1995). 
Both parties agree that George sustained a work injury to his back, a part of his 
body as a whole, and that he continues to work full time for Bottling Group, making 
more than he did at the time of the injury in 2017. (Claimant’s Post-Hearing Brief, p. 1; 
Defendants’ Post-Hearing Brief, p. 2).  This means George will be compensated 
functionally under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v).  See Iowa Code § 85.34(2)(v) (stating 
“if an employee who is eligible for compensation under this paragraph returns to work . . 
. for which the employee receives . . . the same or greater salary, wages, or earnings 
than the employee received at the time of the injury, the employee shall be 
compensated based only upon the employee’s functional impairment resulting from the 
injury . . .”).  Compensation for functional loss for an injury to the whole body is 
determined by taking the number of weeks allowed for the whole body, which is 500 
weeks, multiplied by the extent or percentage of impairment determined using the AMA 
Guides. Iowa Code § 85.34(2)(x). The Workers' Compensation Commissioner has 
adopted the AMA Guides 5th Edition for evaluating permanent impairment. 876 IAC 2.4. 
The record contains three opinions addressing permanent impairment to 
George’s low back—one from Dr. Boarini, one from Dr. Bansal, and one from Dr. Chen.

Both Dr. Boarini and Dr. Chen assigned George 8 percent permanent impairment to the 
whole body, and Dr. Bansal assigned him 12 percent whole body impairment.   
Ratings for injuries to the spine, such as George’s, are covered by Chapter 15 of 
the AMA Guides.  See AMA Guides, p. 373.  That chapter explains that spinal 
impairments are given using one of two methods: (1) the diagnosis-related estimate 
(DRE) or (2) the range-of-motion (ROM).  Id. at 379.  DRE is the primary methodology 
used to evaluate individuals that have a distinct spinal injury.  Id.  Both Dr. Bansal and 
Dr. Chen used the DRE method to evaluate George’s permanent impairment. Dr. 
Boarini’s report does not articulate what method he used to assign George’s impairment 
rating.  The DRE method provides five diagnosis-related categories for each region of 
the spine. Id. at 381. Individuals are assigned to a category based upon symptoms, 
signs, appropriate diagnostic test results, and the presence of fractures and/or 
dislocations. Id.  The five DRE lumbar categories are contained in Table 15-3.  Id. at 
384.  Both Dr. Bansal and Dr. Chen cited to Table 15-3 in their proposed impairment 
ratings; they just differ on which category they assign to George’s injury—Dr. Bansal 
places the injury in DRE Lumbar Category III and Dr. Chen places his injury in DRE 
Lumbar Category II.  
Dr. Boarini, the treating physician in this case, diagnosed George with a 
herniated disk at L4-L5, and lumbar radiculopathy.  According to Table 15-3, individuals 
with herniated disks and radiculopathy can be assigned to either Category II or III. See 
AMA Guides, p. 384.  The differentiating factor is in how the herniated disk is treated.  
Id.  If the disk and resulting radiculopathy are sufficiently addressed with conservative 
care, then the injury falls into DRE Category II. Id. However, if the injury requires 
surgery to address the patient’s symptoms, then the injury falls into DRE Category III. 
Id. This interpretation is reinforced by the explanation contained on page 383 of the 
AMA Guides.  That explanation reads: 
If the individual had a radiculopathy caused by a herniated disk or 
lateral spinal stenosis that responded to conservative treatment and 
currently has no radicular symptoms or signs, he or she is placed in 
category II, since at MMI there is no radiculopathy.  Category III is for 
individuals with a symptomatic radiculopathy, either after medical or 
surgical treatment, or for individuals who have a history of previous 
radiculopathy caused by disk herniation or lateral spinal stenosis but have 
improved or become asymptomatic following surgery.  
Id.   
 
George underwent hemilaminotomy and discectomy surgery on February 26, 
2019.  Both parties agree this surgery was necessitated by the July 6, 2017, work injury.  
Given this, George’s injury is properly assigned to DRE Category III.   
Dr. Bansal’s permanent impairment rating is supported by the evidence; it is 
adopted.  He assigned George 12 percent permanent impairment to the whole body for 
his lumbar injury.  George is entitled to 60 weeks of permanent partial disability benefits

ORDER 
THEREFORE, IT IS ORDERED: 
 
Defendants shall pay George sixty (60) weeks of permanent partial disability 
benefits at the rate of five hundred thirty-one and 47/100 dollars ($531.47) per week 
commencing on June 2, 2019.  Defendants are entitled to a credit for permanent partial 
disability benefits already paid.  
Defendants shall pay accrued weekly benefits in a lump sum together with 
interest at an annual rate equal to the one-year treasury constant maturity published by 
the federal reserve in the most recent H15 report settled as of the date of injury, plus 
two percent.    
Defendants shall file subsequent reports of injury (SROI) as required by this 
agency pursuant to rules 876 IAC 3.1(2) and 876 IAC 11.7.
