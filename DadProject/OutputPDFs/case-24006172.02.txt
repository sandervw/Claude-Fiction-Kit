June 3, 2025, claimant, Mason Akers, filed an application for alternate care under Iowa

claimant requests defendants authorize a surgery recommended by Steven Brown,

FINDINGS OF FACT 
On May 19, 2024, claimant sustained a work-related injury to his left knee. (See 
Petition; Defendants’ Answer).  According to the medical records, claimant was skipping 
and twisted his left knee. (Ex. 2, p. 6).  Defendants admitted liability for the injury and 
authorized treatment with Steven Brown, D.O. at PCI. (See Defendants’ Answer; 
Hearing Testimony).   
According to the medical records, claimant attended physical therapy and had a 
corticosteroid injection. (Ex. 2, p. 6).  His knee complaints continued.  On January 31, 
2025, an MRI was taken of his left knee. (Id.).  It showed a complex tear of the posterior 
horn of his medial meniscus. (Id.).  Dr. Brown recommended surgery.  On April 15, 
2025, claimant underwent a diagnostic arthroscopic surgery of his left knee with Dr. 
Brown at St. Luke’s Surgery Center. (Ex. 2, p. 6).  That surgery revealed a full-thickness 
tear of his anterior cruciate ligament off the lateral femoral condyle.   
Claimant had a follow-up appointment with Dr. Brown on April 28, 2025. (Ex. 2, p. 
6).  Dr. Brown recommended a second surgery to repair the tear—a left knee 
arthroscopic anterior cruciate ligament reconstruction with quadriceps autograft, medial 
meniscal repair. (Id.).  He also recommended follow-up care including physical therapy 
at Rock Valley PT.  (Id.). Finally, he recommended that claimant continue to attend 
physical therapy up until the second surgery. (Id.).  After the first surgery, claimant 
attended physical therapy at Winneshiek Medical Center (WMC) in Decorah, Iowa, 
where claimant attends college. (Id.; Ex. 1, p. 2; Hearing Testimony).

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Under Iowa law, an employer who has accepted compensability for a workplace 
injury has a right to control the care provided to the injured employee.  Ramirez-Trujillo 
v. Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 N.W.2d 759, 769 (Iowa 2016).  The relevant statute provides 
as follows: 
For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish 
reasonable services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has the 
right to choose the care. . . . The treatment must be offered promptly and 
be reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the 
employee. If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the care 
offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such dissatisfaction 
to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the employer and 
the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited to treat the 
injury. If the employer and employee cannot agree on such alternate care, 
the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable proofs of the 
necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
Iowa Code § 85.27(4).   
Defendants’ “obligation under the statute is confined to reasonable care for the 
diagnosis and treatment of work-related injuries.” Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 
N.W.2d 122, 124 (Iowa 1995) (emphasis in original). In other words, the “obligation 
under the statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.”  Id. An 
application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because claimant is 
dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving. Mere dissatisfaction with the medical 
care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical care. Rather, 
the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not reasonably 
suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the claimant. See 
Iowa Code § 85.27(4). By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment and seeking 
alternate care, claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is 
unreasonable. See Iowa R. App. P 6.904(3)(e); Long, 528 N.W.2d at 124.  Ultimately, 
determining whether care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact. Long, 
528 N.W.2d at 123. 
An employer's right to select the provider of medical treatment for the injured 
worker does not include the right to determine how an injured worker should be 
diagnosed, evaluated, treated, or other matters of professional medical judgment. 
Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 866389 (Declaratory Ruling, May 18, 1988).  An 
employer is not entitled to interfere with the medical judgment of its own treating 
physician. Pote v. Mickow Corp., File No. 694639 (Review-Reopening Decision June 
17, 1986).  When a designated physician refers a patient to another physician, that

Both parties agreed that Dr. Brown is the authorized treater.  On April 28, 2025, 
Dr. Brown recommended a 2nd knee surgery, follow-up care at PCI, and post-operative 
physical therapy at Rock Valley PT. (Ex. 2, p. 6).  Defendants are not allowed to 
encroach on Dr. Brown’s treatment recommendations. The care defendants are offering 
is not reasonable.  Additionally, the undersigned finds no merit to defendants’ argument 
that this matter is not ripe because claimant has yet to attend post-operative therapy at 
a location of defendants’ choosing.  Every alternate care proceeding is prospective in 
nature; however, the care has already been ordered by Dr. Brown, a designated 
authorized provider.  This claim is ripe for adjudication.  
ORDER 
THEREFORE, IT IS ORDERED: 
The claimant's petition for alternate medical care is granted. 
Defendants shall immediately authorize the left knee arthroscopic anterior 
cruciate ligament reconstruction with quadriceps autograft and medial meniscal repair, 
as well as follow-up care with Dr. Brown and post-operative physical therapy at Rock 
Valley PT.
