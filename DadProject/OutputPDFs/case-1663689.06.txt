Introduction 
This is an administrative appeal from a decision by a Deputy acting with the 
Workers’ Compensation Commissioner’s authorization dismissing Petitioner’s 
application for alternate care that she brought pursuant to Iowa Code § 85.27(4). The 
Court convened an unreported hearing by Zoom on January 26, 2024.  Petitioner 
appeared by and through counsel, Mindi Vervaecke.  Respondent appeared by and 
through counsel, Nathan McConkey.  
Background Facts and Proceedings 
 
On December 27, 2018, Teresa Walton (“Teresa”), sustained an injury to her left 
knee, left hip, and back while working at Compass Group, and she sought workers’ 
compensation benefits from Compass Group and its insurer, New Hampshire 
Insurance Company (collectively “Compass”).  Ultimately, on June 20, 2022, Iowa 
Workers’ Compensation Commissioner Joseph S. Cortese, II, filed his Appeal Decision 
in which he concluded Teresa’s left knee, left hip, and back conditions were causally 
related to her work injury, and he affirmed the employer’s responsibility to pay for 
reasonable medical care to Teresa arising out of her injury. 
 
Over four years later, on July 27, 2023, Teresa filed an alternate care petition 
seeking treatment recommended by her authorized physical therapist, Sarah 
Elkenberry, PT, and Brandt Corcan, PTA.  Compass filed an answer in which it

On August 10, 2023, Deputy Garrish-Lampe issued her decision dismissing 
Teresa’s alternate care petition without prejudice.  In doing so, Deputy Garrish-Lampe 
reasoned that since Compass denied liability for the claimed alternate care, she had 
no choice but to dismiss the alternate care petition.  Deputy Garrish-Lampe reached 
this conclusion because she held that the summary proceedings under which an 
alternate care petition are decided as provided for in Iowa Code § 85.27(4) are not 
designed to adjudicate claims where the compensability of a claim is disputed.   
Deputy Garrish-Lampe’s decision constituted final agency action.  Teresa timely 
sought judicial review of the dismissal of her alternate care petition.  In her judicial 
review brief, Teresa raises a single claim:  Whether issue preclusion applies to bar 
Compass from relitigating causation for Teresa’s ongoing medical care.  The Court will 
examine Teresa’s claim in light of the following legal principles.    
Standard Of Review 
Chapter 17A of the Iowa Code governs judicial review of administrative agency 
action. The district court acts in an appellate capacity to correct errors of law on the 
part of the agency. Meyer v. IBP, Inc., 710 N.W.2d 213, 219 (Iowa 2006).  The court 
“may grant relief if the agency action has prejudiced the substantial rights of the 
petitioner, and the agency action meets one of the enumerated criteria contained in 
section 17A.19(10)(a) through (n).” Burton v. Hilltop Care Cntr., 813 N.W.2d 250, 256 
(Iowa 2012) (quoting Evercom Sys., Inc. v. Iowa Utilities Bd., 805 N.W.2d 758, 762 
(Iowa 2011)). Where an agency has been “clearly vested” with a fact-finding function, 
the appropriate “standard of review [on appeal] depends on the aspect of the agency's 
decision that forms the basis of the petition for judicial review”—that is, whether it 
involves an issue of 1) findings of fact, 2) interpretation of law, or 3) application of law 
to fact. Burton, 813 N.W.2d at 256.

Review of findings of fact:  The courts use a substantial evidence standard 
when considering challenges to findings of fact in agency decisions.  A reviewing court 
can only disturb factual findings if they are “not supported by substantial evidence in 
the record before the court when that record is reviewed as a whole.”  Burton, 813 
N.W.2d at 256 (quoting Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(f)).  The Iowa Supreme Court has 
outlined the court’s guidelines when reviewing substantial evidence claims under the 
17A.19 standard as follows: 
When reviewing a finding of fact for substantial evidence, we judge the 
finding in light of all the relevant evidence in the record cited by any 
party that detracts from that finding as well as all of the relevant 
evidence in the record cited by any party that supports it.  Our review of 
the record is fairly intensive, and we do not simply rubber stamp the 
agency finding of fact. 
 
Evidence is not insubstantial merely because different conclusions may 
be drawn from the evidence. To that end, evidence may be substantial 
even though we may have drawn a different conclusion as fact finder. 
Our task, therefore, is not to determine whether the evidence supports a 
different finding; rather, our task is to determine whether substantial 
evidence, viewing the record as a whole, supports the findings actually 
made. 
 
Cedar Rapids Cmty. Sch. Dist. v. Pease, 807 N.W.2d 839, 845 (Iowa 2011) (internal 
citations and quotation marks omitted). 
Application of law to fact:  When a party challenges the ultimate conclusion 
reached by the agency, then the challenge is to the agency's application of the law to 
the facts.  In that event, the question on review is whether the agency abused its 
discretion by, for example, employing wholly irrational reasoning or ignoring important 
and relevant evidence.  Meyer, 710 N.W.2d at 219.  The court will only reverse the 
agency’s application of law to the facts if it is irrational, illogical, or wholly 
unjustifiable. Neal v. Annett Holdings, Inc., 814 N.W.2d 512, 518 (Iowa 2012).

Analysis 
 
There is no dispute that Teresa suffered a compensable work-related injury on 
December 27, 2018.  As a result, Compass was required to provide Teresa with 
reasonable medical services and supplies. See, Iowa Code § 85.27(1).  Under chapter 
85, in return for an employer's obligation “to furnish reasonable services and supplies 
to treat an injured employee,” the employer generally possesses the right to choose the 
care received.  See, Iowa Code § 85.27(4).  Section 85.27(4) further provides, in 
relevant part: 
If the employer chooses the care, the employer shall hold the employee 
harmless for the cost of care until the employer notifies the employee that the 
employer is no longer authorizing all or any part of the care and the reason for 
the change in authorization .... The treatment must be offered promptly and be 
reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the 
employee.  
 
If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the care offered, the employee 
should communicate the basis of such dissatisfaction to the employer, in 
writing if requested, following which the employer and the employee may agree 
to alternate care reasonably suited to treat the injury.  
 
If the employer and employee cannot agree on such alternate care, the 
commissioner may, upon application and reasonable proofs of the 
necessity therefor, allow and order other care. (emphasis added) 
 
Id. 
Alternate medical care proceedings are summary proceedings. See, Iowa Admin. 
Code r.876–4.1, 876–4 .48(7); See also Iowa Code § 85.27(4); McGowan v. Brandt 
Constr. Co., No. 09-1033, 2010 WL 2079704, at *4 (describing alternate medical care 
proceedings as summary proceedings).  In fact, the Commissioner must issue a 
decision within ten days of the filing of an alternate care petition when a telephone 
hearing is held or within fourteen days of an in-person hearing.  See, Iowa Code § 
85.27(4); see also Iowa Admin. Code r.876–4.1, 876–4 .48(14).  All discovery must be 
completed before the hearing. See, also Iowa Admin. Code r.876–4.1, 876–4 .48(9).

Because of the summary nature of the proceedings, Administrative rule  
876–4 .48(6) provides that: 
4.48(6) Employer liability.  Application cannot be filed under this rule if 
the liability of the employer is an issue.  If an application is filed where the 
liability of the employer is an issue, the application will be dismissed without 
prejudice.   
 
In this case, Deputy Garrish-Lampe concluded that dismissal without prejudice was 
appropriate under Rule 4.86(6) as Compass denied liability because it denied that the 
alternate medical care Teresa was requesting was casually connected to her original 
work injury.  Teresa challenges this decision on judicial review contending that issue 
preclusion applies to prevent Compass from relitigating causation. 
The Iowa Supreme Court has adopted the following test for when issue 
preclusion may be utilized in a subsequent court proceeding, holding:  
(1) The issue concluded must be identical;  
 
(2) The issue must have been raised and litigated in the prior action;  
 
(3) The issue must have been material and relevant to the disposition 
of the prior action; and 
 
(4) The determination made of the issue in the prior action must have been 
necessary and essential to the resulting judgment. 
 
Hunter v. City of Des Moines, 300 N.W.2d 121, 123 (Iowa 1981).  
 
The Court concludes that Teresa has not met this legal test.  As the Iowa Court 
of Appeals has previously held, an employer may properly admit to an injury arising 
out of and in the course of employment while still contesting liability for all of the 
consequences and any disability claimed to result from such injury. See, McGowan v. 
Brandt Constr. Co., No. 09–1033, 2010 WL 2079704, at *4 (Iowa Ct. App. May 26, 
2010).  While this unpublished opinion is not binding precedent on this Court, the 
Court finds the legal analysis of this case to be persuasive and the Court will follow it.

Additionally, in NID Inc. v. Monahan, No. 14-0292, 2015 WL 1332332 (Iowa Ct. 
App. March 25, 2015), the Iowa Court of Appeals rejected an attempt to apply the 
doctrine of judicial estoppel to prevent an employer from denying causation and 
liability in an alternate care proceeding.  Read together, these cases stand for the 
proposition that Compass is well within its rights to contest causation in an alternate 
care proceeding as neither issue preclusion nor judicial estoppel prevents it from doing 
so.  That is because there has not been a previous finding that Teresa’s need for an 
updated MRI is casually related to her work injury, so no issue preclusion applies. 
When Compass denied liability and supported its denial with expert opinion 
evidence, Deputy Garrish-Lampe was required to dismiss Teresa’s alternate care 
petition without prejudice.  Consequently, the Court concludes that there is no legal or 
factual basis that justifies overturning the agency’s action in this case. 
In reaching this decision, the Court is not unsympathetic to the petitioner’s 
plight in this case as apparently even Dr. Kim recognizes that an updated MRI may be 
necessary to further evaluate and direct her treatment.  Of course, Petitioner may 
continue to advance her claim through alternate proceedings available to her under 
chapter 85; however, as her counsel points out, such litigation tends to be drawn out 
and protracted.   
This means that, even if the Petitioner is ultimately successful in proving that 
the medical care she has requested is causally related to her work injury, such care 
may be delayed for many months or even years.  The Court can certainly conceive of a 
statutory or regulatory framework where contested claims such as this one where the 
sole dispute is limited to causation could easily be decided on a more expedited basis 
while still protecting an employer’s interests.  But that is not the current system in
