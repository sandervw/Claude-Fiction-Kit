STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A.  The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Beau Vosatka.  
Claimant appeared through attorney, MaKayla Augustine.  Defendants appeared 
through their attorney, Tyler Laflin. 
The alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on August 29, 2024.  The 
proceedings were digitally recorded on agency recording software, Quality Management 
Suite.  This recording constitutes the official record of the proceeding.  Pursuant to the 
Commissioner’s Order, the undersigned has been delegated authority to issue a final 
agency decision in this alternate medical care proceeding.  Therefore, this ruling is 
designated final agency action and any appeal of the decision would be to the Iowa 
District Court pursuant to Iowa Code section 17A. 
The record consists of claimant’s exhibits 1 and defense exhibits A and B, which 
were received without objection.  The defendants do not dispute liability for claimant’s 
August 2023 work injury, which caused a condition in his right leg. 
ISSUE 
The issue presented for resolution is whether the care offered by the defendants 
is unreasonable.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
The claimant is a 38-year-old man who sustained an injury to his right leg and 
other body parts on or about August 14, 2023.  The nature of the injury is a gunshot 
wound with bullet fragments in his leg.  The defendants have authorized Ryan Dunlay, 
M.D., to treat the condition.  It appears in this record that Dr. Dunlay provided 
reasonable treatment for the claimant’s condition through January 2024.  On January 
19, 2024, Dr. Dunlay released Mr. Vosatka from his care, stating the following: 
PLAN: I discussed with Beau had a long discussion with him there is a 
large disconnect between his presentation and objective findings and his 
subjective findings with regard to specifically his right thigh.  He did inquire 
last time about lead poisoning and I directed him previously to his primary 
care, though I think the risk of lead poisoning is extremely low and there 
are no signs of infection or negative effects objectively to the foreign body 
bullet fragment in his right thigh.  Despite this, he has had struggles mostly 
subjective in nature with regard to his ability to regain function.  He does 
state that he plans to get a second opinion in Iowa City with one of the 
traumatologists.  I encouraged him that would be a reasonable thing given 
his ongoing complaints.  However, from my standpoint I do not have 
anything further to offer him.  I think the morbidity of any surgery directed 
at removing the foreign body in this thigh outweighs the potential for 
improvement in his function and strength.  However, I am only 1 opinion.  
We had a long and frank discussion in this regard. 
(Defendants’ Exhibit A, pages 1-2 (errors in original)) 
Thereafter, claimant arranged a visit with another physician from Genesis and 
then returned to Dr. Dunlay in March 2024.  Dr. Dunlay documented this visit as follows: 
He complains of pain in the lateral thigh.  He reports that his thigh was 
swollen and tender 3 weeks ago but it has been improving.  He is still 
trying to do his exercises but says he can’t get off of the cane.  He did get 
a second opinion from Dr. Cutts at Genesis who told him he is unlikely to 
benefit from excision.  He is taking Meloxicam. 
…. 
PLAN:  I reviewed x-rays with the patient.  I explained that my 
recommendation has not changed in regards to excising the foreign 
bodies.  He understands and accepts this  He is at MMI and was provided 
with a work note stating he is able to return without restrictions as of today  
Questions were invited and answered to his satisfaction.  He will return as 
needed. 
(Def. Ex. A, p. 3 (errors in original))

REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Iowa Code Section 85.27 (2023). 
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See 
Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  Determining what care is 
reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.  Id.  The employer’s obligation turns 
on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.  Id.; Harned v. Farmland 
Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983).   
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not

reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995). 
An employer’s statutory right is to select the providers of care and the employer 
may consider cost and other pertinent factors when exercising its choice. Long, at 124. 
An employer (typically) is not a licensed health care provider and does not possess 
medical expertise. Accordingly, an employer does not have the right to control the 
methods the providers choose to evaluate, diagnose and treat the injured employee. An 
employer is not entitled to control a licensed health care provider’s exercise of 
professional judgment. Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 866389 (Declaratory 
Ruling, May 18, 1988). An employer’s failure to follow recommendations of an 
authorized physician in matters of treatment is commonly a failure to provide reasonable 
treatment. Boggs v. Cargill, Inc., File No. 1050396 (Alt. Care Dec. January 31, 1994). 
 “Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.”  
Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122, 123 (Iowa 1995). 
In Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 433, 437 (Iowa 1997), the 
supreme court held that “when evidence is presented to the commissioner that the 
employer-authorized medical care has not been effective and that such care is ‘inferior 
or less extensive’ than other available care requested by the employee, . . . the 
commissioner is justified by section 85.27 to order the alternate care.” 
I find that the care offered by the defendants is inferior and less extensive than 
the care claimant is seeking.  Since January and March 2024, the defendants have 
offered no care to the claimant for the bullet injury in his right thigh.  Dr. Dunlay has 
specifically stated that he has no further care to offer for this condition.  In fact, he 
documented that there “is a large disconnect between his presentation and the objective 
findings …”  (Def. Ex. A, p. 1)  I interpret this statement to mean that Dr. Dunlay 
believes Mr. Vosatka should not be having as much pain in his right leg as he claims he 
is having.  The medical notes indicate Mr. Vosatka is still using a cane and has other 
serious symptoms of pain and loss of function.  Mr. Vosatka has clearly voiced his 
ongoing complaints of pain and disability to Dr. Dunlay.  In response, Dr. Dunlay told 
him that his is only one opinion and encouraged him to seek another opinion. 
At hearing defendants argued that Mr. Vosatka should return to Dr. Dunlay to 
determine whether pain management treatment would be appropriate or effective.  I find 
that Dr. Dunlay has clearly opined that he has nothing to offer the claimant by way of 
further treatment and it is unnecessary to return to a physician who has stated that he 
has no treatment to offer for his condition. 
Since claimant has not attended any visit with a pain clinic at this time and there 
are no specific recommendations from a pain doctor, this order should not be 
interpreted as a blank check to receive any treatment recommended by a pain doctor.  
The pain clinic should examine and evaluate the claimant and determine whether there 
is any treatment which may effectively treat his pain complaints and his loss of function. 
It is entirely possible, unfortunately, that there may be no pain treatment which can

effectively treat his condition.  I find, however, that it is unreasonable not to try given the 
significant ongoing complaints claimant has. In this record, the defendants are still

ORDER 
THEREFORE IT IS ORDERED: 
The claimant's petition for alternate medical care is GRANTED.  Defendants shall 
immediately authorize an appointment with a pain clinic.
