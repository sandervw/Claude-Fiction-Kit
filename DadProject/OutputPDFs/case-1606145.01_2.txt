RULING ON MOTION TO DISMISS 
 
The above captioned matter came before the Court on July 26, 2024. Jeffrey Lanz 
represented the Petitioners (“Grain Millers” and “Nationwide”). The Respondent, Eric Ross, 
(“Ross”) was represented by Dennis McElwain and Jennifer Zupp. After hearing the arguments of 
counsel and reviewing relevant case law and the court file, including filings by both the parties, 
the Court now enters the following ruling.  
I. 
BACKGROUND FACTS. 
On September 19, 2015, Ross sustained a work-related injury that resulted in the 
amputation of his right leg. Cert Rec. 32. Following the injury, Ross received care at the Mayo 
Clinic in Rochester, Minnesota where he underwent a number of procedures for treatment. Id. Part 
of his treatment involved being fitted for a prosthetic limb that prosthetic company, Limb Lab, 
provided. Id. From December 2023 to February 2024, Grain Millers, Nationwide, and Limb Lab 
negotiated the costs associated with the prosthetic limb. Id at 32-33. Grain Millers and Nationwide 
wanted to transfer Ross’ care to Hanger Clinic, but Ross insisted on staying with Limb Lab based 
on the excellent level of care he was already receiving. Id at 33. Grain Millers and Nationwide

committed to pay $25,000 for the prosthetic limb from Limb Lab, but maintained that they disputed

Ross petitioned the Iowa Worker’s Compensation Commission for a decision on alternate

Limb Lab was based solely on economic concerns. The Commissioner stated that while cost may 
be a factor an employer may consider when selecting providers, in this case, cost should not be a 
criterion in providing Ross with a replacement for his amputated right leg. Id at 35. Due to Ross’

another provider due to a slight cost difference was unnecessary and unreasonable. Id.  
On May 14, 2024, Grain Millers and Nationwide filed a Petition for Judicial Review 
contesting the Commissioner’s Alternate Medical Care Decision. On May 22, 2024, Ross filed a 
motion to dismiss the petition asserting the issues Grain Miller and Nationwide raise are moot 
because they agreed to pay for care after the Commissioner ordered to do so. Grain Millers and 
Nationwide agree that the issue is moot but assert that the public importance exception to mootness 
should apply because the problem is likely to recur in the future given Ross’ likely need for 
additional further prosthetic needs.

II. 
STANDARD OF REVIEW. 
 
In determining whether to grant the motion to dismiss, a court views the well-pled facts of 
the petition “in the light most favorable to the plaintiff with doubts resolved in that party's favor.” 
Haupt v. Miller, 514 N.W.2d 905, 911 (Iowa 1994). A motion to dismiss must stand or fall on the 
exclusive contents of the petition. Id. “A motion to dismiss admits the allegations of the petition 
and waives any ambiguity or uncertainty in the petition.” Leuchtenmacher v. Farm Bureau Mutual 
Ins., 460 N.W.2d 858, 861 (Iowa 1990). “The petition should be construed in a light most favorable 
to the plaintiff with doubts resolved in that party's favor in ruling on the motion.” Sanford v. 
Manternach, 601 N.W.2d 360, 363 (Iowa 1999). A motion to dismiss is sustainable only when it 
appears to a certainty that the plaintiff would not be entitled to relief under any state of facts that 
could be proved in support of the claims asserted. Haupt, 514 N.W.2d at 911 (citing Bindel v. Iowa 
Mfg. Co., 197 N.W.2d 552, 555 (Iowa 1972)). Therefore, a motion to dismiss must be on legal 
grounds. Robbins v. Heritage Acres, 578 N.W.2d 262, 264 (Iowa Ct. App. 1998). 
III. 
ANALYSIS. 
The issue here is whether the court should apply the public importance exception to the 
mootness doctrine in this case. “Courts exist to decide cases, not academic questions of law. For 
this reason, a court will generally decline to hear a case when, because of changed circumstances, 
the court's decision will no longer matter. This is known as the doctrine of mootness.” Riley Drive 
Ent. I, Inc. v. Reynolds, 970 N.W.2d 289, 296 (Iowa 2022) (quoting Homan v. Branstad, 864 
N.W.2d 321, 328 (Iowa 2015)). In limited circumstances, the court may hear the case when there 
is a mootness exception. See Riley Drive Ent. I, Inc., 864 N.W.2d at 296. Even if an issue is moot, 
an exception can apply – one such exception is the public importance exception. Id at 298. For the 
public importance exception to apply, the court considers the following factors: 1) the private or

public nature of the issue; 2) the desirability of an authoritative adjudication to guide public 
officials in their future conduct; 3) the likelihood of the recurrence of the issue; and 4) the 
likelihood the issue will recur yet evade appellate review. Maghee v. State, 773 N.W.2d 228, 234 
(Iowa 2009); H.D. Supply Mgmt. & N.H. Ins. Co. v. Smith, No. 23-1656, 2024 WL 4222878, *3 
(Iowa Ct. App. Sept. 18, 2024). 
Here, clearly, the issue is moot because Grain Millers and Nationwide have already paid 
$25,000 to Limb Lab for Ross’ prosthetic limb, which he has already received. The appropriate 
avenue to dispute a Commissioner’s decision is to file for judicial review, seek a stay under Iowa 
Code section 17A and Iowa Code section 86.26(1) and post a bond. Rather, Grain Millers and 
Nationwide chose to pay for the care ordered by the Commissioner in the alternate medical care 
decision. Therefore, no matter the Court’s decision, it will not affect the outcome as care has been 
completed, deeming the issue moot.  
As for an exception to mootness, the Court finds that Grain Millers and Nationwide have 
not demonstrated a sufficient need for the public importance exception to apply in this case. As to 
the first factor, as noted by Ross, the issue in this case is a private one between an employer and 
its insurance carrier, and an injured employee. While the Iowa Worker’s Compensation 
Commission regularly hears and decides alternate medical care decisions, there is not public 
interest at stake in this case. The first factor for the Court to consider has not been met.    
The second factor has also not been met.  Iowa Code section 85.27 already provides that 
employers must provide reasonably prompt medical care, and section 86.26 already provides that 
if a party is aggrieved by agency action, a party may file an appeal and obtain a stay by posting a 
bond. Iowa Code §§ 85.27 and 86.26. There is no need for this case to serve as a guide for public 
officials in their future conduct.

Turning to the third factor, the issue is not likely to recur as employers, insurance carriers, 
and providers regularly negotiate pricing when it comes to medical costs. In this case, Grain 
Millers and Nationwide even negotiated to bring the price down significantly from what Limb Lab 
was originally requesting. Cert. Rec. 64, 66. Antidotally, counsel for the parties acknowledged that 
in their years of practice this had not been a reoccurring issue. The Court acknowledges counsel’s 
comments are not controlling evidence; however, the Court does find it worth noting when 
considering the likelihood of the reoccurrence of the issue presented by this case.    
Finally, as to the fourth factor, employers and insurance carriers have avenues to pursue 
judicial review that include appealing the Commissioner’s decision, seeking a stay, and posting a 
bond while the district court decides the outcome of the case. In this case, Grain Millers and 
Nationwide declined to pursue this course of action. The Court recognizes the morality of Grain 
Millers and Nationwide’s decision and its benefit to Ross, however, the Court cannot ignore the 
procedural avenue available to them. The fourth factor has not been met.     
For all these reasons, Grain Millers and Nationwide fail to meet the four-part test of the 
public importance exception to the mootness doctrine and the motion to dismiss the petition for 
judicial review must be granted. 
IV. 
DISPOSITION. 
IT IS THEREFORE ORDERED THAT Respondent’s Motion to Dismiss is 
GRANTED.  
IT IS FURTHER ORDERED THAT Petitioners’ Petition for Judicial Review is 
DISMISSED.  Costs are assessed against Petitioners.
