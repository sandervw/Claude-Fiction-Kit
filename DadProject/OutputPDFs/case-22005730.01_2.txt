On September 17, 2025, the undersigned filed an arbitration decision in this 
case.  On October 7, 2025, defendant filed a motion for order nunc pro tunc.   
Defendant correctly contends that the undersigned made a scrivener’s error with 
respect to the dollar figure of defendant’s permanent partial disability (PPD) credit.  On 
page 8 of  the arbitration decision, the undersigned wrote that the parties had stipulated 
that Tyson paid Rojas $8,498.00 in permanent partial benefits prior to hearing. 
However, according to the hearing report, the parties stipulated to the amounts outlined 
in Defendant’s Exhibits D and F.  Those exhibits show that defendant overpaid 
claimant’s temporary total benefits (TTD) by $864.40, which they then claimed as a 
PPD credit. (See Exhibit D).  Additionally, defendant also later issued a PPD benefit 
check in the amount of $8,498.80. (See Exhibit F).  Accordingly, defendant’s total credit 
should have been articulated as $9,362.40. It is appropriate to grant defendant’s motion 
for order nunc pro tunc in this case.  
The phrase, “nunc pro tunc” means “now for then.”  See:  Black’s Law Dictionary, 
page 1218 (Revised 4th Edition 1968).  The definition in Black’s Law Dictionary further 
provides:  “A phrase applied to acts allowed to be done after the time when they should 
be done, with a retroactive effect, i.e., with the same effect as if regularly done.”  Black’s 
at 1218.  A nunc pro tunc order “is not for the purpose of correcting judicial thinking, a 
judicial conclusion, or a mistake of law.”  Headley v. Headley, 172 N.W.2d 104, 108 
(Iowa 1969).  The nunc pro tunc order can be employed to correct obvious errors or to 
make an order conform to the judge’s original intent.  Graber v. Iowa Dist. Court for 
Washington County, 410 N.W.2d 224, 229 (Iowa 1987).  Brinson v. Spee Dee Delivery 
Service, No. 8-754/06-2074 (Iowa App. 11/13/2008) (Iowa App., 2008). 
Defendant’s motion for order nunc pro tunc is sustained.
