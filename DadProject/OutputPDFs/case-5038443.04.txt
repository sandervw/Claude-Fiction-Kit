STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A.  The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Melynda Reida.  
Claimant appeared personally and through her attorney, Sara Riley.  Defendants 
appeared through their attorney, Ed Rose. 
 
This case came before the undersigned for a telephonic hearing on February 24, 
2025.  The proceedings were digitally recorded.  That recording constitutes the official 
record of this proceeding.   
 
Pursuant to the Commissioner’s July 21, 2023 Order, the undersigned has been 
delegated authority to issue a final agency decision in this alternate medical care 
proceeding.  Therefore, this ruling is designated final agency action. Any appeal of the 
decision would be to the Iowa District Court pursuant to Iowa Code section 17A. 
 
The record consists of claimant’s exhibits 1-2, which include a total of 5 pages.  
Defendants did not introduce a separate set of exhibits.  Claimant testified on her own 
behalf.  No other witnesses were called to testify. 
 
ISSUE 
 
The issue presented is whether claimant is entitled to an order for alternate 
medical care, specifically for ongoing and future medication and Botox treatment.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
The undersigned having considered all the evidence in the record finds: 
 
Ms. Reida sustained two separate work injuries.  The first occurred on March 18, 
2010.  The second work injury occurred on March 17, 2011.  Both were consolidated 
and proceeded to an arbitration hearing, resulting in an arbitration decision filed on 
September 9, 2014.  This case also resulted in a review-reopening proceeding in which 
past, outstanding medical expenses were awarded to claimant.   
Claimant now returns for an alternate medical care hearing, seeking additional 
and ongoing medical treatment.  Claimant testified that she has outstanding medical 
expenses that have not yet been paid by defendants.  Specifically, she noted that she 
obtained a Botox injection on December 18, 2024.  Her medical provider, Physicians 
Clinic of Iowa, notified her prior to her February 18, 2025 appointment that the Botox 
injection expense remains unpaid by defendants.   
Ms. Reida also testified about difficulties getting prescriptions filled and paid.  
She testified that she has current prescriptions for methocarbamol and carisoprodol.  
She testified that she sought to have a recent prescription from her physician for 
methocarbamol filled but it was not authorized by defendants.  She noted that 
prescription has now been filled, but introduced written evidence that defendants will 
only fill that prescription once without claimant being re-evaluated by her physician. 
Review of the claimant’s exhibits demonstrates that claimant’s authorized 
neurologist is Shereen Chang, M.D.  Dr. Chang relays in Claimant’s Exhibit 1 that Ms. 
Reida will require Botox injections as well as muscle relaxants.  She further relays that 
claimant believes methocarbamol is the most effective muscle relaxant for her condition.  
Dr. Chang reiterates that this treatment regimen will be necessary for the remainder of 
claimant’s life.  (Claimant’s Exhibit 1) 
Defendants apparently obtained a medical peer review to determine the medical 
necessity and reasonableness of Dr. Chang’s prescription.  Defendants’ medical 
reviewer is Kevin Kohan, D.O., who appears to be licensed in California.  Dr. Kohan 
purportedly “modifies” the recommendation and prescription of Dr. Chang.   
Specifically, Dr. Kohan opined that the treatment regimen using methocarbamol 
is reasonable but opines that no refills should be permitted without re-evaluation.  Dr. 
Kohan requires that documentation of efficacy is required for any future prescriptions to 
be considered medically necessary. 
Considering the competing medical opinions, I find that the medical opinion of Dr. 
Chang is most convincing and reasonable.  Dr. Chang evaluated claimant personally 
and has provided claimant care since 2023.  Dr. Chang is clearly in a position where 
she has observed claimant, has knowledge of her ongoing condition, and is situated to 
provide the best, most reasonable, and medically necessary treatment for Ms. Reida.  
Dr. Kohan is not claimant’s treating physician, has not evaluated claimant personally,

REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Section 85.27.  Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening October 16, 1975). 
 
Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.  Long 
v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  The employer’s obligation turns on 
the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.  Id.; Harned v. Farmland Foods, 
Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983).   
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).   
An employer’s right to select the provider of medical treatment to an injured 
worker does not include the right to determine how an injured worker should be 
diagnosed, evaluated, treated, or other matters of professional medical judgment.  
Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 866389 (Declaratory Ruling, May 19, 1988).   
 
In this case, claimant sought an award of past medical expenses, including the 
recent Botox injection in December 2024 as well as the muscle relaxants noted above.  
Alternate medical care proceedings are prospective in nature, and an award of past 
medical expenses cannot be used in these expedited proceedings.  Claimant must file a 
judgment with the Iowa District Court and seek enforcement of the prior arbitration 
decision and/or pursue a new contested case proceeding before this agency for an 
award of past medical expenses.  This proceeding can only consider future treatment. 
 
That being said, claimant has proven that Dr. Chang recommends ongoing 
treatment, including Botox injections and the use of a muscle relaxant.  At this time,

ORDER 
 
THEREFORE, IT IS ORDERED: 
 
The claimant's petition for alternate medical care is granted in part and denied in 
part. 
Claimant must pursue payment of past, outstanding medical expenses via 
another procedural mechanism, and any request for award of past medical expenses is 
denied as part of this alternate medical care proceeding. 
Defendants shall immediately authorize and promptly pay for the medical 
treatment recommended by the authorized neurologist, Dr. Chang, including but not 
necessarily limited to the recommended ongoing and future Botox injections as well as 
a muscle relaxer, currently prescribed as methocarbamol.
