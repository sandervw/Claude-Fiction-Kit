STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A.  The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Danielle Sieperda 
Black.  Claimant appeared personally and through her attorney, Erik Bair.  Defendants 
appeared through their attorney, Thomas Shires. 
 
The alternate medical care claim came on for a telephonic hearing on March 24, 
2025.  The proceedings were digitally recorded.  That recording constitutes the official 
record of this proceeding.  Pursuant to the Commissioner’s July 21, 2023 Order, the 
undersigned has been delegated authority to issue a final agency decision in this 
alternate medical care proceeding.  Therefore, this ruling is designated final agency 
action and any appeal of the decision would be to the Iowa District Court pursuant to 
Iowa Code section 17A. 
 
Claimant offered exhibits 1-2, which include six pages.  Defendants offered 
exhibit A, which includes two pages.  All exhibits were received without objection.  
Claimant testified on her own behalf.  No other witnesses were called to testify and the 
evidentiary record closed at the conclusion of the alternate medical care hearing.   
 
ISSUE 
 
The issue presented for resolution is whether the claimant is entitled to 
authorization of care through and at the direction of Matthew Pingree, M.D., at the Mayo 
Clinic.  Specifically, claimant requests an order authorizing a recommended peripheral 
nerve stimulation implant recommended by Dr. Pingree.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
The undersigned having considered all the evidence in the record finds: 
 
Claimant, Danielle Sieperda Black, sustained an injury to her left arm on 
December 3, 2019, as a result of her work activities with the employer.  According to 
medical records in evidence, claimant experienced pain in her left wrist and hand after 
attempting to manually move a patient in bed.  Defendants admitted the work injury and 
have authorized medical care since the 2019 work injury.  At the commencement of the 
hearing, defendants clarified their answer and admitted current causal connection of 
claimant’s condition to the December 3, 2019 work injury.  Their admission was 
accepted and the hearing proceeded through receipt of both written evidence as well as 
claimant’s testimony. 
 
Ms. Sieperda Black submitted to eight surgeries on her left arm since the date of 
injury.  Defendants acknowledge they selected the initial treating medical provider, and 
through a series of referrals, claimant’s authorized medical care has transferred to Dr. 
Pingree at Mayo Clinic.  While not specifically selecting Dr. Pingree, defendants 
acknowledge they have authorized his treatment and paid for his treatment through 
March 3, 2025.  Claimant last submitted to evaluation with Dr. Pingree on March 3, 
2025.  At that appointment, Dr. Pingree recommended claimant for a peripheral nerve 
stimulator implant.  Defendants have not authorized the treatment recommended by Dr. 
Pingree. 
 
Instead, defendants scheduled an independent medical evaluation to be 
performed by Douglas Martin, M.D. on March 26, 2025.  Defendants scheduled this 
evaluation on March 4, 2025, prior to receipt of the medical records and 
recommendations from Dr. Pingree.  Defendants contend they are conducting a 
reasonable investigation and should be given an additional couple of days to permit this 
investigation to move forward with the evaluation by Dr. Martin. 
 
Claimant testified she is required to undergo a psychiatric evaluation in advance 
of a stimulator implant.  She also testified that she is required to submit to nerve blocks 
and mapping of her nerves prior to the peripheral nerve stimulator implantation.  
Claimant desires to move forward with the recommended treatment.  I find that the 
peripheral nerve stimulator implant is recommended by an authorized medical provider.  
I find that the nerve stimulator, as well as the necessary psychiatric evaluation, nerve 
mapping, and nerve blocks, are reasonable and necessary medical treatment for 
claimant’s December 3, 2019 left arm injury.  There is no contrary evidence in this 
record. 
 
While I acknowledge defendants’ desire to pursue further evaluation and 
investigation of this claim, they are realistically attempting to interfere with the 
recommendations of the authorized medical provider, Dr. Pingree.  Claimant desires to 
move forward with the recommended medical care, as recommended by Dr. Pingree.  I 
find defendants are not currently offering any medical care for claimant’s left arm injury

REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
“The employer is obliged to further reasonable services and supplies to treat an 
injured employee.”  Iowa Code section 85.27(4).  In fact, the employer is required to 
furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, chiropractic, podiatric, 
physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance and hospital services and supplies for all 
conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The employer shall also 
allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred for those services.  
The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except where the employer 
has denied liability for the injury.  Section 85.27.  Holbert v. Townsend Engineering Co., 
Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening 
October 16, 1975). 
 
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See Iowa 
R. App. P 14(f)(5); Bell Bros. Heating & Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d 193, 209 
(Iowa 2010); Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  Determining 
what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.  Long v. Roberts Dairy 
Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  The employer’s obligation turns on the question of 
reasonable necessity, not desirability.  Id.; Harned v. Farmland Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 
98 (Iowa 1983).   
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care she has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).   
An employer’s right to select the provider of medical treatment to an injured 
worker does not include the right to determine how an injured worker should be 
diagnosed, evaluated, treated, or other matters of professional medical judgment.  
Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 866389 (Declaratory Ruling, May 19, 1988).   
Reasonable care includes care necessary to diagnose the condition and 
defendants are not entitled to interfere with the medical judgment of its own treating 
physician.  Pote v. Mickow Corp., File No. 694639 (Review-Reopening Decision 
June 17, 1986). 
When a designated physician refers a patient to another physician, that physician 
acts as the defendant employer’s agent.  Permission for the referral from defendant is 
not necessary.  Kittrell v. Allen Memorial Hospital, Thirty-fourth Biennial Report of the 
Industrial Commissioner, 164 (Arb. November 1, 1979) (aff’d by industrial

Defendants selected the initial medical provider for claimant’s injury and 
acknowledge that she has received various referrals that ultimately resulted in her 
treating with Dr. Pingree at Mayo Clinic.  I conclude, therefore, that Dr. Pingree is an 
authorized medical provider.  Although an authorized medical provider is now 
recommending additional medical care, defendants have not authorized the 
recommended care.  Specifically, Dr. Pingree recommended a peripheral nerve 
stimulator implant.  Defendants do not currently possess any evidence to suggest this 
treatment is unreasonable.  Nor have defendants presented evidence of an alternate, 
reasonable medical treatment plan for claimant’s injury.  Nevertheless, defendants have 
not authorized the recommended care. 
 
Defendants delay in authorizing care results in no care currently being authorized 
for treatment of claimant’s left arm injury.  Defendants are not entitled to interfere with 
the treatment recommendations of their authorized medical provider.  Therefore, I 
conclude claimant has proven defendants are not providing prompt, reasonable medical 
care that is reasonably suited to treat her injury.  Iowa Code section 85.27.   
 
Moreover, claimant has identified specific treatment available for her injury, the 
peripheral nerve stimulator implant and related psychiatric evaluation and nerve blocks.  
Defendants failed to authorize any medical care for the conditions.  Claimant has 
proven that the lack of care is ineffective to treat her condition and that it is inferior and 
less extensive than other recommended medical care.  Therefore, I conclude claimant is 
entitled to an order authorizing the recommended psychiatric evaluation, nerve blocks, 
nerve mapping, and peripheral nerve stimulator implantation.  Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. 
v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 433 (Iowa 1997). 
 
Defendants contend that they should be permitted additional time, two days, to 
complete further investigation in hopes of securing causation opinions, as well as 
opinions pertaining to maximum medical improvement.  Defendants remain entitled to 
pursue further evaluation under Iowa Code section 85.39, and claimant has 
acknowledged at hearing that she will attend the evaluation scheduled to occur on 
March 26, 2025.  However, defendants cannot ignore or interfere with the 
recommendations of their authorized medical provider. 
 
ORDER 
 
THEREFORE IT IS ORDERED: 
 
The claimant's petition for alternate medical care is granted.   
 
Defendants shall immediately authorize and pay for the treatment recommended 
by Dr. Pingree at Mayo Clinic, including the recommendation for a diagnostic nerve
