STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
  
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A. The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Eric Ross. Claimant 
appeared personally and through his attorney, Dennis McElwain. Defendants appeared 
through their attorney, Jeffrey Lanz. 
  
The alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on March 6, 2024. The 
proceedings were digitally recorded. That recording constitutes the official record of this 
proceeding. Pursuant to the Commissioner's July 21, 2023, Order, the undersigned has 
been delegated authority to issue a final agency decision in this alternate medical care 
proceeding. Therefore, this ruling is designated final agency action and any appeal of 
the decision would be to the Iowa District Court pursuant to Iowa Code section 17A. 
  
The record consists of Claimant's Exhibits 1 through 9, which include a total of 10 
pages. Defendants offered Exhibits A through F, which include a total of 10 pages. All 
exhibits were received without objection. Claimant testified on his own behalf. 
Defendants called Jan Saunders. The evidentiary record closed at the conclusion of the 
alternate medical care hearing. 
   
ISSUE 
  
 
The issue presented for resolution is whether claimant is entitled to ongoing 
prosthetic care through Limb Lab, including authorization of a temporary socket.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
  
Having considered all the evidence and testimony in the record, the undersigned 
finds: 
  
Claimant, Eric Ross, sustained a work-related injury on September 19, 2015, 
resulting in the amputation of his right leg. Following the injury, Mr. Ross was 
transported to the Mayo Clinic in Rochester, Minnesota.  The Mayo Clinic has served as 
Mr. Ross’ authorized provider ever since. More specifically, Fantley C. Smither, M.D., 
has served as Mr. Ross’ authorized treating physician since 2015.  Trenton Kerrigan, 
CPO, of Limb Lab, has served as Mr. Ross’ authorized treating prosthetic provider since 
2015. (Claimant’s Exhibit 5, page 6) 
 
Mr. Ross underwent a bone lengthening procedure in January 2017 and an 
abdominal muscle transplant to the residual limb in May 2017.  Both procedures were 
performed to enable fitting with a prosthetic device.  Thereafter, he was fitted for a 
second prosthetic device, an “Endolite Lynx System,” in conjunction with a Hi-Fi Socket.  
Unfortunately, due to his complex residual limb, Mr. Ross underwent two-staged 
osseointegration surgeries in April 2021 and January 2022. (Ex. 5, p. 6)  The 
osseointegration is a process whereby a connection to a prosthesis is inserted directly 
into a user’s bone. (Id.)  More recently, Mr. Ross underwent an implant removal 
procedure on September 25, 2023, due to infection. (Ex. 2, p. 2) 
 
 
On December 1, 2023, Dr. Smither, in consultation with Mr. Kerrigan, 
recommended Mr. Ross be fitted for another Hi-Fi Socket device for daily use. (Ex. 2, p. 
2; Ex. 5, p. 6)  Dr. Smither recommended this specific socket design as the claimant’s 
residual limb has an unusual shape due to his multiple surgeries. (Id.)  Dr. Smither and 
Mr. Kerrigan opined that the Hi-Fi Socket is medically necessary. (Ex. 5, p. 6)  Limb Lab 
subsequently provided an estimate for the socket to defendants.  Following receipt of 
Limb Lab’s estimate, defendants began negotiations with Limb Lab to reduce the cost of 
the device.  When defendants failed to receive what they believed was a fair price for 
the socket, defendants instructed Limb Lab to close Mr. Ross’ file as, “[T]he carrier can 
direct care in Iowa, and they are going to find a different provider.” (Ex. 3, p. 3; 
Defendants’ Exhibit C, page 3) 
 
On January 18, 2024, Mr. Ross received a text message notifying him of an 
appointment with Hanger Clinic scheduled for February 8, 2024.  The text noted that the 
purpose of the appointment was for a second opinion regarding the recommended 
socket.  
 
On February 8, 2024, Dr. Smither examined Mr. Ross and revised his initial 
recommendation for the Hi-Fi Socket to a quadrilateral design with a “Revo” device to 
adjust socket tightness. (Ex. D, p. 5)  The recommendation did not include a reference 
to any specific prosthetic company.

Later that day, Mr. Ross presented to the Hanger Clinic for a second opinion. 
(Ex. E, pp. 6-8)  Hanger made a cast for the recommended device and produced an 
estimate for the quadrilateral socket to defendants. (Ex. 6, p. 7)  Defendants authorized 
the treatment through Hanger.  In response, Mr. Ross requested that the recommended 
socket be authorized through Limb Lab, his preferred provider. (Ex. 7, p. 8) 
 
Between February 8, 2024, and February 21, 2024, the parties attempted to 
negotiate the price of the quadrilateral socket design to be provided by Limb Lab.  
During negotiations, defendants offered to continue care through Limb Lab if Limb Lab 
would commit to providing the quadrilateral socket for $25,000.00. (Ex. 7, p. 8; Ex. 8, p. 
9)  On February 26, 2024, defendants provided Limb Lab with a letter authorizing them 
to provide Mr. Ross with the temporary socket as recommended by Dr. Smither on 
February 8, 2024. (Ex. F, p. 9)  In the letter, defendants also provided Limb Lab with 
notice of a fee dispute pursuant to 876 Iowa Administrative Code 10-3.  Defendants 
believe Limb Lab’s bill is excessive or unnecessary based on the previously mentioned 
estimate from Hanger Clinic.   
 
Mr. Ross has agreed to be fitted with the quadrilateral socket with the Revo 
device to adjust socket tightness; however, he objects to transferring his prosthetic care 
from Limb Lab to Hanger.  Mr. Ross testified that Mr. Kerrigan or a representative from 
Limb Lab has attended the majority of his appointments with Dr. Smither. (Claimant’s 
Testimony)  He considers the quality of care he has received through Limb Lab to be 
“excellent.”  He testified that he has established a close bond with the people at Limb 
Lab, especially Mr. Kerrigan. (Claimant’s Testimony)  He does not believe he could 
receive the same level of care through a different provider. (Claimant’s Testimony) 
 
Mr. Ross contends that cost is the sole reason defendants refuse to authorize 
ongoing care through Limb Lab.  Mr. Ross further contends delaying prosthetic care for 
over three months is an unreasonable interference with the authorized treating 
physician’s recommendations, an abuse of their right to choose providers, and an 
unreasonable delay in the attempt to invoke fee dispute resolution procedures. 
 
Jan Saunders is a Certified Prosthetist/Orthotist and the Director of Prosthetics at 
Paradigm.  Mr. Saunders has overseen Mr. Ross’ prosthetics case since sometime in 
2020. (Ex. 4, p. 4)  Mr. Saunders testified to his negotiations with Limb Lab, the 
differences between the mentioned prosthetic devices, and the standard costs 
associated with each.    
   
CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
  
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence. Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3). 
  
Iowa Code section 85.27(4) provides, in relevant part:

     For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish 
reasonable services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has 
the right to choose the care. . . . The treatment must be offered promptly 
and be reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience 
to the employee. If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the 
care offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such 
dissatisfaction to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the 
employer and the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited 
to treat the injury. If the employer and employee cannot agree on such 
alternate care, the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable 
proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
  
By challenging the employer's choice of treatment — and seeking alternate care 
— claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable. See 
Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e); Bell Bros. Heating & Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 
N.W.2d 193, 209 (Iowa 2010); Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995). 
Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact. Long v. 
Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995). The employer's obligation turns on the 
question of reasonable necessity, not desirability. Id.; Harned v. Farmland Foods, Inc., 
331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983). 
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).       
In Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 433, 437 (Iowa 1997), the 
supreme court held that “when evidence is presented to the commissioner that the 
employer-authorized medical care has not been effective and that such care is ‘inferior 
or less extensive’ than other available care requested by the employee, . . . the 
commissioner is justified by section 85.27 to order the alternate care.”  
 The record indicates Dr. Smither recommended claimant be fitted for a “Hi-Fi” 
Socket device on December 1, 2023. (Ex. 2, p. 2)  Mr. Kerrigan agreed with Dr. 
Smither’s recommendation. (Ex. 5, p. 6)  Defendants did not immediately authorize the 
requested socket device.  Instead, they began negotiating down the cost of the device 
through Limb Lab.  At one point, Mr. Saunders, acting on behalf of defendants, 
instructed Limb Lab to close claimant’s file as the defendant insurer would be using a 
different provider.  
 
 On February 8, 2024, Dr. Smither revised his initial recommendation for the Hi-Fi 
Socket to a quadrilateral design with a “Revo” device to adjust socket tightness. (Ex. E, 
p. 8)  Claimant agreed to be fitted with the quadrilateral socket with the Revo device to 
adjust socket tightness; however, he objects to transferring his prosthetic care from 
Limb Lab to Hanger.

 Defendants have agreed to authorize the quadrilateral socket with the Revo 
device with both Hanger Clinic and Limb Lab; however, there is a dispute regarding the 
reasonableness of the fee charged by Limb Lab.  As such, claimant has not been 
scheduled to return to Limb Lab to be provided with the replacement socket.  This 
alternate medical care proceeding is not the proper venue for a fee dispute; as such, 
this decision will only address claimant’s entitlement to alternate medical care.   
 
 Claimant argues that it has been three months since Dr. Smither initially 
recommended and ordered the socket, yet he still lacks a prosthetic device.  Claimant 
further points out that Limb Lab has been his authorized prosthetic provider since 2015.  
Lastly, claimant asserts that costs should not be a factor in considering care in this 
matter.   
  
 Defendants' issue with Limb Lab, as highlighted in the attorney’s correspondence 
and in argument at hearing, is motivated by economic considerations. Cost is not, and 
should not, be a criterion in providing claimant with a replacement for his amputated 
right leg. That being said, under the workers’ compensation laws of this state, the 
employer may consider cost and other pertinent factors when selecting providers. Long 
v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  Defendants have demonstrated 
that Hanger Clinic could provide the recommended quadrilateral socket at a lower cost 
than Limb Lab is currently willing to offer.  However, cost is but one factor when 
evaluating the reasonableness of the treatment offered.   
 
 Limb Lab, the authorized prosthetic provider, has the ability to provide claimant 
with the quadrilateral socket recommended by Dr. Smither.  Claimant has worked with 
Limb Lab and Mr. Kerrigan since 2015.  Claimant testified to the excellent care he has 
received from Limb Lab and his belief that he could not receive the same level of care 
from a different provider.  Hanger Clinic has only evaluated claimant on one occasion 
for a second opinion.  Limb Lab and Mr. Kerrigan have far greater experience with 
claimant’s medical presentation and more familiarity with claimant’s needs than does 
Hanger Clinic.  Claimant is requesting that he receive ongoing care through Limb Lab, 
the authorized prosthetic provider.  Given claimant’s longstanding relationship with Limb 
Lab and Mr. Kerrigan, I find a transfer of care to Hanger Clinic because of a slight 
difference in cost to be unnecessary and unreasonable. 
 
 Therefore, I find claimant has carried his burden of proving entitlement to the 
requested care with Limb Lab.
