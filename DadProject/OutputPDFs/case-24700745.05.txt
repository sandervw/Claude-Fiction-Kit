STATEMENT OF THE CASE  
On April 22, 2025, the claimant filed a petition for alternate medical care pursuant 
to Iowa Code 85.27(4) and 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.48.  The defendant filed an 
answer admitting liability for injuries to the claimant’s bilateral shoulders, bilateral legs, 
bilateral arms, back, neck, hip, and mental health.  
The undersigned presided over the hearing held electronically and recorded 
digitally on May 12, 2025.  That recording constitutes the official record of the 
proceeding pursuant to 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.48(12).  The claimant 
participated through her attorney, Saffin Parrish-Sams.  The defendant participated 
through their attorney, Charles Showalter.   
Prior to the hearing, the claimant submitted ten pages of exhibits, marked as 
Exhibits 1-5.  The defendant submitted four pages of exhibits, marked as Exhibits A-B.  
The defendant objected to Claimant’s proposed Exhibit 5, on the basis that it was not 
timely filed, it was irrelevant, and that it contained information concerning settlement 
discussions.  After hearing arguments on the record, the undersigned overruled the 
defendant’s objection.  While the proposed exhibit was received the morning of the 
hearing, this often happens in alternate medical care proceedings, due to the expedited 
nature of the contested case proceedings.  Additionally, in reviewing the proposed 
exhibit, the undersigned noted that the proposed exhibits contained potentially relevant 
information.  Finally, the undersigned noted that the proposal of a consent order is not 
akin to a traditional settlement communication, which would ordinarily be inappropriate

On July 21, 2023, the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commissioner issued an

ISSUE 
The issues under consideration were: 
1. Whether the defendant should be ordered to provide the correct contact and 
billing information to Plains Area Mental Health Clinic (“PAMHC”) related to 
the claimant’s future care. 
2. Whether the defendant should be ordered to work with Medicaid to reverse 
certain charges from August 22, 2024, to March 3, 2025. 
3. Whether the defendant should be ordered to pay past charges from PAMHC 
from August 22, 2024, to March 3, 2025. 
4. Whether the imposition of sanctions pursuant to 876 Iowa Administrative 
Code 4.36 is appropriate. 
FINDINGS OF FACT

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Iowa Code 85.27(4) provides, in relevant part: 
For purposes of this section, the employer is obligated to furnish 
reasonable services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has 
the right to choose the care….  The treatment must be offered promptly 
and be reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience 
to the employee.  If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the 
care offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such 
dissatisfaction to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the 
employer and the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited 
to treat the injury.  If the employer and employee cannot agree on such 
alternate care, the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable 
proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
Iowa Code 85.27(4). See Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 433 (Iowa 
1997).   
“Iowa Code section 85.27(4) affords an employer who does not contest the 
compensability of a workplace injury a qualified statutory right to control the medical 
care provided to an injured employee.”  Ramirez-Trujillo v. Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 
N.W.2d 759, 769 (Iowa 2016) (citing R.R. Donnelly & Sons v. Barnett, 670 N.W.2d 190, 
195, 197 (Iowa 2003)).  “In enacting the right-to-choose provision in section 85.27(4), 
our legislature sought to balance the interests of injured employees against the 
competing interests of their employers.”  Ramirez, 878 N.W.2d at 770-71 (citing Bell 
Bros., 779 N.W.2d at 202, 207; IBP, Inc. v. Harker, 633 N.W.2d 322, 326-27 (Iowa 
2001)).

The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except where the 
employer has denied liability for the injury.  Iowa Code section 85.27; Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening, October 16, 1975).  An employer’s right to select 
the provider of medical treatment to an injured worker does not include the right to 
determine how an injured worker should be diagnosed, evaluated, treated, or other 
matters of professional medical judgment.  Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 
866389 (Declaratory Ruling, May 19, 1988).  Reasonable care includes care necessary 
to diagnose the condition, and defendants are not entitled to interfere with the medical 
judgment of its own treating physician.  Pote v. Mickow Corp., File No. 694639 (Review-
Reopening Decision, June 17, 1986).   
The employer must furnish “reasonable medical services and supplies and 
reasonable and necessary appliances to treat an injured employee.”  Stone Container 
Corp. v. Castle, 657 N.W.2d 485, 490 (Iowa 2003)(emphasis in original).  Such 
employer-provided care “must be offered promptly and be reasonably suited to treat the 
injury without undue inconvenience to the employee.”  Iowa Code section 85.27(4).  
More specifically in Long, the court noted that “the employer’s obligation under the 
statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.”  Long v. Roberts 
Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122, 128 (Iowa 1995).   
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment - and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See e.g. 
Iowa R. App. P. 14(f)(5); Bell Bros. Heating and Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d 
193, 209 (Iowa 2010); Long, 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  More specifically in Long, 
the court noted that “the employer’s obligation under the statute turns on the question of 
reasonable necessity, not desirability.”  Long, 528 N.W.2d at 128.   
An injured employee dissatisfied with the employer-furnished care (or lack 
thereof) may share the employee’s discontent with the employer and if the parties 
cannot reach an agreement on alternate care, “the commissioner may, upon application 
and reasonable proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and order the care.”  Id.  
“Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.”  Long, 528 
N.W.2d at 123; Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 436.  As the party seeking 
relief in the form of alternate care, the employee bears the burden of proving that the 
authorized care is unreasonable.  Id. at 124; Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d at 209; Pirelli-
Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 436.  Because “the employer’s obligation under the 
statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability,” an injured 
employee’s dissatisfaction with employer-provided care, standing alone, is not enough 
to find such care unreasonable.  Id.  However, if substantial evidence exists that the 
care authorized by the employer was ineffective, it may be found that the care was not 
reasonable.  Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 437.

There are four issues in this alternate care proceeding.  I will address them 
individually.  The first is whether Tyson should be ordered to provide the PAMHC with 
“the correct contact and billing information to send all future medical claims for 
payment.”  Defendant’s counsel represented during the hearing that Tyson has provided 
PAMHC with this information.  Defendant’s counsel pointed to the email sent from 
PAMHC on May 8, 2025, and provided a professional representation that the correct 
contact and billing information has been provided to PAMHC.  Claimant’s counsel 
disagreed and indicated that the PAMHC email amounted only to an acknowledgment 
of a receipt of a request for information. 
 
In reviewing the evidence, I find that claimant’s argument is more persuasive on 
this issue.  The defendant shall promptly provide PAMHC with information indicating 
that PAMHC is an authorized provider and indicating to where PAMHC should forward 
medical billing through Tyson.   
 
The second and third issues are best analyzed on a combined basis.  These 
issues are whether Tyson should be ordered to work with Medicaid to “get the 08/22/24 
through 03/03/25 charges (and any additional charges since that time) reversed” and 
whether Tyson should be ordered to “pay [PAMHC] for the 08/22/24 through 03/03/25 
dates of service (and any additional dates of service since that time.”   
 
The claimant argued that the hearing that certain other alternate care decisions 
support the notion that a defendant can be ordered to pay for past medical billing 
through an alternate care hearing.  The claimant also argued that language in Iowa 
Code section 85.27(4), specifically a of the last sentence of Iowa Code section 85.27(4) 
provides the opportunity for a Deputy Commissioner to order a defendant to pay past 
medical billing.  Iowa Code section 85.27(4) states in pertinent part, “[i]f the employer 
and employee cannot agree on such alternate care, the commissioner may, upon 
application and reasonable proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and order other care.”  
See Iowa Code section 85.27(4)(emphasis added).  The claimant argues that “other 
care” in Iowa Code section 85.27(4) refers to things like paying medical bills through an 
alternate care proceeding.  The undersigned is not authorized by Iowa law to engage in 
statutory construction.  However, the plain language of the statute and the context of 
Iowa Code section 85.27(4) indicates that “other care” would not refer to ordering the 
payment of medical billing. 
 
During the hearing, the claimant cited a case in support of the proposition that a 
Deputy Commissioner may order a defendant to pay for certain medical billing through 
an alternate care proceeding.  The first case is Hanawalt v. American Bottling Co.  In 
Hanawalt, the claimant’s allegations already proceeded through an arbitration 
proceeding.  Hanawalt v. American Bottling Co., File No. 20008990.09 (Alt. Care July 
24, 2024).  The claimant then had to file a number of alternate medical care petitions

seeking certain care.  Id.  These petitions were granted, including mental health 
appointments, approval of a gym membership, massage therapy, and decompression 
table therapy.  Id.  In a prior proceeding, the presiding deputy also found that the 
claimant could direct his own care.  Id.  The claimant then submitting billing from several 
chiropractors that remained unpaid for care recommended by an authorized treating 
provider.  Id.  The deputy found that the defendant’s conduct was unreasonable due to 
prior orders and that the billing provided should have been paid based upon past orders 
in prior alternate care proceedings.  Id.   
 
The defendant cites to Arnzen v. Tyson Foods, Inc., in support of their argument 
that an alternate care hearing operates prospectively, not retrospectively.  In Arnzen, 
the claimant underwent a carpal tunnel surgery by an unauthorized treating provider.  
Arnzen v. Tyson Foods, Inc., File No. 5062268.01 (Alt. Care Nov. 7, 2018).  In citing to 
a number of cases, the deputy in Arnzen noted that:  
As routinely stated by this agency, a decision in an alternate medical care 
proceeding operates prospectively only, not retroactively.  The claimant’s 
application for alternate care should be dismissed without prejudice when 
the claimant seeks payment for medical care that had been provided prior 
to the time the alternate care petition was filed. 
  The deputy then cited to a number of cases supporting this proposition, 
including: Moline v. Nordstrom, File No. 1273226 (December 21, 2000); Donisi v. Norrell 
Services, File No. 1276161 (August 8, 2000); Mobayed v. AMS Services, Inc., File No. 
1168048 (May 20, 1997); and, Massie v. Madison Avenue Dairy Queen, File No. 
1055168 (November 3, 1995).     
 
In another case, Dunham v. UPS, Inc., a claimant sought reimbursement and/or 
payment for authorized medical care.  Dunham v. UPS, Inc., File No. 5062713.01 (Alt. 
Care Feb. 28, 2020).  In Dunham, the claimant underwent a number of surgeries.  Id.  
The claimant then sought follow-up care, for which they had to pay out of pocket, and 
requested an order that the defendants reimburse providers for billing remaining unpaid 
for past medical care.  Id.  The deputy in that case noted, “[i]n an expedited alternate 
medical care proceeding this agency does not determine or order payment for past 
medical care, but looks at whether the defendants are offering reasonable care at the 
present time.”  Id.  The deputy declined to ruled on the defendants’ obligation regarding 
any past reimbursement for medical billing.   
 
The greater weight of agency precedent lends support to the proposition that 
alternate care hearings are meant to be prospective, not retrospective.  Additionally, as 
noted in Dunham, the purpose of an alternate care proceeding is to look at whether 
reasonable care was offered at the present time, and not to determine or order payment

for past medical care.  It would be inappropriate in this case to order the defendant to 
pay for past medical billing or to work with Medicaid to reverse charges paid by 
Medicaid.  Therefore, the claimant’s petition as to these two issues is dismissed without 
prejudice.  It should be noted that there is nothing in this order that precludes the 
claimant from seeking payment of these bills through an arbitration proceeding.

she now faced the inconvenience of having to deal with a subrogation lien.

their request for sanctions.  In Erlbacher, the claimant had to file a number of alternate

Like the deputy in Erlbacher, I too am tempted to order sanctions in this case 
pursuant to 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.36; however, I decline to do so.  The 
claimant has continued to treat with authorized providers at PAMHC.  There has been 
no delay in her treatment.  The only issue is with payment for medical billing.  These 
issues have been rectified and/or are inappropriate for an expedited alternate care 
proceeding.  Therefore, I conclude the imposition of sanctions is not appropriate in this 
case.   
However, the defendant is skating on very thin ice.  In a previous alternate 
medical care decision, I declined to sanction the defendant despite their blatant 
disobedience of a consent order.  I again am declining to sanction the defendant in this 
alternate care proceeding despite what is arguably negligence of their obligations under 
Iowa Code section 85.27 to provide the claimant with medical care.  If the defendant 
continues the documented behaviors in prior alternate care proceedings and the instant 
proceeding, they will find themselves sanctioned. 
IT IS THEREFORE ORDERED: 
1. The claimant’s petition for alternate care is granted in part, dismissed without 
prejudice in part, and denied in part: 
  
a. The defendant shall promptly provide PAMHC with authorization for 
treatment, including information on where to send billing for Ms. 
Jimenez’s mental health care treatment. 
b. The alternate care petition as it relates to the claimant’s request that 
the defendant work with Medicaid to reimburse Medicaid for certain 
bills and/or that the defendant pay any outstanding balances for care 
with PAMHC is dismissed without prejudice. 
c. The claimant’s request for sanctions is denied.
