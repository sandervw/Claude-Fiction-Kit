The Petitioners’ Petition for Judicial Review came before the Court on February 10, 
2025. The Respondent filed cross-appeal with this court on February 14, 2025. A hearing was 
held before this Court on June 13, 2025. The Petitioners were represented by Taelor J. Cooper-

I. 
BACKGROUND FACTS AND PROCEEDINGS.  
 
Ronald Hodges (“Respondent”) has been employed as a Roman Catholic Priest for the 
Diocese of Davenport since January 2011. D0016, Pet. Br. at 6 (4/23/2025). There are two dates 
of injury that are relevant to the motion before the Court: November 3, 2013 and July 18, 2014. 
On November 3, 2013, Respondent sustained a work injury to his left foot, and Petitioners paid 
all medical bills related to this injury. Id. at 7. However, Petitioners paid no weekly indemnity 
benefits. 
 
On July 18, 2014, Respondent sustained another work injury to his left foot and received 
weekly indemnity benefits for his injury via temporary total disability benefits from GuideOne

II. 
SCOPE AND STANDARD OF REVIEW. 
The Iowa Administrative Procedure Act, Iowa Code chapter 17A, governs the scope of 
the Court’s review in workers' compensation cases. Iowa Code § 10A.322 (2023); Meyer v. IBP, 
Inc., 710 N.W.2d 213, 218 (Iowa 2006). “Under the Act, we may only interfere with the 
commissioner's decision if it is erroneous under one of the grounds enumerated in the statute, 
and a party's substantial rights have been prejudiced.” Meyer, 710 N.W.2d at 218. A party

challenging agency action bears the burden of demonstrating the action's invalidity and resulting 
prejudice. Iowa Code § 17A.19(8)(a). This can be shown in various ways, including proof the 
action was ultra vires; legally erroneous; unsupported by substantial evidence in the record when 
that record is viewed as a whole; or otherwise unreasonable, arbitrary, capricious, or an abuse of 
discretion. See id. § 17A.19(10). The district court acts in an appellate capacity to correct errors 
of law on the part of the agency. Grundmeyer v. Weyerhaeuser Co., 649 N.W.2d 744, 748 (Iowa 
2002). 
“If the claim of error lies with the agency's findings of fact, the proper question on review 
is whether substantial evidence supports those findings of fact” when the record is viewed as a 
whole. Meyer, 710 N.W.2d at 219. Factual findings regarding the award of workers' 
compensation benefits are within the commissioner's discretion, so the court is bound by the 
commissioner's findings of fact if they are supported by substantial evidence. Mycogen Seeds v. 
Sands, 686 N.W.2d 457, 464-65 (Iowa 2004). Substantial evidence is defined as evidence of the 
quality and quantity “that would be deemed sufficient by a neutral, detached, and reasonable 
person, to establish the fact at issue when the consequences resulting from the establishment of 
that fact are understood to be serious and of great importance.” Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(f)(1); 
Mycogen, 686 N.W.2d at 464. The application of the law to the facts is also an enterprise vested 
in the commissioner. Mycogen, 686 N.W.2d at 465. Accordingly, the Court will reverse only if 
the commissioner's application was “irrational, illogical, or wholly unjustifiable.”  Id.; Iowa 
Code § 17A.19(10)(l). This standard requires the Court to allocate some deference to the 
commissioner's application of law to the facts, but less than it gives to the agency's findings of 
fact. Larson Mfg. Co. v. Thorson, 763 N.W.2d 842, 850 (Iowa 2009).

a. Statute of Limitations. 
The Petitioners claim that the Commissioner erred in holding that the statute of 
limitations date for the July 2014 injury date would not expire until November 2019. The 
Commissioner held, in relevant part: 
[T]he Iowa Supreme Court interpreted Iowa Code section 85.26(2) in Beier Glass 
Co. v. Brundige, 329 N.W.2d 280 (Iowa 1983). In that case, the Court held that the 
statute of limitations in Iowa Code section 85.26(2) does not expire until three years 
after approval of the agreement for settlement. In Beier Glass, no weekly benefits 
were paid or agreed upon in the agreement for settlement. Nevertheless, the Iowa 
Supreme Court concluded, “the legislature used the words weekly benefits, not to 
restrict the nature of the initial award, but merely to extend the three-year limitation 
period as far as possible, beyond the last payment of benefits received by the 
worker.” Id. at 286. It would seem odd that an injured worker that receives only 
medical benefits under an agreement for settlement would have a three-year statute 
of limitations after the agreement for settlement is approved while an injured 
worker that received weekly benefits prior to the settlement but no additional 
weekly benefits after the settlement has less than three years after the agreement 
for settlement to pursue a review-reopening proceeding. It would seem particularly 
odd that an injured worker would have less than three years to pursue review 
reopening after approval of an agreement for settlement given the Iowa Supreme 
Court’s analysis that the intention of the legislature was “to extend the three-year 
limitations period as far as possible.” Id. I find claimant had three years from the 
date the agreement for settlement was approved to pursue a review reopening 
before the statute of limitations expired. Id. Under this analysis, the statute of 
limitations for the 2014 injury date would not expire until November 2019. 
 
D0007, Certified Rec. at 8 (3/14/2025). 
 
Respondent argues that Beier Glass should apply to this case, and that its application 
would extend the deadline for filing to November 23, 2019. D0018, Respondent’s Br. at p. 26 
(5/23/2025). 
The Iowa Supreme Court held in Beier Glass that, “the limitation period commences on 
the date of the award or filing of the memorandum of agreement when no weekly benefits are 
awarded initially.” 329 N.W.2d 280, 287 (Iowa 1983) (emphasis added). This line seems to 
apply to a narrow class of cases where no weekly benefits are awarded initially, rather than 
E-FILED                    CVCV068632 - 2025 AUG 05 02:25 PM             POLK

applying to all cases.  
Iowa Code section 85.26(2) (2023) states “an award for payments or an agreement for 
settlement… may be reviewed upon commencement of reopening proceedings by the employer 
or the employee within three years from the date of the last payment of weekly benefits made 
under the award or agreement.” In the present case, the Respondent was paid weekly indemnity 
benefits before the settlement agreement via temporary and permanent disability. The last 
payment of weekly indemnity worker’s compensation benefits was paid on March 5, 2015. The 
statute of limitations, therefore, was on March 5, 2018. Beier Glass does not apply to the present 
case because weekly benefits were paid.  
In the present case, the Commissioner misinterpreted Beier Glass to push the statute of 
limitations out as far as possible. Iowa Code section 85.26(2) clearly states that the clock begins 
running on the date of last payment of weekly benefits. This interpretation of Beier Glass would 
totally nullify the “within three years from the date of the last payment of weekly benefits” 
language of section 85.26(2). The Legislature perhaps did not contemplate a scenario in which 
no weekly benefits are paid, so Beier Glass provides a starting point for clock to begin ticking in 
such a scenario. Beier Glass does not, however, give Courts free reign to disregard section 
85.26(2) and move the starting timer for the statute of limitations wherever it pleases.  
Because weekly indemnity benefits were paid by the Petitioner to the Respondent, the 
last of which were paid on March 5, 2015, the statute of limitations to review and reopen this 
matter would have been on March 5, 2018. Because the Respondent admitted his only Review-
Reopening Petition was filed on June 27, 2022 (D0007 at 156), this Court holds that the statute 
of limitations has run.

b. Moffit Factors. 
Even if the statute of limitations had not run, the Respondent would not be able to show 
that the wages he was paid were actually weekly workers’ compensation benefits. In his opinion, 
the Commissioner relied on Moffitt v. Super Valu Stores, in which former Commissioner Post 
established a three-factor test to determine whether wages were paid in lieu of worker’s 
compensation indemnity benefits. File No. 10059425 (App. June 19, 1998). The factors 
considered are: 
(1) the employer was aware of the work-related nature of the injury; 
(2) the employee was off work due to the injury; and 
(3) the employer paid regular salary payments to the injured worker while they 
were off work as a substitute for the workers' compensation payments that it 
otherwise would have been obligated to pay. 
 
Id. at 7 
If the true nature of the payment was worker’s compensation weekly benefits, that is 
controlling rather than the label of salary assigned by the employer. Id. at 5. The label the 
employer gives the benefits is of little importance. Per Iowa Code section 85.26(1) (2023), if no 
indemnity benefits have been paid, a claimant must file a Petition within two years from the date 
of injury. However, if indemnity benefits have been paid, the claimant has three years from the 
date of the last indemnity benefit payment to file. 
i. Moffitt element one. 
The first element of the Moffitt test is whether the employer was aware of the work-
related nature of the injury. The initial disputed issue is whether payment of Respondent’s salary 
between February 1, 2019 and June 30, 2019 constituted payment of wages in lieu of indemnity 
benefits. Petitioners do not dispute that the Respondent was experiencing work issues related to

his work as a priest prior to and during his leave of absence, or that the Diocese was aware of the 
injury. Therefore, element one of the Moffitt test is met. 
ii. Moffitt element two. 
The second element of the Moffitt test is whether the employee was off work due to the 
injury. The Commissioner concluded that because the Respondent was taken off work “at least in 
part, due to the work injury,” that this Moffitt element was met. D0007 at 13. However, just 
because the Respondent was managing issues related to his work injury does not warrant the 
conclusion that he was off work due to the injury. The Respondent was managing several non-
work-related medical injuries, and no physician took him off work because of his work injury. 
Id. at 15. 
The record, as this Court sees it, does not support the Commissioner’s conclusion. 
Nothing in the record suggests that the Bishop of Davenport, Thomas Zinkula, took the 
Respondent off work because of his injuries. Respondent was working as his full capability 
before his leave of absence since his light duty restrictions allowed it, which the Commissioner 
acknowledged in his decision. Id. at 14. The conclusion that the Respondent was off work as a 
result of his work injury is not supported by substantial evidence in the record. Therefore, 
element two of the Moffitt test is not met. 
iii.  Moffitt element three.  
The third element of the Moffitt test is whether the employer paid regular salary payments 
to the injured worker while they were off work as a substitute for the workers' compensation 
payments that it otherwise would have been obligated to pay. The Respondent testified at an 
administrative hearing that he did receive his “regular salary” during the five months he was on 
medical leave. D0007 at 176-7. However, Tiara Hatfield, the director of human resources and

risk management for the Diocese, testified that the Diocese has no sick pay for priests. Id. at 212. 
She further testified that priests do not have to report when they are using sick time, and they are 
paid their regular salary regardless of whether they work or do not work due to sickness. Id. at 
179, 212. When a priest in the Diocese is sick, they communicate with the parish that they are 
not available to perform their parish duties. Id. at 179. There is no limitation on the number of 
sick days a priest can take in a year, and the Diocese does not monitor how many days a priest 
takes off work. Id. at 179-80. Respondent did not request any workers’ compensation benefits 
during this time. Id. at 81. 
The second half of Moffitt’s third element is whether the employer would have been 
otherwise obligated to pay indemnity benefits. For wages to act as compensation in order to 
extend the statute of limitations to three years from the last date of payment, the compensations 
benefit must be actually due and payable for the period of time for which wages or salary are 
paid. Boyd v. Ankeny Cmty Sch. Dist., File No. 1225693, (App. December 31, 2003). 
The Court agrees with Petitioners that the Commissioner erred in his ruling. This Court 
finds that there is not substantial evidence in the record showing that the Diocese made regular 
salary payments to the Respondent as a substitute for workers’ compensation payments, or that 
the Diocese would have been otherwise obligated to pay indemnity benefits. Considering the 
Diocese has no sick pay scheme for priests, and that priests may take as much sick time as they 
need while still receiving their regular salary, it is hard for this Court to see how the 
Respondent’s salary could have been paid as a substitute. Therefore, element three of the Moffitt 
test is note met.
