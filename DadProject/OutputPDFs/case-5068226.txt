STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Carlos Alvarado, has filed a petition for arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits against Arconic, Inc., employer and Indemnity Insurance 
Company of North America, insurer, both as defendants for an accepted work injury 
occurring on November 2, 2016.  
In accordance with agency scheduling procedures and pursuant to the Order of 
the Commissioner in the matter of the Coronavirus/COVID-19 Impact on Hearings, the 
hearing was held on August 11, 2020, and considered fully submitted upon the same. 
Parties declined the opportunity to brief.  
The record consists of Joint Exhibit 1 through 6 and Claimant’s Exhibits 1 
through 2 along with the testimony of claimant and Lisa Travis Hilbert.   
ISSUES 
The extent of claimant’s permanent disability; 
The assessment of costs.

STIPULATIONS 
The parties stipulate the claimant sustained a work-related injury on November 2, 
2016. The injury was the cause of some temporary disability during a period of 
recovery, entitlement to which is no longer in dispute. While the parties agree the 
claimant has sustained an industrial disability and that the commencement date for 
permanent partial disability benefits is September 29, 2017, they disagree as to the 
extent of any permanent disability. 
At the time of the injury the claimant’s gross earnings were $1,308.91. The 
claimant was single and entitled to two exemptions. Based on the foregoing the weekly 
benefit rate is $760.21. 
Defendants waive all affirmative defenses. There are no medical benefits in 
dispute. 
Prior to the hearing the claimant was paid 25 weeks of compensation at the rate 
of $875.18. Defendant seek a credit of that amount against any award of permanent 
partial disability. 
The parties agree the claimant paid the costs identified in Exhibit 1:3. 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
At the time of the hearing the claimant was a 48-year-old person. He was born in 
Mexico and has received a 9th or 10th grade education. He obtained his GED in 1994 
after immigrating to the United States. Following the GED, he obtained a certificate in 
heating and air conditioning from Scott Community College. (Exhibit 1:3) Despite 
obtaining that degree, he has not worked in the HVAC field.  
His past work history has been primarily heavy manual labor. He worked as a 
machine operator at IBP for nearly a decade, then moved on to Porter Dayco where he 
assembled hydraulic hoses for John Deere. He left this position because it was too 
physically demanding. After the assembly position, he worked as a packer for Oscar 
Meyer.  
Claimant is right-handed and testified that he had no physical problems in his 
right shoulder prior to the accepted work injury. He underwent, and passed, a pre-
employment physical with defendant employer.  
For the defendant employer, claimant earned approximately $14.50 per hour and 
at the time of his injury was working a job classified as medium to heavy duty. (JE 2:7) 
As part of his essential duties, he would stack sheets of metal. Sometimes he would lift 
the sheets of metal onto the machine and other times, a crane was used to lift the metal 
and claimant would guide the sheets into place. He would operate the machine to cut 
the metal and then remove the cut pieces. He was required to frequently push and pull 
weights up to 35 pounds and then occasionally push and pull up to 70 pounds. (JE 7:2)

On or about November 2, 2016, claimant stumbled while attempting to climb into 
his fork lift truck. He caught himself with both arms but pulled his right shoulder. (JE 1:1) 
He felt immediate pain and reported it to his supervisor who directed him to the 
defendants’ onsite medical care. During the medical examination, he was unable to 
abduct or raise the right arm and had pinpoint discomfort at the posterior shoulder. (JE 
2:4) He received conservative treatment and was returned to work without restrictions. 
(JE 2:4)  
On December 15, 2016, claimant underwent an MRI which showed a 
full-thickness tear involving much of the supraspinatus and infraspinatus tendons, 
possible partial tear of the subscapularis tendon and reactive bone marrow edema in 
the greater tuberosity. (JE 4:38-39) 
On January 20, 2017, he was seen at ORA Orthopedics by Suleman M. Hussain, 
M.D. (JE 3:22) Claimant reported throbbing pain in the right shoulder radiating down the 
arm. (JE 3:21) On examination, he had reduced range of motion and reduced strength 
on the right compared to the left. Id. Due to the massive retracted rotator cuff tear 
involving the supraspinatus, fragments of a great tuberosity and a high riding humerus, 
surgery was planned. (JE 3:22) He was returned to light duty work with no overhead 
exertions. Id. Surgery took place on February 28, 2017. (JE 5:40) Claimant underwent 
arthroscopic repair of a massive retracted rotator cuff tear involving the supraspinatus 
and infraspinatus tendons, subpectoral biceps tenodesis, subacromial bursectomy and 
acromioplasty, and debridement of the type 2 superior labral tear. (JE 5:40-41). He was 
okayed for light duty, seated desk work with the left arm and no lifting, pushing or pulling 
anything greater than 1-2 pounds with the left. (JE 3:23)  
On March 17, 2017, he was returned to light duty, seated desk work but no 
driving. (JE 3:23) During his April visit, he reported burning during and after physical 
therapy and numbness in the right hand. (JE 3:25) The sling was discontinued and 
claimant was ordered to continue physical therapy. (JE 3:25)  He was returned to light 
duty work and allowed to drive a personal vehicle. (JE 3:25) By August 23, 2017, 
claimant had improved range of motion but maintained the same pain complaints. (JE 
3:20) Dr. Hussain advised claimant that he would only be able to obtain 85-90 percent

conducted on August 29, 2017. (JE 6:42) During the examination, claimant exhibited 
minor limitations of range of motion and decreased strength in the right shoulder and 
significant decrease in grip strength. (JE 6:44) He was limited in reaching away from his 
body as well as reaching above shoulder height. (JE 6:45) claimant was concerned that

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Claimant has sustained a work injury to his right shoulder resulting in permanent 
work restrictions. The sole issue is extent. Defendants argue claimant’s loss is minimal. 
He is earning more today than at the time of his injury. He has few restrictions and has 
not sought out or received any medical care since early 2018.  
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa Rule of Appellate 
Procedure 6.14(6). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996).

The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony.  The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question.  The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances.  The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); 
Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995).  Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994).  Unrebutted expert medical 
testimony cannot be summarily rejected.  Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 
N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 1994). 
Because claimant has sustained a whole body injury, he is entitled to be 
compensated pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.34 (2017). 
Industrial disability was defined in Diederich v. Tri-City R. Co., 219 Iowa 587, 258 
N.W. 899 (1935) as follows: "It is therefore plain that the legislature intended the term 
'disability' to mean 'industrial disability' or loss of earning capacity and not a mere 
'functional disability' to be computed in the terms of percentages of the total physical 
and mental ability of a normal man." 
Functional impairment is an element to be considered in determining industrial 
disability which is the reduction of earning capacity, but consideration must also be 
given to the injured employee's age, education, qualifications, experience, motivation, 
loss of earnings, severity and situs of the injury, work restrictions, inability to engage in 
employment for which the employee is fitted and the employer's offer of work or failure 
to so offer.  McSpadden v. Big Ben Coal Co., 288 N.W.2d 181 (Iowa 1980); Olson v. 
Goodyear Service Stores, 255 Iowa 1112, 125 N.W.2d 251 (1963); Barton v. Nevada 
Poultry Co., 253 Iowa 285, 110 N.W.2d 660 (1961). 
Claimant is a 48-year-old immigrant who has a GED with some post-secondary 
training in HVAC, an occupation he has not pursued. He worked various plant positions 
and settled with the defendant employer in 2004 where he has worked since. He has 
held steady employment since 1991, working nearly a decade for IBP. Claimant is 
motivated to return to work and did so as his body and restrictions allowed. While he 
has not sustained a dramatic loss of earnings, there is some loss given that claimant’s 
previous position is a higher pay rate than his current position. Further, he testified 
credibly that he no longer works as many overtime hours. His current position is also an 
accommodated one. He cannot undertake all the essential tasks of his position due to 
the severe tear in his right rotator cuff and the resulting reduced range of motion and 
loss of strength. Dr. Hussain estimated that claimant’s post-injury shoulder would be 
approximately 85-90 percent of the original function.

Dr. Hussain opined claimant’s impairment was 5 percent while the IME, Dr. Hart, 
assessed a 10 percent impairment and imposed a few more restrictions such as 
avoiding repetitious activity with the right upper extremity and no overhead use of the 
right upper extremity.  
Dr. Hart’s restrictions and impairment rating are more consistent with the 
claimant’s complaints as well as his physical exertions. Claimant struggles with 
overhead work and asked for additional accommodations as well as assistance from 
time to time from his co-workers. He testified credibly that his right shoulder is sore, 
weak and tired. His past work history has been as a laborer whether it was at IBP or 
Oscar Mayer. Based on the claimant’s testimony, his accommodated work, his reduced 
access to future employment, his restrictions, age and education, and work experience 
it is determined that he has sustained a 20 percent industrial loss.  
Claimant also requests an assessment of costs. 876 IAC 5.33 allows for the 
assessment of costs at the discretion of the deputy. Given that claimant has prevailed in 
this matter, the assessment of costs against defendant employer and insurer is 
appropriate.  
ORDER 
THEREFORE, it is ordered: 
That defendants employer and insurer are to pay unto claimant one hundred 
(100) weeks of permanent partial disability benefits at the rate of seven hundred sixty 
and 21/100 dollars ($760.21) per week from September 29, 2017. 
That defendants shall pay accrued weekly benefits in a lump sum. 
That defendants shall pay interest on unpaid weekly benefits awarded herein as 
set forth in Iowa Code section 85.30. 
That defendants are entitled to a credit of twenty-five (25) weeks of 
compensation paid at a rate of eight hundred seventy-five and 18/100 dollars ($875.18) 
per week.  
That defendants shall pay the costs of this matter pursuant to rule 876 IAC 4.33.
