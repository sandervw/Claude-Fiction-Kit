STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
On February 17, 2025, the claimant filed a petition for alternate medical care 
pursuant to Iowa Code 85.27(4) and 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.48.  The 
defendant filed an answer admitting liability for injuries noted in the petition. 
The undersigned presided over the hearing held via telephone and recorded 
digitally on February 27, 2025.  That recording constitutes the official record of the 
proceeding pursuant to 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.48(12).  Claimant participated 
through his attorney, Nick Cooling.  The defendant participated through their attorney, 
Abigail Wenninghoff.   
Prior to the hearing, the claimant submitted six pages of exhibits, marked as 
Exhibits 1-4.  The defendant submitted no exhibits.  The defendant objected to the 
claimant’s proposed exhibits as both late, pursuant to my February 18, 2025, prehearing 
order, and as already in evidence in another matter.  The exhibits were admitted in their 
entirety over the defendant’s objection.     
 
On July 21, 2023, the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commissioner issued an 
order delegating authority to deputy workers’ compensation commissioners, such as the 
undersigned, to issue final agency decisions on applications for alternate care.  
Consequently, this decision constitutes final agency action, and there is no appeal to 
the commissioner.  Judicial review in a district court pursuant to Iowa Code Chapter 17A 
is the avenue for an appeal. 
ISSUE 
The issue under consideration is whether the claimant should be allowed to 
choose their own physician who would be deemed an authorized provider for ongoing 
care.  Also, there is a request from the claimant for sanctions pursuant to 876 Iowa 
Administrative Code 4.36.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
Claimant, Adam Podnar, alleges that he sustained injuries to his left hand, left 
arm, and right arm on or about August 6, 2024, while working for Hormel Foods Corp. 
(“Hormel”).   
 
The claimant previously filed an alternate care petition seeking the same care 
sought in the instant case, namely care with an orthopedic hand specialist.  The file 
number for that contested case proceeding was 24700824.02.  This matter was 
previously scheduled for an alternate care hearing on February 4, 2025.  Prior to the 
scheduled hearing in File Number 24700824.02, it became apparent to the undersigned 
that a hearing may not be necessary.  Thus, the undersigned reached out to the parties 
proposing a consent order.  The parties agreed to a consent order, including certain 
terms and deadlines.  The undersigned issued a consent order on February 4, 2025, 
which stated in pertinent part: 
The defendant consents, stipulates, and agrees to authorize treatment 
with an orthopedic specialist for the claimant.  The defendant also agreed 
to arrange an appointment for the authorized treatment as soon as 
possible, but no later than seven days from the date of this order.   
See Podnar v. Hormel Foods Corp., File No. 24700824.02 (Consent Order, February 4, 
2025).

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Iowa Code 85.27(4) provides, in relevant part: 
For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish reasonable 
services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has the right to 
choose the care….  The treatment must be offered promptly and be 
reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the 
employee.  If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the care 
offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such dissatisfaction 
to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the employer and 
the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited to treat the 
injury.  If the employer and employee cannot agree on such alternate care, 
the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable proofs of the 
necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
Iowa Code 85.27(4). See Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 433 (Iowa 
1997).   
“Iowa Code section 85.27(4) affords an employer who does not contest the 
compensability of a workplace injury a qualified statutory right to control the medical 
care provided to an injured employee.”  Ramirez-Trujillo v. Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 
N.W.2d 759, 769 (Iowa 2016) (citing R.R. Donnelly & Sons v. Barnett, 670 N.W.2d 190, 
195, 197 (Iowa 2003)).  “In enacting the right-to-choose provision in section 85.27(4), 
our legislature sought to balance the interests of injured employees against the 
competing interests of their employers.”  Ramirez-Trujillo, 878 N.W.2d at 770-71 (citing 
Bell Bros., 779 N.W.2d at 202, 207; IBP, Inc. v. Harker, 633 N.W.2d 322, 326-27 (Iowa 
2001)).   
The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except where the 
employer has denied liability for the injury.  Iowa Code section 85.27; Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening, October 16, 1975).  An employer’s right to select 
the provider of medical treatment to an injured worker does not include the right to 
determine how an injured worker should be diagnosed, evaluated, treated, or other 
matters of professional medical judgment.  Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 
866389 (Declaratory Ruling, May 18, 1988).  Reasonable care includes care necessary 
to diagnose the condition, and defendants are not entitled to interfere with the medical 
judgment of its own treating physician.  Pote v. Mickow Corp., File No. 694639 (Review-
Reopening Decision, June 17, 1986).

The employer must furnish “reasonable medical services and supplies and 
reasonable and necessary appliances to treat an injured employee.”  Stone Container 
Corp. v. Castle, 657 N.W.2d 485, 490 (Iowa 2003)(emphasis in original).  Such 
employer-provided care “must be offered promptly and be reasonably suited to treat the 
injury without undue inconvenience to the employee.”  Iowa Code section 85.27(4).  
More specifically in Long, the court noted that “the employer’s obligation under the 
statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.”  Long v. Roberts 
Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122, 128 (Iowa 1995).   
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment - and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See e.g. 
Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e); Bell Bros. Heating and Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 
N.W.2d 193, 209 (Iowa 2010); Long, 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  More specifically in 
Long, the court noted that “the employer’s obligation under the statute turns on the 
question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.”  Long, 528 N.W.2d at 128.   
An injured employee dissatisfied with the employer-furnished care (or lack 
thereof) may share the employee’s discontent with the employer and if the parties 
cannot reach an agreement on alternate care, “the commissioner may, upon application 
and reasonable proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and order the care.”  Id.  
“Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.”  Long, 528 
N.W.2d at 123; Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 436.  As the party seeking 
relief in the form of alternate care, the employee bears the burden of proving that the 
authorized care is unreasonable.  Id. at 124; Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d at 209; Pirelli-
Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 436.  Because “the employer’s obligation under the 
statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability,” an injured 
employee’s dissatisfaction with employer-provided care, standing alone, is not enough 
to find such care unreasonable.  Id.  However, if substantial evidence exists that the 
care authorized by the employer was ineffective, it may be found that the care was not 
reasonable.  Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 437.   
 
The claimant requests that Mr. Podnar be able to select a physician of his own 
choosing to be deemed an authorized provider “for purposes of ongoing care, MMI, and 
rating.”  They argue that the defendant failed to abide by the consent order and failed to 
undertake reasonable efforts to secure an appointment for Mr. Podnar pursuant to Dr. 
Ott’s recommendations.  They also argue that imposition of sanctions against the 
defendant is appropriate considering the defendant’s failure to abide by the terms of the 
consent order.   
 
The defendant argues that they have undertaken reasonable efforts and “done all 
due diligence to obtain the recommended medical care and will continue to do so…” 
and that penalizing the defendant for their failure to abide by the terms of the consent 
order would violate the defendant’s due process rights.  The defendant further argues 
that they cannot control the schedules of orthopedic providers, and “welcomes 
[c]laimant to assist with this process to monitor progress or perhaps make suggestions 
of his own.”

A referral was made for medical care for the claimant on November 25, 2024.  
We are now over 90 days since that referral.  The defendant has undertaken some, but 
not all, reasonable efforts to arrange for care with a hand specialist for the claimant 
pursuant to Dr. Ott’s recommendations.  Reaching out to three providers in an effort to 
find the recommended care for Mr. Podnar is reasonable considering Dr. Ott 
recommended referral to a relatively unique specialist.  It certainly stands to reason that 
arranging for this care may take some time.  Nevertheless, I am concerned by the 
defendant’s lack of effort in only reaching out to one provider at a time, especially after 
the issuance of a consent order in which the defendant expressly agreed to arrange for 
an appointment within seven days of the date of the order.   
 
The defendant’s conduct, while concerning, does not rise to the level of 
abandoning care or revoking the defendant’s right to continue to control medical care.  
The defendant will be given one final opportunity to arrange for an appointment with a 
provider as recommended by Dr. Ott, and agreed to in the prior consent order with 
certain instructions.  It was apparent from the conduct of counsel during the hearing that 
there have been communication problems in this matter.  Therefore, I will lay out a 
timeline for the parties to act.   
First, within three days of the date of this order, claimant’s counsel and 
defendant’s counsel shall confer on potential providers.  The claimant shall provide the 
defendant with the names of three providers that the claimant finds acceptable.  
Second, within seven days of the date of this order, the defendant shall contact no 
fewer than three additional providers, including those provided by claimant, not 
previously contacted, of the specialty recommended by Dr. Ott in an attempt to arrange 
an appointment.  Third, within seven days of the date of this order, the parties shall file a 
certification that they conferred as to potential providers.  Fourth, the defendant shall file 
with the Agency a certification of the providers contacted which includes the dates and 
times on which they contacted the provider, and any response from the provider.  Fifth, 
the defendant shall arrange for an appointment as quickly as possible after receiving an 
acceptance.  Any appointment should be arranged within twenty days of the date of this 
order.  Should the defendant fail to abide by any of the aforementioned timelines, 
additional sanctions will be considered.   
Next, I turn to the issue of whether sanctions are appropriate in this matter.  876 
Iowa Administrative Code 4.36 provides:  
If any party to a contested case or an attorney representing such party shall 
fail to comply with these rules or any order of a deputy workers’ 
compensation commissioner … the deputy workers’ compensation 
commissioner … may impose sanctions which may include dismissing the 
action without prejudice, excluding or limiting evidence, assessing costs or 
expenses, and closing the record in whole or in part to further activity by the 
party.   
See 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.36.  The parties agreed to a consent order on 
February 4, 2025.  The undersigned issued said order pursuant to the agreement of the

parties.  This consent order functions as an order of the undersigned, a deputy workers’ 
compensation commissioner.  Failure to abide by this order is conduct subject to 
sanctions pursuant to 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.36.  I find that a sanction in this 
matter is appropriate due to the defendant’s failure to abide by the consent order.  
 
The appropriate sanction based upon the conduct of the defendant is to assess 
the costs of the claimant’s attorney’s fees and costs in filing the alternate care petition in 
this matter, including the claimant’s attorney’s reasonable preparation for and 
attendance at the alternate care hearing.  But for the defendant’s failure to arrange for 
care as recommended by Dr. Ott pursuant to the terms of the consent order, the 
claimant would not have needed to file the alternate medical care petition in the instant 
matter.   
 
IT IS THEREFORE ORDERED: 
1. The claimant’s petition for alternate care is granted with conditions as follows: 
 
a. Within three days of the date of this order, claimant’s counsel and 
defendant’s counsel shall confer on potential providers.  The claimant 
shall provide the defendant with the names of three providers that the 
claimant finds acceptable.   
b. Within seven days of the date of this order, the defendant shall contact 
no fewer than three additional providers, including those provided by 
claimant, not previously contacted, of the specialty recommended by 
Dr. Ott in an attempt to arrange an appointment.   
c. Within seven days of the date of this order, the parties shall file a 
certification that they conferred as to potential providers.   
d. The defendant shall file with the Agency a certification of the providers 
contacted which includes the dates and times on which they contacted 
the provider, and any response from the provider.   
e. The defendant shall arrange for an appointment as quickly as possible 
after receiving an acceptance.  Any appointment should be arranged 
within twenty days of the date of this order.   
  
2. The defendant shall reimburse claimant’s counsel for the costs of claimant’s 
counsel’s fees and costs in filing the alternate care petition in this matter, 
including claimant’s counsel’s reasonable preparation for and attendance at 
the alternate care hearing.
