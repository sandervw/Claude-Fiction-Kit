Claimant Melissa Scott seeks workers’ compensation benefits from the 
defendants, employer Vermeer Manufacturing Company (Vermeer) and insurance

C ON C LU SION S OF LAW. 
Workers’ compensation is “a creature of statute.” Darrow v. Quaker Oats Co., 
570 N.W.2d 649, 652 (Iowa 1997). This means an injured employee’s “right to workers' 
compensation is purely statutory.” Downs v. A & H Const., Ltd., 481 N.W.2d 520, 527 
(Iowa 1992).  And “it is the legislature’s prerogative to fix the conditions under which the 
act’s benefits may be obtained.” Darrow, 570 N.W.2d at 652. 
The legislature enacted the workers' compensation statute primarily for the 
benefit of the worker and the worker's dependents. Therefore, we apply 
the statute broadly and liberally in keeping with the humanitarian objective 
of the statute. We will not defeat the statute's beneficent purpose by 
reading something into it that is not there, or by a narrow and strained 
construction. 
Gregory v. Second Inj. Fund of Iowa, 777 N.W.2d 395, 399 (Iowa 2010) (quoting 
Holstein Elec. v. Breyfogle, 756 N.W.2d 812, 815–16 (Iowa 2008) (citations omitted)).  
“Although the workers' compensation statute is to be liberally construed in favor 
of the worker, the statute is not to be expanded by reading something into it that is not 
there.” Downs v. A & H Const., Ltd., 481 N.W.2d 520, 527 (Iowa 1992) (citing Cedar 
Rapids Community School Dist. v. Cady, 278 N.W.2d 298 (Iowa 1979)). “To determine 
legislative intent, we look to the language chosen by the legislature and not what the 
legislature might have said.” Ramirez-Trujillo v. Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 N.W.2d 759,

(Iowa 2016) (citing Schadendorf v. Snap-On Tools Corp., 757 N.W.2d 330, 337 (Iowa 
2008)). The “broad purpose of workers’ compensation” is “to award compensation 
(apart from medical benefits), not for the injury itself, but the disability produced by a 
physical injury.” Bell Bros. Heating & Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d 193, (Iowa 
2010) (citing 4 Arthur Larson & Lex K. Larson, Larson’s Workers’ Compensation Law 
§ 80.02, at 80–2 (2009)).  
In 2017, the Iowa legislature amended the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Act. 
See 2017 Iowa Acts, ch. 23. The 2017 amendments took effect on July 1, 2017. Id. at § 
24(1); see also Iowa Code § 3.7(1). Because the injury at issue in this case occurred 
after July 1, 2017, the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Act, as amended in 2017, applies. 
See Stiles v. Annett Holdings, Inc., Iowa Workers’ Comp. Comm’r No. 5064673, 2020 
WL 6037539 *2 (App., Oct. 2, 2020). The stipulated injury date means the 2022 edition 
of the Iowa Code applies in this case.

 “The basis of compensation shall be the weekly earnings of the injured 
employee at the time of the injury.” Iowa Code § 85.36. For purposes of the statute, 
“Weekly earnings means gross salary, wages, or earnings of an employee to which 
such employee would have been entitled had the employee worked the customary 
hours for the full pay period in which the employee was injured, as regularly required by 
the employee’s employer for the work or employ for which the employee was employed, 
computed or determined” in accordance with the subsections of the statute, based on 
how the employee was paid. Id.  
The parties disagree on how to calculate Scott’s weekly earnings at the time of 
injury. The defendants contend Scott’s earnings must be calculated under section 
85.36(9). Scott argues section 85.36(6) governs. 
Iowa Code section 85.36(9) provides: 
If an employee earns either no wages or less than the usual weekly 
earnings of the regular full-time adult laborer in the line of industry in which 
the employee is injured in that locality, the weekly earnings shall be one-
fiftieth of the total earnings which the employee has earned from all 
employment during the twelve calendar months immediately preceding the 
injury. 
And under section 85.36(6), 
In the case of an employee who is paid on a daily or hourly basis, or by 
the output of the employee, the weekly earnings shall be computed by 
dividing by thirteen the earnings, including shift differential pay but not 
including overtime or premium pay, of the employee earned in the employ 
of the employer in the last completed period of thirteen consecutive 
calendar weeks immediately preceding the injury. If the employee was

Page 10 
 
absent from employment for reasons personal to the employee during part 
of the thirteen calendar weeks preceding the injury, the employee’s weekly 
earnings shall be the amount the employee would have earned had the 
employee worked when work was available to other employees of the 
employer in a similar occupation. A week which does not fairly reflect the 
employee’s customary earnings shall be replaced by the closest previous 
week with earnings that fairly represent the employee’s customary 
earnings. 
The Iowa Supreme Court considered what triggers application of section 85.36(9) 
in Swiss Colony, Inc. v. Deutmeyer, 789 N.W.2d 129 (Iowa 2010). At the agency level in 
that case, “the commissioner candidly acknowledged that ‘[n]either party offered 
evidence as to whether or not [the claimant]’s earnings or hours at [the employer] were 
lower or higher than a regular full-time laborer in the line of industry in which [the 
claimant] was injured and in that locality.’” Id. at 134. Despite the lack of evidence in the 
record, the Commissioner concluded the claimant was a part-time employee because 
he worked less than 40 hours per week. Id. at 134–35. The court found that “[s]uch a 
conclusion is not consistent with the language of the statute or our prior precedent” 
because “[t]he language in section 85.36(9) distinguishes full- and part-time employees 
on the basis of weekly earnings, not the number of hours worked per week.” Id. 
The court went on the hold that “the principle of liberal construction does not vest 
this court with an editor’s pen with the power to add or detract from the legislature’s 
handiwork. Had the legislature intended to establish the forty-hour week as standard for 
full-time employment it could have done so.” Id. (citing Hornby v. State, 559 N.W.2d 23, 
25 (Iowa 1997)). The court determined that by its language choice in the statutory text, 
“the legislature necessarily recognized that the forty-hour week is not the standard for 
every industry within the state by making ‘earnings’ the operative factor. As a result, 
section 85.36(9) is applicable only where a claimant earns less than the usual weekly 
earnings of a full-time adult laborer in his or her ‘line of industry.’” Id.  
The court then found that the Commissioner correctly observed that there was no 
evidence in the record of the usual weekly earnings of laborers in the claimant’s field. Id. 
Therefore, the court ruled that the Commissioner’s conclusion that the claimant was a 
part-time employee was not supported by substantial evidence. Id. The court reversed 
the Commissioner’s decision and found that there was no reason to remand the case 
for the taking of additional evidence because its opinion restated existing law that was 
almost 20 years old. Id. at 136. 
This case is similar to Deutmeyer. As in that case, there is an insufficient basis in 
the evidence from which to reach a finding with respect to what the usual weekly 
earnings of the regular full-time adult laborer in the line of industry in which the claimant 
was injured in that locality. Such a finding is a mandatory “preliminary factual finding” to 
trigger application of section 85.39(9). Id. (quoting King v. City of Mt. Pleasant, 474 
N.W.2d 564, 566 (Iowa 1991)). Because the evidence in the record is insufficient to 
support such a factual finding, section 85.39(9) does not govern in this case. See id. at 
134–36.

Section 85.36(6) governs “[i]n the case of an employee who is paid on a . . . 
hourly basis.” The evidence shows and the parties agree that Vermeer paid Scott on an 
hourly basis. Further supporting the application of subsection (6) here is that doing so 
allows for the achievement of the "‘ultimate objective’” of workers’ compensation, which 
is “‘reflecting fairly the claimant’s probable future earning loss.’” Hanigan v. Hedstrom 
Concrete Products, Inc., 524 N.W.2d 158, 160 (Iowa 1994) (quoting 2 Arthur Larson, 
Workmen’s Compensation Law § 60.11, at 10–622 (1994)). 
For purposes of the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Act, “‘Gross earnings’ means 
recurring payments by the employer to the employee for employment, before any 
authorized or lawfully required deduction or withholding of funds by the employer,

The analysis of this issue begins with the statutory text. One change the 2017

Work offered at the employer’s principal place of business or established

“Because penalty benefits are a creature of statute, our discussion begins with 
an examination of the statutory parameters for such benefits.” Keystone Nursing Care 
Ctr. v. Craddock, 705 N.W.2d 299, 307 (Iowa 2005). Under Iowa Code section 
86.13(4)(a),2 
If a denial, a delay in payment, or a termination of benefits occurs without 
reasonable or probable cause or excuse known to the employer or 
insurance carrier at the time of the denial, delay in payment, or termination 
of benefits, the workers' compensation commissioner shall award benefits 
in addition to those benefits payable under this chapter, or chapter 85, 
85A, or 85B, up to fifty percent of the amount of benefits that were denied, 
delayed, or terminated without reasonable or probable cause or excuse. 
This provision “codifies, in the workers’ compensation insurance context, the 
common law rule that insurers with good faith disputes over the legal or factual validity 
of claims can challenge them, if their arguments for doing so present fairly debatable 
issues.” Covia v. Robinson, 507 N.W.2d 411, 412 (Iowa 1993) (citing Dirks v. Farm 
Bureau Mut. Ins. Co., 465 N.W.2d 857, 861 (Iowa 1991) and Dolan v. Aid Ins. Co., 431 
N.W.2d 790, 794 (Iowa 1988)). “The purpose or goal of the statute is both punishment 
and deterrence.” Robbennolt v. Snap-On Tools Corp., 555 N.W.2d 229, 237 (Iowa 
1996). 
The statute codifies a burden-shifting framework for determining whether penalty 
benefits must be awarded in a workers’ compensation case. See 2009 Iowa Acts ch. 
179, § 110 (codified at Iowa Code § 86.13(4)(b)); see also Pettengill v. Am. Blue Ribbon 
Holdings, LLC, 875 N.W.2d 740, 746–47 (Iowa App. 2015) as amended (Feb. 16, 2016) 
(discussing the burden-shifting required by the two-factor statutory test). The employee 
bears the burden to establish a prima facie case for penalty benefits. See Iowa Code 
§ 86.13(4)(b). To do so, the employee must demonstrate a denial, delay in payment, or 
termination of workers’ compensation benefits. Id. at § 86.13(4)(b)(1). If the employee 
fails to prove a denial, delay, or termination, there can be no award of penalty benefits 
and the analysis stops. See id. at § 86.13(4)(b); see also Pettengill, 875 N.W.2d at 747. 
However, if the employee makes the requisite showing, the burden of proof shifts to the 
employer. See id. at § 86.13(4)(b); see also Pettengill, 875 N.W.2d at 747. 
Because the evidence shows that no health care provider prescribed work 
restrictions from November 1, 2022, through September 12, 2023, Scott is not entitled 
to benefits and there can be no penalty. However, Scott has met her burden of proof

the time of the ... termination.” Id. § 86.13(4)(c)(3)

On rate, the parties fleshed out their dispute in post-hearing briefs. However, 
there is no indication that the defendants conducted an investigation regarding Scott’s 
rate that looked into “the usual weekly earnings of the regular full-time adult laborer in 
the line of industry in which the employee is injured in that locality,” as section 85.36(9) 
requires. The Iowa Supreme Court observed in Deutmeyer: 
This court's decision in King, 474 N.W.2d at 565, was announced almost 
twenty years prior to the evidentiary hearing in this case. King established 
the requirement for a preliminary factual finding of lower earnings prior to 
the application of section 85.36(9). We do not regard our opinion in this 
case as establishing new law, but simply applying existing law. 
789 N.W.2d at 136.  
The court issued Deutmeyer in 2010, almost 14 years ago, which means that the 
rule on which this case was decided—that the finding necessary to trigger application of 
section 86.35(9) is the amount of earnings, not the hours worked—has been the law in 
Iowa for over 30 years. See id. at 134–36; see also Def. Post-Hearing Brief, pp. 4–5 
(noting “what distinguishes full from part-time workers is weekly earnings, NOT the 
number of hours worked per week”) (citing id.). Simply put, even assuming arguendo 
the defendants conducted an investigation into Scott’s rate, there is no indication it 
focused on the information required to pay benefits under section 86.35(9) in 
accordance with Iowa Supreme Court precedent that is over 30 years old. A penalty is 
therefore appropriate on the rate of compensation paid before hearing. 
Moving to the refusal to pay benefits in October of 2022, there is no indication in 
the record showing that refusal was preceded by an investigation or that the defendants 
contemporaneously conveyed the basis for the refusal to Scott. The undersigned will 
give the defendants the benefit of the doubt by assuming that the reasons in the 
defendants’ post-hearing brief as to why Scott is not entitled to TTD benefits during the 
time in question are why it refused to pay such benefits even though the brief contains 
no argument to that effect on the question of penalty.  
The first reason the defendants assert is that Scott refused an offer of suitable 
work. However, as found above, there is no indication in the record that Vermeer made 
a written communication of suitable work—in fact, the evidence shows it is more likely 
than not no such written offer was made. Had Vermeer made a written offer, it would be 
in evidence. Instead, the defendants chose not to produce emails between Scott and 
Bauer regarding her dwindling PTO and need to report to work, which generates the 
inference that such written communication does not support the defendants’ arguments 
in this case. Based on the evidence in this case, it is not fairly debatable that Vermeer

made a written offer of suitable work to Scott that she refused.  And even if Vermeer 
made such an offer, it would have been after the missed time in October due to a 
doctor’s note.  
With respect to the TTD benefits to which Scott is entitled for the time she missed 
at Vermeer in October of 2022 due to the injury, Vermeer has not shown there is a fairly 
debatable reason for its failure to pay before the date of hearing. Scott had a doctor’s 
note taking her off work. She sent the note to Vermeer. At a minimum, the defendants 
should have investigated after receiving Scott’s petition initiating this contested case 
proceeding and communicated the basis for their denial after doing so. There is scant 
evidence in the record to support a finding that it did either of these things. 
Lastly, there is the lack of communication regarding the termination of benefits 
that occurred three or four weeks before the date of hearing. The evidentiary record 
contains one letter from the defendants regarding termination of benefits—often called 
an Auxier letter. See Auxier v. Woodward State Hosp.-Sch., 266 N.W.2d 139, 142–43 
(Iowa 1987) (holding that the due process clauses of the state and federal constitutions 
require written notice of the termination of workers’ compensation benefits). Thus, there 
is an insufficient basis from which to conclude the defendants timely issued a letter to 
Scott regarding the termination of compensation. The defendants failed to meet their 
burden on the penalty issue for any of the three bases which Scott identified.  
The following factors are used in determining the amount of penalty benefits: 
 The length of the delay; 
 The number of the delays; 
 The information available to the employer regarding the employee's injuries 
and wages; and  
 The prior penalties imposed against the employer under section 86.13. 
Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 238. 
The communication between the parties in this case has been less than ideal, 
from Scott seeking care to the termination of her TTD benefits. Scott filed the petition 
that initiated this case on December 5, 2022. In the petition, Scott identifies, among 
other disputed issues, weekly compensation rate, entitlement to healing period and TTD 
benefits, and penalty. Further, Scott identified the time during which she was disabled 
as September 29, 2022, to present. 
The defendants answered on January 13, 2023. With respect to the asserted 
time disabled, the answer states “they deny for lack of knowledge.” With respect to the 
disputed issues identified by Scott in Paragraph 10 of her petition, the defendants stated 
“they admit paragraph 10.”

The undersigned finds that the date on which an investigation should have been 
triggered was January 13, 2023, the date on which the defendants filed their answer 
with the agency. The evidence shows that as the latest date on which the defendants

There is an insufficient basis in the record from which to conclude the defendants

M e d i c a l  B e n e f i t s .  
Scott seeks a decision ordering the defendants to pay the medical expenses 
detailed in Exhibit 2. The hearing report does not reflect why the defendants dispute 
their responsibility to pay such medical expenses. Neither Scott nor the defendants 
addressed the issue in post-hearing briefing. 
For all injuries compensable under Iowa Code chapter 85 or 85A, the employer 
must “furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, chiropractic, podiatric, 
physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance, and hospital services and supplies therefor 
and shall allow reasonably necessary transportation expenses incurred for such 
services.” Iowa Code § 85.27(1). This responsibility is coupled with the right to choose 
care for the employee’s work injury. Id. at § 85.27(4). “[A]n employer who authorizes 
care is responsible for the cost of care up to the time when the employer notifies the 
employee it is no longer authorizing care.” Ramirez-Trujillo v. Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 
N.W.2d 759, 771 (Iowa 2016). 
The statute obligates the employer to monitor the care and determine if, and 
when, it no longer wishes to authorize care. Id. at 771. “An employer can easily 
reconsider whether it wishes to authorize further care when an authorized medical 
provider indicates an employee requires no further care for a workplace injury or when 
the employer authorizes a new provider to take over an employee’s care.” Id. at 772. 
“Section 85.27(4) balances this minimal burden with a significant corresponding 

SCOTT V. VERMEER MANUFACTURING COMPANY 
Page 25 
 
benefit—a means of extinguishing the employer's ongoing obligation to pay for medical 
expenses following its acknowledgment of compensability and exercise of the right to 
choose care.” Id. 
“Nothing in the statute prevents an employee from obtaining unauthorized care” 
and “reimbursement for expenses incurred in seeking unauthorized care upon an 
adjudication of compensability.” Id. at 773 (citing Bell Bros. Heating & Air Condition v. 
Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d 193, 205–06 (Iowa 2010) and R.R. Donnelly & Sons v. Barnett, 670 
N.W.2d 190, 197 (Iowa 2003)). The Commissioner has the authority to order the 
employer to pay for unauthorized care. Id. (citing W. Side Transp. v. Cordell, 601 
N.W.2d 691, 693 (Iowa 1999)). To recover expenses incurred in seeking unauthorized 
care, the employee must prove, by a preponderance of the evidence that the care was:  
1) Reasonable; and 
2) Beneficial. Id. (citing Bell Bros., 779 N.W.2d at 206).  
The determination of whether unauthorized care was reasonable and beneficial 
is based on the totality of the circumstances. Id. “[T]he reasonableness of unauthorized 
treatment can normally only be fully evaluated in light of the effectiveness of the 
treatment.” Bell Bros., 779 N.W.2d at 206 (citing Linn Care Ctr. v. Cannon, 704 P.2d 
539, 540 (1985)). “[U]nauthorized medical care is beneficial if it provides a more 
favorable medical outcome than would likely have been achieved by the care authorized 
by the employer.” Id. 
Whether the defendants are responsible under the law to pay for unauthorized 
care depends on the totality of the circumstances. Such a conclusion depends on 
finding the care was beneficial, which is based on the medical outcome. Because Scott 
had not reached MMI at the time of hearing, this question is not ripe for adjudication at 
present. Therefore, the issue of whether the defendants must pay the medical expenses 
detailed in Exhibit 2 must be bifurcated, along with the other disputed issues relating to 
permanency, to be decided after Scott has reached MMI.
