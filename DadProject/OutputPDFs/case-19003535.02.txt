Brunk brings this petition for judicial review of a decision rendered by the Worker’s 
Compensation Commission pursuant to Iowa Code Chapters 17A and 86. Brunk appears through 
Jacob Peters. Glenwood Resource Center and the State of Iowa appear through Jonathan 
Bergman. The Court, having reviewed the underlying record and considering applicable law, 
makes the following findings.  
Findings of Fact 
 
The following facts are supported by substantial evidence in the agency record. 
Glenwood Resource Center (Glenwood) is a facility run by the Iowa Department of Human 
Services. Louis Ray Brunk, the Claimant herein, has been employed by Glenwood since 2003. In 
2013, Brunk suffered a workplace injury to his right shoulder. He still has some issues with his 
right shoulder. In the agreement for settlement, Brunk and Glenwood stipulated that Brunk 
sustained an industrial disability of 22.5%. (JE10, p. 102). Brunk received workers compensation 
for this injury. 
At issue in the present matter is an injury incurred by Brunk on July 22, 2019. On said 
day, Brunk was on his paid lunch break with other employees. A co-employee complained of

Brunk attempted to pop his co-worker’s back, Brunk’s back “popped”, and he fell to the floor in

Procedural Posture 
This matter was initially arbitrated before Deputy Workers’ Compensation Commissioner 
Ben Humphrey on March 23, 2022. An arbitration decision was rendered on September 28, 
2022. Therein, the deputy commissioner made several findings. The arbiter first found that 
Brunk’s injury was not the result of horseplay, and the injury was incurred in the course of his 
employment. Secondly, he found that Brunk’s injury is an unscheduled injury. Brunk sustained 
an eight percent functional impairment to the whole body. Having incurred an eight percent 
impairment, the deputy commissioner found Brunk was entitled to forty weeks of permanent 
partial disability. Importantly, the deputy commissioner found that the State was not entitled to 
apportionment for Brunk’s injury based on the 2013 shoulder injury. The arbiter reasoned that 
because the 2013 shoulder injury is an industrial injury whilst the 2019 back injury is a 
functional impairment, “[T]here is no basis for giving the State a credit for PPD benefits paid to 
compensate Brunk for the industrial disability he has due to a prior shoulder injury in order to

Standard of Review 
Judicial review of workers' compensation cases is governed by Iowa Code chapter 17A. 
“The district court acts in an appellate capacity in judicial-review proceedings.” Jackson v. 
Bridgestone Americas Tire Operations, LLC, 973 N.W.2d 882, 886 (Iowa Ct. App. 2021). The 
interpretation of workers' compensation statutes and case law has not been clearly vested by a 
provision of law in the discretion of the agency. Larson Mfg. Co., Inc. v. Thorson, 763 N.W.2d

Conclusions of Law 
A permanent partial disability exists when it is medically indicated that maximum 
medical improvement (MMI) has been reached and the extent of the injury can be determined 
using the guides published by the American Medical Association. IOWA CODE §85.34(2). 
Scheduled disabilities are those enumerated in §85.34(2)(a-u) and they are compensated in 
accordance with the statute. Unscheduled body parts are compensated pursuant to the 
formulation provided in §85.34(2)(v) for up to five hundred weeks; the back is an unscheduled 
disability. At the time of Brunk’s 2013 shoulder injury, the shoulder was an unscheduled 
member of the body. See IOWA CODE §85.34 (2013). When a work injury results in permanent 
disability to an unscheduled body part, the disability is considered to be to the body as a whole.  
“If an employee who is eligible for compensation” for a permanent partial disability 
returns to work and “receive[s] the same or greater salary, wages, or earnings than the employee 
received at the time of the injury, the employee shall be compensated based only upon the 
employee's functional impairment resulting from the injury, and not in relation to the employee's 
earning capacity.” IOWA CODE §85.34(2)(v). When calculating compensation for a permanent 
partial disability, the workers’ compensation commissioner must prevent double recoveries for 
successive disabilities, disabilities suffered in the course of other employment, and disabilities

the employee has already been compensated for. To effectuate this, the legislature provides in 
pertinent part that:  
An employer is liable for compensating only that portion of an 
employee's disability that arises out of and in the course of the 
employee's employment with the employer and that relates to the 
injury that serves as the basis for the employee's claim for 
compensation… An employer is not liable for compensating an 
employee's preexisting disability that arose out of and in the course 
of employment from a prior injury with the employer, to the extent 
that the employee's preexisting disability has already been 
compensated. 
IOWA CODE §85.34(7).  
The legislative intent of the latter half of this statute is to prevent double recoveries and 
double reductions. Warren Properties v. Stewart, 864 N.W.2d 307, 315 (Iowa 2015), as 
corrected (July 1, 2015). “When a successive injury increases a preexisting permanent disability 
to the body as a whole, the benefits provided for the successive injury must not include a double 
recovery for the first disability or a double reduction for the first disability.” Id. at 315-316.  
When apportioning an award for a successive disability, the statute makes no distinction 
between the type of disability the employee incurred, be it scheduled or unscheduled. P.M. 
Lattner Mfg. Co. v. Rife, 2023 WL 3862594, page 4 (Iowa Ct. App. 2023). Further, just because 
the statute does not provide an express means or method of apportionment does not mean that 
benefits cannot be apportioned. See Id.  In scenarios where there is a successive disability with 
the same employer, the preexisting disability must be credited so that only the successive 
disability is determined based on (1) the earning capacity possessed when the successive injury 
occurred, and (2) the reduction in earning capacity, or disability, caused by the successive injury. 
See Warren, 864 N.W.2d at 318; IOWA CODE §85.34(2)(u). “This formula requires the

Analysis 
Because the workers’ compensation commission has not been vested with the authority to 
interpret law, the Court need not give deference to the commission’s conclusion of law. Larson, 
763 N.W.2d at 850. This Court does defer to the commissioner’s conclusions of facts which are 
supported by substantial evidence. Id. Here, the underlying material facts are not in dispute: 
Brunk was injured in 2013 and received permanent partial disability benefits from Glenwood, 
and Brunk was injured again in 2019 and is petitioning for additional permanent partial disability 
benefits from Glenwood. Glenwood asserts that because Brunk was previously compensated for 
a whole-body impairment, he cannot be compensated again for another whole-body impairment 
that did not increase his whole-body impairment rating.  
 “An employer is not liable for compensating an employee's preexisting disability that 
arose out of and in the course of employment from a prior injury with the employer, to the extent 
that the employee's preexisting disability has already been compensated.” IOWA CODE §85.34(7). 
When a work injury results in permanent disability to an unscheduled body part, the disability is 
considered to be to the body as a whole. Brunk’s medical history now includes two whole-body 
impairments. For Brunk to be compensated for the 2019 injury, it must have increased his 
disability rating to his body as a whole. “When a successive injury increases a preexisting 
permanent disability to the body as a whole, the benefits provided for the successive injury must

not include a double recovery for the first disability or a double reduction for the first disability.” 
Warren, 864 N.W.2d at 315.  
In 2013, Brunk injured his shoulder while employed at Glenwood. Brunk and Glenwood 
stipulated a 22.5% industrial loss and Brunk received 112.5 – 22.5% of 500 – weeks of 
permanent partial disability benefits. The shoulder was an unscheduled body part. Brunk 
returned to work at Glenwood and made the same or greater salary after this injury. In 2019, 
Brunk suffered an 8% functional loss stemming from a back injury while employed at 
Glenwood. The back is also an unscheduled body part. Brunk has since returned to work and is 
earning the same or greater salary. Because Brunk has suffered two whole-body impairments, 
and the successive injury did not increase his whole-body injury rating, Brunk is not entitled to 
additional disability benefits.  
Conclusion  
For the reasons stated herein, the decision of the workers’ compensation commissioner is 
AFFIRMED.
