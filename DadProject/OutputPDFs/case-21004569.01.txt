STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
The claimant, Tovarce Washington, filed a petition for arbitration and seeks 
workers’ compensation benefits from Linn County, Iowa, a self-insured employer.  The 
claimant was represented by Jason Neifert.  The defendant was represented by Cory 
Speth. 
The matter came on for hearing on August 8, 2023, before deputy workers’ 
compensation commissioner Joe Walsh originating in Des Moines, Iowa via Zoom 
videoconference.  Counsel did an excellent job of narrowing the hearing issues as well 
as the evidence in the case.  The record in the case consists of Joint Exhibits 1 through 
20.  The claimant testified at hearing, in addition to Steve Estenson, Linn County Risk 
Manager.  Marla Happel served as the court reporter.  The matter was fully submitted 
on September 29, 2023. 
ISSUES 
The parties submitted the following issues for determination: 
1. Whether the injury is a cause of any permanent disability, and if so, the nature 
and extent of such disability. 
STIPULATIONS 
Through the hearing report, the parties stipulated to the following: 
1.  The parties had an employer-employee relationship. 
2. Claimant sustained an injury which arose out of and in the course of 
employment on May 29, 2020.  This injury resulted in some temporary 
disability.  Temporary disability benefits are not in dispute.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
Claimant, Tovarce Washington, was 35 years old as of the date of hearing.  
(Transcript, page 10)  He testified live and under oath at hearing.  I find his hearing 
testimony to be generally credible.  He was not a stellar historian; however, his 
testimony generally was consistent with other portions of the record.  There was nothing 
about his demeanor which caused the undersigned any concern for his truthfulness. 
Mr. Washington now resides in Cedar Rapids, Iowa.  He has an associate 
degree in psychology from Liberty University in Lynchburg, Virginia.  Since obtaining his 
degree, he has worked primarily in youth counseling and corrections.  All of his work 
history has included “hands on” requirements, meaning he has needed to be able to

his lower back on the right side.  (Tr., pp. 21-22)  He was seen at St. Luke’s Hospital

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The primary question submitted is whether Mr. Washington’s stipulated work 
injury is a cause of any permanency. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based. A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause. A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible. George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability. 
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is

also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); 
Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995). Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted expert medical 
testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 
N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 1994). 
It has long been the law of Iowa that Iowa employers take an employee subject 
to any active or dormant health problems and must exercise care to avoid injury to both 
the weak and infirm and the strong and healthy. Hanson v. Dickinson, 188 Iowa 728, 
176 N.W. 823 (1920). A material aggravation, worsening, lighting up or acceleration of 
any prior condition has been a viewed as a compensable event ever since initial 
enactment of our workers’ compensation statutes. Ziegler v. United States Gypsum Co., 
252 Iowa 613; 106 N.W.2d 591 (1961). While a claimant must show that the injury 
proximately caused the medical condition sought to be compensable, it is well 
established in Iowa that a cause is “proximate” when it is a substantial factor in bringing 
about that condition. It need not be the only causative factor, or even the primary or the 
most substantial cause to be compensable under the Iowa workers’ compensation 
system. Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994); Blacksmith v. All-
American, Inc., 290 N.W.2d 348 (Iowa 1980). 
There are two expert medical opinions in the record, Dr. Broghammer and Dr. 
Segal.  I find the opinion of Dr. Segal to be more convincing and more compelling than 
the opinion of Dr. Broghammer.  I find it to be more consistent with the underlying facts 
of the case.  Specifically, the weight of the evidence supports a finding that while Mr. 
Washington did have prior incidents of injury to his low back, he was not having ongoing 
low back symptoms or disability at the time of his work injury in May 2020.  In fact, there 
is no credible medical evidence in the record pointing to the earlier injuries as being 
even a partial cause of his permanent disability.  Moreover, Dr. Broghammer’s own 
opinion seems to acknowledge that Mr. Washinton’s ongoing symptoms are at least 
partially related to his work injury. 
Instead of focusing on the medical opinions, the County suggests that Mr. 
Washington was dishonest.  While I agree with the County, and Dr. Broghammer, that 
Mr. Washington was not a particularly good historian, I do not see any evidence that he 
was dishonest about anything at all related to his work injury.  On the contrary, while he 
did not recall specific dates, his testimony is found to be credible and honest. 
Having found that Mr. Washington sustained a permanent disability, I must now 
assess the nature and extent of this disability.  I find that the disability should be 
evaluated under Iowa Code Section 85.34(2)(v).  I further find that he was terminated, 
and his disability must be evaluated for loss of earning capacity.

Since claimant has an impairment to the body as a whole, an industrial disability 
has been sustained. Industrial disability was defined in Diederich v. Tri-City Ry. Co. of 
Iowa, 219 Iowa 587, 258 N.W. 899 (1935) as follows: "It is therefore plain that the 
Legislature intended the term 'disability' to mean 'industrial disability' or loss of earning 
capacity and not a mere 'functional disability' to be computed in terms of percentages of 
the total physical and mental ability of a normal man." 
 
Functional impairment is an element to be considered in determining industrial 
disability which is the reduction of earning capacity, but consideration must also be 
given to the injured employee's age, education, qualifications, experience, motivation, 
loss of earnings, severity and situs of the injury, work restrictions, inability to engage in 
employment for which the employee is fitted and the employer's offer of work or failure 
to so offer. McSpadden v. Big Ben Coal Co., 288 N.W.2d 181 (Iowa 1980); Olson v. 
Goodyear Service Stores, 255 Iowa 1112, 125 N.W.2d 251 (1963); Barton v. Nevada 
Poultry Co., 253 Iowa 285, 110 N.W.2d 660 (1961). 
 
Compensation for permanent partial disability shall begin at the termination of the 
healing period. Compensation shall be paid in relation to 500 weeks as the disability 
bears to the body as a whole. Section 85.34. 
The refusal of defendant-employer to return claimant to work in any capacity is, 
by itself, significant evidence of a lack of employability.  Pierson v. O’Bryan Brothers, 
File No. 951206  (App. January 20, 1995).  Meeks v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co., File 
No. 876894, (App. January 22, 1993); See also, 10-84 Larson’s Workers’ 
Compensation Law, section 84.01; Sunbeam Corp. v. Bates, 271 Ark. 609 S.W.2d 102 
(1980); Army & Air Force Exchange Service v. Neuman, 278 F. Supp. 865 (W.D. La. 
1967); Leonardo v. Uncas Manufacturing Co., 77 R.I. 245, 75 A.2d 188 (1950).  An 
employer who chooses to preclude an injured worker’s re-entry into its workforce likely 
demonstrates by its own action that the worker has incurred a substantial loss of 
earning capacity.  As has previously been explained in numerous decisions of this 
agency, if the employer in whose employ the disability occurred is unwilling to 
accommodate the disability, there is no reason to expect some other employer to have 
more incentive to do so.  Estes v. Exide Technologies, File No. 5013809 (App. 
December 12, 2006). 
Having reviewed all of the factors of industrial disability, I find that Mr. 
Washington has sustained a 35 percent loss of earning capacity.  I find that Mr. 
Washington is young, employable, and bright; however, his injury has resulted in his 
inability to go “hands on” and use restraints with youth or others which has been a key 
component of all of his past relevant employment.  The employer terminated and did not 
offer any work.  The work he is attempting to do now does not appear viable based 
upon his description of performing long distance deliveries in his condition.  I suspect 
that he will have to attempt to secure employment in a different sector using some of his 
college level skills counseling without the hands-on component.  While he has fairly 
significant impairment and medical restrictions as outlined by Dr. Segal, I find it likely 
that he will be able to use his education to his advantage.  Thus, it is somewhat difficult
