Regarding the statutory requirement referenced by petitioners the court finds 
that the following passage governs the court’s review of the entire record.  
This court has long held that the commissioner must “state the 
evidence relied upon and [ ] detail reasons for his conclusions.” 
Catalfo v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co., 213 N.W.2d 506, 510 (Iowa 
1973); accord Terwilliger v. Snap–On Tools Corp., 529 N.W.2d 267, 
274 (Iowa 1995); Armstrong v. State of Iowa Bldgs. & Grounds, 382 
N.W.2d 161, 166 (Iowa 1986). Moreover, the commissioner's decision 
must be “sufficiently detailed to show the path he has taken through 
conflicting evidence.” Catalfo, 213 N.W.2d at 510. We have 
refrained, however, from reading “unnecessary and burdensome” 
requirements into the statute. Terwilliger, 529 N.W.2d at 274. Thus 
we have held the commissioner need not discuss every evidentiary 
fact and the basis for its acceptance or rejection so long as the 
commissioner's analytical process can be followed on appeal. Id. So 
also have we held the commissioner's duty to furnish a reasoned 
opinion satisfied if “it is possible to work backward ... and to deduce 
what must have been [the agency's] legal conclusions and [its] 
findings of fact.” Norland, 412 N.W.2d at 909 (quoting Ward v. Iowa 
Dep't of Transp., 304 N.W.2d 236, 239 (Iowa 1981)).6 
 
In conjunction with its review of the entire record the court will conduct its review 
consistent with these standards. The court does not find the as a matter of law that 
the commissioner’s failure to address the proposed findings of fact in the motion to 
reconsider requires this court grant the petitioners’ petition for judicial review. 
                                                 
“However, a remand for agency fact-finding is unnecessary when the facts are 
established as a matter of law. The reviewing court can determine the facts as a 
matter of law when the relevant evidence is both uncontradicted and reasonable 
minds could not draw different inferences from the evidence.”).

II. MEYER DID NOT SUSTAIN AN INJURY ARISING OUT OF 
AND IN THE COURSE OF HIS EMPLOYMENT WITH 
QUALITY MANUFACTURING.  
 
Petitioners assert that the commission erred when it found Meyer sustained 
an injury which arose out of and in the course of his employment with Quality. In 
reviewing the entire record, the court finds that there is substantial evidence to 
support the commissioner’s decision that Meyer suffered a work-related injury.  
The arbitration decision meticulously summarizes the evidence presented, 
both the evidence that supported the deputy’s decision and the evidence that 
detracted from it. The deputy found Meyer’s testimony credible and consistent 
throughout his reports of injury to his treating physicians and at trial. The deputy 
set forth the medical evidence presented by the physicians who treated and 
examined Meyer. In his review of this evidence, he determined that the medical 
opinions of Drs. Bansal and Logan were more convincing than Dr. Hawk’s.  
The commissioner on appeal noted that he performed a de novo review of 
the evidentiary record and the detailed arguments of the parties. He concluded 
upon his review that the deputy’s decision should be affirmed. He noted that the 
deputy’s decision was well-reasoned. He noted that the deputy found Meyer to be a 
credible witness as did the commissioner. He further stated he could find nothing 
in the record that would cause him to reverse the deputy’s decision.

Bansal and Logan’s medical opinions on this issue more convincing than the

 Petitioners assert the commission erred by finding Meyer sustained a 
permanent injury. Petitioners assert that the evidence does not support the 
commissioner’s decision that Meyer suffered a permanent work-related injury.  
The evidence in the record that supports Meyer suffered a permanent injury 
comes specifically from Dr. Bansal. He opined that

tube without a hoist, further aggravating his cervical spondylosis and 
resulting in a permanently aggravated cervical discogenic condition.8 
 
Petitioners assert that this history is fictitious since Meyer was not working on the 
Raptor roller on February 2, 2021. Consequently, petitioners assert Dr. Bansal’s 
opinion cannot establish permanency.

Meyer’s disability is industrial and assessed under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v).
