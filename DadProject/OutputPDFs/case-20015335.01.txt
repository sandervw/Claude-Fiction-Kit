Arbitration on January 28, 2021, seeking workers’ compensation benefits from Marcus 
Lumber Company (Employer) and Redwood Fire and Casualty Company (Insurance 
Carrier).  In her Petition, Donna alleged that Steve had contracted Covid-19 while working 
for Marcus Lumber which ultimately caused his death.  Donna asserted that Steve had 
contracted Covid-19 out of and in the course of his employment with Marcus Lumber and 
sought recovery of medical expenses, death benefits and burial costs as a result.  Marcus

STATEMENT OF THE FACTS 
Donna Bolton is the surviving spouse of Steve Bolton who died in December 2020, 
due to complications from being infected with the coronavirus (COVID).  At the time of 
Steve’s death he was employed by Marcus Lumber where he had worked since 1981.

driver and salesman.  Steve was responsible for selling overhead doors, purchasing 
power tools and occasionally driving a delivery truck.  The primary issue in this case is 
whether or not the Workers’ Compensation Commissioner erred as a matter of law in 
determining that the Petitioner had not met her burden of proof to establish that her 
husband death arose out of and in the course of his employment with Marcus Lumber 
Company.

In reaching his conclusions, Dr. Freeman noted that 19.5 percent of

DECISION OF WORKERS’ COMPENSATION COMMISSIONER 
Based on the foregoing findings of fact, the Workers’ Compensation Deputy 
Commissioner concluded that the Petitioner had failed to establish by a preponderance 
of the evidence that Steve’s work was a substantial factor in causing him to contract 
Covid-19, which caused his death. 
In reaching this conclusion, the Deputy Commissioner considered Steve’s work 
environment, his interaction with people at work, the mask and social distancing policies 
in place at Marcus Lumber and that Steve complied with those requirements at work, and 
that there were infections at Marcus Lumber.  The Deputy Commissioner also considered 
Steve’s activities outside of work,  that Steve did not follow CDC Guidelines in his personal 
life outside of his household, that he did not wear a mask during frequent interactions with 
family members multiple times per week in November, that he did not socially distance or 
wear a mask during a three-hour Thanksgiving dinner with multiple members of his family 
that did not live in his household, that he did not wear a mask when riding in an enclosed 
fire vehicle with another firefighter on November 26, 2020, nor when he fought the fire or 
assisted injured people that day, that he did not wear a mask or socially distance at a 90-
minute meeting on November 16, 2020, at the fire department, and that he occasionally 
went out for lunch and stopped for coffee and food in the morning on the way to work.

The Deputy Commissioner also considered the evidence that none of Steve’s 
family members were symptomatic or tested positive for Covid-19 but noted that there 
was no evidence that any of them had taken a Covid-19 test within 14 days of Steve 
becoming symptomatic. 
The Deputy Commissioner also considered the expert reports of Dr. Freeman and 
Dr. Golub and noted their reports involving infection rates at Marcus Lumber as well as 
the community at large.  The Deputy Commissioner further noted, however, that neither 
expert provided information regarding the percentage of people who had Covid-19-like 
symptoms who actually took Covid tests and those that had symptoms who did not seek

regarding infection rates for asymptomatic people in Cherokee or Plymouth Counties

96 percent. 
Finally, the Deputy Commissioner concluded that Dr. Freeman’s report was not 
persuasive in light of the fact that Steve followed CDC Guidelines at work but not outside 
of work, and that no evidence of contract tracing was presented.  As a result, the Deputy 
Commissioner found that the Petitioner failed to meet her burden of proof that Steve’s 
work was a substantial factor in causing him to contract Covid-19.

STANDARD OF REVIEW 
A person or party who has exhausted all adequate administrative remedies and 
who is aggrieved or adversely affected by a final agency motion is entitled to judicial 
review.  Iowa Code § 17A.19(1)(2023).  In exercising the power of judicial review 
conferred by Iowa Code § 17A.19, the district court acts in an appellate capacity to review 
agency action and correct errors of law.  Hanson v. Reichelt, 452 N.W.2d 164, 166 (Iowa 
1990).  The district court’s review is circumscribed.  Review of agency action is at law, 
not de novo, and is limited to the record made before the agency.  Taylor v. Iowa Dept. 
of Job Serv., 362 N.W.2d 534, 537 (Iowa 1985).   The court may not substitute its 
judgment for that of the agency.  Mercy Health Center v. State Health Facilities Council, 
360 N.W.2d 808, 809 (Iowa 1985).  The court may not usurp the agency’s function of 
making factual findings.  McSpadden v. Big Ben Coal Co., 388 N.W.2d 181, 186 (Iowa 
1980).  Iowa Code 17A.19(10)(a)-(n) provides: 
The court may affirm the agency action or remand to the agency for further 
proceedings.  The Court shall reverse, modify, or grant any other 
appropriate relief from the agency action, equitable or legal and including 
declaratory relief, if it determines that substantial rights of the person 
seeking judicial relief have been prejudiced because the agency action is: 
a. Unconstitutional on its face or as applied or is based upon a provision of 
the law that is unconstitutional on its face or as applied; 
b. Beyond the authority delegated to the agency by any provision of law or 
in violation of any provision of law; 
c. Based upon an erroneous interpretation of a provision of law whose 
interpretation has not clearly been vested by a provision of law in the 
discretion of the agency; 
d. Based upon a procedure or decision-making process prohibited by law 
or was taken without following the prescribed procedure or decision-
making process; 
e. The product of decision making undertaken by persons who were 
improperly constituted as a decision-making body, were motivated by an 
improper purpose, or were subject to disqualification; 
f. Based upon a determination of fact clearly vested by a provision of law 
in the discretion of the agency that is not supported by substantial

evidence in the record before the court when that record is viewed as a 
whole …; 
g. Action other than a rule that is inconsistent with a rule of the agency; 
h. Action other than a rule that is inconsistent with the agency’s prior 
practice or precedents, unless the agency has justified that 
inconsistency by stating credible reasons sufficient to indicate a fair and 
rational basis for the inconsistency; 
i. The product of reasoning that is so illogical as to render it wholly 
irrational; 
j. The product of a decision-making process in which the agency did not 
consider a relevant and important matter relating to the propriety or 
desirability of the action in question that a rational decision maker in 
similar circumstances would have considered prior to taking that action; 
k. Not required by law and its negative impact on the private rights affected 
is so grossly disproportionate to the benefits accruing to the public 
interest from the action that it must necessarily be deemed to lack any 
foundation in rational agency policy; 
l. Based upon an irrational, illogical, or wholly unjustifiable interpretation 
of a provision of law whose interpretation has clearly been vested by a 
provision of law in the discretion of the agency; 
m. Based upon an irrational, illogical, or wholly unjustifiable application of 
law to fact that has clearly been vested by a provision of law in the 
discretion of the agency; 
n. Otherwise unreasonable, arbitrary, capricious, or an abuse of discretion. 
Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(a)-(n)(2023). 
Furthermore, an agency’s findings must be supported by substantial evidence in 
the record when that record is reviewable as a whole.  Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(f)(2023); 
Norland v. Iowa Dept. of Job Serv., 412 N.W.2d 904, 913 (Iowa 1987).  “Record viewed 
as a whole” means that the adequacy of the evidence in the record before the court to 
support a particular finding of fact must be judged in light of all the relevant evidence in 
the record cited by any party that detracts from the findings as well as all of the relevant 
evidence in the record cited by any party that supports it, including any determinations of 
veracity by the presiding officer who personally observed the demeanor of the witnesses 
and the agency’s explanation of why the relevant evidence in the record supports its 
material findings of fact. Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(f)(3)(2023).  The evidence need not 
E-FILED                    CVCV041342 - 2024 FEB 19 02:01 PM             PLYMOUTH

amount to a preponderance in order to be substantial evidence, but a mere scintilla will 
not suffice.  Elliot v. Iowa Dept. of Transp., 377 N.W.2d 250, 256 (Iowa 1985).  Substantial 
evidence means the quantity and quality of evidence that would be deemed sufficient by 
a neutral, detached and reasonable person, to establish the fact at issue when the 
consequences resulting from the establishment of that fact are understood to be serious 
and of great importance.  Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(f)(1)(2023).  The fact that two 
inconsistent conclusions can be drawn from the evidence does not mean that one of those 
conclusions is unsupported by substantial evidence.  Moore v. Iowa Dept. of Transp., 473 
N.W.2d 230, 232 (Iowa 1991).  The relevant inquiry is not whether the evidence might 
support a different finding, but whether the evidence supports the findings actually made. 
Id. 
Nearly all disputes are won or lost with the agency.  Sellers v. Employment Appeal 
Bd., 531 N.W.2d 645, 646 (Iowa App. 1995).  An agency decision is final if it is supported 
by substantial evidence and contains no errors of law.  Robbenolt v. Snap-On Tools Corp., 
555 N.W.2d 229, 234 (Iowa 1996).  A reviewing court is not bound by an agency’s 
erroneous conclusions of law. Bridgestone-Firestone v. Employment Appeal Board, 570 
N.W.2d 85, 90 (Iowa 1997). 
CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
The primary purpose of the worker’s compensation statute is to benefit the worker 
and his or her dependents, insofar as the statutory requirements permit. McSpadden v. 
Big Ben Coal Company, 288 N.W.2d 181, 188 (1980).  It is well settled in Iowa that for an 
injury to be compensable, it must occur both in the course of and arise out of employment. 
Miedema v. The Dial Company, 551 N.W.2d 309, 311 (Iowa 1996); Iowa Code § 85.3(1) 
E-FILED                    CVCV041342 - 2024 FEB 19 02:01 PM             PLYMOUTH    
CLERK OF DISTRICT COURT                    Page 25 of 33

26 
 
(1995).  The two tests are separate and distinct, and both must be satisfied in order. Id.  
The words “in the course of” simply refers to the time, place and circumstances of the 
injury. Hanson v. Reichelt, 452 N.W.2d 164, 166 (Iowa 1990).  So, an injury occurs in the 
course of employment when it happens within the period of employment at a place the 
employee may reasonably be, and while the employee is doing work or something 
incidental to it. Id. at 167. 
 
Judicial review of the Worker’s Compensation Commissioner’s decision is not de 
novo, and the commissioner’s findings have the force of a jury verdict. Holmes v. Bruce 
Motor Freight, Inc., 215 N.W.2d 296, 298 (Iowa 1974).  The commissioner, not the court, 
weighs the evidence, and the court broadly and liberally construes the commissioner’s 
findings to uphold rather than defeat his decision. Id.  The question is not whether the 
evidence is sufficient to support a finding the commissioner did not make, but whether the 
evidence supports the finding he did make. Id.  The commissioner, as the fact finder, 
determines the weight to be given to any expert testimony. Sherman v. Pella Corp., 576 
N.W.2d 312, 321 (Iowa 1998).  Such weight depends on the accuracy of the facts relied 
upon by the expert and other surrounding circumstances. Id.  The commissioner may 
accept or reject the expert opinion in whole or in part. Id.   
ISSUES RAISED ON JUDICIAL REVIEW 
 
The Petitioner asserts four issues on Judicial Review: 
1. Whether the Commissioner erred as a matter of law when he failed to make 
findings of fact regarding credibility of witnesses. 
2. Whether the Commissioner erred a matter of law when he applied a fault 
standard to deny benefits.

27 
 
3. Whether the Commissioner erred as a matter of law when he required petitioner 
to meet a burden of proof greater than a preponderance of the evidence. 
4. Whether the Commissioner erred as a matter of law when he failed to make 
findings of fact regarding the qualifications of expert witnesses to render an opinion on 
the issues of the case. 
In essence, the Petitioner asserts that the evidence presented established that the 
greater risk of exposure to Covid-19 for Steve occurred at his employment and not in the 
community in general and therefore it was more likely than not that Steve became infected 
at work.   
I. 
Regarding the first issue, whether the Commissioner failed to make necessary 
credibility findings, the Court finds that this argument is without merit.  As the 
Commissioner simply adopted the analysis and conclusions of the Deputy Commissioner, 
the Court will focus on that analysis herein.  A review of the record and the Deputy 
Commissioners ruling shows that the Deputy Commissioner found the various witnesses 
to be credible.  The Deputy Commissioner did not question the factual assertions made 
by the various witnesses involving the working environment at Marcus Lumber, the 
number of employees who tested positive for Covid-19, the use of CDC guidelines at work 
and Steve’s behaviors and activities outside of work.  Instead, the Deputy Commissioner 
accepted the facts as presented and made an independent analysis of said evidence to 
determine whether or not the Petitioner had met her burden of proof that Steve was 
exposed to Covid-19 at work which then led to his death.  An explicit credibility finding is 
not required when the ultimate issue to be decided does not depend on whether some 
E-FILED                    CVCV041342 - 2024 FEB 19 02:01 PM             PLYMOUTH    
CLERK OF DISTRICT COURT                    Page 27 of 33

28 
 
witnesses were credible while others were not.  Magana v. Tyson Fresh Meats, Inc., File 
Nos., 5015475-76, 2005 IA Wrk. Comp. LEXIS 817 (Arb. Dec. 19, 2005).    All that is 
required is that the commissioner state the evidence relied upon and to provide the 
reasons for his conclusion.  Terwillinger v. Snap-On Tools Corp., 529 N.W.2d 267, 274 
(Iowa 1995).   
II. 
Regarding the second issue, whether the Commissioner improperly applied a fault 
standard to deny benefits, the Court finds that this argument is also without merit.   While 
the Deputy Commissioner did find that Steve failed to follow CDC Guidelines outside of 
his work at Marcus Lumber, the Deputy Commissioner was not making a fault 
determination regarding how Steve contracted Covid-19.  As the Petitioner acknowledges 
in her Brief, mask wearing outside of work was a relevant consideration for the Deputy 
Commissioner in determining whether Steve contracted Covid-19 as result of his 
employment at Marcus Lumber or from some outside source.  According to medical 
guidance, whether or not a person wore a mask at various times is a relevant 
consideration to the probability they might contract Covid-19.  In this case, the Deputy 
Commissioner had to determine whether the evidence presented supported a finding that 
it was more probable than not that Steve contracted Covid-19 from exposure at work as 
compared to exposure from other venues.  To conduct this analysis, the Deputy 
Commissioner had to compare Steve’s actions at work with his actions outside of work.  
This consideration of Steve’s actions or inactions does not blame Steve, or place fault on 
Steve, for his contraction of the Covid-19 virus, it simply goes towards the Deputy 
Commissioner’s analysis of whether or not the Petitioner met her burden of proof that it 
E-FILED                    CVCV041342 - 2024 FEB 19 02:01 PM             PLYMOUTH    
CLERK OF DISTRICT COURT                    Page 28 of 33

29 
 
was more probable than not that Steve contracted the virus through his work at Marcus 
Lumber.  
III. 
Regarding the third issue, whether the Commissioner held the Petitioner to a 
burden of proof greater than a preponderance of the evidence, the Court also finds that 
this argument is without merit.  While the Petitioner couches his third argument in 
language that the Commissioner held the Petitioner to a higher standard of care, the 
argument presented in the Petitioner’s Brief is that the evidence presented does not 
support the Commissioner’s ultimate findings and ruling.  Accordingly, the issue before 
this Court on review is whether or not there is substantial evidence in the record to support 
the Commissioner’s ultimate ruling.  As has repeatedly been held by the Supreme Court, 
this court is bound by the factual findings of the Commissioner so long as they are 
supported by substantial evidence in the record.  Neal v. Annett Holdings, Inc., 814 
N.W.2d 512, 518 (Iowa 2012).   Substantial evidence is not lacking simply because it is 
possible to draw different conclusions from the same evidence.  Riley v. Oscar Mayer 
Foods Corp., 532 N.W.2d 489, 491-492 (Iowa Ct. App 1995).  While it was certainly 
possible that the Commissioner could have concluded from the evidence presented that 
it was possible that Steve contracted Covid-19 due to exposure at his place of 
employment, it is equally possible the Commissioner could have concluded from the 
evidence presented that Steve could have contracted Covid-19 from a source other than 
his employment.  That is the underlying problem in this case.  Had Steve’s behaviors 
been the same at work as away from work, (i.e. social distancing/mask wearing etc.) it 
could easily be argued that the probability that he contracted Covid at work was greater 
E-FILED                    CVCV041342 - 2024 FEB 19 02:01 PM             PLYMOUTH    
CLERK OF DISTRICT COURT                    Page 29 of 33

30 
 
that elsewhere.  The evidence, however, shows that this was not the case.  It is certainly 
reasonable for the Commissioner to conclude, based on the evidence before him, that 
the likelihood Steve contracted Covid outside of work was at least equal to the likelihood 
he contracted it at work due to his failure to use preventative measures outside of work.  
A preponderance of the evidence standard is not met if the evidence can be viewed as 
50/50.   The bottom line in this case is that no one can know with any degree of certainty 
where Steve contracted the Covid virus or whether it was more probable than not that he 
contracted at work versus outside of work.  The record reflects that the positivity rate in 
Cherokee County was extremely high from November 2020 to January 2021, with the 
number showing that at least 1 in 5 residents had been infected.  In addition, the evidence 
presented indicated that the protective measures taken at Marcus Lumber would have 
helped reduce the potential for spread of the virus and that said precautions were not 
used by Steve elsewhere.  While other evidence may suggest that it was in fact more 
likely that Steve contracted the virus at work, this court does not weigh the evidence, it 
only determines whether substantial evidence supports the Commissioner’s ultimate 
findings and ruling, which this court concludes it does. 
The Deputy Commissioner (incorporated by the Commissioner), repeatedly 
referenced the preponderance of the evidence standard, and the analysis shown in the 
Arbitration Decision reflects that this correct standard was used.

Regarding the fourth and final issue raised, whether the Commissioner erred in 
failing to make findings of fact regarding the qualifications of each expert to render an 
opinion on the ultimate issue, the Court also finds that this argument is without merit.   As

before, it is not the duty of this Court to reweigh the conflicting opinions of the experts in 
this case.  Again, the standard for review by this Court is whether or not there is 
substantial evidence in the record to support the Commissioner’s acceptance of Dr. 
Golub’s opinion over the opinion of Dr. Freeman.  The weight to be given to conflicting 
experts’ opinions is within the province of the Commissioner.  Cedar Rapids Cmty. Sch. 
Dist. V. Pease, 807 N.W.2d 839, 852 (Iowa 2011).  
In this case, the Commissioner set out the facts from the record that supported his 
analysis of the experts’ opinions and why the opinion of Dr. Golub was accepted over 
those of Dr. Freeman.  In particular, the Commissioner noted that the evidence showed 
that Steve followed CDC guidelines at work, and did not outside of work; that no evidence 
was presented regarding the threat of spread by asymptomatic persons in the community; 
that no evidence of contact tracing was presented, that while Steve worked significant 
hours Marcus Lumber he was socially distanced and/or had a mask on the majority of 
that time. 
The bottom line in this case is that it is basically impossible for anyone to know 
how Steve contracted the Covid virus.  While the evidence presented shows that there 
are some factors that would suggest it was more probable that he contracted the virus at 
work, there are other factors which suggest that it was more probable that he contracted 
the virus outside of work.   The burden is on the Petitioner, by a preponderance of the 
evidence, to show that hit was more probable that Steve contracted the virus at work.  
She has simply failed to meet that burden and the Commissioner’s findings to that extent 
are supported by substantial evidence in the record.
