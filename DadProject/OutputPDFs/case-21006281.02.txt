STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Stephen Kinter, claimant, filed a petition in arbitration and seeks workers’ 
compensation benefits from defendants, Masterbrand Cabinets, Inc. d/b/a Fortune 
Brands Home & Security (“Masterbrand Cabinets”), as the employer and American 
Zurich Insurance Company, as the insurance carrier.   
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised 
or discussed in this decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations. 
The evidentiary record includes Joint Exhibits 1 through 10, Claimant’s Exhibits 
11 through 15, and Employer’s Exhibits A through E.  All exhibits were received into the 
evidentiary record without objection.    
Claimant testified on his own behalf.  No other witnesses testified live at the 
hearing.  The evidentiary record closed at the conclusion of the arbitration hearing.  The 
parties requested the opportunity to file post-hearing briefs.  Their request was granted. 
This case was considered fully submitted to the undersigned upon the parties filing their 
post-hearing briefs on November 17, 2023.

ISSUES 
The parties submitted the following disputed issues for resolution: 
1. Whether claimant is entitled to payment of additional healing period benefits 
as a result of an underpayment of the weekly rate between April 6, 2021 and 
February 20, 2022. 
2. Whether the April 1, 2021 injury caused permanent disability. 
3. The nature of the alleged injury as a scheduled member or unscheduled 
injury. 
4. The extent of claimant’s entitlement to permanent disability, if any. 
5. Claimant’s average gross weekly wages on the date of injury and the 
corresponding weekly workers’ compensation rate. 
6. Whether claimant is entitled to reimbursement of his independent medical 
evaluation expense pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.39. 
7. Whether costs should be assessed against either party. 
At the commencement of the arbitration hearing, the parties agreed to stipulate to 
an average weekly wage of $2,231.72 and a corresponding weekly workers’ 
compensation rate of $1,382.01.  (Transcript, pages 6-9)  The parties’ stipulation was 
accepted at the time of hearing and is now binding on the parties. 
The parties’ stipulation as to the weekly workers’ compensation rate also 
resolved the pending dispute about whether claimant is entitled to payment of additional 
healing period benefits.  At the time of hearing, the undersigned verbally ordered 
defendants to pay any additional weekly benefits owed to claimant as a result of an 
underpayment of the weekly rate during the claimed healing period.  (Tr., pp. 8-9)  No 
additional facts or conclusions of law will be entered relative to the weekly rate issue or 
the healing period claim. 
Additionally, at the commencement of the hearing, defendants admitted that 
claimant is entitled to reimbursement of his independent medical evaluation expenses 
pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.39 in some amount.  (Tr., pp. 9-10)

On April 1, 2021, claimant was operating a semi for the employer in Oregon.  As

approximately 65 to 70 miles per hour at the time of impact.  Photos of claimant’s truck

in this accident. 
Mr. Kinter described the impact and how it threw his body forward and 
subsequently threw him backwards.  He testified he was holding the steering wheel at 
the time of impact and saw the other semi prior to the time of impact.  Mr. Kinter testified 
that, immediately after the accident, he experienced symptoms all over his body but 
especially experienced symptoms in his neck and bilateral hands.

to care through Sarvenaz Jabbari, M.D.  Ultimately, claimant reported neck and bilateral 
hand conditions.  By May 4, 2021, Dr. Jabbari opined that claimant could return to work

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based. A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause. A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible. George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996). 
 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability. 
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is

also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); 
Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995). Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted expert medical 
testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 
N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 1994). 
The parties submitted a dispute about whether this injury should be compensated 
as a scheduled member injury to the arm or arms, or whether it should be compensated 
as an unscheduled injury with industrial disability.  Under the Iowa Workers' 
Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is compensated either for a loss or loss 
of use of a scheduled member under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(a)-(u) or for loss of 
earning capacity under section 85.34(2)(v).  In this instance, claimant proved he 
sustained a permanent injury to his neck as well as his arms.  The neck is not a 
scheduled injury outlined in Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(a)-(u).  Therefore, claimant has 
proven that his injury involves an unscheduled injury and should be compensated under 
Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v). 
Claimant was terminated by the employer.  Therefore, any entitlement to 
permanent disability is evaluated with industrial disability.  Since claimant has an 
impairment to the body as a whole, an industrial disability has been sustained.  
Industrial disability was defined in Diederich v. Tri-City R. Co., 219 Iowa 587, 258 N.W. 
899 (1935) as follows:  "It is therefore plain that the Legislature intended the term 
'disability' to mean 'industrial disability' or loss of earning capacity and not a mere 
'functional disability' to be computed in terms of percentages of the total physical and 
mental ability of a normal man." 
Functional impairment is an element to be considered in determining industrial 
disability which is the reduction of earning capacity, but consideration must also be 
given to the injured employee's age, education, qualifications, experience, motivation, 
loss of earnings, severity and situs of the injury, work restrictions, inability to engage in 
employment for which the employee is fitted and the employer's offer of work or failure 
to so offer.  McSpadden v. Big Ben Coal Co., 288 N.W.2d 181 (Iowa 1980); Olson v. 
Goodyear Service Stores, 255 Iowa 1112, 125 N.W.2d 251 (1963); Barton v. Nevada 
Poultry Co., 253 Iowa 285, 110 N.W.2d 660 (1961).  Moreover, “[a] determination of the 
reduction in the employee’s earning capacity caused by the disability shall take into 
account the permanent partial disability of the employee and the number of years in the 
future it was reasonably anticipated that the employee would work at the time of the 
injury.”  Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v).  Compensation shall be paid in relation to 500 
weeks as the disability bears to the body as a whole.  Section 85.34(2)(v). 
Having considered claimant’s age, education, qualifications, experience, 
motivation, loss of earnings, severity and situs of the injury, work restrictions, proximity

to retirement, as well as all other industrial disability factors outlined by Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(v) and the Iowa Supreme Court, I found that claimant proved a 30 
percent loss of future earning capacity as a result of the April 1, 2021 work injury.  Given 
this finding, I conclude claimant proved a 30 percent industrial disability.  This entitles 
claimant to an award of 150 weeks of permanent partial disability benefits.  Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(v). 
The parties submitted two additional disputed issues for determination.  The first 
is the amount claimant is entitled to for reimbursement of his independent medical 
evaluation under Iowa Code section 85.39.  Defendants concede claimant is entitled to 
reimbursement in some amount under Iowa Code section 85.39.  (Tr., pp. 9-10)  
Claimant seeks award of the entirety of the fees charged by Dr. Kuhnlein, or $4,740.90.  
Defendants assert that Dr. Kuhnlein’s charge of $2,190 for drafting the impairment 
rating is reasonable and apparently conceded.  (Defendants’ Post-Hearing Brief, pp. 16-
17) 
Dr. Kuhnlein’s invoice is included at Joint Exhibit 10.  Dr. Kuhnlein charges 
$750.00 for the initial hour of his examination as well as an additional $927.50 for 
additional time to complete the examination.  Iowa Code section 85.39(2) provides, “An 
employer is only liable to reimburse an employee for the cost of an examination 
conducted pursuant to this subsection” and that the determination of the 
reasonableness of the fee “shall be based on the typical fee charged by a medical 
provider to perform an impairment rating in the local area where the examination is 
conducted.”  Dr. Kuhnlein’s examination fees total $1,677.50.  Dr. Kuhnlein offers an 
unrebutted opinion that his fees are reasonable.  (Joint Ex. 8, p. 75)  I find that Dr. 
Kuhnlein’s examination fees are reasonable and typical for the local area where Dr. 
Kuhnlein performed his evaluation.  I conclude that claimant is entitled to 
reimbursement of those examination fees pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.39(2). 
Claimant also seeks assessment of costs.  Specifically, claimant seeks the 
expense of Dr. Kuhnlein’s report fee as a cost of this proceeding.  Assessment of costs 
is a discretionary function of this agency.  Iowa Code section 10A.328.  Permissible 
costs are outlined in agency rule 876 IAC 4.33. 
Rule 876 IAC 4.33(6) permits assessment of “the reasonable costs of obtaining 
no more than two doctors’ or practitioners’ reports.”  However, the amount assessed as 
a cost is limited to the cost of producing the report for submission into evidence in lieu of 
the testimony of the expert.  Des Moines Area Regional Transit Authority v. Young, 867 
N.W.2d 839, 843 (Iowa 2015).  In this case, Dr. Kuhnlein did not testify live or via 
deposition.  Instead, claimant elected to introduce Dr. Kuhnlein’s report in lieu of such 
testimony.  I conclude that Dr. Kuhnlein’s report, therefore, qualifies for potential 
reimbursement as a cost under 876 IAC 4.33(6).  Pursuant to P.M. Lattner 
Manufacturing Co. v. Rife, 2 N.W.3d 859, 867 (Iowa 2024), the cost of generating Dr. 
Kuhnlein’s report is actually reimbursable under Iowa Code section 85.39 as part of the 
independent medical evaluation fees.  Therefore, I conclude that Dr. Kuhnlein’s report 
fee is reasonable and typical for the locality where it was performed and can be

ORDER 
THEREFORE IT IS ORDERED: 
Defendants shall pay healing period benefits from April 6, 2021 through February 
20, 2022. 
Defendants shall pay claimant one hundred fifty (150) weeks of permanent partial 
disability benefits commencing on February 21, 2022. 
All weekly benefits shall be paid at the rate of one thousand three hundred 
eighty-two and 01/100 dollars ($1,382.01) per week. 
Defendants are entitled to the stipulated credit for benefits paid to date. 
Interest shall be payable on all late-paid weekly benefits at an annual rate equal 
to the one-year treasury constant maturity published by the federal reserve in the most 
recent H15 report settled as of the date of injury, plus two percent. See Gamble v. AG 
Leader Technology File No. 5054686 (App. Apr. 24, 2018). 
Pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.39, defendants shall reimburse claimant’s 
independent medical evaluation fee in the amount of one thousand six hundred 
seventy-seven and 50/100 dollars ($1,677.50). 
Defendants shall reimburse the cost of claimant’s independent medical 
evaluation report fee in the amount of two thousand nine hundred forty and 00/100 
dollars ($2,940.00). 
Defendants shall file subsequent reports of injury (SROI) as required by this 
agency pursuant to rules 876 IAC 3.1(2) and 876 IAC 11.7.
