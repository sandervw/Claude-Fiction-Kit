This matter came before the Court on a Petition for Judicial Review from a 
final decision of the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commission.  A hearing was held 
on March 26, 2024. Petitioner, Donald Tuttle, appeared through counsel Dennis 
Currell.  Respondent, Archer Daniels Midland (“ADM”) appeared through counsel, 
Brandon Lobberecht. Upon review of the court file and applicable law, the Court 
enters the following order: 
 
I. 
BACKGROUND FACTS  
Donald Tuttle filed an Alternate Medical Care Petition, seeking adjudication 
of issues arising from ADM’s on-going conduct regarding treatment related to his 
traumatic brain injury. The Deputy dismissed the Petition without hearing, finding 
it lacked jurisdiction. ADM agrees that, to the extent Tuttle seeks alternate medical 
care only related to alleged conduct arising / occurring after July 21, 2023, the Deputy 
should have conducted a hearing. Tuttle agrees remand is appropriate, but resists 
any direction by this Court, limiting or restricting the evidence the agency can 
consider based on res judicata, claim and/or issue preclusion. 
The Deputy’s Order of Dismissal re: Alternate Medical Care dismissed Tuttle’s 
claim for lack of jurisdiction, finding it lacked “the authority to revisit a final decision 
on specific alternate care that the agency has already ordered the defendants to 
authorize in a prior contested case proceeding . . . .” The Deputy noted it “no longer 
has jurisdiction over the claim due to the petition for judicial review” and “there is no

II. 
DISCUSSION 
The parties on appeal raise the issues of res judicata, claim preclusion, and 
issue preclusion.  The Deputy’s Order relied on the district court’s ruling in Hormel 
Foods Corp. v. Tamayo-Perez; that decision was appealed, and subsequently reversed 
in part by the Iowa Court of Appeals.1 The Court of Appeals reversed the portion of 
the district court’s order which the Deputy relied upon, holding “[b]ecause no stay 
was in place, we conclude there was nothing preventing the agency from deciding the 
sixth application for alternate care.”2 As the Deputy has jurisdiction, remand is 
warranted. 
ADM’s request for remand with direction that res judicata principles of claim 
and issue preclusion apply “insofar as Claimant seeks to litigate or relitigate ADM’s 
conduct concerning his medical care before July 21, 2023 as the basis for his claim” 
highlights the challenge of ruling on the parties arguments in this judicial review 
setting. The Deputy did not hold a hearing, and no evidence was presented. It is 
difficult to definitively determine whether Tuttle is raising claims prior to July 2023 
as ADM alleges, or simply relying on facts that took place prior to that time to support 
a new claim. ADM is essentially asking that evidence be limited to events that 
occurred after the July 21, 2023 ruling. It is unclear how much of Tuttle’s 
abandonment claim will rely on evidence prior to July 2023 to prove “cumulative 
conduct” demonstrating abandonment of care.  
Evidentiary issues are clearly within the discretion of the agency. Iowa Code 
section 10A.319 (formerly 86.18) provides: “[e]vidence, process and procedure in 
contested case proceedings . . . shall be as summary as practicable consistent with 
the requirements of chapter 17A.” The Court gives deference to the commission 
because “the commissioner is statutorily required to “[a]dopt and enforce rules

remand with directing limiting claims and/or evidence is not appropriate in this 
situation.
