STATEMENT OF THE CASE   
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A. The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, William 
Black. Claimant appeared telephonically and through his attorney, Luke Guthrie. 
Defendants appeared through their attorney, Madaline McGill. 
Claimant filed the petition on January 24, 2025. The petition alleges an injury 
arising out of and in the course of employment to his right wrist/right upper extremity on 
February 29, 2024. Defendants filed an answer on February 5, 2025, and admitted 
liability for the claim related to the right wrist. 
The alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on February 5, 2025. The 
proceedings were digitally recorded. That recording constitutes the official record of this 
proceeding. Pursuant to the Commissioner’s July 21, 2023 Order, the undersigned has 
been delegated authority to issue a final agency decision in this alternate medical care 
proceeding. Therefore, this ruling is designated final agency action and any appeal of 
the decision would be to the Iowa District Court pursuant to Iowa Code section 17A. 
The record consists of claimant’s exhibit 1 (attached to the petition), consisting of 
three pages, and defendants’ exhibits A and B, consisting of five pages. No witnesses 
were called. Counsel offered oral arguments to support their positions, and defendants 
have also submitted a short addendum outlining their position.

ISSUE 
The issue presented for resolution is whether the claimant is entitled to alternate 
medical care consisting of authorization of the surgical repair recommended by Clifford 
Novak, M.D., including all related medical services and transportation expenses. 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned having considered all of the evidence in the record finds: 
Claimant sustained a work-related injury to his right wrist on February 29, 2024. 
Defendants accepted the injury as compensable and continue to pay benefits to 
claimant. (See Defendants’ Addendum) The parties represented that Dr. Novak is the 
authorized treating physician chosen by defendants to provide medical treatment for 
claimant’s work injury. 
Claimant saw Dr. Novak for a follow-up appointment on November 14, 2024. 
(Claimant’s Exhibit 1, p. 1) The record indicates that Dr. Novak had obtained an MRI of 
claimant’s wrist on November 12, 2024. The MRI was interpreted by a Dr. Van Roekel, 
who felt there was “evidence of a TFC tear at the ulnar attachments and there was ulnar 
subluxation/translation that he felt was due to positioning and ulnar dislocation of the 
ECU.” Dr. Novak discussed it personally with Dr. Van Roekel, who did not believe there 
was obvious evidence of ligamentous injury to the DRUJ. He also reviewed the case 
with his partner, Dr. Chimenti. 
After physical examination, Dr. Novak’s assessment was wrist pain, right; no 
obvious DRUJ ligamentous injury per Dr. Van Roekel; TFC tear, right wrist; and ulnar 
dislocation of the ECU tendon, right wrist, no clinical pain in this area. (Cl. Ex. 1, p. 3) 
Under “plan,” Dr. Novak noted that options for claimant’s TFC tear included observation 
versus injection versus therapy versus surgery. After discussion with Dr. Novak, 
claimant wished to pursue surgery.  
Dr. Novak’s record states that his surgical plan “will be a TFC repair using a bone 
tunnel in the ulna using the Arthrex device.” He indicated he would have his partner 
available for assistance. He also stated they would do neurectomies of the PIN and 
dorsal ulnar sensory nerve and the radiocarpal synovectomy at the same time. Finally, 
his note states that claimant understood that he may have incomplete pain relief 
following surgery, and that there was no plan to do anything about the ECU as he was 
not having pain in that area at the time. However, he may develop pain in the future 
requiring treatment. 
Claimant’s attorney represented at hearing that claimant has already tried 
bracing, injections, and therapy, and continues to have pain. He wants to proceed with 
surgery, and has attempted to schedule it with no success. Defense counsel disagrees 
that claimant has been attempting to schedule surgery, and indicated that defendants 
have been clear that they intend to pursue an independent medical evaluation (IME) 
prior to authorizing surgery.

Defense counsel argued at hearing that defendants have been clear about their 
desire to obtain an IME prior to authorizing surgery, and that defendants have the right 
to decide whether recommended medical treatment is reasonably suited to treat the

by Dr. Novak’s “apprehension” regarding the nature of claimant’s injury and his “desire 
to consult with his peers prior to proceeding with the same.” Defendants also indicate 
that an IME has been scheduled for February 18, 2025, to “further assess [claimant’s] 
injuries.” (Def. Addendum) 
REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Iowa Code section 85.27(4) provides, in relevant part: 
For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish 
reasonable services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has 
the right to choose the care. . . .  The treatment must be offered promptly 
and be reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience 
to the employee. If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the 
care offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such 
dissatisfaction to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the 
employer and the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited 
to treat the injury. If the employer and employee cannot agree on such 
alternate care, the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable 
proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
Iowa Code § 85.27(4). 
Defendants’ “obligation under the statute is confined to reasonable care for the 
diagnosis and treatment of work-related injuries.” Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 
N.W.2d 122, 124 (Iowa 1995) (emphasis in original). In other words, the “obligation 
under the statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.” Id. 
Similarly, an application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained 
because claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving. Mere

dissatisfaction with the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for 
alternate medical care. Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered 
promptly, was not reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly 
inconvenient for the claimant. See Iowa Code § 85.27(4). Thus, by challenging the 
employer’s choice of treatment and seeking alternate care, claimant assumes the 
burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable. See Iowa R. App. P 14(f)(5); 
Long, 528 N.W.2d at 124. Ultimately, determining whether care is reasonable under the 
statute is a question of fact.  Long, 528 N.W.2d at 123. 
An employer’s right to select the provider of medical treatment to an injured 
worker does not include the right to determine how an injured worker should be 
diagnosed, evaluated, treated, or other matters of professional medical judgment.  
Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 866389 (Declaratory Ruling, May 19, 1988). 
Defendants are not entitled to interfere with the medical judgment of its own treating 
physician.  Pote v. Mickow Corp., File No. 694639 (Review-Reopening Decision 
June 17, 1986). 
In other words, while defendants are afforded the right to direct medical care, 
contrary to defendants’ position, that does not include the right to second-guess the 
recommendations of the authorized treating physician. Defendants can send claimant 
for an IME pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.39, but it is unreasonable to delay or deny 
treatment recommended by the authorized treating provider in the meantime. 
In this case, claimant has met his burden to prove that the care offered by 
defendants is unreasonable. Failure to authorize surgery that has been recommended 
by the authorized treating physician is unreasonable. There is nothing in Dr. Novak’s 
November 14, 2024 record that indicates he is apprehensive about his surgical 
recommendation. Consultation with his medical partner regarding the case is not an 
indication of apprehension. This is an admitted injury, and the recommendation for 
surgery was made over two months ago. The fact that defendants did not have the 
medical record or know there was a surgical recommendation until late December/early 
January is no excuse, given defendants’ ongoing duty to investigate claims and provide 
prompt and reasonable medical care. Because claimant has met his burden of proving 
the authorized care is unreasonable, his petition for alternate care must be granted. 
ORDER 
Therefore it is ordered: 
The claimant's petition for alternate medical care is granted. 
Defendants shall immediately authorize and pay for all reasonable medical 
services and transportation expenses consistent with Dr. Novak’s November 14, 2024 
treatment plan, including the recommended surgery.
