STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
  
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A. The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Terrence Williams. 
Claimant appeared through his attorney, Robert Rosenstiel. Defendants appeared 
through their attorney, Ed Rose. 
  
The alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on July 17, 2025. The 
proceedings were digitally recorded. That recording constitutes the official record of this 
proceeding. Pursuant to the Commissioner’s July 21, 2023, Order, the undersigned has 
been delegated authority to issue a final agency decision in this alternate medical care 
proceeding. Therefore, this ruling is designated final agency action and any appeal of 
the decision would be to the Iowa District Court pursuant to Iowa Code section 17A. 
  
The record consists of Claimant’s Exhibit 1, which includes a total of 9 pages.  
No witnesses were called to testify. Counsel were permitted an opportunity to argue 
their cases, and the record closed on July 17, 2025. 
    
ISSUE 
  
The issue presented for resolution is whether the claimant is entitled to surgery 
as recommended by Justin Munns, M.D.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
  
The undersigned, having considered all the evidence in the record, finds: 
  
Terrence Williams sustained a work-related injury on July 1, 2024.  Defendants 
accepted the injury and provided medical care.   
 
On February 27, 2025, defendants authorized Mr. Williams to present to ORA 
Orthopedics for an evaluation. (Exhibit 1, p. 1)  Waqas Hussain, M.D. assessed Mr. 
Williams with chronic left wrist strain with a low-grade dorsal capsular strain, with 
possible intra-articular pathology. (Id.)  Following the examination, Dr. Hussain ordered 
an MRI arthrogram for further investigation. (Id.) 
 
The March 12, 2025, MRI arthrogram revealed advanced tenosynovitis at the 
crossing of the index and middle finger extensor tendons, compatible with intersection 
syndrome. (Ex. 1, p. 3)  Following a review of the MRI arthrogram, Dr. Hussain referred 
Mr. Williams to Justin Munns, M.D., an upper extremity specialist. (Ex. 1, p. 4) 
 
Initially, Dr. Munns recommended conservative treatment consisting of a 
cortisone injection and occupational therapy. (Ex. 1, pp. 6-7)  However, when this 
treatment failed to alleviate Mr. Williams’ symptoms, Dr. Munns discussed the possibility 
of surgical intervention.   
 
On May 14, 2025, Dr. Munns recommended a left wrist extensor 
tenosynovectomy. (Ex. 1, p. 8)  Mr. Williams understood the risks involved with surgery 
and wished to proceed.  As of July 17, 2025, defendants have not authorized the 
recommended surgery. 
 
At hearing, claimant’s attorney reported that his client would like to proceed with 
the surgery as recommended by Dr. Munns, an authorized treating provider.  Defense 
counsel represented that defendants have contacted Dr. Munns to confirm whether the 
need for surgery is causally related to the July 1, 2024, work injury.  Although he could 
not provide a specific date for the communication, defense counsel believed it occurred 
“a couple weeks ago.” He was hopeful that Dr. Munns would respond before the July 
17, 2025, hearing, but no response had been received at the time of hearing.  
 
I find that defendants have authorized care through Dr. Munns, who has provided 
a recommendation consistent with his medical expertise and one that appears 
reasonable on its face.  Defendants offer no basis to challenge the reasonableness of 
Dr. Munns’ recommendation at this time.  While defendants are entitled to a reasonable 
period to conduct an investigation and seek clarification from the provider, the delay in 
obtaining Dr. Munns’ opinion is bordering on unreasonable.  This concern is 
compounded by the absence of any evidence showing that such an investigation has 
actually been undertaken.

In failing to authorize the care recommended by Dr. Munns, defendants have not 
offered prompt medical care, nor have they offered reasonable medical care that is 
suited to treat claimant’s injury.  Fortunately for defendants, it appears unlikely that Dr. 
Munns will be able to perform the surgery in the immediate future.  If Dr. Munns 
ultimately provides a causation opinion that is unfavorable to Mr. Williams’ position, 
defendants would remain within their rights to deny liability for the recommended 
procedure.   
   
REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
   
Under Iowa law, the employer is required to provide care to an injured employee 
and is permitted to choose the care. Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 
433 (Iowa 1997). 
 
[T]he employer is obliged to furnish reasonable services and supplies to 
treat an injured employee, and has the right to choose the care .... The 
treatment must be offered promptly and be reasonably suited to treat the 
injury without undue inconvenience to the employee. If the employee has 
reason to be dissatisfied with the care offered, the employee should 
communicate the basis of such dissatisfaction to the employer, in writing if 
requested, following which the employer and the employee may agree to 
alternate care reasonably suited to treat the injury. If the employer and 
employee cannot agree on such alternate care, the commissioner may, 
upon application and reasonable proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and 
order other care. 
  
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment - and seeking alternate care - 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable. See Iowa 
R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e); Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995). 
Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact. Id. The 
employer’s obligation turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability. Id.; 
Harned v. Farmland Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983). 
  
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving. Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care. Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant. Long, 528 N.W.2d at 122. 
  
An employer's right to select the provider of medical treatment to an injured 
worker does not include the right to determine how an injured worker should be 
diagnosed, evaluated, treated, or other matters of professional medical judgment. 
Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 866389 (Declaratory Ruling, May 18, 1988).

Reasonable care includes care necessary to diagnose the condition and 
defendants are not entitled to interfere with the medical judgment of its own treating 
physician. Pote v. Mickow Corp., File No. 694639 (Review-Reopening, June 17, 1986). 
 
When a designated physician refers a patient to another physician, that physician 
acts as the defendant employer's agent. Permission for the referral from defendant is 
not necessary. Kittrell v. Allen Memorial Hospital, Thirty-fourth Biennial Report of the 
Industrial Commissioner, 164 (Arb. November 1, 1979) (aff'd by industrial 
commissioner); See also Limoges v. Meier Auto Salvage, I Iowa Industrial 
Commissioner Reports 207 (1981). 
  
In this case, defendants have authorized medical care; however, they have not 
authorized the surgical recommendation of Dr. Munns, claimant’s authorized treating 
physician.  Defendants have not authorized prompt medical care or care that is 
reasonably suited to treat claimant's injury. Defendants' actions are an interference with 
the treating physician's recommendations.  Therefore, I conclude that claimant has 
established entitlement to alternate medical care. 
 
Defendants were permitted to select the authorized medical provider, Dr. Munns. 
Iowa Code section 85.27(4).  Having exercised that right to select the authorized 
provider, defendants may not ignore Dr. Munns’ recommendations.  Defendants may 
not interfere with Dr. Munns’ professional medical judgment, whether by design or 
delay.  Given that claimant has already been waiting over two months for authorization 
of the recommended surgery, I conclude that claimant has proven that there has been 
an unreasonable delay in providing care. 
 
It is evident that defendants have authorized reasonable and appropriate 
treatment in the past.  At hearing, defendants acknowledged that Dr. Munns is 
claimant’s authorized treating physician.  Given this information, I cannot find that 
defendants have abandoned care. 
 
I conclude that defendants continue to maintain the right to direct medical care, 
provided they act promptly to schedule and authorize the care ordered in this decision.  
If defendants fail to timely authorize necessary medical care recommended by the 
authorized physician, they risk losing their right to direct future medical care.   
 
ORDER 
  
THEREFORE, IT IS ORDERED: 
  
Claimant’s petition for alternate medical care is granted. 
 
Defendants shall promptly authorize and pay for the recommended surgery with 
Dr. Munns.
