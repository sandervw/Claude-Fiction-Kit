STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
Claimant, Solange Nagiciro, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits against Loparex, LLC, employer, and Sentry Insurance, insurer, 
both as defendants. This case was heard on March 4, 2024, and considered fully 
submitted on the same date.  
 
The record consists of Joint Exhibits 1-3, Claimant’s Exhibits 1-2, and 
Defendants’ Exhibits A-F, along with the testimony of claimant, via Interpreter Patrick 
Munyamahoro.  
ISSUES 
1. Whether claimant is entitled to temporary total or temporary partial benefits from 
August 23, 2022 to the present; 
 
2. In addition to the scheduled member disability to the left upper extremity, 
claimant seeks a finding of entitlement to industrial disability benefits;  
 
3. The extent of the disability;  
 
4. The commencement date of benefits;

STIPULATIONS 
 
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised 
or discussed in this decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations. 
 
The parties stipulate claimant sustained a work injury to the upper left extremity 
on August 23, 2022. They agree that the injury was the cause of some temporary 
disability during a period of recovery and that the injury was the cause of some 
permanent disability.  
 
At the time of the injury the claimant’s gross earnings were $765.16 per week. 
The claimant was single and entitled to one exemption. Based on the foregoing the 
parties agree the weekly benefit rate is $488.22. 
 
Defendants waive all affirmative defenses.  
 
Defendants have agreed to pay the independent medical examiner fee once the 
claimant has provided proof of payment to the defendants. Prior to the hearing the 
claimant was paid 15 weeks of compensation at the rate of $488.22. 
 
Defendants further agree to pay the filing fee if the claimant is awarded such fee 
in the decision.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based. A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause. A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible. George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996).

The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability. 
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); 
Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995). Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted expert medical 
testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 
N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 1994). 
Claimant seeks a running healing period award.  
Section 85.34(1) provides that healing period benefits are payable to an injured 
worker who has suffered permanent partial disability until (1) the worker has returned to 
work; (2) the worker is medically capable of returning to substantially similar 
employment; or (3) the worker has achieved maximum medical recovery.  The healing 
period can be considered the period during which there is a reasonable expectation of 
improvement of the disabling condition.  See Armstrong Tire & Rubber Co. v. Kubli, 
Iowa App 312 N.W.2d 60 (1981).  Healing period benefits can be interrupted or 
intermittent.  Teel v. McCord, 394 N.W.2d 405 (Iowa 1986). 
 
In regard to the running healing award, the claimant asserts that although she 
has no loss of wages, she has not fully returned to work because she has not been 
working the job she was doing prior to the injuries. Prior to the injuries she was using 
both hands, and since the injury she is only using one hand and performing light duty 
work. 
 
Claimant also argues that she is not at maximum medical improvement due to an 
“inconclusive finding” by the FCE examiner. However, the FCE finding was that the 
results were invalid due to lack of effort, inconsistent effort, and hyperbolic pain 
responses; not that there was an inconclusive report. The FCE examiner makes no 
finding regarding whether claimant is or is not at MMI. Dr. Broghammer recounted that 
Dr. Hogue placed claimant at MMI on March 21, 2023 and that the claimant 
corroborated that history and had no specific corrections to the history as outlined. (CE 
1:4)  
 
However, there does not need to be a finding that the claimant is at maximum 
medical improvement. Healing period benefits are only allowed up until the claimant has 
returned to work or substantially similar work. It may be the claimant is not doing 
substantially similar work, but she has returned to work and is being paid the same 
wage as she was earning prior to the injury. Thus, by statute, she is no longer entitled to 
temporary benefits. See Iowa Code Section 85.34(1). Therefore, claimant is not entitled 
to additional healing period benefits.

Claimant also argues she is entitled to an industrial disability based upon Dr. 
Hogue’s expert opinion when he converted the 5 percent upper extremity disability 
impairment into a 3 percent body as a whole injury. 
 
As the Iowa Supreme Court shared in Chavez v. MS Tech. LLC, 972 N.W.2d 
662, 666-67 (Iowa 2022): 
Under section 85.34, the classification of a workers’ compensation 
claimant's injury as either scheduled or unscheduled determines the 
extent of the claimant's entitlement to permanent partial disability 
benefits. Floyd v. Quaker Oats, 646 N.W.2d 105, 109 (Iowa 2002). 
Paragraphs (a) through (u) of section 85.34 govern permanent partial 
disability payments for injuries to specific members of the body and 
provide a schedule of benefits for injuries to those specific members. 
Second Inj. Fund of Iowa v. Nelson, 544 N.W.2d 258, 269 (Iowa 
1995); see also Iowa Code § 85.34(2)(a)–(u). Disabilities resulting 
from injuries other than those listed in paragraphs (a) through (u) of 
section 85.34 are considered unscheduled injuries that allow for benefits 
based on the injury to the body as a whole and are evaluated according to 
the industrial method.  Second Inj. Fund of Iowa, 544 N.W.2d at 269; see 
also Iowa Code § 85.34(2)(v). 
 
If an injury is classified as a scheduled member injury to claimant’s arm under 
Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(m), the claimant is eligible for a percentage of 250 weeks of 
pay based on the impairment rating of the injury. In contrast, if the injury is classified as 
an unscheduled whole body injury under section 85.34(2)(v), the claimant is eligible for 
payment for the functional impairment resulting from the injury on a 500-week 
schedule and additional compensation if the claimant did not return to work earning the 
same or greater wages as before the injury. 
 
It appears the claimant has a misunderstanding about the expert witness report. 
The 3 percent body as a whole impairment rating Dr. Hogue issued was a conversion of 
the 5 percent upper extremity disability impairment. It is not an expert conclusion that 
claimant sustained a whole body disability.  
 
A medical examiner’s numerical conversion does not convert from a scheduled 
member disability into an industrial disability. The characterization of a disability 
depends upon the situs of the injury. In this particular case only the left upper extremity 
suffered an injury. She sought treatment for the left upper extremity. The surgery was to 
the left upper extremity. Physical therapy was to the left upper extremity and even 
claimant’s own expert, Dr. Broghammer, gave an impairment rating and restrictions 
solely for the left upper extremity.  
 
There is no medical evidence to suggest that she sustained a whole body injury 
or that sequela from the left upper extremity injury has created a whole body injury. It is 
found claimant sustained an injury to the left upper extremity only.

Scheduled injuries are subject to recovery based on the code.  
Compensation for permanent partial disability shall begin when it is 
medically indicated that maximum medical improvement from the injury 
has been reached and that the extent of loss or percentage of permanent 
impairment can be determined by use of the guides to the evaluation of 
permanent impairment, published by the American medical association, as 
adopted by the workers' compensation commissioner by rule pursuant to 
chapter 17A… 
Iowa Code § 85.34(2) 
 
Dr. Hogue assessed a 5 percent upper left extremity impairment rating and Dr. 
Broghammer assessed a 7 percent upper left extremity impairment rating. The 
difference appears to be that Dr. Hogue assigned only an impairment rating for the 
sensory deficit and none for the loss of range of motion. Dr. Broghammer’s report 
contains detailed measurements of claimant’s loss of range of motion and is consistent 
with claimant’s testimony at hearing and her invalid FCE performance, as well as her 
continued work conduct wherein she primarily uses only her right hand and therefore, 
his 7 percent impairment rating is accepted herein.  
 
In the closing argument, claimant raised the possibility that there was bias in Dr. 
Hogue’s report. There was no evidence provided that the doctor had bias. Further, even 
assuming Dr. Hogue was biased, the findings above can be based solely on the 
opinions of claimant’s own expert. Dr. Broghammer assigned impairment ratings only to 
the left arm and did not consider any other body part as injured, nor did he find any 
other body part injured in his examination.   
 
Claimant also suggests that Dr. Hogue created ambiguity by his conversion from 
a left upper extremity impairment rating to a whole body rating. Even if this were true, it 
is claimant’s obligation to prove each element of her case by a preponderance of the 
evidence. Any ambiguity in causation or extent of disability that exists should be 
addressed through medical evidence brought forth by the claimant. 
 
That the injury would affect claimant’s children or the people that rely on her are 
unfortunately not factors that can be considered when evaluating a scheduled member 
disability, nor are they factors which can be considered when evaluating the loss of 
industrial disability which measures the claimant’s loss of access to the workforce and 
does not take into consideration the number of people that rely on an injured worker for 
support.  
 
Based on the foregoing and the undisputed medical evidence, it is found that the 
claimant sustained a scheduled member disability to the left upper extremity. Pursuant 
to the expert testimony in the record, the scheduled member disability is 7 percent. The 
commencement date for permanent partial disability benefits is March 21, 2023, when 
claimant returned to work.
