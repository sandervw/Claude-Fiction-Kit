I.  
STATEMEN T OF TH E C AS E. 
Claimant Larry Saul seeks workers’ compensation benefits from the defendants, 
employer BMC Aggregates, L.C. (BMC) and insurance carrier BITCO National 
Insurance Company (BITCO). The undersigned presided over an arbitration hearing on 
April 25, 2024. Saul participated personally and through attorney Benjamin R. Roth. The 
defendants participated by and through attorney Jeffrey W. Lanz. 
II.  
ISSU ES . 
Under Iowa Administrative Code rule 876—4.19(3)(f), the parties jointly 
submitted a hearing report defining the claims, defenses, and issues submitted to the 
presiding deputy commissioner. The hearing report was approved and entered into the 
record via an order because it is a correct representation of the disputed issues and 
stipulations in this case. The parties identified the following disputed issues in the 
hearing report: 
1) What is the nature and extent of permanent disability, if any, caused by the 
alleged injury? 
2) What is the commencement date for permanency benefits, if any are 
awarded? 
3) Is Saul entitled to payment of the medical expenses in Exhibit 6?

III .  
STIP U LATION S. 
 
In the hearing report, the parties entered into the following stipulations: 
1) An employer-employee relationship existed between Saul and BMC at the 
time of the alleged injury. 
2) Saul sustained an injury on August 1, 2019, which arose out of and in the 
course of his employment with BMC. 
3) The alleged injury is a cause of temporary disability during a period of 
recovery, but Saul’s entitlement to temporary disability or healing period 
benefits is no longer in dispute. 
4) The alleged injury is a cause of permanent disability, but Saul is entitled to 
benefits based only on the extent of permanent impairment because he 
returned to work with BMC earning the same or more than at the time of 
injury and he remained employed there at that earnings level as of the date 
of hearing. 
5) At the time of the stipulated injury: 
a) Saul’s gross earnings were $1,033.29 per week. 
b) Saul was married. 
c) Saul was entitled to two exemptions. 
The parties’ stipulations in the hearing report are accepted and incorporated into 
this arbitration decision. The parties are bound by their stipulations. This decision 
contains no discussion of any factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations 
except as necessary for clarity with respect to disputed factual and legal issues.

B a c k .  
For years, Saul has mowed lawns as a side job. (Hearing Transcript page 9) He 
hauls his own mower in his truck to properties, where he loads and unloads it to do the 
job. (Hr’g Tr. p. 9) Before the work injury, while unloading his mower, he “popped 
vertebrae 4 out” on two separate occasions. (Hr’g Tr. pp. 9–10)  
The prior back issues caused Saul pain in his back. (Hr’g Tr. p. 10) He 
experienced no radiating pain into his legs. (Hr’g Tr. p. 10) After each instance, he 
received chiropractic care that alleviated his symptoms, and he experienced no 
lingering issues. (Hr’g Tr. pp. 9–10) The care resulted in complete pain relief. (Hr’g Tr. 
p. 10) 
Saul did not receive care from a medical doctor for these back issues because 
the chiropractic care was effective. (Hr’g Tr. pp. 10–11) Before the stipulated work 
injury, Saul did not have any problems performing his job at BMC due to any back 
issues. (Hr’g Tr. p. 11) No doctor prescribed him any work restrictions relating to a 
health condition before the stipulated date of injury. (Hr’g Tr. p. 11) The weight of the 
evidence establishes that these two back injuries were temporary in nature and 
resolved before the stipulated work injury.

W o r k  I n j u r y.  
BMC operates a quarry. (Hr’g Tr. p. 12) It hired Saul to work at the quarry in 
1997. (Hr’g Tr. p. 12) Saul’s job was to run a dredge, both on the date of injury and at 
the time of hearing. (Hr’g Tr. pp. 12, 47)  
Saul’s job duties and hours worked depended on what tasks had to be completed 
on a given day. (Hr’g Tr. p. 13) When Saul was on the dredge, he would sit most of the 
time and monitor the controls. (Hr’g Tr. p. 14) Saul’s job duties included repairing pumps 
that broke down and when a pump needed to be fixed, he might be on his feet working 
up to twelve hours—however long it took to fix the pump. (Hr’g Tr. pp. 12, 14) His job 
duties also include carrying five-gallon buckets of oil and laying pipe and hose to pump 
water out of pits. (Hr’g Tr. pp. 12–15)  
On August 1, 2019, Saul and coworkers were repairing a system to dry sand. 
(Hr’g Tr. p. 16) An exhaust pipe measuring 32 inches in diameter had a hole in it and 
was leaking exhaust and dust, so they had to remove, repair, and reinstall it. (Hr’g Tr. p. 
16) After repairing the exhaust pipe, Saul and his coworkers were attempting to reinstall 
it with a skid loader. (Hr’g Tr. p. 16) There was a pipe nearby that was cemented into 
the ground. (Hr’g Tr. 16) They had the exhaust pipe chained to the skid loader’s bucket 
to secure it as they lowered it and it was Saul’s job to remove the chain from the 
exhaust pipe. (Hr’g Tr. p. 16) 
After Saul removed the chain securing the exhaust pipe to the skid loader, the 
exhaust pipe rolled out and struck the nearby pipe, breaking it loose from the concrete. 
(Hr’g Tr. pp. 16–17) Saul’s coworkers attempted to warn him, but he could not get out of 
the way before the broken-off pipe fell and struck him in his head and right shoulder, 
knocking off his hard hat. (Hr’g Tr. pp. 17, 44) The force of the pipe striking Saul did not 
cause him to fall to the ground. (Hr’g Tr. p. 44)  
Saul did not immediately notice any symptoms relating to the pipe striking him. 
(Hr’g Tr. p. 17) He finished out his day at work and went home. (Hr’g Tr. p. 17) When 
Saul woke up the next morning, he could not get out of bed because of pain in his back 
and legs. (Hr’g Tr. pp. 17–18) He called his boss and reported his injury and symptoms. 
(Hr’g Tr. p. 18)

This section addresses the narrow issue of the extent of permanent impairment 
to Saul’s lumbar spine caused by the stipulated work injury. Under the Guides, spinal 
impairment rating is performed using one of two methods: 
1) Diagnosis-Related Estimates (DRE) method; or 
 
2) Range-of-Motion (ROM) method. Guides, § 15.2. 
The Guides provide directions on which method to use given an injured worker’s 
condition. See id.  
The DRE method “is the principal methodology used to evaluate an individual 
who has had a distinct injury.” Id. According to the Guides, “When the cause of the 
impairment is not easily determined and if the impairment can be well characterized by 
the DRE method, the evaluator should use the DRE method.” Id. The DRE method has 
five diagnosis-related categories for each of the three spinal regions. Id. at § 15.3. 
Assigning the correct category is based on either “symptoms, signs, and appropriate 
diagnostic test results” or “the presence of fractures and/or dislocations with or without 
clinical symptoms.” Id. 
The Guides direct the ROM method is used in several situations: 
1. When an impairment is not caused by an injury, if the cause of the 
condition is uncertain and the DRE method does not apply, or an 
individual cannot be easily categorized in a DRE class. [. . .] 
2. When there is multilevel involvement in the same spinal region (e.g., 
fractures at multiple levels, disk herniations, or stenosis with 
radiculopathy at multiple levels or bilaterally). 
3. Where there is alteration of motion segment integrity (e.g., fusions) at 
multiple levels in the same spinal region, unless there is involvement of 
the corticospinal tract (then use the DRE method for corticospinal tract 
involvement). 
4. Where there is recurrent radiculopathy caused by a new (recurrent) 
disk herniation or a recurrent injury in the same spinal region. 
5. Where there are multiple episodes of other pathology producing 
alteration of motion segment integrity and/or radiculopathy. 
Id. at § 15.2.

The Guides provide “Specific Procedures and Directions” for performing an 
impairment rating of the spine. Id. at § 15.2a. The fourth step directs the evaluator: 
Determine whether the individual has multilevel involvement or multiple 
recurrences/occasions within the same region of the spine. Use the ROM 
method if: 
a. there are fractures at more than one level in a spinal region, 
b. there is radiculopathy bilaterally or at multiple levels in the same

d. there is recurrent disk herniation or stenosis with radiculopathy at the

C ON C LU SION S OF LAW. 
Workers’ compensation is “a creature of statute.” Darrow v. Quaker Oats Co., 
570 N.W.2d 649, 652 (Iowa 1997). Consequently, an injured employee’s “right to 
workers' compensation is purely statutory.” Downs v. A & H Const., Ltd.,  481 N.W.2d 
520, 527 (Iowa 1992).  And “it is the legislature’s prerogative to fix the conditions under 
which the act’s benefits may be obtained.” Darrow, 570 N.W.2d at 652. Thus, 
consideration of a disputed issue in a case before the agency begins with the language 
of the relevant statute to see if there exists ambiguity as to its meaning when applied to 
the facts of the case. Chavez v. MS Tech. LLC, 972 N.W.2d 662, 667 (Iowa 2022); see 
also Second Injury Fund of Iowa v. Strable, No. 24-0056, --- N.W.3d ---, 2024 WL 
5100098, at *5 (Iowa, Dec. 13, 2024) (“We started (and ended), as we do here, with the 
language of the relevant statute . . .”).

B e n e f i t s .  
The Iowa Supreme Court has held: 
The legislature enacted the workers' compensation statute primarily for the 
benefit of the worker and the worker's dependents. Therefore, we apply 
the statute broadly and liberally in keeping with the humanitarian objective 
of the statute. We will not defeat the statute's beneficent purpose by 
reading something into it that is not there, or by a narrow and strained 
construction. 
Gregory v. Second Inj. Fund of Iowa, 777 N.W.2d 395, 399 (Iowa 2010) (quoting 
Holstein Elec. v. Breyfogle, 756 N.W.2d 812, 815–16 (Iowa 2008) (citations omitted)).  
“Although the workers' compensation statute is to be liberally construed in favor 
of the worker, the statute is not to be expanded by reading something into it that is not 
there.” Downs v. A & H Const., Ltd., 481 N.W.2d 520, 527 (Iowa 1992) (citing Cedar 
Rapids Cmty. Sch. Dist. v. Cady, 278 N.W.2d 298 (Iowa 1979)). “To determine 
legislative intent, we look to the language chosen by the legislature and not what the 
legislature might have said.” Ramirez-Trujillo v. Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 N.W.2d 759, 
(Iowa 2016) (citing Schadendorf v. Snap-On Tools Corp., 757 N.W.2d 330, 337 (Iowa 
2008)). The “broad purpose of workers’ compensation” is “to award compensation 
(apart from medical benefits), not for the injury itself, but the disability produced by a 
physical injury.” Bell Bros. Heating & Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d 193, 200 
(Iowa 2010) (citing 4 Arthur Larson & Lex K. Larson, Larson’s Workers’ Compensation 
Law § 80.02, at 80–2 (2009)).  
Under Iowa Code section 85.3(1) (2019), an employer covered by the Iowa 
Workers’ Compensation Act must “provide, secure, and pay compensation according to 
the provisions of this chapter for any and all personal injuries sustained by an 
employee arising out of and in the course of the employment, and in such cases, the 
employer shall be relieved from other liability for recovery of damages or other 
compensation for such personal injury.” Entitled “Permanent disabilities,” section 85.34

provides, “Compensation for permanent disabilities and during a healing period for 
permanent partial disabilities shall be payable as provided in this section.” And section 
85.34(2) states: 
The compensation shall be based upon the extent of the disability and 
upon the basis of eighty percent per week of the employee’s average 
spendable weekly earnings, but not more than a weekly benefit amount, 
rounded to the nearest dollar, equal to one hundred eighty-four percent of 
the statewide average weekly wage paid employees as determined by the

and in effect at the time of the injury. 
Before 2017, permanent partial disability to an unscheduled body part caused by 
a work injury was “compensated by the industrial disability method which takes into 
account the loss of earning capacity.” Gilleland v. Armstrong Rubber Co., 524 N.W.2de 
404, 407 (Iowa 1994) (citing Mortimer, 502 N.W.2d at 14–15). An industrial disability 
analysis was used regardless of whether the injured employee returned to work with the 
defendant-employer or the level of earnings at the time of hearing relative to the date of 
injury. Mannes v. Fleetguard, Inc., 770 N.W.2d 826, 830 (Iowa 2009) (quoting Oscar 
Mayer Foods Corp. v. Tasler, 483 N.W.2d 824, 831 (Iowa 1992)); see also Quaker Oats 
Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 158 (Iowa 1996); Arrow-Acme Corp. v. Bellamy, 500 
N.W.2d 92, 95 (Iowa App. 1993). With the 2017 amendments, the legislature carved out 
an exception to this general rule and created a mandatory bifurcated litigation process 
on the issue of permanent disability under certain circumstances. See 2017 Iowa Acts 
ch. 23, § 8 (now codified at Iowa Code § 85.34(2)(v)). The statute now articulates an 
exception and the circumstances triggering the bifurcated litigation process as follows: 
If an employee who is eligible for compensation under this paragraph 
returns to work or is offered work for which the employee receives or 
would receive the same or greater salary, wages, or earnings than the 
employee received at the time of the injury, the employee shall be 
compensated based only upon the employee’s functional impairment 
resulting from the injury, and not in relation to the employee’s earning 
capacity. Notwithstanding section 85.26, subsection 2, if an employee who 
is eligible for compensation under this paragraph returns to work with the 
same employer and is compensated based only upon the employee's 
functional impairment resulting from the injury as provided in this 
paragraph and is terminated from employment by that employer, the 
award or agreement for settlement for benefits under this chapter shall be 
reviewed upon commencement of reopening proceedings by the 
employee for a determination of any reduction in the employee's earning 
capacity caused by the employee's permanent partial disability. 
Iowa Code § 85.34(2)(v).  
Thus, the 2017 amendments changed the statute so that its text expressly 
incorporates the agency’s review-reopening process to create a mandatory bifurcated

review-reopening is a process by which a determination of compensation is revisited 
due to a change in the claimant’s condition. Kohlhaas v. Hog Slat, Inc., 777 N.W.2d 
387, 391–95 (Iowa 2009). The bifurcated litigation process created in section 
85.34(2)(v) allows a claimant to seek a new agency determination of permanent

The Guides further explain its approach to impairment in pertinent part: 
The medical judgment used to determine the original impairment 
percentages could not account for the diversity or complexity of work but 
could account for daily activities common to most people. Work is not 
included in the clinical judgment for impairment percentages for several 
reasons: (1) work involves many simple and complex activities; (2) work is 
highly individualized, making generalizations inaccurate; (3) impairment 
percentages are unchanged for stable conditions, but work and 
occupations change; and (4) impairments interact with such other factors 
as the worker’s age, education, and prior work experience to determine 
the extent of work disability. For example, an individual who receives a 
30% whole person impairment due to pericardial heart disease is

Before the 2017 amendments to the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Act, 
entitlement to permanency benefits began with the occurrence of one of the “alternative 
markers of the end of the healing period.” Waldinger Corp. v. Mettler, 817 N.W.2d 1, 9 
(Iowa 2012); see also Evenson v. Winnebago Indus., 881 N.W.2d 360, 372 (Iowa 2016). 
The legislature amended the statute in 2017 so that permanency benefits must 
commence when “the employee has returned to work or it is medically indicated that 
significant improvement from the injury is not anticipated or until the employee is 
medically capable of returning to employment substantially similar to the employment in 
which the employee was engaged at the time of injury, whichever occurs first.” Iowa 
Code § 85.34(1). 

SAUL V. BMC AGGREGATES, L.C. 
Page 40 
 
The Iowa Supreme Court has explained maximum medical improvement (MMI) in 
workers’ compensation thusly: 
“MMI” is a term of art commonly used by the commissioner, attorneys 
practicing in the field of workers' compensation law, and medical providers 
expressing opinions affecting claimants' entitlement to healing period 
benefits and permanent partial disability benefits under Iowa Code section 
85.34. The term is used as an alternative means of expressing the point at 
which “it is medically indicated that significant improvement from the injury 
is not anticipated.” Iowa Code § 85.34(1). A treatise on Iowa workers' 
compensation law uses “maximum recuperation” as an alternative moniker 
for the MMI concept. See 15 James R. Lawyer, Iowa Practice Series: 
Workers' Compensation, § 13:3, at 135 (2011). 
Waldinger, 817 N.W. 2d at 6, n. 3. 
Before the 2017 amendments, the Iowa Supreme Court held that an injured 
employee may have more than one healing period relating to a work injury. Id., at 7–10. 
Subsequent healing periods may occur after the employee reaches MMI. Id. at 8. For 
example, if the claimant achieves MMI and is later rendered temporarily disabled and 
cannot work after surgery necessitated by the work injury, a new healing period begins. 
Id. at 8. Thus, the statute “leaves room for the possibility that continuing medical 
treatment provided by the employer under section 85.27 can result in a series of 
intermittent invasive treatments, periods of temporary disability from work and 
convalescence, serial MMI dates, and revised permanent disability ratings following a 
single work-related injury.” Id. at 9.  
The question is whether the change to section 85.34(1) made by the 2017 
amendments abrogates Iowa Supreme Court precedent on when permanency benefits 
are due and whether multiple MMI dates, periods of recuperation, and permanency 
ratings are possible under the law. Nothing in the statutory text after the 2017 
amendments took effect gives reason to doubt the vitality of this precedential caselaw. 
Permanency benefits are due when permanent impairment may be determined, which is 
when the injured worker has reached MMI. This lack of language expressly prohibiting 
payment of permanency benefits during a subsequent period of temporary disability or 
healing period after a subsequent surgery means that Evenson, at least with respect to 
concurrent payments of permanency and temporary disability benefits remains good 
law. 
The evidence shows Saul reached MMI on September 28, 2020, after the first 
surgery Dr. Abernathey performed. He then underwent additional care, including a 
surgery that necessitated another healing period, which ended when he reached MMI 
on July 21, 2021. Applying Evenson here, Saul’s entitlement to permanency benefits 
began when he reached MMI after the first surgery and Dr. Abernathey assigned an 
impairment rating, on September 28, 2020. His subsequent surgery and period of 
temporary convalescence does not implicate his entitlement to permanency benefits 
that came due after he reached MMI following the first surgery.

For all compensable injuries under Iowa Code chapter 85 or 85A, the employer 
must “furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, chiropractic, podiatric,

and shall allow reasonably necessary transportation expenses incurred for such

The defendants’ denial of liability means they lost the right to choose the care

his expense, and seek reimbursement for such care through this contested case 
proceeding. See Trade Prof’ls, 661 N.W.2d at 121–25 (affirming on judicial review an 
agency decision ordering the payment of medical expenses for unauthorized care 
because the defendants denied liability for the alleged injury and therefore lost the right 
to control care).

The defendants lost the ability to assert an authorization defense when they denied 
liability for the condition. The medical expenses in Claimant’s Exhibit 6 are reasonable 
and necessary. The defendants must pay the medical expenses detailed in the exhibit.

delayed, or terminated without reasonable or probable cause or excuse. 
This provision “codifies, in the workers’ compensation insurance context, the common 
law rule that insurers with good faith disputes over the legal or factual validity of claims 
can challenge them, if their arguments for doing so present fairly debatable issues.” 
Covia v. Robinson, 507 N.W.2d 411, 412 (Iowa 1993) (citing Dirks v. Farm Bureau Mut. 
Ins. Co., 465 N.W.2d 857, 861 (Iowa 1991) and Dolan v. Aid Ins. Co., 431 N.W.2d 790, 
794 (Iowa 1988)). “The purpose or goal of the statute is both punishment and 
deterrence.” Robbennolt v. Snap-On Tools Corp., 555 N.W.2d 229, 237 (Iowa 1996). 
The legislature established in Iowa Code section 86.13(4)(b) a burden-shifting 
framework for determining whether penalty benefits must be awarded in a workers’ 
compensation case. See 2009 Iowa Acts ch. 179, § 110 (codified at Iowa Code 
§ 86.13(4)(b)); see also Pettengill v. Am. Blue Ribbon Holdings, LLC, 875 N.W.2d 740, 
746–47 (Iowa App. 2015) as amended (Feb. 16, 2016) (discussing the burden-shifting 
required by the two-factor statutory test). The employee bears the burden to establish a 
prima facie case for penalty benefits. See Iowa Code § 86.13(4)(b). To do so, the 
employee must demonstrate a denial, delay in payment, or termination of workers’ 
compensation benefits. Iowa Code § 86.13(4)(b)(1). If the employee fails to prove a 
denial, delay, or termination, there can be no award of penalty benefits and the analysis 
stops. See id. at § 86.13(4)(b); see also Pettengill, 875 N.W.2d at 747. However, if the 
employee makes the requisite showing, the burden of proof shifts to the employer. See 
id. at § 86.13(4)(b); see also Pettengill, 875 N.W.2d at 747. 
Here, Saul contends the defendants ceased paying permanency benefits when 
he underwent the second surgery by Dr. Abernathey on January 5, 2021. According to 
Saul, the defendants did not communicate with him that they were going to cease 
paying permanency benefits or why. Saul argues that this resulted in 20 weeks of 
permanency benefits to which he was entitled being delayed by a week or more from 
January 19, 2021, through June 7, 2021. 
Saul also alleges the defendants ceased paying permanency benefits after 
paying 34.143 weeks of benefits out of the 35 weeks to which he is entitled under Dr. 
Abernathey’s opinion on permanent impairment on September 28, 2020. The last 
payment was on June 1, 2021. The remaining 0.857 weeks had not been paid as of the 
hearing date. 
Moreover, Saul contends that the defendants resumed paying permanency 
benefits on August 13, 2021, paying an additional 12.857 weeks. This was after Dr. 
Abernathey’s second impairment rating. However, rather than pay the difference owed 
under Dr. Abernathey’s initial impairment rating in a lump sum and resuming weekly 
benefits for the additional percentage under his second rating, the defendants paid four

To avoid an award of penalty benefits, the employer must “prove a reasonable or 
probable cause or excuse for the denial, delay in payment, or termination of benefits.” 
Iowa Code§ 86.13(4)(b)(2). An excuse must meet all of the following criteria to be “a 
reasonable or probable cause or excuse” under the statute: 
(1) The excuse was preceded by a reasonable investigation and 
evaluation by the employer or insurance carrier into whether benefits were 
owed to the employee. 
(2) The results of the reasonable investigation and evaluation were the 
actual basis upon which the employer or insurance carrier 
contemporaneously relied to deny, delay payment of, or terminate 
benefits. 
(3) The employer or insurance carrier contemporaneously conveyed the 
basis for the denial, delay in payment, or termination of benefits to the 
employee at the time of the denial, delay, or termination of benefits. 
Id. § 86.13(4)(c).  
This paragraph creates a mandatory timeline for the employer to follow in 
showing it had a “reasonable or probable cause or excuse” for the 
termination of benefits. Iowa Code § 86.13(4)(c)(1)-(3). First, the 
employer's excuse for the termination must have been preceded by an 
investigation. Id. § 86.13(4)(c)(1). Second, the results of the investigation 
were “the actual basis ... contemporaneously ” relied on by the employer 
in terminating the benefits. Third, the employer 
“contemporaneously conveyed the basis for the ... termination of benefits 
to the employee at the time of the ... termination.” Id. § 86.13(4)(c)(3) 
Pettengill, 875 N.W.2d at 747 (emphasis in original). “An employer cannot unilaterally 
decide to terminate an employee's benefits without adhering to Iowa Code section 
86.13; to allow otherwise would contradict the language of that section.” Id. 
“A ‘reasonable basis’ for denial of the claim exists if the claim is ‘fairly 
debatable.’” Keystone Nursing Care Ctr., 705 N.W.2d at 307 (quoting Christensen v. 
Snap-On Tools Corp., 554 N.W.2d 254, 260 (Iowa 1996)). A claim may be fairly 
debatable because of a good faith legal or factual dispute. See Covia, 507 N.W.2d at

Page 44 
 
416 (finding a jurisdictional issue fairly debatable because there were “viable arguments 
in favor of either party”).  “[T]he reasonableness of the employer’s denial or termination 
of benefits does not turn on whether the employer was right. The issue is whether there 
was a reasonable basis for the employer’s position that no benefits were owing.” 
Keystone Nursing Care Ctr., 705 N.W.2d at 307–08.  
The defendants assert that ceasing payment of permanency benefits at the time 
of the second surgery, when Saul was entitled to 20 more weeks under Dr. 
Abernathey’s first impairment rating, was reasonable, but they cite no legal argument in 
support of this position. They seem to argue that the language in section 85.34(2) that 
permanency benefits “shall begin when it is medically indicated that maximum medical 
improvement from the injury has been reached” supports ending payment of 
permanency benefits. The problem with this argument is the statutory text mandates 
commencement of payment of permanency benefits when the injured employee 
reaches MMI and gives no express permission to stop paying benefits after 
commencement for any reason.  
The combination of requiring payment when an injured employee reaches MMI 
and providing no authorization to stop payment pursuant to the post-MMI impairment 
rating leaves the defendants without a basis in the statutory text to support their 
position. If the legislature had intended to undo Iowa Supreme Court precedent on 
continuing payment of permanency benefits during a subsequent healing period, it 
would have added text to the statute when amending it stating that an employer may 
stop payment of permanency benefits while subsequently paying temporary disability or 
healing period benefits. Indeed, the legislature added language to what is now section 
85.34(3) as part of the 2017 amendments that precludes an employee from receiving 
PPD benefits if the employee is receiving compensation for permanent total disability. 
See 2017 Iowa Acts ch. 23, § 10. This demonstrates the legislature knows how to craft 
such a statutory provision when it intends to do so. Tellingly, there is no similar 
provision prohibiting concurrent receipt of temporary disability or healing period benefits 
and permanency benefits. The question is not fairly debatable. 
Further, the defendants do not provide evidence of any contemporaneous written 
notification to Saul that they were ceasing payment of permanency benefits or why. The 
undersigned finds the defendants’ position with respect to permanency benefits owed 
pursuant to Dr. Abernathey’s first impairment rating is not fairly debatable and a penalty 
must be paid under the statute. Even accepting arguendo that they had a statutory right 
to stop payment when Saul missed work for the disc reherniation, they failed to give 
contemporaneous notice of what they were doing and why, in violation of section 86.13. 
The defendants contend that their decision to choose to pay permanency 
benefits based on Dr. Abernathey’s 9 percent impairment rating instead of Dr. Fields’s 
10 percent rating is reasonable because the extent of permanent impairment was fairly 
debatable. The undersigned agrees because, even though Dr. Abernathey’s 
methodology and reasoning is not clearly described, it was reasonable for the 
defendants to pay benefits pursuant to the opinion of the treating neurosurgeon. Saul is 
not entitled to a penalty for the 1 percent difference.

 The length of the delay; 
 The number of the delays;

Each week of permanency benefits constitutes an individual delay. The length

Iowa Code section 85.39 governs examinations of injured employees to 
determine the extent of permanent impairment or disability caused by a work injury. Mid 
American Const. LLC v. Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d 838, 847–48 (Iowa 2024). Under subsection 
1, the employee must attend an examination by a physician of the employer’s choice or 
be disqualified from benefits for the period of refusal. An opinion on causation or the 
extent of permanent impairment or disability by a doctor retained by the employer 
triggers the injured employee’s right to an IME under subsection 2. 
“In subsection 2, the legislature leveled the playing field between the employer 
and the employee.” Id. 2 N.W.3d at 848. “The statutory process balances the competing 
interests of the employer and employee and permits the employee to obtain an 
independent medical examination at the employer's expense.” Des Moines Area Reg'l 
Transit Auth. v. Young, 867 N.W.2d 839, 844 (Iowa 2015).  
Subsection 2 entitles the employee to receive an examination if the employee 
believes the evaluation made by the employer's physician was too low. Iowa Code § 
85.39(2); Young, 867 N.W.2d at 844. The employee's own examination “is known as an 
IME because the examination is independent of the examination done by the physician 
selected by the employer.” Young, 867 N.W.2d at 843–44. 
Subsection 2 further provides, “A determination of the reasonableness of a fee 
for an examination … shall be based on the typical fee charged by a medical provider to 

SAUL V. BMC AGGREGATES, L.C. 
Page 46 
 
perform an impairment rating in the local area where the examination is conducted.” 
Under the Iowa Supreme Court’s holding in Sandlin, “The reasonableness of the fees 
will be analyzed based on what physicians in that area typically charge to perform the 
impairment rating examination—which may include ‘the costs of reviewing medical 
records, conducting a physical examination, opining on causation, assessing permanent 
impairment, assigning restrictions, and addressing further treatment recommendations.’” 
2 N.W.3d at 851–52 (quoting Turner v. NCI Bldg. Sys., Inc., Iowa Workers’ Comp. 
Comm'r No. 1652235.01, 2022 WL 1787301, at *26 (Feb. 24, 2022)). This analysis 
under the statute “effectively necessitates fact-finding by the commissioner on the fees 
typically charged in the local area where the examination is performed. Parties may 
offer evidence of the fees typically charged in that locality to support or challenge the 
fee charged in a particular case.” Id. at 849. The court concluded “‘many IMEs come 
before the agency, which allows the [commissioner] to use agency experience and 
expertise to conclude that [the physician's] charges are in line with those of an 
occupational medicine doctor with a practice in the ... [local] area performing an 
evaluation of permanent impairment in accordance with the [AMA] Guides.’” Id. (quoting 
Nguyen v. Hy-Vee, Iowa Workers’ Comp. Comm'r, No. 21003118.03, 2023 WL 
7128682, at *32 (Arb. Oct. 20, 2023)).

limitations; 
 Comparison of medical findings with the impairment criteria; and 
 Explanation of how the impairment rating was calculated.

Full and complete reporting provides the best opportunity for physicians to

the onset and course of the condition, symptoms, findings on previous

any temporal associations with the medical condition and work, frequency, intensity and

The Guides provide a “Sample Report for Permanent Medical Impairment” for 
physicians to follow to help ensure they adhere to the process in the Guides. Id., pp. 
23–24. The sample includes a section entitled “Identifiers” with information about the 
injured employee and examination. Id., p. 23. The next section is entitled, “Introduction,” 
and consists of, “Purpose (impairment or IME evaluation, personal injury, workers’ 
compensation) and procedures (who performed the exam, patient consent, location of 
examination.” Id. Following that is the “Narrative History,” which includes, “Chief 
complaints, history of injury or illness, occupational history, past medical history, family 
history, social history, [and] review of symptoms.” Id. After that is the section entitled, 
“Medical record review,” which is described as, “Chronology of medical evaluation, 
diagnostic studies, and treatment for the injury or illness.” Id.  
Next is a section detailing the physician’s physical examination. Id. After that, a 
section discussing diagnostic studies. Id., p. 24. Then the section, “Diagnoses and 
Impairments,” with the note, “If requested, discuss work relatedness, causation, 
apportionment, restrictions, accommodations, assistive devices.” Id.  
The report then has a section entitled, “Impairment Rating Criteria,” which 
includes, “MMI residual function, limitations of activities of daily living, [and] prognosis.” 
Id. This is followed by the “Impairment Rating and Rationale,” which includes the body 
part or system being rated, the chapter number of the Guides used, the table number of 
the Guides used, the percentage impairment of the whole person, and “[d]iscussion of 
rationale of impairment rating and any possible inconsistencies in the examination.” Id. 
The report template concludes with a section entitled, “Recommendations,” which 
is for, “Further diagnostic or therapeutic follow-up care.” Id. Then there is a section 
labeled, “Work ability, work restrictions.” Id. This final section includes the explanatory 
note, “If requested, review abilities and limitations in reference to essential job activities.” 
Id.

the rationale for directing a physician to write a report contained in the Guides is 
accurate. See id., § 2.3, p. 18. “A clear, accurate, and complete report is essential to 
support a rating of permanent impairment.” Id., § 2.6, p. 21.

The Guides reflect what agency experience and expertise reinforces:  The IME 
report is important in the determination of an employee’s entitlement to workers’ 
compensation benefits, especially in contested cases before the agency. The AMA has 
established criteria in the Guides so that doctors are providing the information injured 
workers, employers, insurance carriers, and tribunals such as the agency need to 
equitably determine an individual’s entitlement to workers’ compensation benefits based 
on the injury they sustained and the disability the injury has caused. Presumably, this is

adherence to the Guides when performing the ROM method, the undersigned believes 
that the costs are reasonable. Saul is entitled to the entirety of the $4,312.50 without an 
arbitrary reduction that ignores the IME’s substantive compliance with the Guides, as 
required by the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Act.
