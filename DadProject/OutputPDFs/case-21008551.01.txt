STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
The claimant, Jose Valdez, filed two petitions for arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from employer Smithfield Foods, Inc., (“Smithfield”) and their 
insurer Safety National Casualty Corporation.  James Byrne appeared on behalf of the 
claimant.  Michael Miller appeared on behalf of the defendants.  Heather Adams, a 
representative of the defendants, was also present. 
 
The matter came on for hearing on April 24, 2024, before Deputy Workers’ 
Compensation Commissioner Andrew M. Phillips.  Pursuant to an order of the Iowa 
Workers’ Compensation Commissioner, the hearing occurred electronically via Zoom.  
The matter proceeded without significant difficulty.    
 
The record in this case consists of Joint Exhibits 1-10, Claimant’s Exhibits 1-10, 
and Defendants’ Exhibits A-M.  The exhibits were received into the record without 
objection. 
The claimant testified on his own behalf with the assistance of interpreter Andrew 
Freund.  Heather Adams testified on behalf of the defendants.  Karrie Lucas was 
appointed the official reporter and custodian of the notes of the proceeding.  The 
evidentiary record closed at the conclusion of the hearing, and the matter was 
considered fully submitted following briefing by the parties on June 4, 2024.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned, having considered all of the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
Jose Angel Valdez-Jimenez, the claimant, was 41 years old at the time of the 
hearing.  (Testimony).  He is currently married, but in the midst of divorce proceedings 
in Mexico.  (Testimony; Claimant’s Exhibit 3:62).  He has three children.  (Testimony).  
One minor child resides with him, and his other two minor children live in Mexico with 
his former wife.  (Testimony).  He sends money back to Mexico to support his minor 
children.  (Testimony).

The claimant is originally from Mexico, and speaks Spanish.  (Testimony).  He 
could not converse in English, and only knew minimal phrases.  (Testimony).  As such, 
he testified through the assistance of an interpreter.  (Testimony).   
While growing up in Mexico, Mr. Valdez attended about nine years of schooling.  
(Testimony).  His parents could not afford to send him to additional years of school.  
(Testimony).   
In 2002, Mr. Valdez immigrated to the United States.  (Testimony).  His first job in 
the United States was working on a farm packing peaches, nectarines, prunes, and 
grapes.  (Testimony).  He needed to lift boxes of fruit weighing more than 60 pounds in 
this job.  (Testimony).  He worked this job for about 10 years.  (Testimony).   
Mr. Valdez then moved to Iowa in 2012 in order to help his parents with some 
ongoing paperwork and health issues.  (Testimony).  He began a job with Tyson Foods, 
in Denison, Iowa.  (Testimony).  He slaughtered cows and took their hides off using an 
electric knife.  (Testimony).  This required him to use both of his hands.  (Testimony).   
The claimant then returned to San Bernardino, California.  (Testimony).  He 
again worked in agriculture, primarily harvesting tomatoes and strawberries.  
(Testimony).  This required him to lift boxes containing the various fruits and push them.  
(Testimony).   
He then returned to Iowa, where he worked in the same cattle slaughterhouse 
position with Tyson in Denison, Iowa.  (Testimony).  He left Tyson to work as a bacon 
packer in Harlan, Iowa.  (Testimony).  This required him to pack bacon in packages and 
then place the packages into a larger box.  (Testimony).  He had to lift more than 25 
pounds, and use his hands and shoulders.  (Testimony).  Next, Mr. Valdez worked for 
Farmland, in Carroll, Iowa.  (Testimony).  He started packing hams, and then moved on 
to operating a forklift and being a machine operator.  (Testimony).  He used his hands 
and shoulders in lifting hams weighing in excess of 50 pounds.  (Testimony).   
Near the end of 2020, Mr. Valdez took a position with Smithfield.  (Testimony).  
Prior to beginning employment, he passed a physical.  (Testimony).  When he started 
with Smithfield, Mr. Valdez packaged bacon into boxes and then placed full boxes onto 
a pallet.  (Testimony).  The boxes of bacon weighed between 25 and 50 pounds.  
(Testimony).  By 2021, he earned about nineteen and 00/100 dollars ($19.00) per hour.  
(Testimony).   
On June 29, 2021, Mr. Valdez was working on the bacon line when a machine 
broke down.  (Testimony).  This resulted in an issue with damaged plastic on the line.  
(Testimony).  Mr. Valdez reached in and pulled a roll of plastic when a cutter came out 
of the line and cut him on his left thumb and palm.  (Testimony).  The result of the cut 
was a partial amputation of his left thumb.  (Testimony).  Accordingly, Mr. Valdez looked 
down at his bloody hand and noticed he was missing a portion of his thumb.  
(Testimony).  He immediately felt pain and began to panic.  (Testimony).  He was 
stabilized in the nurse’s office at Smithfield with active bleeding and an amputated left

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa Rule of Appellate 
Procedure 6.904(3).   
Causation 
The parties acknowledge, and agree, that the claimant sustained injuries to his 
left hand on June 29, 2021, when a machine lacerated his hand and partially amputated 
his left thumb.  They further agree, and acknowledge, that Mr. Valdez sustained 

VALDEZ V. SMITHFIELD FOODS, INC. 
Page 35 
 
permanent disability to his left hand due to this incident.  Additionally, the parties 
stipulated that the claimant experienced a mental health sequela as a result of his June 
29, 2021, injury.  However, there are disputes as to whether or not the mental health 
issues were a cause of permanent disability in the claimant.   
Mr. Valdez alleges that he sustained injuries to his right shoulder and his lower 
back as a sequela of his left hand injury.  Specifically, Mr. Valdez alleges that his 
overuse of his right hand and the need to bend while working shaving hogs caused his 
right shoulder and lower back issues.  The defendants dispute these allegations.  The 
defendants assert that the claimant’s right shoulder and low back injuries were not 
caused by the claimant’s work at Smithfield; however, if it is determined that the 
claimant sustained injuries to his right shoulder and lower back, the defendants assert 
that they were caused by a cumulative injury with a date of injury in July of 2022.   
To receive workers’ compensation benefits, an injured employee must prove, by 
a preponderance of the evidence, that the employee’s injuries arose out of, and in the 
course of the employee’s employment with the employer.  2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 
528 N.W.2d 124, 128 (Iowa 1995).  The words “arising out of” refer to the cause or 
source of the injury.  The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place and 
circumstances of the injury.  Id.  An injury arises out of employment when a causal 
relationship exists between the employment and the injury.  Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 
552 N.W.2d 143, 151 (Iowa 1996).  The injury must be a rational consequence of a 
hazard connected with the employment and not merely incidental to the employment.  
Koehler Elec. v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1, 3 (Iowa 2000).  The Iowa Supreme Court has held 
that an injury occurs “in the course of employment” when:  
it is within the period of employment at a place where the employee 
reasonably may be in performing his duties, and while he is fulfilling those 
duties or engaged in doing something incidental thereto.  An injury in the 
course of employment embraces all injuries received while employed in 
furthering the employer’s business and injuries received on the employer’s 
premises, provided that the employee’s presence must ordinarily be 
required at the place of the injury, or, if not so required, employee’s 
departure from the usual place of employment must not amount to an 
abandonment of employment or be an act wholly foreign to his usual work.  
An employee does not cease to be in the course of his employment merely 
because he is not actually engaged in doing some specifically prescribed 
task, if, in the course of his employment, he does some act which he deems 
necessary for the benefit or interest of his employer.    
Farmers Elevator Co., Kingsley v. Manning, 286 N.W.2d 174, 177 (Iowa 1979).    
 
A personal injury contemplated by the workers’ compensation law means an 
injury, the impairment of health or a disease resulting from an injury which comes about, 
not through the natural building up and tearing down of the human body, but because of 
trauma.  The injury must be something that acts extraneously to the natural processes 
of nature and thereby impairs the health, interrupts or otherwise destroys or damages a 

VALDEZ V. SMITHFIELD FOODS, INC. 
Page 36 
 
part of all of the body.  Although many injuries have a traumatic onset, there is no 
requirement for a special incident or unusual occurrence.  Injuries which result from 
cumulative trauma are compensable.  However, increased disability from a prior injury, 
even if brought about by further work, does not constitute a new injury.  St. Luke’s Hosp. 
v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); Ellingson v. Fleetguard, Inc., 599 N.W.2d 440 
(Iowa 1999); McKeever Custom Cabinets v. Smith, 379 N.W.2d 368 (Iowa 1985).  An 
occupational disease covered by Iowa Code 85A is specifically excluded from the 
definition of personal injury.  Iowa Code 85.61(4)(b); Iowa Code section 85A.8; Iowa 
Code section 85A.14. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is 
probable, rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 
148 (Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); 
Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996).    
The question of medical causation is “essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony.”  Cedar Rapids Cmty. Sch. Dist. v. Pease, 807 N.W.2d 839, 844-45 (Iowa 
2011).  The commissioner, as the trier of fact, must “weigh the evidence and measure 
the credibility of witnesses.”  Id.  The trier of fact may accept or reject expert testimony, 
even if uncontroverted, in whole or in part.  Frye, 569 N.W.2d at 156.  When considering 
the weight of an expert opinion, the fact-finder may consider whether the examination 
occurred shortly after the claimant was injured, the compensation arrangement, the 
nature and extent of the examination, the expert’s education, experience, training, and 
practice, and “all other factors which bear upon the weight and value” of the opinion.  
Rockwell Graphic Sys., Inc. v. Prince, 366 N.W.2d 187, 192 (Iowa 1985).  Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected.  Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 1994).  Supportive lay testimony may be used 
to buttress expert testimony, and therefore is also relevant and material to the causation 
question.    
Iowa employers take an employee subject to any active or dormant health 
problems, and must exercise care to avoid injury to both the weak and infirm and the 
strong and healthy.  Hanson v. Dickinson, 188 Iowa 728, 176 N.W. 823 (1920).  While a 
claimant must show that the injury proximately caused the medical condition sought to 
be compensable, it is well established that a cause is “proximate” when it is a 
substantial factor, or even the primary or most substantial cause to be compensable 
under the Iowa workers’ compensation system.  Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 
N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994); Blacksmith v. All-American, Inc., 290 N.W.2d 348 (Iowa 
1980).    
It is well established in workers’ compensation that “if a claimant had a 
preexisting condition or disability, aggravated, accelerated, worsened, or ‘lighted up’ by 
an injury which arose out of and in the course of employment resulting in a disability 

VALDEZ V. SMITHFIELD FOODS, INC. 
Page 37 
 
found to exist,” the claimant is entitled to compensation.  Iowa Dep’t. of Transp. v. Van 
Cannon, 459 N.W.2d 900, 904 (Iowa 1990).  The Iowa Supreme Court has held, 
[A] disease which under any rational work is likely to progress so as to finally 
disable an employee does not become a “personal injury” under our 
Workmen’s Compensation Act merely because it reaches a point of 
disablement while work an employer is being pursued.  It is only when there 
is direct causal connection between exertion of the employment and the 
injury that a compensation award can be made.  The question is whether 
the diseased condition was the cause or whether the employment was a 
proximate contributing cause.   
Musselman v. Ce. Tel. Co., 261 Iowa 352, 359-60, 154 N.W.2d 128, 132 (1967).   
 
It is also well settled in Iowa that an employer is liable for all consequences that 
naturally and proximately flow from an accident to an employee in the usual course of 
their employment.  Oldham v. Scofield & Welch, 222 Iowa 764, 767-68, 255 N.W. 480, 
482 (1936).  Further disability is compensable when the further disability is the 
proximate result of the original injury.  Id.   
 
Before examining whether the claimant’s mental health sequela was a cause of 
permanent disability, it is important to examine whether or not the claimant proved, by a 
preponderance of the evidence, that he sustained injuries to his right shoulder and 
lower back that arose out of, and in the course of, his employment with Smithfield.

that influence mental and behavioral impairment.  In addition, the authors 
are unaware of data that show the reliability of the impairment percentages.

Rehabilitation of the America Psychiatric Association advised Guides 
contributors against the use of percentages in the chapter on mental and 
behavioral disorders of the fourth edition, and that remains the opinion of 
the authors of the present chapter.

percentage of impairment of the whole person; however, the following 
approach may be useful in estimating the extent of mental impairments.  Not 
everyone who has a mental or behavioral disorder is limited in the ability to 
perform activities of daily living; however, there are individuals with less than 
chronic, but still unremitting, impairments who are severely limited in some 
areas of functioning.

consider five factors.  Id. at 364-365.  These factors are: (1) the effects of treatment; (2)

function of the individual:  
1. Understanding and memory relate to the individual’s ability to remember 
procedures related to work; to understand and remember short, simple 
instructions; and to understand and remember detailed instructions.

The Guides look at several different areas in arriving at an impairment rating.  Id. 
at 361-362.  The first of these is activities of daily living, which includes self-care, 
personal hygiene, communication, ambulation, travel, sexual function, and sleep.  Id.

individual is capable of initiating and participating in these activities independent of 
supervision or direction.  Id.  The examiner is to consider the overall degree of 
restriction along with the number of restricted activities.  Id.  The second is social 
functioning.  Id. at 362.  This includes the ability to get along with others, such as family 
members, friends, neighbors, grocery clerks, landlords, or bus drivers.  Id.  An impaired

to permit the timely completion of tasks commonly found in activities of daily living or

The fourth item is deterioration or decompensation in complex or worklike settings.  Id.

There is a dispute as to how to compensate the claimant.  Generally, permanent 
partial disability falls into two categories; a scheduled member, as defined by Iowa Code 
section 85.34(a) – (u), or a loss of earning capacity, also known as industrial disability, 
as defined by Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v).  Lauhoff Grain Co. v. McIntosh, 395 
N.W.2d 834 (Iowa 1986); Blacksmith v. All-American, Inc., 290 N.W.2d 348 (Iowa 
1980); Dailey v. Pooley Lumber Co., 233 Iowa 758, 10 N.W.2d 569 (1943); Soukup v. 
Shores Co., 222 Iowa 272, 268 N.W. 598 (1936); Diederich v. Tri-City Ry. Co. of Iowa, 
219 Iowa 587, 258 N.W. 899 (1935).   
Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v) provides an alternative to the scheduled member 
and/or industrial disability compensation methods.  Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v) 
states, in relevant part:  
If an employee who is eligible for compensation under this paragraph 
returns to work or is offered work for which the employee receives or would 
receive the same or greater salary, wages, or earnings than the employee 
received at the time of the injury, the employee shall be compensated based 
only upon the employee’s functional impairment resulting from the injury, 
and not in relation to the employee’s earning capacity.

Page 49 
 
In determining whether the above provision of Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v) 
applies, there is a comparison between the claimant’s pre- and post-injury salary, 
wages, or earnings.  McCoy v. Menard, Inc., File No. 1651840.01 (App. April 9, 2021).  
A claimant’s hourly wage must be considered in tandem with the actual hours worked 
by that claimant or offered by the employer.  Id.   
In this case, both parties acknowledge that the claimant is working for an 
increased hourly wage.  There is no evidence presented that the claimant is working for 
less hours.  In fact, the claimant seems to be working the same, or more, hours, than at 
the time of his injury.  Therefore, the provisions of Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v) apply, 
and the claimant shall be compensated based upon a functional impairment.  The 
functional impairment to the claimant’s left hand is 28 percent.  This converted to a 25 
percent impairment of the left upper extremity.  See Table 16-2, Guides, page 439.  This 
converts further to a 15 percent impairment of the whole person.  See Table 16-3, 
Guides, page 439.  The functional impairment for the claimant’s body as a whole due to 
his mental sequela stemming from the same date of injury is 25 percent.  Since the 
claimant has an impairment to the body as a whole from his mental health sequela, the 
functional impairments are combined in order to arrive at the final impairment rating 
using the Combined Values Chart on page 604 of the Guides.  In combining the 
impairment ratings, I arrive at a 36 percent whole person impairment.  This totals 180 
weeks (0.36 x 500 weeks = 180 weeks).   
Compensation for permanent partial disability shall begin when it is medically 
indicated that MMI from the injury has been reached, and that the extent of loss or 
percentage of permanent impairment can be determined by use of the Guides.  See 
Iowa Code section 85.34(2).   
Dr. Lesiak placed the claimant at MMI for his left hand as of January 24, 2024, 
with no opinion as to the claimant’s permanent impairment.  He provided him 
restrictions against working in cold environments.  Dr. Bansal concurred with Dr. 
Lesiak’s MMI date regarding the claimant’s left hand.  He provided a permanent 
impairment rating for the claimant’s left hand.  He then opined that the claimant reached 
MMI on March 4, 2024, for his mental health issues.  However, it was not until Dr. Patra 
issued an impairment opinion on March 8, 2024, that an impairment could be 
determined by the Guides.  Dr. Chen placed Mr. Valdez at MMI as of January 24, 2024.  
However, his opinion was not issued until March 15, 2024.  Therefore, the proper 
commencement date for benefits is March 8, 2024.

An employer is only liable to reimburse an employee for the cost of an

employee is being examined is determined to be compensable under this

Iowa Code section 85.39(2).     
The Iowa Supreme Court determined that an “examination” required “to perform 
an impairment rating” must include certain steps as prescribed by the AMA Guides.  Mid 
American Construction, LLC v. Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d 838 (Iowa 2024).  These steps 
include: determining whether an injured worker achieved MMI, reviewing past medical 
records to ascertain knowledge as to any past injuries to the injured worker, opining as 
to whether the work injury caused the impairment, conducting a physical examination of 
the claimant in order “to assess the employee’s status and perform accurate 
measurements…”, and determining a diagnosis.  Id.   
Defendants are responsible only for reasonable fees associated with claimant’s 
independent medical examination.  The claimant has the burden of proving the 
reasonableness of the expenses incurred for the examination.  See Schintgen v. 
Economy Fire & Casualty Co., File No. 855298 (App. April 26, 1991).  This burden may 
be met either by “…evidence of the fees typically charged in that locality to support or 
challenge the fee charged in a particular case…” or by agency expertise in adjudicating 
claims in the local area where the examination was conducted.  Mid American 
Construction, LLC v. Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d 838 (Iowa 2024).   
An opinion finding a lack of causation is tantamount to a zero percent impairment 
rating.  Kern v. Fenchel, Doster & Buck, P.L.C., 2021 WL 3890603 (Iowa App. 2021).     
Finally, it is well accepted Iowa law that a claimant is only entitled to 
reimbursement for one IME pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.39.  Larson Mfg. Co. Inc. 
v. Thorson, 763 N.W.2d 842, 861 (Iowa 2009).

Costs 
The claimant seeks an assessment of costs as listed in Claimant’s Exhibit 10.  
Costs are to be assessed at the discretion of the deputy commissioner hearing the 
case.  See 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.33; Iowa Code section 10A.328.  876 Iowa 
Administrative Code 4.33(6) provides:  
[c]osts taxed by the workers’ compensation commissioner or a deputy 
commissioner shall be (1) attendance of a certified shorthand reporter or 
presence of mechanical means at hearings and evidential depositions, (2) 
transcription costs when appropriate, (3) costs of service of the original 
notice and subpoenas, (4) witness fees and expenses as provided by Iowa 
Code sections 622.69 and 622.72, (5) the costs of doctors’ and 
practitioners’ deposition testimony, provided that said costs do not exceed 
the amounts provided by Iowa Code sections 622.69 and 622.72, (6) the 
reasonable costs of obtaining no more than two doctors’ or practitioners’ 
reports, (7) filing fees when appropriate, including convenience fees 
incurred by using the WCES payment gateway, and (8) costs of persons 
reviewing health service disputes.   
Pursuant to the holding in Des Moines Area Regional Transit Authority v. Young, 
867 N.W.2d 839 (Iowa 2015), only the report of an IME physician, and not the 
examination itself, can be taxed as a cost according to 876 IAC 4.33(6).  The Iowa 
Supreme Court reasoned, “a physician’s report becomes a cost incurred in a hearing 
because it is used as evidence in lieu of the doctor’s testimony,” while “[t]he underlying 
medical expenses associated with the examination do not become costs of a report 
needed for a hearing, just as they do not become costs of the testimony or deposition.”  
Id.  (noting additionally that “[i]n the context of the assessment of costs, the expenses of 
the underlying medical treatment and examination are not part of the costs of the report 
or deposition”).  The commissioner has found this rationale applicable to expenses 
incurred by vocational experts.  See  Kirkendall v. Cargill Meat Solutions Corp., File No. 
5055494 (App. December 17, 2018); Voshell v. Compass Group, USA, Inc., File No. 
5056857 (App. September 27, 2019).
