STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
 
Timothy Pruis, claimant, filed a petition in arbitration seeking penalty benefits 
from Medplast, employer and Continental Indemnity Company, insurance carrier, as 
defendants. Hearing was held via Zoom videoconference on November 29, 2023. 
     
 
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing. On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations. All of those 
stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration decision and 
no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised or discussed 
in this decision. The parties are now bound by their stipulations.  
 
 
Timothy Pruis was the only witness to testify live at trial. The evidentiary record 
also includes joint exhibits 1-14 and claimant’s exhibits 1-9. Defendants did not offer 
any separate exhibits. All exhibits were received without objection. The evidentiary 
record closed at the conclusion of the arbitration hearing.  
      
 
The parties submitted post-hearing briefs on January 22, 2024, at which time the 
case was fully submitted to the undersigned.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
 
The undersigned, having considered all the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
 
 
 Claimant, Pruis, sustained a work-related injury on August 11, 2016. An 
arbitration hearing was held on July 22, 2020. (Joint Exhibit 1, p. 1) An arbitration 
decision was issued on November 23, 2020. (JE 1) In the underlying arbitration 
decision, defendants disputed whether claimant sustained any permanent disability as 
the result of the work injury and offered medical opinions to support their position. (JE 1) 
Defendants were ordered to pay permanent total disability benefits from August 11, 
2016, through the date of the arbitration hearing and into the future during the period of 
claimant’s continued disability. Benefits were not owed during claimant’s periods of 
employment with subsequent employers. (JE 1, p. 25) 
 
 
On December 1, 2020, defendants filed a notice of appeal to the Iowa Workers’ 
Compensation Commissioner. Defendants appealed the issues of whether claimant 
sustained work-related injuries to his head, neck, vision, and a mental injury, which 
resulted in any permanent disability. Defendants also appealed the finding that claimant 
is permanently and totally disabled as the result of the work injury. Additionally, 
defendants appealed the order to reimburse claimant for requested medical expenses 
and reimbursement of the claimant’s independent medical examination. (JE 2, pp. 27-

On April 28, 2021, the Commissioner issued the appeal decision in this case. 
The appeal affirmed the arbitration decision in its entirety. (JE 2, pp. 28-29)

the defendants filed a motion to stay the agency judgment. On July 3, 2021, the District 
Court for Polk County entered an Order granting the motion to stay. (Claimant’s Exhibit 
5)  
 
 
On October 14, 2021, a Ruling on Petition for Judicial Review was entered which 
denied the petition for judicial review and affirmed the agency’s action in its entirety. (JE 
3, pp. 30-60) The District Court noted the petitioners alleged that the agency erred in 
finding that Pruis sustained injuries to his head, neck, vision, and mental health as a 
result of the August 11, 2016 work injury and in determining that the claimant was 
permanently and totally disabled as the result of the work injury. (JE 3, p. 34) The 
decision also stated that the stay that the District Court previously ordered was lifted. 
(JE 3, p. 59) During cross-examination, claimant testified that it was his belief that the 
District Court’s stay was not revoked at any time. (Tr., p. 24) However, the Ruling on 
Petition for Judicial Review issued on October 14, 2021 stated that the stay previously 
ordered was lifted. (JE 3, p. 59)

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence. Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 
 
 
The issue in this case is whether penalty benefits are appropriate because 
defendants did not pay any permanent partial disability benefits until the case was no 
longer on appeal. 
 
 
Iowa Code section 86.131 governs penalty benefits. It provides:

Iowa Code section 86.13(4). 
 
 
If weekly compensation benefits are not fully paid when due, section 86.13 
requires that additional benefits be awarded unless the employer shows reasonable 
cause or excuse for the delay or denial. Weekly compensation payments are due at the 
end of the compensation week. Robbennolt v. Snap-on Tools Corp., 555 N.W.2d 229, 
235 (Iowa 1996). 
 
 
It is not disputed that permanent total benefits as ordered by the commissioner 
were delayed. I conclude that claimant has demonstrated weekly benefits, in the 
amount of $110,712.55, that accrued from October 19, 2018 through December 15, 
2022 and paid on December 22, 2022 were late. I further conclude claimant has 
demonstrated that the weekly benefit check issued on January 3, 2023, in the amount of 
$746.62, was also late. As a result, the burden shifts to the defendants to show a 
reasonable or probable excuse for the delay in payment.

Next, a determination must be made on whether penalty benefits are appropriate 
for the late payments. The question is whether defendants had a reasonable cause or 
excuse for those delayed payments.    
 
 
In Christensen v. Snap-on Tools Corp., 554 N.W.2d 254 (Iowa 1996), and 
Robbennolt v. Snap-on Tools Corp., 555 N.W.2d 229 (Iowa 1996), the Supreme Court 
said: 
 
Based on the plain language of section 86.13, we hold an employee is 
entitled to penalty benefits if there has been a delay in payment unless the 
employer proves a reasonable cause or excuse.  A reasonable cause or 
excuse exists if either (1) the delay was necessary for the insurer to 
investigate the claim or (2) the employer had a reasonable basis to 
contest the employee’s entitlement to benefits.  A “reasonable basis” for 
denial of the claim exists if the claim is “fairly debatable.” 
 
Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 260. 
 
The Supreme Court has stated: 
 
 
(1) If the employer has a reason for the delay and conveys that reason 
to the employee contemporaneously with the beginning of the delay, no 
penalty will be imposed if the reason is of such character that a 
reasonable fact-finder could conclude that it is a "reasonable or probable 
cause or excuse" under Iowa Code section 86.13.  In that case, we will 
defer to the decision of the commissioner.  See Christensen, 554 N.W.2d 
at 260 (substantial evidence found to support commissioner’s finding of 
legitimate reason for delay pending receipt of medical report); Robbennolt, 
555 N.W.2d at 236. 
 
(2) If no reason is given for the delay or if the “reason” is not one that 
a reasonable fact-finder could accept, we will hold that no such cause or 
excuse exists and remand to the commissioner for the sole purpose of 
assessing penalties under section 86.13.  See Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 
261. 
 
(3) Reasonable causes or excuses include (a) a delay for the 
employer to investigate the claim, Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 260; 
Kiesecker v. Webster City Meats, Inc., 528 N.W.2d at 109, 111 (Iowa 
1995); or (b) the employer had a reasonable basis to contest the 
claimthe “fairly debatable” basis for delay.  See Christensen, 554 
N.W.2d at 260 (holding two-month delay to obtain employer’s own medical 
report reasonable under the circumstances).  
 
(4) For the purpose of applying section 86.13, the benefits that are 
underpaid as well as late-paid benefits are subject to penalties, unless the 
employer establishes reasonable and probable cause or excuse.  
Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 237 (underpayment resulting from application

of wrong wage base; in absence of excuse, commissioner required to 
apply penalty). 
If we were to construe [section 86.13] to permit the 
avoidance of penalty if any amount of compensation benefits 
are paid, the purpose of the penalty statute would be 
frustrated.  For these reasons, we conclude section 86.13 is 
applicable when payment of compensation is not timely . . . 
or when the full amount of compensation is not paid. Id. 
 
(5) For purposes of determining whether there has been a delay, 
payments are “made” when (a) the check addressed to a claimant is 
mailed (Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 236; Kiesecker, 528 N.W.2d at 112), 
or (b) the check is delivered personally to the claimant by the employer or 
its workers’ compensation insurer.  Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 235.   
 
(6) In determining the amount of penalty, the commissioner is to 
consider factors such as the length of the delay, the number of delays, the 
information available to the employer regarding the employee’s injury and 
wages, and the employer’s past record of penalties.  Robbennolt, 555 
N.W.2d at 238. 
 
(7) An employer’s bare assertion that a claim is “fairly debatable” does 
not make it so.  A fair reading of Christensen and Robbennolt, makes it 
clear that the employer must assert facts upon which the commissioner 
could reasonably find that the claim was “fairly debatable.”  See 
Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 260. 
 
Meyers v. Holiday Express Corp., 557 N.W.2d 502 (Iowa 1996).   
 
 
Penalty is not imposed for delayed interest payments. Davidson v. Bruce, 593 
N.W.2d 833, 840 (Iowa App. 1999). Schadendorf v. Snap-On Tools Corp., 757 N.W.2d 
330, 338 (Iowa 2008).   
 
 
When an employee’s claim for benefits is fairly debatable based on a good faith 
dispute over the employee’s factual or legal entitlement to benefits, an award of penalty 
benefits is not appropriate under the statute. Whether the issue was fairly debatable 
turns on whether there was a disputed factual dispute that, if resolved in favor of the 
employer, would have supported the employer's denial of compensability. Gilbert v. USF 
Holland, Inc., 637 N.W.2d 194 (Iowa 2001). 
 
 
In Wilian Holdings Constr. Products, Inc. v. Rice, No. 9-550/08-1964, filed 
September 17, 2009 (Iowa Ct. App.), 776 N.W. 2d 301 (Table), the Court of Appeals 
found penalty was not appropriate for the delay in paying benefits when the arbitration 
decision noted causation and industrial disability were “close issues”. The Court noted 
that “…Perhaps the most reliable method of establishing that the insurers legal position 
is reasonable is to show that some judge in the relevant jurisdiction has accepted it as 
correct…” (quoting William T. Barker & Paul E.B. Glad, Use of Summary Judgment in 
Defense of Bad Faith Actions Involving First-Party Insurance, 30 Torts & Ins. L.J. 49, 83 
(1994). See also Rodda v. Vermeer Mfg., 734 N.W. 2d, 480, 485 (Iowa 2007).

In order to avoid penalty in this case, defendants need to show the delay in 
paying benefits had a reasonable basis and that defendants followed the requirements 
under Iowa Code section 86.13(4)(c). 
 
 
The Iowa Supreme Court has explained this second statutory requirement as 
follows: 
 
A reasonable cause or excuse exists if either (1) the delay was necessary 
for the insurer to investigate the claim or (2) the employer had a reasonable 
basis to contest the employee's entitlement to benefits. A “reasonable 
basis” for denial of the claim exists if the claim is “fairly debatable.” 
 
Keystone Nursing Care Ctr. v. Craddock, 705 N.W.2d 299, 307 (Iowa 2005).  
 
 
The reasonableness of the employer's actions “does not turn on whether the 
employer was right. The issue is whether there was a reasonable basis for the 
employer's position that no benefits were owing.” Id. at 307–08. Stated another way, the 
“focus is on the existence of a debatable issue, not on which party was correct.” Bellville 
v. Farm Bureau Mut. Ins. Co., 702 N.W.2d 468, 473–74 (Iowa 2005). In Rodda v. 
Vermeer Mfg., 734 N.W.2d 480, 483 (Iowa 2007), the court explained: 
 
A reasonable basis for denying insurance benefits exists if the claim is “fairly 
debatable” as to either a matter of fact or law. A claim is “fairly debatable” 
when it is open to dispute on any logical basis. Whether a claim is “fairly 
debatable” can generally be determined by the court as a matter of law. If 
the court determines that the defendant had no reasonable basis upon 
which to deny the employee's benefits, it must then determine if the 
defendant knew, or should have known, that the basis for denying the 
employee's claim was unreasonable. 
 
Id. (Internal citations and quotations omitted.) In City of Madrid v. Blasnitz, 742 N.W.2d 
77, 84 (Iowa 2007), the Iowa Supreme Court stated that when there are facts that 
create a genuine dispute between the parties, the claimant’s case is fairly debatable as 
a matter of law.   
 
 
Claimant asserts the defendants’ delay in payment of the weekly benefits 
accrued during the pendency of the appeal was not reasonable. Additionally, claimant 
asserts defendants failed to re-evaluate the case promptly after the arbitration decision, 
the commissioner’s appeal decision, and the District Court’s ruling on defendants’ 
petition for judicial review. Claimant also alleges defendants failed to 
contemporaneously convey the basis for the continued delay or denial of benefits to the 
claimant.   
 
 
Defendants contend that benefits were not payable to claimant during the 
pendency of the appeals.

First, we address the time the arbitration decision was on appeal to the 
Commissioner. The commissioner has addressed the issue of intra-agency appeals.  
He stated:   
 
[T]he deputy’s decision was only a proposed decision of a presiding 
officer under Iowa Code section 17A.15 as prescribed by Iowa Code 
section 86.17. Such a proposed decision only becomes a final agency 
decision when no appeal is taken to the commissioner within the statutory 
20 day appeal period. After such appeal is filed and briefed, the 
commissioner is free to change or modify any finding of fact or conclusion 
of law in an appeal decision. Iowa Code section 17A.15(3), Iowa Code 
section 86.24(2). An enforceable judgment from the district court can only 
be obtained from a decision by a deputy commissioner if a timely appeal 
has not been taken and the decision became final by the passage of time. 
Iowa Code section 86.42. By agency rule, all issues presented on intra-
agency appeal are reviewed by the commissioner de novo. 876 IAC 
4.28(7). Consequently, there was no finality to the deputy’s arbitration 
decision of February 28, 2005. Therefore, if defendants’ denial of benefits 
was fairly debatable before the decision, it was almost certainly fairly 
debatable after the decision. 
   
Millenkamp v. Millenkamp Cattle, Inc., File No. 5011148 (Rehearing December 10, 
2007). In the present case, the defendants’ denial of benefits at the time of the 
arbitration hearing was fairly debatable. There is nothing in the record to indicate that 
anything occurred during the hearing to render the denial of benefits unreasonable or to 
impose a duty upon defendants to re-evaluate their past denial of benefits. In the 
present case, defendants’ denial of benefits was fairly debatable before the arbitration 
decision, it remained fairly debatable after the proposed agency decision issued by the 
deputy. Therefore, claimant is not entitled to penalty benefits during intra-agency 
appeal.   
 
 
We now turn to the time the Commissioner’s decision was on appeal to the 
District Court. Under Iowa law, the filing of a petition for judicial review does not itself 
stay execution or enforcement of any agency action. See Iowa Code section 17A.19(5).  
Pursuant to Iowa Code section 86.26, for injuries occurring before July 1, 2017, an 
award of benefits by the commissioner can be converted into a judgement even when a 
petition for judicial review has been filed as long as the District Court has not entered a 
stay. A petitioner may seek an order for stay, not with the agency, but rather with the 
appropriate District Court. See Iowa Code section 86.26 (2016). The Iowa 
Administrative Procedure Act sets forth the process for a petitioner to obtain a stay of 
execution or enforcement of the agency action during the pendency of judicial review. 
See Iowa Code section 17A.19(5).   
 
In the present case, when defendants filed their petition for judicial review, they 
also filed a motion to stay enforcement of agency action in the appropriate District 
Court. (JE 9, JE 10) Claimant filed a response to the motion to stay. (JE 11) A contested 

PRUIS V. MEDPLAST, INC. 
Page 9 
 
hearing was held on the motion to stay on June 23, 2021. (CE 5, p. 9) The District Court 
considered the four factors set forth in Iowa Code section 17A.19(5)2 for a stay. The 
statute allows for a stay, after the court has considered and balanced the following 
factors:   
 
1. The extent to which the applicant is likely to prevail when the court 
finally disposes of the matter; 
2. The extent to which the applicant will suffer irreparable injury if relief is 
not granted; 
3. The extent to which the grant of relief to the applicant will substantially 
harm other parties to the proceedings; and  
4. The extent to which the public interest relied upon by the agency is 
sufficient to justify the agency’s action in the circumstances. 
 
(CE 5, pp. 9-10) (citing Iowa Code section 17A.19(5)(c)(2021). 
 
 
After considering these four factors, the District Court stated, “the petitioners’ 
motion for stay is granted. Any enforcement of or execution upon the agency award 
which is the subject matter of this proceeding is stayed until final resolution or upon 
further order of this court.” (CE 5, p. 13) I conclude that the stay granted by the District 
Court constitutes a reasonable basis for the defendants to delay or deny payment of 
benefits. Any action by the undersigned that is inconsistent with the District Court’s 
order to stay would be an informal modification of the order which created the stay. 
WIlimek v. Danker, 671 N.W.2d 25 (Iowa 2003). Therefore, claimant is not entitled to 
penalty benefits while the case was on appeal to the District Court.     
 
 
As noted above, on November 4, 2021, defendants filed a timely appeal of the 
District Court’s decision. (JE 6) On November 9, 2021, defendants filed notice of filing 
an appeal bond pursuant to Iowa Rule of Appellate Procedure 6.601. (JE 5, pp. 81-85). 
   
 
Defendants have a right to appeal. In this case, defendants exercised their right 
to appeal. Additionally, defendants utilized the judicial processes to stay the agency’s 
award during the pendency of their appeal to the District Court. Further, while the 
District Court decision was on appeal, defendants posted an appeal bond. (JE 5, pp. 83-
84) The Court of Appeals issued their appeal decision on December 7, 2022. (JE 4, p. 
61) Defendants did not take any further appeal. On December 22, 2022, defendants 
issued payment for the benefits that accrued in the amount of $110,712.55. (CE 3, p. 7)

the claim at the various stages of the appeal process and defendants failed to 
contemporaneously convey the basis for their continued delay or denial of benefits to 
the claimant. I do not find claimant’s argument to be persuasive. Defendants set forth

their position for denial of permanency benefits at the time of the arbitration decision. 
(JE 1) In the arbitration decision claimant sought penalty benefits on the basis that

Claimant’s assertion that defendants failed to timely re-evaluate the case 
promptly after the arbitration decision, the commissioner’s appeal decision, and District

conclude that defendants timely filing the appeals is evidence that they timely re-
evaluated their case at each level. The evidentiary record in the underlying case 
contains competing medical opinions regarding causation. (JE 1) The arbitration

evidence in the underlying action was “close” on the issues of causation and industrial 
disability and the issues were “fairly debatable as a matter of law” and penalty benefits 
were not appropriate. See Wilian Holdings Constr. Products, Inc. v. Rice, 2009 WL

pendency of the appeals. An issue is fairly debatable when viable arguments exist in 
favor of each party, or substantial evidence exists which supports a finding favorable to 
the employer. Here, defendants pursued their appeal over issues that were fairly 
debatable and the defendants should be allowed to pursue their appeal on a fairly 
debatable issue. I find the delay during the pendency of the appeal was reasonable.

accrued from October 19, 2018 through December 15, 2022, but were not paid until

defendants as to why the payment for benefits through December 15, 2022 was not

The factors to be considered in determining the amount of the penalty include the 
length of the delay, the number of delays, the information available to the employer and 
the employer’s past record of penalties. Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 238. 
 
 
Based upon the factors identified in Robbennolt for determining the amount of 
penalty the undersigned concludes that penalty in the range of $1,000.00 is appropriate.  
This takes into consideration, the number of delays, the relatively short length of the 
delays, and also the lack of any evidence concerning the employer/insurance carriers’ 
past record of penalties.
