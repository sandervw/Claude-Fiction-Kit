On January 24, 2024, Donica Juergens filed an original notice and petition for 
alternate medical care under Iowa Code section 85.27, invoking the provisions of rule 
876 IAC 4.48.  On February 5, 2024, Defendants filed an Answer admitting liability for 
the claim relating to the right shoulder.  For purposes of the alternate medical care 
proceeding, defendants denied liability for all other conditions.  As such, this alternate 
medical care decision will only address treatment requested for the right shoulder 
condition. 
This alternate medical care claim came on for hearing before the undersigned on 
February 6, 2024, at 8:30 a.m.  The proceedings were recorded digitally and constitute 
the official record of the hearing.  By an order filed by the workers’ compensation 
commissioner, this decision is designated final agency action.  Any appeal would be a 
petition for judicial review under Iowa Code section 17A.15(1).         
The record consists of Claimant’s Exhibits 1 through 5, which include a total of 10 
pages, and Defendants’ Exhibits A and B, which include a total of 3 pages.  Ms. 
Juergens was the only witness to provide testimony.  Counsel for both parties provided 
argument.      
ISSUE 
The issue presented for resolution is whether claimant is entitled to the 
authorization of Stanley Mathew, M.D. as a treating physician and whether claimant 
should be permitted to control and select her own treatment due to an alleged 
abandonment of care by defendants.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
Having considered all the evidence and testimony in the record, the undersigned 
finds: 
Claimant, Donica Juergens, sustained a work-related injury to her right shoulder 
on August 1, 2022.  Defendants authorized medical care for the work injury.  Matthew 
Bollier, M.D. served as claimant’s authorized treating surgeon.  Dr. Bollier performed a 
right shoulder arthroscopy, distal clavicle excision, extensive debridement, and rotator 
cuff repair on March 29, 2023. 
Post-surgically she noted some improvement in her symptoms; however, she 
continued to experience global pain in the right shoulder.  She also experienced 
constant tingling and intermittent numbness in her right arm.  (See Exhibit 1, page 1)  
Dr. Bollier believed claimant’s ongoing pain was muscular and myofascial in nature. (Ex. 
1, p. 3)  He reported no concern for a new rotator cuff tear or further injury that would 
require additional surgery. (Id.)  He recommended against an updated MRI and placed 
claimant at maximum medical improvement as of July 17, 2023. (Id.)  He further 
recommended temporary restrictions until a functional capacity evaluation could be 
completed. (Id.)  The August 9, 2023, functional capacity evaluation was deemed 
invalid. 
Claimant contends she requested additional treatment for her alleged conditions 
in a July 20, 2023, electronic correspondence to defendants.  More specifically, claimant 
contends she informed defendants of her ongoing muscular pain and requested to be 
seen by a physical medicine rehabilitation specialist as Dr. Bollier does not treat 
muscular conditions. (Claimant’s Brief)  Claimant asserts defendants informed her that 
no further treatment would be authorized.  Defendants acknowledge that claimant 
produced an electronic correspondence to her claims adjuster on July 20, 2023; 
however, they dispute the contents of the correspondence.  Unfortunately, the electronic 
correspondence is not in the evidentiary record.   
 According to defendants, claimant’s July 20, 2023, correspondence asserted 
that she was entitled to see a physical medicine rehab doctor for the myofascial and 
muscular problems diagnosed by Dr. Bollier.  Defendants replied, “Unless Dr. Bollier 
refers you to another doctor, no further treatment is authorized, other than the FCE.”  
Defendants assert this exchange cannot be construed as a conveyance of claimant’s 
dissatisfaction of care.   
In any event, claimant began presenting to Stanley Mathew, M.D. on September 
11, 2023. She reported numbness and tingling in her right arm, pain in her right 
shoulder that radiated to her neck and right elbow, bilateral carpal tunnel syndrome, 
enthesopathy of the right shoulder, pain and tenderness in the right trapezius/right 
cervical paraspinal/deltoid/pectoralis, and a frozen shoulder on the right. (See Ex. 3, p. 
5) 
In a November 21, 2023, correspondence, Dr. Mathew opined that all  of 
claimant’s reported conditions are causally related to the August 1, 2022, work injury. 
(Ex. 5, pp. 8-10)

Claimant produced the November 21, 2023, correspondence, as well as medical 
records from Dr. Mathew’s office, to defendants on December 1, 2023. (Ex. 3, p. 5)  
Claimant then relayed that she was dissatisfied with the care that she had received from 
defendants and demanded they authorize medical care with Dr. Mathew. (Ex. 3, p. 6) 
Counsel for defendants responded to claimant’s request via e-mail on December 
19, 2023. (Ex. 4, p. 7)  In the e-mail, defendants’ attorney relayed that he was reviewing 
claimant’s file and, once completed, he would provide a response to claimant’s 
requests. (Id.) 
One month later, defendants relayed their position that no requests for additional 
care from Dr. Bollier were made following the August 9, 2023, functional capacity 
evaluation. (Ex. A, p. 1)  Nevertheless, defendants relayed they were requesting a 
follow-up appointment with Dr. Bollier. (Id.) 
On February 2, 2024, Dr. Bollier’s office communicated to defense counsel that 
Dr. Bollier declined to see her back in clinic given her MMI status. (Ex. B, p. 3)  
Defendants promptly inquired as to whether Dr. Nepola, another shoulder specialist at 
University of Iowa Hospitals and Clinics, would see claimant for a second opinion.  On 
February 5, 2024, Dr. Nepola declined the opportunity to evaluate claimant.  As of the 
date of hearing, defendants were still working to schedule claimant for an evaluation 
with a shoulder specialist.   
Defendants contend they have not denied treatment for claimant’s right shoulder 
injury, and they will authorize a second opinion evaluation with a shoulder specialist.   
CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law. The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services. The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury. Section 85.27. Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening October 16, 1975).     
By challenging the employer's choice of treatment — and seeking alternate care 
— claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable. See 
Iowa R. App. P 6.904(3)(e); Bell Bros. Heating and Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 
N.W.2d 193, 209 (Iowa 2010); Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995). 
Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact. Long v. 
Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995). The employer's obligation turns on the 
question of reasonable necessity, not desirability. Id.; Harned v. Farmland Foods, Inc., 
331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983). 
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical

care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).       
To establish a claim for alternative medical care, an employee must show that 
the medical care furnished by the employer is unreasonable.  Bell Bros. Heating and Air 
Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d 193, 209 (Iowa 2010). 
In Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 433, 437 (Iowa 1997), the 
supreme court held that “when evidence is presented to the commissioner that the 
employer-authorized medical care has not been effective and that such care is ‘inferior 
or less extensive’ than other available care requested by the employee, . . . the 
commissioner is justified by section 85.27 to order the alternate care.” 
The alternate medical care procedure requires the claimant to express 
dissatisfaction with the care provided.  Claimant asserts this first occurred on or about 
July 20, 2023, via e-mail.  According to defendants, claimant’s July 20, 2023, 
correspondence asserted that she was entitled to see a physical medicine rehabilitation 
specialist for the myofascial and muscular problems diagnosed by Dr. Bollier.  
Defendants relayed to claimant that Dr. Bollier did not refer her to a physical medicine 
rehabilitation specialist and, as such, claimant’s request would not be authorized.  I do 
not find claimant conveyed her dissatisfaction of care in the July 20, 2023, 
correspondence. 
Instead, I find claimant first conveyed her dissatisfaction of care via electronic 
correspondence on December 1, 2023.  Defendants responded to the correspondence 
18 days later, on December 19, 2023, noting a decision would be made once defense 
counsel had the opportunity to receive and review claimant’s file.  A slight delay while 
obtaining and reviewing medical records can be reasonably anticipated.  One month 
later, defendants agreed to schedule claimant for a follow-up appointment with Dr. 
Bollier.  When Dr. Bollier declined the opportunity to reevaluate claimant, defendants 
promptly looked for an alternative.  I do not find that defendants abandoned care during 
this period of time. 
It is the claimant's burden to show that the care offered by defendants is not 
reasonable or presents an undue inconvenience to the claimant.  To date, defendants 
have provided claimant with reasonable and necessary care.  This treatment has 
included medications, injections, physical therapy, diagnostic imaging, and surgical 
intervention.  While defendants are not authorizing Dr. Mathew as an authorized treating 
physician, they have agreed to send claimant for a second opinion with a different 
physician other than Dr. Bollier.  Defendants assert they have been diligently searching 
for a provider and intend to authorize a second opinion evaluation regarding the right 
shoulder.   
Given that claimant continues to suffer with right shoulder complaints, the care 
requested is certainly reasonable.  However, desirability of a certain course of action is 
not the legal standard utilized in alternate medical care proceedings.  Long v. Roberts 
Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).

At this time, I cannot find that defendants have abandoned care.  Defendants 
may select an appropriate physician to conduct an updated evaluation of claimant’s 
shoulder so long as the choice is reasonable and otherwise comports with Iowa law.   
Therefore, I conclude that claimant has failed to prove that the care offered by 
defendants has been unreasonable. Claimant has not carried her burden and for that 
reason her alternate care petition is denied. 
ORDER 
THEREFORE, IT IS ORDERED:       
Claimant's petition for alternate medical care is denied.
