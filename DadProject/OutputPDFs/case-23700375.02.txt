STATEMENT OF THE CASE   
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A.  The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Ruth Thomas.  
Claimant appeared personally and through her attorney, Christopher Spaulding.  
Defendant appeared through their attorney, Bryan Brooks.   
The alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on November 18, 2024. 
The proceedings were digitally recorded.  That recording constitutes the official record 
of this proceeding.  Pursuant to the Commissioner’s July 21, 2023 Order, the 
undersigned has been delegated authority to issue a final agency decision in this 
alternate medical care proceeding.  Therefore, this ruling is designated final agency 
action and any appeal of the decision would be to the Iowa District Court pursuant to 
Iowa Code section 17A.   
The evidentiary record consists of Claimant’s Exhibits 1 and 2 and claimant’s 
testimony during the telephonic hearing.   
At the outset of the hearing defendant admitted responsibility for the November 
28, 2022 work injury, but was not willing to admit responsibility for the surgical 
procedure recommended by the authorized doctor, Dr. Hart.   
ISSUE   
The issue for resolution is whether the claimant is entitled to alternate medical 
care consisting of surgery on the left knee.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
Defendant accepts liability for an injury to the left knee occurring on November 
28, 2022.  Dr. Hart, the authorized treating physician, has recommended a total knee 
arthroplasty be performed by Dr. Demik.  (Claimant’s Exhibit 1) Claimant requested the 
treatment as recommended by Dr. Hart.  (Cl. Ex. 2) There is no evidence defendant 
responded to this request.   
Ms. Thomas testified at the alternate care hearing.  She saw Dr. Hart on 
approximately October 16, 2024.  (Testimony) Dr. Hart told her that the recommended 
total knee arthroplasty would help her knee pain.  Dr. Hart previously performed a 
partial knee arthroplasty.  (Testimony) It is her understanding that a plate or some piece 
from the partial knee replacement has slipped and that is what is causing her pain.  
(Testimony)  She said Dr. Hart recommended that Dr. Demik perform the total knee 
arthroplasty because that doctor is more experienced than Dr. Hart.  (Testimony)  Ms. 
Thomas wants to have the operation.  (Testimony)  
At the alternate care hearing defendant denied that the recommended treatment 
is related to the work injury. Given defendant’s position, the procedural history to this 
case is relevant.  The agency held an arbitration hearing in the underlying case on June 
25, 20241.  (Agency File No. 23700375.01; Alt Care Recording) In the arbitration 
hearing defendant stipulated that claimant sustained an injury which arose out of and in 
the course of her employment on November 28, 2022.  Defendant also stipulated that 
claimant sustained permanent disability to her left lower extremity.  (Alt. Care 
Recording)  At the time of the arbitration hearing, Ms. Thomas made a claim for 
alternate medical care and defendant disputed claimant’s entitlement to medical 
treatment for her left knee.  (Alt. Care Recording)  After the hearing, defendant 
requested an opinion from Dr. Hart which was dated August 9, 2024.  (Alt. Care 
Recording)  The parties submitted post-hearing briefs on September 4, 2024.  (Alt. Care 
Recording)  In the post-hearing brief defendant stated,  
Dr. Hart finally responded to the report on August 9, 2023 [sic] and 
indicated that despite Claimant’s work as a housekeeper aggravating her 
pain, the pain itself is related to her knee replacement.  For those reasons 
Defendants [sic] acknowledge, Claimant is entitled to alternate medical 
care related to injections based on subsequent evidence obtained after 
the hearing.   
(Alt. Care Recording) 
I find that defendant accepted responsibility for the left knee injury of November

REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence. Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3).  
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers’ compensation law. The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services. The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury. Section 85.27. Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening October 16, 1975).  
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable. See Iowa 
Rule of Appellate Procedure 6.904(3); Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 
(Iowa 1995). Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of 
fact. Id. The employer’s obligation turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not 
desirability. Id.; Harned v. Farmland Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983).  
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving. Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care. Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant. Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  
An employer does not have the right to control the methods the providers choose 
to evaluate, diagnose and treat the injured employee. An employer is not entitled to 
control a licensed healthcare provider’s exercise of professional judgment. Assmann v. 
Blue Star Foods, File No. 866389 (Declaratory Ruling, May 18, 1988). An employer’s 
failure to follow recommendations of an authorized physician in matters of treatment is 
commonly a failure to provide reasonable treatment. Boggs v. Cargill, Inc., File No. 
1050396 (Alt. Care, January 31, 1994).  
The Iowa Supreme Court has applied the principles of judicial estoppel to 
alternate medical care proceedings in the workers’ compensation context. See

Winnebago Industries, Inc. v. Haverly, 727 N.W.2d 567 (Iowa 2006); Tyson Foods, Inc. 
v. Hedlund, 740 N.W.2d 192 (Iowa 2007); Judicial estoppel is “a commonsense doctrine 
that prohibits a party who has successfully and unequivocally asserted a position in one 
proceeding from asserting an inconsistent position in a subsequent proceeding.” 
Hedlund, 740 N.W.2d at 196. Its purpose is “to protect the integrity of the judicial 
process.” Id. For judicial estoppel to apply, the party’s inconsistent position must be 
“judicially accepted by the commissioner at the first alternate medical care hearing.” 
Judicial acceptance means that the “previous, inconsistent assertion was material to the 
holding in the first proceeding.” Id. at 197. 
One court has summarized the principle of judicial estoppel in the context of 
alternate medical care proceedings as follows:  
Indeed, to allow an employer to control the medical care of an 
employee while simultaneously allowing that same employer to free itself 
of any obligation to later pay for medical care or benefits is a contravention 
of justice and fairness. Such a result, as the Iowa Supreme Court related, 
“simply does not advance the policy goal of avoiding inconsistent and 
misleading results.” Hedlund, 740 N.W.2d at 198. It does not respect 
judicial integrity. Generally, once an employer admits liability for a claim in 
an alternate medical care proceeding, it is bound by that assertion. See 
Winnebago, 727 N.W.2d at 575 (explaining that it would take a “significant 
change in the facts after the admission of liability” in order to later deny 
liability); Hedlund, 740 N.W.2d at 198-99 (describing the critical role that 
admitting liability plays in an alternate medical care proceeding). This is 
because “[l]iability is normally an important component of the course of an 
alternate medical care proceeding.” Hedlund 740 N.W.2d at 198. Without 
first admitting liability, the workers’ compensation commissioner cannot 
hear the case. Iowa Admin. Code r. 876—48.7.  
Spencer v. Annett Holdings, Inc., 905 F.Supp.2d 953, 985 (S.D. Iowa 2012).  
I conclude that defendant accepted liability for claimant’s left knee injury of 
November 28, 2022 in the underlying arbitration case. In their post-hearing brief 
defendant stipulated it would provide future medical care for claimant. Defendant can 
only change their position regarding liability if there is a “. . . significant change in the 
facts after the admission of liability.” Winnebago, 727 N.W.2d at 575.  
The authorized treating physician, Dr. Hart, has indicated claimant now requires 
a left total knee replacement. (Ex. 1)  Defendant denies liability for claimant’s left knee 
pain because she is no longer working for the defendant-employer.  However, claimant 
was also no longer working for the defendant-employer at the time of the underlying 
arbitration decision.  Defendant has not submitted any evidence indicating claimant’s 
need for the left total knee replacement is not caused by the November 28, 2022 work 
injury.  There has been no significant change in facts since defendant agreed to provide 
claimant with future medical care for her work injury.  Because defendant has failed to

provide any evidence there has been a significant change in the facts after the 
admission of liability, defendant is estopped from denying liability for the requested 
medical treatment. Hormel Foods Corp. v. Tamayo-Perez, File No. 23-0212, filed 
December 20, 2023 (Iowa Ct. App.); 2 N.W.3d 66 (Table).   
Defendant agreed to provide claimant with future medical care for the accepted 
November 28, 2022 left knee injury.  The authorized treating physician, Dr. Hart, has 
recommended that Dr. Demik perform a total knee arthroplasty.  (Cl. Ex. 1)  Defendant 
has denied that treatment recommendation.  Defendant has not offered any new 
evidence to indicate that there has been a significant change in the fact since their 
admission of liability.  I conclude that defendant’s denial of the requested medical care 
is unreasonable.  Claimant has carried her burden of proof that she is entitled to the 
requested medical care.   
ORDER 
THEREFORE IT IS ORDERED:   
Claimant’s petition for alternate medical care is granted.  Defendant shall 
authorize and pay for claimant’s total knee replacement on the left knee as 
recommended by Dr. Hart.
