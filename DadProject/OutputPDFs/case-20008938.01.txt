STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
Claimant, Shawn Nolan, filed a petition in arbitration for workers’ compensation 
benefits against Anderson Corporation a/k/a Eagle Window & Door, Inc., as employer, 
and Old Republic Insurance Company, as insurance carrier. This case came before the 
undersigned for an arbitration hearing on October 3, 2023. This case proceeded to a 
live video hearing via Zoom.   
 
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the hearing. On the 
hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations. Those stipulations were 
accepted and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be made 
or discussed. The parties are now bound by their stipulations.  
 
The evidentiary record includes Joint Exhibits 1 through 12, Claimant’s Exhibits 1 
through 10, and Defendants’ Exhibits A through L. Claimant testified on his own behalf. 
The evidentiary record closed on October 31, 2023. All parties filed their post-hearing 
briefs on December 5, 2023, at which time the case was deemed fully submitted to the 
undersigned.

Importantly, the basic rule is that a subsequent injury, whether an aggravation of 
the original injury or a new and distinct injury, is compensable if it is the direct and 
natural result of a compensable primary injury. 
 
 
On July 26, 2022, Nolan developed sharper and more intense low back pain in

kneel. A subsequent MRI of the lumbar spine was said to be unchanged from June 14,

obscure the true nature of the case, which is a further medical complication flowing from 
a compensable injury. This is the progression of a compensable injury associated with a 
normal movement/exertion.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based. A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause. A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible. George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996). 
 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability. 
Supportive, lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, 
is also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. St. Luke's Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); 
Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995). Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted expert medical 
testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 
N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 1994).

The Iowa Supreme Court held long ago that “where an accident occurs to an 
employee in the usual course of his employment, the employer is liable for all 
consequences that naturally and proximately flow from the accident.” Oldham v. 
Scofield & Welch, 266 N.W. 480, 482 (1936).   
 
The first issue to be addressed is whether the July 26, 2022 incident was an 
intervening or superseding cause of disability. 
 
Professor Larson has written extensively in the area of subsequent injuries which 
always begins with the “natural result” analysis, writing: 
 
The basic rule is that a subsequent injury, whether an aggravation of the 
original injury or a new and distinct injury, is compensable if it is the direct 
and natural result of a compensable primary injury. The simplest 
application of this principle is the rule that all the medical consequences 
and sequelae that flow from the primary injury are compensable.   
 
Larson's Workers' Compensation Law, section 10.01, 10-3 (internal citations omitted) 
 
I found the July 26, 2022, incident was not an intervening or superseding cause 
of disability. See Carl A. Nelson & Co. v. Sloan, 873 N.W.2d 552, 555 (Iowa Ct. App. 
2015). The greater weight of evidence supports a finding that Nolan was still 
symptomatic with his condition when he bent over and attempted to kneel. This normal 
movement further aggravated his low back condition. I found Nolan’s current low back 
condition remains causally related to the July 16, 2020, work injury. Such a finding is 
consistent with Dr. Kuhnlein’s opinion that claimant did not sustain a new injury on July 
26, 2022. 
 
The next issue to be addressed is whether Nolan is entitled to temporary total, or 
healing period, benefits from August 2, 2022 through January 11, 2023. Having found 
that claimant carried his burden of proving a causal connection between the July 16, 
2020 work injury and the lost time between August 2, 2022 and January 11, 2023, I find 
claimant is entitled to an award of healing period benefits during this period of time. 
 
Nolan next asserts a claim for permanent disability resulting from the July 16, 
2020 work injury. In this regard, I found the opinions and impairment assessment of Dr. 
Kuhnlein to be the most convincing and accurate in the evidentiary record. Nolan 
carried his burden of proving the July 16, 2020 work injury was a cause of permanent 
disability.   
 
Under the Iowa Workers' Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is 
compensated either for a loss, or loss of use, of a scheduled member under Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(a)-(u) or for loss of earning capacity under section 85.34(2)(v). Nolan 
sustained an injury to the low back. The back is an unscheduled injury that is 
compensated under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v).

Since the claimant has an impairment to the body as a whole, an industrial 
disability has been sustained. Industrial disability was defined in Diederich v. Tri-City 
Ry. Co. of Iowa, 219 Iowa 587, 258 N.W. 899 (1935), as follows: “It is therefore plain 
that the Legislature intended the term ‘disability’ to mean ‘industrial disability’ or loss of 
earning capacity and not a mere ‘functional disability’ to be computed in the terms of 
percentages of the total physical and mental ability of a normal man.” 
 
Functional impairment is an element to be considered in determining industrial 
disability which is the reduction of earning capacity, but consideration must also be 
given to the injured employee's age, education, qualifications, experience, motivation, 
loss of earnings, severity and situs of the injury, work restrictions, inability to engage in 
employment for which the employee is fitted and the employer's offer of work or failure 
to so offer. McSpadden v. Big Ben Coal Co., 288 N.W.2d 181 (Iowa 1980); Olson v. 
Goodyear Service Stores, 255 Iowa 1112, 125 N.W.2d 251 (1963); Barton v. Nevada 
Poultry Co., 253 Iowa 285, 110 N.W.2d 660 (1961). 
 
Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v) provides that the undersigned shall take into 
account the number of years into the future it was reasonably anticipated that the 
employee would work at the time of the injury. The section does not indicate how this 
Agency is supposed to consider this factor, and the parties did not present any evidence 
or argument regarding the same. That being said, I have considered the number of 
years into the future it was reasonably anticipated that the employee would work at the 
time of the injury. Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v). 
 
In this instance, having considered the relevant industrial disability factors 
outlined by the Iowa Supreme Court, I found that claimant proved he sustained a 20 
percent loss of future earning capacity. Pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v), 
industrial disability is paid in relation to 500 weeks as the disability bears to the body as 
a whole. A 20 percent loss of earning capacity entitles claimant to an award of 100 
weeks of permanent partial disability benefits. Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v). 
 
The parties have stipulated that claimant’s gross weekly earnings were $458.69.  
The parties have also stipulated that claimant was single and entitled to three 
exemptions on the date of injury. The parties assert that the proper rate for workers’ 
compensation benefits is $319.53. The parties’ stipulations are accepted, and I find the 
claimant’s rate is $319.53.   
 
The parties dispute the commencement date for permanent partial disability 
benefits. For injuries occurring on or after July 1, 2017, the commencement date for 
permanent partial disability benefits is the date of maximum medical improvement. 
Having found Nolan produced sufficient evidence to prove his current low back 
condition remains causally related to the July 16, 2020 work injury, I find Nolan reached 
maximum medical improvement on February 1, 2023. (Ex. 1, p. 21) As such, I find 
permanent partial disability benefits shall commence on February 2, 2023.

Lastly, claimant seeks reimbursement of the fees associated with Dr. Kuhnlein’s 
independent medical examination under Iowa Code section 85.39. Section 85.39 
permits an employee to be reimbursed for subsequent examination by a physician of 
the employee's choice where an employer-retained physician has previously evaluated 
permanent disability and the employee believes that the initial evaluation is too low. 
 
Dr. Abernathey, an authorized physician, assessed claimant’s permanent 
impairment on May 7, 2021. (JE 4, p. 59) Dr. Kuhnlein conducted an independent 
medical examination of claimant on February 23, 2022. (Ex. 1, p. 1) It is clear a 
physician assessed claimant’s permanent impairment prior to claimant seeking out an 
alternative impairment rating from Dr. Kuhnlein. I find claimant is entitled to 
reimbursement of Dr. Kuhnlein’s February 23, 2022 IME under Iowa Code section 
85.39. 
 
Finally, claimant seeks assessment of his costs. Costs are assessed at the 
discretion of the agency. Iowa Code section 86.40. Claimant has prevailed in his claim 
for permanent partial disability benefits. Therefore, I conclude it is appropriate to assess 
claimant's costs in some amount. 
 
The only cost submitted by the claimant was the cost of Dr. Kuhnlein’s IME 
report, had his claim for reimbursement under Iowa Code section 85.39 failed. Having 
determined Dr. Kuhnlein’s report is reimbursable under section 85.39, the issue of costs 
is moot.
