STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Steven Hein, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits against Liberty Holdings, Inc. d/b/a Liberty Ready Mix, Inc., 
employer, and Donegal Insurance Company, insurer, both as defendants, for an alleged 
injury occurring on or about August 31, 2023. Claimant amended his petition on August 
8, 2024, adding the Second Injury Fund of Iowa as a defendant and alleging prior losses 
to his bilateral wrists in 1994; to his right knee in 1986 and on December 23, 1998; and 
to his left foot on February 13, 2010. 
A virtual hearing was held on June 10, 2025, and the case was considered fully 
submitted as of July 18, 2025. The record consists of Joint Exhibits 1-14, Claimant’s 
Exhibits 1-7, 9-10, Defendant employer and insurer Exhibits A-C, and the Second Injury 
Fund’s Exhibits AA-EE along with the testimony of the claimant.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW  
Claimant asserts that he is permanently and totally disabled as a result of either 
his August 31, 2023, work injury or a combination of injuries allowing him recovery 
under the Second Injury Fund of Iowa framework. In the alternative, claimant argues 
that he sustained a shoulder and arm injury as well as left shoulder injury due to 
overwork or overcompensation.  
Defendants argue that claimant stipulated that he sustained a shoulder injury on 
August 31, 2023, and that claimant is precluded from presenting a contrary argument.

Page 11 
 
Further, defendant employer and insurer argue that claimant did not sustain a separate 
biceps injury but rather the biceps is part of the shoulder.  
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
 
A possibility of causation is not sufficient; a probability is necessary. Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996) (citing Holmes v. Bruce 
Motor Freight, Inc., 215 N.W.2d 296, 297 (Iowa 1974)). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 
621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 
(Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
No expert has opined claimant sustained a neck injury arising out of and in the 
course of his employment on August 31, 2023. Dr. Bansal wrote “insufficient medical 
information to determine” whether the neck tenderness and guarding that existed at the 
time of his April 23, 2025, evaluation was related to the work injury of August 31, 2023. 
(CE 1, p. 27) While there are reports of neck pain documented in the medical records, 
causation falls within the expertise of medical professionals. No expert has opined that 
claimant sustained a neck injury arising out of the August 31, 2023, injury. Mere 
mentions of neck pain in the medical records or by examiners such as Mr. Short are 
insufficient to create a causal link between the work injury and the symptoms. 
It is found claimant did not sustain a neck injury arising out of and in the course 
of employment even if that issue was properly before the agency.

The Iowa Supreme Court has held that injuries involving the structures of the 
shoulder remain shoulder injuries even if pain and symptoms extend beyond the 
shoulder itself. Chavez v. MS Tech. LLC, 972 N.W.2d 662 (Iowa 2022). The injured 
worker in Chavez argued that “damage to the proximal side of the joint is considered an 
unscheduled whole body injury, damage to the distal side of the joint is considered a 
scheduled arm injury, and damage within the glenohumeral joint is considered a 
scheduled shoulder injury.” Chavez, 972 N.W.2d 662, 667  Chavez’s injury included a 
biceps tendon tear. (Id.) The Supreme Court stated that “it is impossible to evaluate 
shoulder impairment without some evaluation of the muscles, tendons, etc. that make 
the shoulder function.” Chavez, 972 N.W.2d 662, 669 The biceps tendons are part of 
the body parts that make the shoulder function and a tear of the biceps tendon is not 
considered a separate arm injury but rather part of the overall shoulder. This has been 
upheld in subsequent appellate rulings Alberto Lopez Hernandez v. Innovairre 
International, LLC,  No. 22004482.01, 2024 WL 4264323, at *8 (Sept. 13, 2024); also 
see, Rodney Ruff v. Senior Housing Health Care, Inc.,  No. 1655383.01, 2022 WL 
17171242, at *18 (Oct. 19, 2022) (differentiating between injuries to the biceps and 
injuries to the biceps tendon, noting that “the biceps tendon itself originates in the 
shoulder as defined by Iowa law.”). 
Dr. Bansal assigns a separate rating for loss of strength in the elbow, but that 
loss of strength stems from the biceps tendon injury, not a separate injury to the elbow. 
The only loss of function noted for the elbow is in elbow flexion. (CE 1, p. 21) There is 
no separate diagnosis for the elbow by Dr. Bansal. (CE 1, p. 24) Dr. Bansal noted that 
the mechanism of injury affected the “long head of the biceps tendon” and that the tear 
or detachment was in the long head of the biceps tendon. (CE 1, p. 25) Thus, the injury 
involves the structures of the shoulder and remain a shoulder injury even though there 
is pain and dysfunction extending into the biceps and elbow.

2024)

Claimant argues he is entitled to a full 14% whereas defendants argue that the

The Supreme Court of Iowa Court held “[u]nder the fresh-start rule, if the 
employee sustains a new work-related injury after commencing work for a new 
employer, any resulting loss of earning capacity is measured as a diminution of the new, 
complete earning capacity that existed at the time the employment with the new 
employer commenced.” Roberts Dairy v. Billick, 861 N.W.2d 814 (Iowa 2015). The 
fresh-start rule makes clear that if the earning capacity has been reevaluated through a 
change in employment, then prior disabilities from different employers cannot be used 
to reduce benefits owed for a subsequent injury. In 2002, the Iowa Supreme Court again 
reiterated that an injured worker obtains a fresh start through free market principles 
when he or she leaves a job where he or she was injured and commences new 
employment with a different employer. Venegas v. IBP, Inc., 638 N.W.2d 699 (Iowa 
2002).  
However, in Iowa Code Section 85.34(7) provides that an “employer is not liable 
for compensating an employee's preexisting disability that arose out of and in the 
course of employment from a prior injury with the employer, to the extent that the 
employee's preexisting disability has already been compensated.” Iowa Code § 
85.34(7).  In other words, the 2023 resulting impairment needed to be greater than the 
2009 impairment, but in this case the impairment assigned by Dr Bansal for the 2009 
injury was the same percentage as the impairment assigned by Dr. Bansal for the 2023 
injury. Thus, no further recovery can be awarded for the right shoulder injury.

Claimant seeks reimbursement for the full amount of the IME performed by Dr. 
Bansal in the amount of $4,981. (CE 10, p. 68)Iowa Code section 85.39 permits an 
employee to be reimbursed for subsequent examination by a physician of the 
employee's choice where an employer-retained physician has previously evaluated 
“permanent disability” and the employee believes that the initial evaluation is too low.   
Iowa Code § 85.39(2) (2023).  The section also permits reimbursement for reasonably 
necessary transportation expenses incurred and for any wage loss occasioned by the 
employee attending the subsequent examination.  Iowa Code § 85.39 (1), (2).   
The Iowa Supreme Court has provided a literal interpretation of the plain 
language of Iowa Code section 85.39, stating that section only allows the employee to 
obtain an independent medical evaluation at the employer’s expense if an evaluation of 
permanent disability has already been made by an employer retained physician.  Des 
Moines Area Reg’l Transit Auth. v. Young, 867 N.W.2d 839, 847 (Iowa 2015). Under 
the Young decision, an employee can only obtain an IME at the employer's expense if 
an evaluation of permanent disability has been made by an employer-retained 
physician. Id. 
An employer is only liable to reimburse an employee for the cost of an 
examination conducted pursuant to this subsection if the injury for which the employee 
is being examined is determined to be compensable.  Iowa Code § 85.39(2) (2017).  
“An employer is not liable for the cost of such examination if the injury for which the 
employee is being examined is determined not to have a compensable injury. A 
determination of the reasonableness of a fee for an examination made pursuant to this 
subsection, shall be based on the typical fee charged by a medical provider to perform 
an impairment rating in the local area where the examination is conducted.”  Iowa Code 
§ 85.39(2); Tweeten v. Tweeten, 999 N.W.2d 270, 282 (Iowa 2023).
