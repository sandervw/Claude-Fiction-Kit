STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Jorge Montalvo, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from Titan Tire Corporation, employer, and Liberty Mutual 
Insurance Company, insurance carrier, both as defendants, as a result of a stipulated 
injury sustained on November 18, 2021. This matter came on for hearing before Deputy 
Workers’ Compensation Commissioner Erica J. Fitch, via videoconference, using Zoom. 
The proceedings were translated by Patricia Hillock. The record in this case consists of 
joint exhibits 1 through 11, claimant’s exhibits 1 through 11, defendants’ exhibits A 
through J, and the testimony of the claimant.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The first issue for determination is whether claimant is entitled to temporary 
disability benefits from November 18, 2021 to July 17, 2022. 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 
Iowa Code section 85.34(1) provides that healing period benefits are payable to 
an injured employee who has suffered permanent partial disability until (1) the employee 
has returned to work; (2) it is medically indicated that significant improvement from the 
injury is not anticipated; or (3) the employee is medically capable of returning to 
employment substantially similar to the employment in which the employee was 
engaged at the time of injury, whichever occurs first.  The healing period can be 
considered the period during which there is a reasonable expectation of improvement of 
the disabling condition.  See Armstrong Tire & Rubber Co. v. Kubli, 312 N.W.2d 60, 65 
(Iowa Ct. App. 1981).  Healing period benefits can be interrupted or intermittent.  Teel v. 
McCord, 394 N.W.2d 405, 406 (Iowa 1986).  
Claimant suffered a stipulated work-related injury on November 18, 2021, after 
which he was removed from work or placed under work restrictions. Defendants offered 
claimant a light duty assignment, which claimant accepted and resulted in claimant’s 
return to work on July 18, 2022. During the time claimant was off work or was under 
restrictions prior to his return to work on light duty, defendants paid claimant weekly 
indemnity benefits. There does not appear to be a debate as to claimant’s entitlement to 
weekly healing period benefits during this period following the injury and prior to 
claimant’s return to work on July 18, 2022.  
Accordingly, claimant is entitled to healing period benefits from November 18, 
2021 through July 17, 2022. The parties stipulated that at the time of the work injury, 
claimant’s gross earnings were $1,515.00 and he was married and entitled to 4 
exemptions. As such, the proper rate of compensation is $977.27. 
The next issue for determination is the nature and extent of permanent disability 
sustained by claimant, including whether claimant sustained an industrial disability or is 
limited to functional loss under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v). 
Under the Iowa Workers' Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is 
compensated either for a loss or loss of use of a scheduled member under Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(a)-(u) or as an unscheduled injury pursuant to the provisions of section 
85.34(2)(v). 
Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v) states:

In all cases of permanent partial disability other than those 
described or referred to in paragraphs “a” through “u”, the compensation 
shall be paid during the number of weeks in relation to five hundred weeks 
as the reduction in the employee’s earning capacity caused by the 
disability bears in relation to the earning capacity that the employee 
possessed when the injury occurred. A determination of the reduction in

of years in the future it was reasonably anticipated that the employee 
would work at the time of the injury. If an employee who is eligible for

functional impairment resulting from the injury, and not in relation to the 
employee’s earning capacity. Notwithstanding section 85.26, subsection 2,

by the employee for a determination of any reduction in the employee’s

In this matter, claimant did return to work at defendant-employer following the 
work injury. Defendants argue claimant’s disability should be evaluated functionally, as 
claimant returned to work at a high hourly rate. Claimant argues the disability is an 
industrial one, as claimant’s gross earnings are less than preinjury. I agree with 
claimant. While claimant’s hourly rate may have increased post-injury, his gross 
earnings are substantially less, due to a combination of less overtime and fewer 
incentive and shift differential pay options. The parties stipulated claimant’s gross 
average weekly wage pre-injury was $1,515.00. Post-injury, the records reveal claimant 
earns closer to $1,000.00 per week. This is a substantial reduction in earnings, despite 
claimant’s hourly rate of pay being higher. Therefore, I find claimant’s disability is ripe 
for consideration as an industrial disability.  
Industrial disability was defined in Diederich v. Tri-City Ry. Co. of Iowa, 219 Iowa 
587, 591, 258 N.W. 899, 902 (1935) as follows:  "It is therefore plain that the Legislature 
intended the term 'disability' to mean 'industrial disability' or loss of earning capacity and 
not a mere 'functional disability' to be computed in terms of percentages of the total 
physical and mental ability of a normal man." 
Functional impairment is an element to be considered in determining industrial 
disability which is the reduction of earning capacity, but consideration must also be 
given to the injured employee's age, education, qualifications, experience, motivation,

loss of earnings, severity and situs of the injury, work restrictions, inability to engage in 
employment for which the employee is fitted and the employer's offer of work or failure 
to so offer.  McSpadden v. Big Ben Coal Co., 288 N.W.2d 181, 192 (Iowa 1980); Olson 
v. Goodyear Service Stores, 255 Iowa 1112, 1120, 125 N.W.2d 251, 256 (1963); Barton 
v. Nevada Poultry Co., 253 Iowa 285, 110 N.W.2d 660, 663 (1961). 
Compensation for permanent partial disability shall begin when it is medically 
indicated that maximum medical improvement from the injury has been reached and 
that the extent of loss or percentage of permanent impairment can be determined by the

Medical Association, as adopted by the Commissioner. Iowa Code section 85.34(2)   
Claimant suffered a stipulated work-related injury, which has resulted in 
permanent impairment and the need for permanent restrictions. A valid FCE, adopted 
by multiple physicians, placed claimant in the light physical demand category with a 
maximum 20-pound lift. This restriction precludes claimant from returning to his 
preinjury position at defendant-employer, as well as to a majority of claimant’s preinjury 
manual labor employment with other employers. Claimant remains employed by 
defendant-employer, currently assigned to a position he held in a light duty capacity. 
Claimant intends to continue working in this position for as long as allowed by 
defendant-employer. He has accepted each position offered by defendant-employer and 
has demonstrated motivation to continue his employment. Despite his motivation and 
willingness to work, claimant’s post-injury earnings are approximately 30 percent less 
than preinjury. The injury has caused a notable loss of earnings, even while remaining 
in the employ of defendant-employer.  
Were claimant not to remain employed at defendant-employer, his job prospects 
are significantly lessened as compared to his preinjury options. Claimant is 53 years of 
age, with very limited education. He is less than fluent in English, a requirement of many 
lighter physical demand category positions. His limited education and language skills 
similarly negatively impacts his likelihood of successful retraining.  
Additionally, claimant has sustained permanent functional impairment. In 2022, 
Dr. Mooney opined claimant sustained an 8 percent impairment relative to his thoracic 
spine. He did not offer an opinion as to potential lumbar impairment, due to a belief that 
claimant required further treatment. Dr. Mooney did not assign any permanent 
impairment to claimant’s hernias. Dr. Bansal evaluated claimant in 2024, following 
claimant’s course of lumbar treatment and with knowledge of claimant’s 
contemporaneous hernia complaints and thoracic condition. Given that Dr. Bansal’s 
opinion was rendered at a time following claimant’s course of lumbar treatment and with 
knowledge of continuing hernia complaints, I find Dr. Bansal’s opinion entitled to greater 
weight. Dr. Bansal opined claimant sustained permanent impairment of 5 percent body 
as a whole for the thoracic spine, 5 percent body as a whole for the lumbar spine, and 6 
percent body as a whole for the combined hernia condition.

Given the above and all other relevant factors of industrial disability, I find 
claimant has sustained a 40 percent loss of earning capacity as a result of the stipulated 
work injury. Such an award entitles claimant to 200 weeks (40 percent x 500 weeks) of 
permanent partial disability benefits, payable at the rate of $977.27.     
The next issue for determination is the commencement date for permanent 
disability benefits.  
Compensation for permanent partial disability shall begin when it is medically 
indicated that maximum medical improvement from the injury has been reached and 
that the extent of loss or percentage of permanent impairment can be determined by the 
use of the guides to the evaluation of permanent impairment, published by the American 
Medical Association, as adopted by the Commissioner. (Iowa Code section 85.34(2))  
Claimant contends the proper commencement date for permanent partial 
disability benefits is July 18, 2022, the date following termination of his healing period. 
Defendants contend the proper date is March 31, 2023, the date claimant achieved MMI 
for his final compensable injury and thus, claimant’s entire permanent impairment could 
be determined.  
In this instance, although claimant had not achieved MMI for all his conditions as 
of the date of his return to work, claimant had achieved MMI for the majority of his 
conditions, as opined by Dr. Mooney, defendants’ evaluating physician. In fact, Dr. 
Mooney opined that not only had claimant achieved MMI for multiple conditions as of 
May 31, 2022, but also that he had sustained permanent impairment of 8 percent whole 
person relative to his thoracic spine injury. It is this 8 percent rating that defendants 
contended should be adopted as claimant’s permanent functional impairment.  
Given claimant had achieved MMI for the majority of his injuries, had received a 
permanent impairment rating, and defendants urged adoption of said impairment rating, 
I cannot adopt defendants’ argued commencement date. Rather, I find the proper 
commencement date for permanent disability benefits is July 18, 2022, as argued by 
claimant.

The next issue for determination is whether claimant is entitled to reimbursement 
for an independent medical examination under Iowa code section 85.39; specifically, 
claimant requests an award of Dr. Bansal’s IME expense in the amount of $4,432.00. 
(CE11, pp. 61-62) 
Iowa Code section 85.39 permits an employee to be reimbursed for subsequent 
examination by a physician of the employee's choice where an employer-retained 
physician has previously evaluated “permanent disability” and the employee believes 
that the initial evaluation is too low.   Iowa Code § 85.39(2) (2023).  The section also 
permits reimbursement for reasonably necessary transportation expenses incurred and 
for any wage loss occasioned by the employee attending the subsequent examination.  
Iowa Code § 85.39 (1), (2).   
The Iowa Supreme Court has provided a literal interpretation of the plain 
language of Iowa Code section 85.39, stating that section only allows the employee to 
obtain an independent medical evaluation at the employer’s expense if an evaluation of 
permanent disability has already been made by an employer retained physician.  Des 
Moines Area Reg’l Transit Auth. v. Young, 867 N.W.2d 839, 847 (Iowa 2015). Under 
the Young decision, an employee can only obtain an IME at the employer's expense if 
an evaluation of permanent disability has been made by an employer-retained 
physician. Id. 
Claimant sustained a compensable work-related injury. Dr. Mooney, a physician 
retained by defendants, opined that claimant sustained permanent impairment by a 
report dated August 26, 2022. Thereafter, in July 2024, claimant underwent an IME with 
Dr. Bansal. Defendants offered no argument as to why claimant is not entitled to 
reimbursement of Dr. Bansal’s IME expense. Accordingly, I find claimant is entitled to 
reimbursement of Dr. Bansal’s IME fee in the amount of $4,432.00.

The next issue for determination is whether claimant is entitled to penalty 
benefits. 
If weekly compensation benefits are not fully paid when due, section 10A.315(4) 
requires that additional benefits be awarded unless the employer shows reasonable 
cause or excuse for the delay or denial.  Iowa Code § 10A.315(4) (2023); Robbennolt v. 
Snap-on Tools Corp., 555 N.W.2d 229, 236-37 (Iowa 1996).  
“Benefits may be delayed, without penalty, during such time as an employer’s 
liability is “fairly debatable”. Kiesecker v. Webster City Custom Meats, Inc., 528 N.W.2d 
109, 111 (Iowa 1995).  A delay attributable to the time required to perform a reasonable 
investigation is not unreasonable.  Id.  
It also is not unreasonable to deny a claim when a good faith issue of law or fact 
makes the employer’s liability fairly debatable.  Rodda v. Vermeer Mfg., 734 N.W.2d 
480, 483 (Iowa 2007).  An issue of law is fairly debatable if viable arguments exist in 
favor of each party.  Covia v. Robinson, 507 N.W.2d 411, 412 (Iowa 1993).  An issue of 
fact is fairly debatable if substantial evidence exists which would support a finding 
favorable to the employer.  Gilbert v. USF Holland, Inc., 637 N.W.2d 194, 199 (Iowa 
2001).  
“An employer’s bare assertion that a claim is ‘fairly debatable’ does not make it 
so.”  Meyers v. Holiday Express Corp., 557 N.W.2d 502, 505 (Iowa 1996).  The 
employer must assert facts upon which the commissioner could reasonably find that the 
claim was “fairly debatable.”  Id.   
If the employer fails to show reasonable cause or excuse for the delay or denial, 
the commissioner shall impose a penalty in an amount up to 50 percent of the amount 
unreasonably delayed or denied.  Christensen v. Snap-on Tools Corp., 554 N.W.2d 
254, 259 (Iowa 1996).  The factors to be considered in determining the amount of the 
penalty include the length of the delay, the number of delays, the information available 
to the employer and the employer’s past record of penalties.  Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d 
at 238. 
 
Claimant seeks an award of penalty benefits for defendants’ underpaid and late-
paid temporary and permanent disability benefits. With respect to temporary disability 
benefits, claimant concedes the payments were relatively regular and timely, but notes 
the benefits were paid at a weekly rate $130.65 lower than claimant’s stipulated rate. 
With respect to the permanent disability benefits, claimant argues defendants did not 
pay the entire rating of their evaluating physician and did not issue payment until over 
one year following Dr. Mooney’s assignment of permanent impairment.   
 
In this instant matter, it is undisputed that defendants paid claimant healing 
period benefits at an incorrect rate that is less than that to which the parties stipulated at 
hearing. Despite this underpayment, I find defendants did not unreasonably delay in

Page 17 
 
 
payment of such benefits because upon learning that claimant’s claimed exemptions 
were different from those utilized in the original computation, defendants promptly 
recomputed claimant’s rate and issued payment for the underpayment and interest. 
Therefore, I do not find a penalty is warranted.  
 
I find defendants’ late payment of permanent disability benefits more 
troublesome. Dr. Mooney, claimant’s chosen opining physician, opined claimant 
sustained permanent disability by a report issued in August 2022. Defendants did not 
issue payment until November 2023. Even assuming defendants were correct in their 
argument regarding the commencement date for permanent disability benefits, the 
asserted date was March 31, 2023, more than six months prior to payment of 
permanent disability benefits. Furthermore, when benefits were issued, defendants paid 
less than the 40 weeks of benefits owed as a result of Dr. Mooney’s opinion, the opinion 
defendants urged the undersigned to adopt. I find an award of penalty benefits is 
warranted given defendants’ delay and lack of required contemporaneous conveyance 
for the reason for the delay. I, therefore, award claimant $5,000.00 in penalty benefits.    
 
The final issue for determination is a specific taxation of costs as outlined in 
Claimant’s Exhibit 11. Claimant seeks taxation of the costs of filing fee and convenience 
charge ($128.75) (CE11, p. 59), as well as transcription fees associated with claimant’s 
deposition ($66.60). (CE11, p. 60) The cost of filing fee and convenience charge are 
allowable costs and are taxed to defendants. While claimant did not offer claimant’s 
deposition transcript into evidence, defendants did offer the transcript as an exhibit. If I 
were to deny claimant taxation of the cost of the transcript based on the fact that 
defendants, and not claimant, offered the exhibit, the practical effect could lead to 
double-introduction of an exhibit or gamesmanship, leading defendants to offer 
transcripts in order to deny claimant the cost. I am not willing to encourage either result. 
Accordingly, I tax the cost of claimant’s deposition transcript as well.
