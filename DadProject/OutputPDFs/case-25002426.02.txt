STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A.  The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Eric Coley, against 
Annett Holdings d/b/a TMC Transportation, employer, and Great West Casualty 
Company, insurer, both as defendants.  Claimant appeared personally and through 
Attorney Nicholas Shall.  Defendants appeared through Attorney Stephen Spencer and 
their representative Kelly Cloude. 
 
The alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on November 12, 2025.  
The proceedings were digitally recorded through Google Meet.  That recording 
constitutes the official record of this proceeding.  Pursuant to the Commissioner’s Order, 
the undersigned has been delegated authority to issue a final agency decision in this 
alternate medical care proceeding.  Therefore, this ruling is designated final agency 
action and any appeal of the decision would be to the Iowa District Court pursuant to 
Iowa Code section 17A. 
 
The record consists of Claimant’s Exhibit 1, pages 1-10, and defense exhibits A 
through D, which were filed prior to the hearing and admitted during the hearing. Kathy 
Coley, spouse of claimant, testified on behalf of the claimant, and Robert Davies and 
Kelly Cloude testified on behalf of defendants.

Claimant alleged he sustained a work-related injury on March 5, 2025, which 
resulted in paraplegia. Defendants accepted responsibility for the work injury and 
subsequent catastrophic impairments. 
 
ISSUE 
 
Whether claimant should be allowed to have the basement renovated to install a 
bedroom and wet room. 
 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
Claimant suffered a work-related injury on March 5, 2025, which has left him 
confined to a wheelchair. There are several modifications that need to be completed in 
order for claimant to return to his home. All parties agree that the plan was to return 
claimant to the home by the time he was done with rehabilitation, but unfortunately due 
to a lack of agreement over the modifications, claimant and his wife are currently living 
in a hotel. The hotel is being paid for by defendants.  
 
I find that the delays were caused by the disagreement over modifications rather 
than any intentional malfeasance on the part of the defendants. Claimant spent one 
month in the hospital and then an aftercare facility in Colorado for five and a half months 
followed by eight weeks at QI Rehab in Nebraska. Two weeks ago, claimant was 
discharged home and is now staying in a hotel.  
 
Ms. Coley testified that someone from the defendants named Maggie Castro 
reached out to her in May 2025. Ms. Coley requested that a contractor she worked with 
before by the name of Jim Lorenz with Lorenz Home Improvement be allowed to bid on 
the work. Ms. Castro agreed. In July, Mr. Lorenz informed Ms. Coley that the insurance 
company would not allow him to bid on the project. Kelly Cloude, defendant insurer’s 
representative, said that claimant did not have the right to choose the contractor. A new 
project manager was assigned by the name of Bruce Santos. He contacted claimant on 
August 14, 2025, regarding the scope of work. On August 20, 2025, plans for the 
bathroom were reviewed. The claimant wished to have a pony wall, a short wall, 
installed in the bathroom to separate the toilet from the shower area. The addition of the 
pony wall reduced the available turning space in the bathroom.  
 
Claimant signed off on a Scope of Work detailing changes to the bathroom 
including “bathroom water proofing / wet room This includes waterproofing the entire 
bathroom to create a wet room. This consists of the use of the Schuller system to 
waterproof the floor before installing tile…[And] the installation of a double sink, one for 
her and the other for him, with wheelchair accessibility.” (CE 5)  
 
October 24, 2025, claimant’s counsel wrote to defendants’ counsel concerning 
the modifications suggested by claimant and his wife. It is claimant’s assertion that the 
original scope of work agreed to by the parties in September would not allow for a safe 
turning radius in the bathroom. (CE 1-2)

The primary dispute is over the bathroom renovation. The plans that were 
provided to claimant and his wife contained inaccurate measurements, and even if the 
measurements on the provided plans were accurate, there was barely enough room for 
claimant to execute turns in the bathroom. Claimant, through his wife, expressed the 
desire to have a space in the basement turned into a bathroom. Mr. Davies, the 
manager in charge of the project, testified that the basement build out would be around 
$10,000 more than an upstairs remodel. However, there is an additional cost of digging 
out the backyard and pouring a concrete path from the street level to the basement exit. 
The cost was approximately $40,000. Mr. Davies recommended that the pony wall be 
removed. All parties agreed that the inclusion of the pony wall was for aesthetic 
purposes and was not medically necessary. Claimant was concerned that even with the 
removal of the pony wall, the dimensions of the bathroom are not sufficient to provide a 
comfortable turning radius for claimant’s wheelchair. Mr. Davies testified that the ADA 
guidelines require a five foot turning radius which could be accommodated with the 
removal of the pony wall.  
 
Claimant also asserts that the basement bedroom would be a better use of the 
space given claimant’s bed needs. Claimant should have a hospital bed with access on 
both sides for medical care. The primary bedroom that is shared by the claimant and 
claimant’s wife has a king size bed. There is no room in the bedroom for a hospital bed. 
Ms. Coley also testified that while the home has two other bedrooms on the same floor, 
one bedroom is being occupied by her daughter and the other bedroom will not have a 
closet as an elevator will be installed in the third bedroom’s closet.  
 
REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Iowa Code Section 85.27 (2013). 
 
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See 
Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  Determining what care is 
reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.  Id.  The employer’s obligation turns 
on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.  Id.; Harned v. Farmland 
Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983).   
 
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).

An employer’s statutory right is to select the providers of care and the employer 
may consider cost and other pertinent factors when exercising its choice. Long, at 124. 
An employer (typically) is not a licensed health care provider and does not possess 
medical expertise. Accordingly, an employer does not have the right to control the 
methods the providers choose to evaluate, diagnose and treat the injured employee. An 
employer is not entitled to control a licensed health care provider’s exercise of 
professional judgment. Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 866389 (Declaratory 
Ruling, May 19, 1988). An employer’s failure to follow recommendations of an 
authorized physician in matters of treatment is commonly a failure to provide reasonable 
treatment. Boggs v. Cargill, Inc., File No. 1050396 (Alt. Care, January 31, 1994). 
 
The issue of whether the care being proffered by defendants is reasonably suited 
to meet the needs of the claimant without undue inconvenience requires a finding of 
what care is being proffered. In the Scope of Work, a wet room with a dual vanity, toilet 
and shower is itemized. According to Mr. Davies, with the removal of the pony wall, 
claimant’s current bathroom can be renovated to provide a shower, toilet and a dual 
vanity within an eight foot, seven inch by eight foot, three inch space. Ms. Coley 
expressed disbelief at this. At this time, it is unknown whether the measurements of the 
existing space are accurate and there are no finalized plans. Because of the cost and 
inconvenience this renovation will require, new measurements and new plans should be 
provided at this time.  
 
If, after review of the new plans, the claimant believes that the current scope of 
work is inadequate, a new alternate care petition should be filed.  
 
THEREFORE IS IT ORDERED:  
 
The defendants shall remeasure the primary bathroom within one week of this 
order.  
 
The defendants shall provide new floor plans for the primary bathroom wet room 
within one week of the measurements being taken.  
 
The defendants shall provide an updated Scope of Work reflecting all previously 
agreed upon changes as well as the proposed bathroom remodel within one week of 
the measurements being taken.  
 
The remainder of the alternate care petition is DENIED at this time.
