STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
 
The claimant, Selena Pena, filed a petition for arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from self-insured employer Wells Enterprises. Judy Freking 
appeared on behalf of the claimant. Steven Durick appeared on behalf of the defendant.   
 
 
The matter came on for hearing on March 13, 2024, before Deputy Workers’ 
Compensation Commissioner Andrew M. Phillips. Pursuant to an order of the Iowa 
Workers’ Compensation Commissioner, the hearing occurred electronically via Zoom.  
The hearing proceeded without significant difficulty.  
 
 
The record in this case consists of Joint Exhibits 1-8, Claimant’s Exhibits 1-8, and 
Defendant’s Exhibits A-K. The claimant filed an objection to several of the defendant’s 
proposed exhibits. Arguments were heard at the opening of the hearing as to the 
objection. The claimant filed their objection on March 12, 2024. Pursuant to the Hearing 
Assignment order issued by the Commissioner on December 29, 2023, and 876 Iowa 
Administrative Code 4.19(3)(d), all written objections are due seven days prior to the 
hearing date. It was determined that the claimant’s objection was not timely, as the 
defendant represented that their exhibits and witness and exhibit lists were timely filed 
prior to the hearing. Even if the claimant’s objection were timely, it was determined that 
the materials to which the claimant objected were relevant pursuant to Iowa Code 
section 17A.14. It was also determined that the claimant failed to prove that the 
materials to which they objected were not unfairly prejudicial. Therefore, the objection 
was overruled, and all of the proposed exhibits were received into the record.   
 
The claimant testified on her own behalf with the assistance of an interpreter, 
Jose Burgos. The interpreter was duly sworn in ahead of testimony. Jon Robinson 
testified on behalf of the defendant. Maria Taylor was appointed the official reporter and 
custodian of the notes of the proceeding. The evidentiary record closed at the

STIPULATIONS 
 
 
Through the hearing report, as reviewed at the commencement of the hearing, 
the parties stipulated and/or established the following: 
 
1. There was an employer-employee relationship at the time of the alleged 
injury.   
 
2. That, if the injury is found to be a cause of permanent disability, the disability 
is a scheduled member disability to the left foot.   
 
3. That, at the time of the work injury, the claimant’s gross earnings were five 
hundred fifty-two and 00/100 dollars ($552.00) per week, she was married 
and entitled to four exemptions. The resulting stipulated weekly compensation 
rate was three hundred ninety three and 67/100 dollars ($393.67).   
 
 
The defendants waived some of their affirmative defenses, but asserted one as 
listed below. The parties marked causation of temporary disability during a period of 
recovery as a disputed issue, but clarified that, since the extent of temporary disability 
was no longer in dispute, no decision was necessary as to that particular issue. Credits 
against any award were no longer in dispute, nor were medical benefits.    
    
The parties are now bound by their stipulations. 
 
ISSUES 
 
The parties submitted the following issues for determination: 
 
1. Whether the claimant sustained an injury, which arose out of and in in the 
course of her employment on September 1, 2019.    
  
2. Whether the alleged injury is a cause of permanent disability. 
 
3. The extent of permanent partial disability benefits, should any be awarded. 
 
4. The proper commencement date for permanent partial disability benefits, 
should any be awarded.   
 
5. Whether imposition of a penalty is warranted, and if so, the extent of that 
penalty. 
 
6. Whether the claimant is entitled to a specific taxation of costs, the amount of 
those costs, and whether the costs were paid.

Dr. Bansal then endeavors to answer questions posed by claimant’s counsel.

plantar fasciitis in her foot due to “…her cumulative walking throughout the workday.”

cited to the Mayo Clinic, which stated, “[o]ccupations that keep you on your feet.  
Factory workers, teachers, and others who spend most of their work hours walking or

that Ms. Pena had “…resultant loading of the 2nd metatarsal with peroneal tendonitis 
secondary to her offloading gait.” (Id.)  
 
Dr. Bansal then endeavored to provide an impairment rating based upon the 
AMA Guides to the Evaluation of Permanent Impairment, Fifth Edition. (Ex. 6, p. 22) 
Based upon the range of motion measurements observed during the claimant’s physical

percent left lower extremity impairment. (Id.) He converted this to a 3 percent whole 
person impairment. (Id.) He recommended intermittent steroid injections for further 
treatment. (Id.) He placed her at MMI for her left foot as of February 28, 2023. (Id.) He 
also recommended permanent restrictions of no walking or standing for more than 30 
minutes at a time, for 3 hours total per day. (Id.)

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence. Iowa Rule of Appellate 
Procedure 6.904(3).   
 
Issue Preclusion 
 
The defendant argues in their post-hearing brief that the claimant’s claims are 
barred by the concept of issue preclusion. Unfortunately, this was not indicated as a 
disputed issue anywhere in the hearing report. The hearing report exists in order to 
define the claims, defenses, and issues submitted to the presiding deputy 
commissioner. The defendant had the opportunity to indicate that this was an issue for 
determination in the hearing report, and failed to do so. Specifically, section 10 of the 
hearing report allows for the parties to include “[a]dditional [i]ssues, [s]tipulations, and/or 
[e]xplanation”. Therefore, the issue is considered waived, and I decline to issue any 
decision regarding this particular issue.

Page 13 
 
Iowa Code section 85.23 notice 
 
Before examining any other issues in this case, it is vital to consider the 
affirmative defenses submitted by the defendant. The first affirmative defense argued by 
the defendant is that the claimant failed to give notice to Wells within 90 days of her 
September 1, 2019 claimed injury date.  
  
Failure to give notice is an affirmative defense which the employer must prove by 
a preponderance of the evidence. DeLong v. Highway Commission, 229 Iowa 700, 295 
N.W. 91 (1940).   
 
Iowa Code section 85.23 provides that an injury is not compensable unless, 
within ninety (90) days of the “date of the occurrence of the injury,” either (1) the 
employer had actual knowledge of the occurrence of an injury, or (2) notice of the 
occurrence of an injury was provided to the employer. On July 1, 2017, “date of the 
occurrence of the injury” was defined to mean “the date that the employee knew or 
should have known that the injury was work related.” Iowa Code section 85.23.   
 
The purpose of this rule is to give the employer an opportunity to timely 
investigate the facts surrounding the injury. Defendants often read this to strictly require 
the defendants to have actual notice rather than constructive or imputed 
notice. However, the second part of Iowa Code section 85.23 allows for something less 
than actual notice. When an employer as a reasonably conscientious manager is 
alerted to the possibility of a potential compensation claim through information which 
makes the employer aware that the injury occurred and that it may be work related 
meets the actual notice alternative to notice. Dillinger v. City of Sioux City, 368 N.W.2d 
176 (Iowa 1985); Robinson v. Dept. of Transp., 296 N.W.2d 809 (Iowa 1980). Actual 
knowledge must include information that the injury might be work connected, but does 
not require claimants to include the specific body parts injured or the specific word 
“injury.” Robinson, 296 N.W.2d at 811.   
 
In this case, Ms. Pena argues that she sustained an injury on September 1, 
2019, when the arch of her left foot collapsed following her working 12 hour days and 
walking while on the job. Ms. Pena claims that she told the Wells on-site nurse about 
her pain in her left foot and consistently requested care; however, she was only told to 
take ibuprofen. This is an interesting claim, as Ms. Pena went out of her way to 
demonstrate that she had a poor memory, yet despite the lack of objective proof 
thereof, she claimed to consistently request care from Wells following September 1, 
2019. Overall, I found her to be an inconsistent historian. Her claims also lacked any 
objective backing, which would have assisted her argument due to her alleged memory 
issues. 
 
Unfortunately, for Ms. Pena, she cannot “have it both ways.” Ms. Pena testified 
during the hearing that she did not have any left foot issues until April 3, 2020; this 
contradicted her acknowledgment in her discovery responses that she developed pain 
in her left foot on September 1, 2019. She further testified that she did not tell anyone at 

PENA V. WELLS ENTERPRISES, INC. 
Page 14 
 
Wells, or decide that September 1, 2019 was her proper injury date. She also sought no 
documented care for her left foot between September 1, 2019 and April 3, 2020. 
Further, her supervisor indicated that she was only told about the alleged injury about 
one week prior to April 3, 2020. Even the medical record from April 3, 2020 indicated 
that Ms. Pena claimed a date of injury to her left lower extremity two months prior.   
 
Most convincingly on this issue is the simple fact that there is no medical 
documentation or documentary evidence of Ms. Pena’s alleged repeated requests for 
care for her left foot until after April 3, 2020. This was confirmed by Mr. Robinson, a 
Wells employee, who testified that Ms. Pena never provided Wells with a notice of injury 
within 90 days of September 1, 2019.   
 
Therefore, I find that the defendant has proven their affirmative defense by a 
preponderance of the evidence. There is no evidence that Ms. Pena provided Wells with 
notice of an injury within 90 days of September 1, 2019. The earliest date that Wells had 
notice, based upon the objective evidence in the record, was late March of 2020. This is 
well past 90 days after September 1, 2019. Based upon this, her claims related to the 
alleged September 1, 2019 date of injury are barred by Iowa Code section 85.23.   
 
Affirmative Defense pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.26 
 
 
I previously found that Ms. Pena’s claims were barred by Iowa Code section 
85.23. Generally, this would conclude my analysis. However, I am also going to review 
the defendant’s second asserted affirmative defense of a bar due to Iowa Code section 
85.26.   
 
The defendants assert an affirmative defense of failure to timely bring a claim 
pursuant to Iowa Code 85.26. Before engaging in any analysis as to any of the other 
disputed issues in this claim, it is important to determine whether or not the claim can 
even stand based upon an analysis of the facts and applicable law. Therefore, I 
continue my review of this case with an analysis of the defendant’s second asserted 
affirmative defense. The alleged date of injury in this matter is September 1, 2019. In 
2017, significant changes were applied to Iowa Code Chapter 85. These changes 
became effective on July 1, 2017.   
 
Iowa Code 85.26 states: 
An original proceeding for benefits under this chapter or chapter 85A, 85B, 
or 86, shall not be maintained in any contested case unless the proceeding 
is commenced within two years from the date of the occurrence of the injury 
for which benefits are claimed, or if weekly compensation benefits are paid 
under section 86.13, within three years from the date of the last payment of 
weekly compensation benefits. For the purposes of this section “date of the 
occurrence of the injury” means the date that the employee knew or should 
have known that the injury was work-related.   
 

PENA V. WELLS ENTERPRISES, INC. 
Page 15 
 
Iowa Code section 85.26(1). Prior to the 2017 statutory changes, the common law 
discovery rule was often applied when analyzing an affirmative defense under Iowa 
Code section 85.26. The Iowa Supreme Court clarified the application of Iowa Code 
section 85.26 in a recent case in noting that, “[t]he general assembly has chosen to limit 
the discovery rule to knowledge that an injury is work-related, not to knowledge of the 
compensable nature of the injury…” Tweeten v. Tweeten, 999 N.W.2d 270, 281 (Iowa 
2023). Therefore, “[f]or purposes of section 85.26, the two-year statutory period begins 
to run when the employee knows or should know that an injury is work-related, without 
regard to whether the injury is also serious enough to be compensable.” Id. at 282.   
 
In this case, Ms. Pena asserts a September 1, 2019 date of injury. Wells asserts 
that no benefits were ever paid regarding this date of injury, as testified to by Mr. 
Robinson. Ms. Pena provided no evidence to rebut this claim. Ms. Pena began 
treatment for her left foot issue on April 3, 2020. During that visit, she alleged feeling 
pain in her left foot in February of 2020. She confirmed during her deposition that she 
believed she injured her left foot on April 3, 2020. During an April 17, 2020, visit with Dr. 
Cassens, she then noted an injury in September of 2019. She confirmed her allegation 
of a September 1, 2019 date of injury during her deposition.   
 
It was not until February 10, 2023, that Ms. Pena filed her original notice and 
petition asserting the September 1, 2019 date of injury. In being as charitable as 
possible to Ms. Pena’s claims, the latest possible date that Ms. Pena could know that 
her injury was work related was April 3, 2020, when she first began treatment for 
complaints of left foot pain. Even this contradicts her deposition testimony. However, 
February 10, 2023, is more than two years from even the latest date upon which Ms. 
Pena knew or should have known her injury was work-related. Additionally, the 
defendant has proven, by a preponderance of the evidence, that they paid no weekly 
compensation benefits for a September 1, 2019 date of injury. Their benefits payments 
related to an alleged April 3, 2020 date of injury, which was previously tried.   
 
Based upon the foregoing, I find that the defendant proved, by a preponderance 
of the evidence, that Ms. Pena failed to file her petition within two years of the date of 
the occurrence of the injury. Therefore, Ms. Pena’s claims are barred by Iowa Code 
section 85.26.   
 
Considering the foregoing analysis based upon both of the affirmative defenses 
submitted by the defendant, there is no need to engage in any further analysis of the 
asserted issues.
