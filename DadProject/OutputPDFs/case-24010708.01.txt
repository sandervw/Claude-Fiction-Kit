STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A. The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Noelle McClain. 
Claimant appeared through her attorney, Darin Luneckas. Defendants appeared 
through their attorney, Timothy Wegman. 
 The alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on September 2, 2025. 
The proceedings were digitally recorded. That recording constitutes the official record of 
this proceeding. Pursuant to the Commissioner’s July 21, 2023, Order, the undersigned 
has been delegated authority to issue a final agency decision in this alternate medical 
care proceeding. Therefore, this ruling is designated final agency action and any appeal 
of the decision would be to the Iowa District Court pursuant to Iowa Code section 17A. 
The record consists of Claimant’s Exhibit 1-3, Defendants’ Exhibit A, and the 
testimony of the claimant. Counsel were permitted an opportunity to argue their cases, 
and the record closed on September 2, 2025.

due to ongoing post-concussive symptoms, headaches, dizziness, and change in 
mental health arising out of and in the course of her employment on or about October 9,

Defendants accepted responsibility for the head and whole person including 
headaches but denied liability for cognitive function and mental health issues.  
Before any benefits can be ordered, including medical benefits, compensability of 
the claim must be established, either by admission of liability or by adjudication.  The 
summary provisions of Iowa Code section 85.27 as more particularly described in rule 
876 IAC 4.48 are not designed to adjudicate disputed compensability of a claim.  
Defendants cannot deny liability and simultaneously direct the course of 
treatment.  Barnhart v. MAQ Incorporated, I Iowa Industrial Comm’r Report 16 (App. 
March 9, 1981). 
Because authorization for the cognitive function and mental health have been 
denied, the alternate care request related to those injuries is dismissed and not 
considered at hearing. 
ISSUE 
 The issue presented for resolution is whether the claimant is entitled to a finding 
that defendants abandoned care and that she may continue care with PCI Neurology 
and Frankie Dunbar, ARNP.  
FINDINGS OF FACT 
 The undersigned, having considered all the evidence in the record, finds: 
Claimant Noelle McClain suffered a head injury arising out of and in the course of 
employment on October 9, 2024. For the purposes of the alternate care proceeding   
defendants accepted the injury to the head and whole person and began to provide care 
with, among other healthcare providers, Shirley Pospisil, M.D., MPH. (Ex.  2)  
 Dr. Pospisil originally referred claimant for a neurology consult on December 23, 
2024. (Ex. 2) Claimant testified at hearing that she was in contact with an agent from 
Gallagher Bassett, a third-party administrator. At least three times, the agent from GB 
led claimant to believe that she would be seen by a neurologist as recommended by Dr. 
Pospisil, but those appointments never came to fruition. In the spring of 2025, claimant 
was sent for an IME with Michael Jacoby, M.D., who noted that claimant “may benefit 
from evaluation and treatment by a headache specialist to reduce unnecessary 
headache prolongation and disability.” (Ex 2)  
Claimant was sent to an ophthalmology neurologist who could not pinpoint the 
etiology of the claimant’s complaints. No further care was offered.  
 Finally, claimant sought treatment on her own with Frankie Dunbar, ARNP, at 
PCI Neurology Clinic. Claimant testified that the care provided by ARNP Dunbar

REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
  Under Iowa law, the employer is required to provide care to an injured 
employee and is permitted to choose the care. Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 
562 N.W.2d 433 (Iowa 1997). 
[T]he employer is obliged to furnish reasonable services and supplies to 
treat an injured employee, and has the right to choose the care .... The 
treatment must be offered promptly and be reasonably suited to treat the 
injury without undue inconvenience to the employee. If the employee has 
reason to be dissatisfied with the care offered, the employee should 
communicate the basis of such dissatisfaction to the employer, in writing if 
requested, following which the employer and the employee may agree to 
alternate care reasonably suited to treat the injury. If the employer and 
employee cannot agree on such alternate care, the commissioner may, 
upon application and reasonable proofs of the necessity therefor, allow 
and order other care. 
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment - and seeking alternate care - 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable. See Iowa 
R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e); Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995). 
Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact. (Id.) The 
employer’s obligation turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability. 
(Id.); Harned v. Farmland Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983).

 An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving. Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care. Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant. Long, 528 N.W.2d at 122. 
 An employer's right to select the provider of medical treatment to an injured 
worker does not include the right to determine how an injured worker should be 
diagnosed, evaluated, treated, or other matters of professional medical judgment. 
Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 866389 (Declaratory Ruling, May 18, 1988). 
Reasonable care includes care necessary to diagnose the condition and 
defendants are not entitled to interfere with the medical judgment of its own treating 
physician. Pote v. Mickow Corp., File No. 694639 (Review-Reopening, June 17, 1986). 
When a designated physician refers a patient to another physician, that physician 
acts as the defendant employer's agent. Permission for the referral from defendant is 
not necessary. Kittrell v. Allen Memorial Hospital, Thirty-fourth Biennial Report of the 
Industrial Commissioner, 164 (Arb. November 1, 1979) (aff'd by industrial 
commissioner); See also Limoges v. Meier Auto Salvage, I Iowa Industrial 
Commissioner Reports 207 (1981). 
 In this case, defendants have authorized medical care, but have not followed the 
recommendations of claimant’s authorized treating physician.  Defendants have not 
authorized prompt medical care or care that is reasonably suited to treat claimant's 
injury. At this time, no care is being proffered to claimant. There are no appointments 
set, and no doctor has been identified. Defendants' actions are an interference with the 
treating physician's recommendations.  Therefore, I conclude that claimant has 
established entitlement to alternate medical care. 
Defendants were permitted to select the authorized medical provider, Dr. 
Pospisil. Iowa Code section 85.27(4).  Having exercised that right to select the 
authorized provider, defendants may not ignore Dr. Pospisil’s recommendations.  
Defendants may not interfere with Dr. Pospisil’s professional medical judgment, whether 
by design or delay.  Given that claimant has already been waiting nearly eight months 
for authorization of a neurology consult, it is found that claimant has proven that there 
has been an unreasonable delay in providing care.  
However, since defendants are willing to authorize care and have identified a 
treatment facility at UIHC, it cannot be said that they have abandoned care; but as there 
is no scheduled appointment, it would be unreasonable to disallow claimant to continue 
care with ARNP Dunbar until new care has been established. Claimant is not expected 
to return to ARNP Dunbar for three months, but ARNP Dunbar has recommended 
additional treatment such as physical therapy and a referral to Physical Medicine and 
Rehabilitation that should be allowed.

ORDER 
 THEREFORE, IT IS ORDERED: 
 Claimant’s petition for alternate medical care is granted. 
Defendants shall promptly authorize and pay for a neurology consult with the 
UIHC as soon as practicably possible. Until new care is established, defendants shall 
authorize and pay for treatment with ARNP Dunbar at PCI Neurology and physical 
therapy at Rock Valley in Cedar Rapids.  
IT IS, THEREFORE, ORDERED that the request for care for cognitive function 
and mental health should be and is hereby dismissed without prejudice. 
IT IS FURTHER ORDERED that if claimant seeks to recover the charges 
incurred in obtaining care for a condition for which defendants denied liability, 
defendants are barred from asserting lack of authorization as a defense to those 
charges.
