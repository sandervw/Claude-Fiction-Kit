This is a judicial review action of a decision by the Commissioner of the Iowa 
Workers’ Compensation Commission regarding jurisdiction of the agency action. The 
Court held a hearing on the matter on December 19, 2025. Andrew W. Bribriesco and 
Sophia R. Gustafson appeared on behalf of the Petitioner, Donald Heckman (“Heckman”). 
Sarah Timko appeared on behalf of Respondent Second Injury Fund of Iowa. Delaney 
Doyle and Caitlin Kilburg appeared on behalf of Respondent, Fives, Inc (“Fives”).  
STATEMENT OF THE CASE  
Donald Heckman was employed by Fives, Inc. as an installation and service 
technician beginning in 2013. (D0012, Arb. Decision, Cert. Rec. at 46–47). His 
employment required regular travel throughout the United States and internationally to 
install, service, and repair equipment at customer locations. (D0014, Transcript, Cert. 
Rec. at 38–39). Heckman resided in Iowa and routinely returned to Iowa between work 
assignments. (D0014, Transcript, Cert. Rec. at 16–17, 20; D0012, Arb. Decision, Cert. 
Rec. at 47).

On October 19, 2016, Heckman sustained a work-related injury while working for

In 2021, Heckman left his employment with Fives to work for another employer. 
(D0014, Transcript, Cert. Rec. at 11–12). In November 2021, Fives rehired Heckman as

authorized and paid for medical treatment, including physical therapy in Iowa, and

STANDARD OF REVIEW 
The Iowa Administrative Procedure Act (IAPA), Iowa Code chapter 17A, governs 
the scope of the Court’s review in workers' compensation cases. Iowa Code § 86.26 
(2021); Meyer v. IBP, Inc., 710 N.W.2d 213, 218 (Iowa 2006). The Court’s review of final 
agency action is “severely circumscribed.”  Sellers v. Emp. Appeal Bd., 531 N.W.2d 645, 
646 (Iowa Ct. App. 1995).  Nearly all disputes are won or lost at the agency level; the 
cardinal rule of administrative law is that judgment calls are within the province of the 
administrative tribunal, not the courts. See id.  
 “Under the [IAPA], we may only interfere with the commissioner's decision if it is 
erroneous under one of the grounds enumerated in the statute, and a party's substantial 
rights have been prejudiced.” Meyer, 710 N.W.2d at 218. The party challenging agency 
action bears the burden of demonstrating the action's invalidity and resulting prejudice.

Iowa Code § 17A.19(8)(a).  This can be shown in a number of ways, including proof the 
action was ultra vires; legally erroneous; unsupported by substantial evidence in the 
record when that record is viewed as a whole; or otherwise, unreasonable, arbitrary, 
capricious, or an abuse of discretion. See id. § 17A.19(10). The district court acts in an 
appellate capacity to correct errors of law on the part of the agency. Grundmeyer v. 
Weyerhaeuser Co., 649 N.W.2d 744, 748 (Iowa 2002). 
A court retains the power to determine whether it has jurisdiction over the cases 
before it. See Tigges v. City of Ames, 356 N.W.2d 503, 512 (Iowa 1985). Thus, review of 
decisions finding subject matter jurisdiction is for the correction of errors at law. Id. 
However, a Court will “examine an agency’s legal conclusions for soundness even when 
related to jurisdiction.” Niday v. Roehl Transp., Inc., 928 N.W.2d 659 (Table), 2019 WL 
1486603, *9 n.3 (Iowa Ct. App. 2019). 
ANALYSIS 
 
A. Iowa Code Section 85.71 
The central issue on judicial review is whether the Iowa Workers’ Compensation 
Commissioner correctly interpreted Iowa Code section 85.71. Specifically, whether the 
agency retained subject matter jurisdiction over claims involving injuries sustained 
outside the borders of Iowa.  
Subject matter jurisdiction refers to the authority of a tribunal to hear and determine 
cases of a specific topic. Heartland Express v. Gardner, 675 N.W.2d 259, 262 (Iowa 
2003). Although the Workers’ Compensation Commissioner has exclusive jurisdiction 
over workers’ compensation claims generally, that authority exists only to the extent 
granted and limited by the legislature. Id. at 262–63. The Iowa Supreme Court has

recognized Iowa Code section 85.71 as an express limitation on the Commissioner’s 
jurisdiction over claims arising from extraterritorial injuries. Id. at 263 n.3; Heartland 
Express, Inc. v. Terry, 631 N.W.2d 260, 265 (Iowa 2001). 
Iowa appellate courts have interpreted section 85.71 as a statute governing subject 
matter jurisdiction. See George H. Wentz, Inc. v. Sabasta, 337 N.W.2d 495, 500 (Iowa 
1983); Christian v. Hirschbach Motor Lines, Inc., 496 N.W.2d 270, 274 (Iowa 1992); 
Henriksen v. Younglove Constr., 540 N.W.2d 254, 258 (Iowa 1995); Heartland Express, 
675 N.W.2d at 263 n.3; Annett Holdings, Inc. v. Allen, 738 N.W.2d 647, 649 (Iowa 2007). 
Under these decisions, the Commissioner doesn’t have the authority to adjudicate an out-
of-state injury unless the claimant establishes one of the statutory bases outlined in 
section 85.71(1). Id.  
In this case, Heckman relies on the 2008 amendment to section 85.71, which 
added subsection (2), providing that the statute shall be construed to confer personal 
jurisdiction over an employee or employer to whom the section is applicable. Iowa Code 
§ 85.71(2). The Commissioner rejected the argument that this amendment transformed 
section 85.71 into a personal-jurisdiction-only statute. 
In Niday v. Roehl Transp., Inc., the Court noted that section 85.71 resembles a 
long-arm statute following the 2008 amendment. 934 N.W.2d 29, 36 n.5 (Iowa Ct. App. 
2019) (Emphasis added). However, this note does not compel this court to come to a 
different result from the Commissioner. In Niday, the Court did not overrule or disapprove 
the Iowa Supreme Court’s repeated characterization of section 85.71 as a subject matter 
jurisdiction statute. Id. The Commissioner properly followed binding Supreme Court 
precedent rather than the note from an unpublished appellate decision.

Accordingly, the Commissioner did not err in interpreting Iowa Code section 85.71 
as governing the agency’s subject matter jurisdiction over extraterritorial injuries. 
B. Application of Iowa Code Section 85.71 
Because section 85.71 governs the Commissioner’s subject matter jurisdiction 
over extraterritorial injuries, Petitioner holds the burden of proving that one of the statutory 
conditions in section 85.71(1) is satisfied. See LaRose v. Curoe, 343 N.W.2d 153 (Iowa 
1983); Annett Holdings, 738 N.W.2d at 650. 
Iowa Code section 85.71(1)(b) and (d) provides:  
1. If an employee, while working outside the territorial limits of this state, suffers  
an injury on account of which the employee, or in the event of death, the  
employee’s dependents, would have been entitled to the benefits provided by this  
chapter had such injury occurred within this state, such employee, or in the event  
of death resulting from such injury, the employee’s dependents, shall be entitled  
to the benefits provided by this chapter, if at the time of such injury any of the  
following is applicable: 
. . .  
b. The employee is working under a contract of hire made in this state and the  
employee regularly works in this state. 
. . .  
d. The employee is working under a contract of hire made in this state for  
employment outside the United States. 
. . .  
Iowa Code Ann. § 85.71. Substantial evidence supports the Commissioner’s 
determination that Petitioner failed to establish the requirements of Iowa Code section 
85.71(1)(b) or (d).  
Heckman failed to establish that he “regularly worked” in the State of Iowa under 
Iowa Code section 85.71(1)(b). Heckman’s employment was travel-based and he did not 
perform work in any one state more than another. Iowa was not a usual or customary 
location for Heckman’s work. While Heckman performed incidental administrative tasks

at his Iowa residence, those tasks were not required to be performed in Iowa. Rather, the 
evidence showed that such tasks could be completed at customer sites or while traveling. 
Heckman also failed to establish jurisdiction under Iowa Code section 85.71(1)(d). 
Although his contract of hire was formed in Iowa, the evidence showed it was for 
employment both within and outside the United States, not solely for employment outside 
the United States. Heckman had not performed any work outside the United States 
between his return to employment in December 2021 and his January 2022 injury. As the 
Commissioner found, section 85.71(1)(d) was enacted to prevent gaps in workers’ 
compensation coverage for employees injured abroad, not to provide an alternative forum 
when coverage is already available. Because Heckman’s injury occurred domestically 
and was compensable under Wisconsin law, no such gap existed. 
The Commissioner applied the correct legal standards and did not act irrationally, 
illogically, or wholly unjustifiably in concluding that Petitioner did not regularly work in Iowa 
for purposes of subsection (1)(b) and did not satisfy subsection (1)(d). See Meyer v. IBP, 
Inc., 710 N.W.2d 213, 219 (Iowa 2006); Larson Mfg. Co. v. Thorson, 763 N.W.2d 842, 850 
(Iowa 2009). 
C. Waiver or Consent 
Having correctly concluded that Iowa Code section 85.71 limits the agency’s 
subject matter jurisdiction, jurisdiction could not be waived or conferred by consent of the 
parties. See Klinge v. Bentien, 725 N.W.2d 13, 15–16 (Iowa 2006). The Commissioner 
therefore did not err in declining to find waiver or consent. 
CONCLUSION

and the final agency action of the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commissioner is
