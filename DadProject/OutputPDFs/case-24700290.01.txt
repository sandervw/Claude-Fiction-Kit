STATEMENT OF THE CASE   
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A. The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Billy Anderson. 
Claimant appeared telephonically and through his attorney, Mark Hedberg. Defendants 
appeared through their attorney, Rebecca Duffy.   
The alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on April 17, 2024. The 
proceedings were digitally recorded. That recording constitutes the official record of this 
proceeding. Pursuant to the Commissioner’s July 21, 2023 Order, the undersigned has 
been delegated authority to issue a final agency decision in this alternate medical care 
proceeding. Therefore, this ruling is designated final agency action, and any appeal of 
the decision would be to the Iowa District Court pursuant to Iowa Code section 17A. 
The record consists of claimant’s exhibit 1 (attached to the petition), consisting of 
two pages, and defendants’ exhibits A through D, consisting of eight pages. Claimant 
provided testimony. No other witnesses were called. Counsel offered oral arguments to 
support their positions, and defendants have also submitted a brief. 
ISSUE 
The issue presented for resolution is whether the claimant is entitled to alternate 
medical care consisting of authorization for medical treatment with Daniel Larose, M.D., 
including potential left shoulder surgery.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned having considered all of the testimony and evidence in the 
record finds: 
Claimant sustained an injury to his left shoulder and wrist when he slipped and 
fell at work on December 16, 2022. Defendants have accepted liability for the injury. 
With respect to the left shoulder, claimant initially saw Scott Reynolds, M.D., at Ortho 
Nebraska. He was diagnosed with left shoulder adhesive capsulitis with a partial rotator 
cuff tear. (Defendants’ Exhibit A, p. 1) He went through conservative treatment, 
including physical therapy and a cortisone injection, which were not helpful. Claimant 
testified that the cortisone injection elevated his blood sugar. Medical records support 
his testimony. 
Claimant has an approximately 20-year history of diabetes. He also has stage 3 
kidney disease. He testified that prior to the work injury, his health was generally good, 
and his A1c was generally around 7.7 or 7.8. He was able to work out three or four days 
per week for 30 to 60 minutes each time, and his diabetes was generally under control. 
Since the work injury, his weight has increased, and he is not able to work out. As a 
result, his A1c levels have gone up, and he requires additional medications to manage 
his diabetes. At the time of hearing, he testified that his A1c was around 7.8 or 7.9. 
 Claimant saw Dr. Reynolds on May 2, 2023. (Def. Ex. A, p. 1) At that time, Dr. 
Reynolds noted that claimant’s diabetes was not well controlled. He also noted 
claimant’s chronic kidney disease, and stated that claimant was potentially going to 
receive a kidney transplant. Claimant testified that no doctor has ever mentioned a 
kidney transplant to him, and he does not know where that information came from. In 
any event, Dr. Reynolds opined that claimant “must get his elevated hemoglobin A1c 
under control before we even think about doing any kind of surgery. Otherwise, it is just 
not worthwhile.” (Def. Ex. A, p. 2) He noted that claimant was not able to exercise much 
at that time, but instructed him to talk to his primary care provider about other ways to 
get his A1c under control. 
Claimant testified that he underwent a shoulder manipulation at some point in 
August 2023. Dr. Reynold’s record, dated November 7, 2023, indicates he had a left 
shoulder manipulation under anesthesia and steroid injection on August 9, 2023. (Def. 
Ex. B, p. 3) Unfortunately, he continued to experience persistent left shoulder pain. 
Claimant testified that the procedure helped for a couple of weeks, but then his pain 
returned. The record also indicates claimant was seeing Dr. Gaddie for his wrist injury, 
and that he believed surgery would be the next step for his wrist. Claimant testified that 
his wrist surgery was scheduled, but cancelled after the insurance carrier denied it due 
to his A1c levels.  
Dr. Reynolds did not have anything additional to offer other than more physical 
therapy, so he and claimant agreed he would get a second opinion. (Def. Ex. B, p. 4) To 
that end, claimant saw Eric Bonness, M.D., on December 5, 2023. (Def. Ex. C, p. 5) Dr. 
Bonness is also at Ortho Nebraska. Dr. Bonness agreed with the diagnosis of adhesive

capsulitis, and told claimant that the condition is a “95% self-limiting process and, 
overall, his prognosis is good.” (Def. Ex. C, p. 6) He noted claimant’s most recent

Claimant returned to Dr. Bonness on February 2, 2024. (Def. Ex. D, p. 7) At that

He stated that hopefully, claimant could get to 7.5 or lower, at which time he they could 
discuss repeat injections versus arthroscopic release and manipulation.  
Claimant testified that he asked the insurance adjustor on his claim if he could 
get a second opinion from a doctor unaffiliated with Ortho Nebraska, but his request 
was denied. Due to his ongoing pain and difficulties, he decided to seek another opinion 
on his own. Claimant saw Daniel Larose, M.D., on February 13, 2024. (Claimant’s 
Exhibit 1, p. 1) Dr. Larose’s impression was “severe capsulitis on [sic] a diabetic patient, 
left shoulder.” His recommendation was for claimant to “work as hard as possible” to 
bring his A1c closer to normal. However, if he was unable to bring it “completely 
normal,” Dr. Larose indicated he would still feel comfortable recommending an 
arthroscopy, capsular release, followed by physical therapy. He also recommended 
stopping physical therapy at that time, as it did not appear to be effective.  
Claimant testified that he is willing to accept the additional risks of surgery given 
his diabetes and kidney disease, because he wants to get his shoulder fixed. He 
testified that he works with restrictions due to his shoulder. He is also unable to 
participate in sporting activities with his teenage daughter, which is something he used 
to do regularly. He feels that his treatment has been dragging on for a year and a half 
and is frustrated with his lack of progress. He testified that Dr. Larose told him he would 
do the surgery in one month even if his A1c was not at 7.5. 
REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Iowa Code section 85.27(4) provides, in relevant part: 
For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish 
reasonable services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has 
the right to choose the care. . . .  The treatment must be offered promptly 
and be reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience 
to the employee. If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the 
care offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such

dissatisfaction to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the 
employer and the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited 
to treat the injury. If the employer and employee cannot agree on such 
alternate care, the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable 
proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
Iowa Code § 85.27(4). 
Defendants’ “obligation under the statute is confined to reasonable care for the 
diagnosis and treatment of work-related injuries.” Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 
N.W.2d 122, 124 (Iowa 1995) (emphasis in original). In other words, the “obligation 
under the statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.” Id. 
Similarly, an application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained 
because claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving. Mere 
dissatisfaction with the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for 
alternate medical care. Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered 
promptly, was not reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly 
inconvenient for the claimant. See Iowa Code § 85.27(4). Thus, by challenging the 
employer’s choice of treatment and seeking alternate care, claimant assumes the 
burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable. See Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e); Long, 528 N.W.2d at 124.   
Ultimately, determining whether care is reasonable under the statute is a 
question of fact.  Long, 528 N.W.2d at 123. 
In this case, claimant has not met his burden to prove that the care offered by 
defendants is unreasonable. Defendants are providing care through the doctors at 
Ortho Nebraska. There is no evidence that the treatment recommendations being made 
are unreasonable. In fact, Dr. Larose has recommended essentially the same treatment. 
The only difference is that Dr. Larose appears willing to perform surgery even if 
claimant’s A1c is not “completely normal.” It is understandable that claimant is frustrated 
with his ongoing pain and limitations, but his desire for treatment and surgery with Dr. 
Larose is not the legal standard for granting alternate care. There is no evidence that 
the care defendants are providing is not being offered promptly, is not reasonably suited 
to treat the injury, or is unduly inconvenient for the claimant. Rather, the evidence 
demonstrates that defendants are meeting their obligation to provide reasonable care 
for the work-related injury.  
Because claimant has not met his burden of proving the authorized care is 
unreasonable, his petition for alternate care must be denied. 
ORDER 
Therefore it is ordered: 
The claimant's petition for alternate medical care is denied.
