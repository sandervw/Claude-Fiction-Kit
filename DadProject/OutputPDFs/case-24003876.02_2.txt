I: ALLEGATIONS OF THE PETITION  
 
 
The Petitioner asks for judicial review pursuant to Iowa Code §§17A.19 and 86.26 
to review of the final agency action of the Iowa Workers' Compensation Commission as 
set forth in the February 10, 2025 Alternate Medical Care Decision ordering dismissal of 
the alternate medical care case.  
 
The Petitioner, in his brief, argued two issues: 1) whether the Commissioner’s 
order violated the constitutional right to due process and 2) whether the Commissioner’s 
dismissal was an erroneous interpretation of the agency’s rule. At hearing, the Petitioner 
conceded that the constitutional argument was not preserved for review and withdrew it 
from consideration.

II. FINDINGS OF FACTS AND PROCEDURAL HISTORY 
 
 
On October 13, 2023, the Petitioner sustained work related injuries to his left 
shoulder, left arm and cervical spine. The Defendants authorized Dr. Kari to treat the 
Petitioner for these injuries. Dr. Kari diagnosed the Petitioner with Complex Regional Pain 
Syndrome (“CRPS”) as part of that treatment. Dr. Kari recommended a trial of a cervical 
spinal cord stimulator. The Defendants denied authorization for the stimulator.  
The Defendants admit liability for the Petitioner’s injury to his left arm, left shoulder, 
and cervical spine but denied the requested medical treatment was causally related to his 
work injury and deny the underlying condition of CRPS was causally related to his work 
injury, based on the opinions of Dr. Joseph Chen.  
On or about January 29, 2025, the Petitioner filed a petition for alternate medical 
care with the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commission. The Petitioner alleged the 
Respondents denied the recommendation of the Petitioner’s authorized treating physician 
for a trial cervical spinal cord stimulator. The Defendants resisted. The Deputy 
Commission dismissed the petition for alternative medical care without a hearing, finding 
the Defendants explicitly disputed the diagnosis of CRPS, a sequela injury, rather than 
disputing the necessity of treatment for CRPS.

The Petitioner argues that he is entitled to a full evidentiary hearing. He further 
asserts the Commission interpreted Iowa Administrative Rule 876-4.48(6) more broadly 
than the language provides. 
 
Rule 876-4.48 sets forth the rules on applications for alternative care, in 
conjunction with Iowa Code § 85.27(4). Applications cannot be filed under this Rule 876-
4.48 if the liability of the employer is an issue and if so, the application is dismissed without 
prejudice. Iowa Administrative Code Rule 876-4.48(6).  
The Iowa Supreme Court dealt with a similar case on a petition for alternative 
medical care in R.R. Donnelly & Sons v. Barnett, 670 N.W.2d 190 (Iowa 2003). In that 
case, Barnett petitioned the commission for alternative medical care when R.R. Donnelly 
denied the recommended treatment. After a hearing, the deputy commissioner dismissed 
the application finding that a portion of Barnett’s claim was psychological and not related 
to the work injury and alternative medical care provisions were not available as long as 
R.R. Donnelly denied liability for a portion of the disability claim.  
The Iowa Supreme Court found that the issue of compensability is “totally 
removed” from the alternative medical care process. R.R. Donnelly & Sons, 670 N.W.2d 
at 196. If this issue does arise, the commissioner cannot order alternate care. The Court 
found that when there was substantial evidence R.R. Donnelly denied compensability of 
a condition or injury Barrett suffered, this was sufficient to support the deputy 
commissioner’s decision to dismiss the application without deciding the merits. Id. at 198.  
In this case, the Defendants point to the reports of Dr. Chen in the commission’s 
record. Dr. Chen explicitly denies a causal connection between the Petitioner’s injury and 
the CRPS diagnosis. As Dr. Chen’s reports provide substantial evidence in support of the
