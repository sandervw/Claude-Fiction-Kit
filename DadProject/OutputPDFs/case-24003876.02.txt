 On January 29, 2025 claimant filed a petition for alternate medical care seeking 
treatment recommended by the authorized treating physician Dr. Maruti Kari. On 
February 5, 2025, defendants filed an answer and motion to dismiss on the basis that 
they were contesting liability for the condition sought to be treated. 
The hearing was held as scheduled on February 7, 2025 at 8:30 AM. According 
to the filings, as well as the statements of counsel, claimant sustained an injury to his 
left shoulder, left arm and cervical spine. In the course of treatment, Dr. Kari diagnosed 
claimant as suffering from complex regional pain syndrome arising out of the 
constellation of injury sustained at a work event on October 13, 2023. To treat this 
diagnosis, Dr. Kari has recommended a trial of a cervical spinal cord stimulator. 
Defendants have refused to provide this treatment, requesting that claimant be seen by 
an independent medical examiner to confirm or reject that the CRPS is related to the 
work injury of October 13, 2023. 
Defendants argue that on the basis of their denial, the alternate medical care 
proceeding should be dismissed. Claimant argues that the condition of CRPS is a 
symptom of the accepted work injuries and therefore the defendants cannot deny care 
for the CRPS and that their actions are prohibited by well-accepted caselaw that deems 
interference with the medical decisions of an authorized treating physician is 
unreasonable.  
Right to choose the care means the right to choose the provider, not the 
treatment modalities recommended by the provider.  Employer must provide the 
treatment, testing, imaging or other treatment modalities recommended by its own

authorized treating physician, even if another consulting physician disagrees with those 
recommendations.  Haack v. Von Hoffman Graphics, File No. 1268172. p. 9 (App. July 
31, 2002) [MRI and x-rays];  Cahill v. S & H Fabricating & Engineering, File No. 
1138063 (Alt Care, May 30, 1997) (work hardening program);  Hawxby v. Hallett 
Materials, File No. 1112821 (Alt Care February 20, 1996);  Leitzen v. Collis, Inc., File 
No. 1084677 (Alt Care, September 9, 1996).  The right to choose the care does not 
authorize the employer to interfere with the medical judgment of its own treating 
physician.  Boggs v. Cargill, Inc., File No. 1050396 (Alt Care, January 31, 1994). 
The parties were asked to submit additional briefing. Defendants presented R.R. 
Donnelly & Sons v. Barnett, 670 N.W.2d 190 (Iowa 2003). The fact pattern of Barnett 
closely matches the fact pattern in this case. In Barnett, the worker’s left hand was 
crushed in a machine. (Id. at 193) Several doctors diagnosed her with CRPS. (Id. at 
193). The authorized treating physician referred claimant to another doctor for a second 
opinion. The second doctor recommended a trial use of a Medtronic SynchroMed 
Infusion System. The matter went before the agency on an alternate care petition and 
the deputy commissioner dismissed the petition on the basis of contested liability. (Id. at 
194) 
The facts are similar in this case. Claimant sustained injuries to the shoulder and 
arm and one doctor has diagnosed CRPS as a sequelae symptom. (Claimant’s Exhibit 
1:3)  
However, another treating physician has opined that claimant does not have 
CRPS, but rather a brachial plexus injury. (Defendants’ Exhibit A:3) The alternate care 
procedures are not designed to determine causation.  
Claimant offered McClish v. The Presbyterian Village, File No. 5060897 (Alt. 
Care, March 12, 2018) as support for the position that the condition that claimant seeks 
to be treated is not separate from the accepted work injuries, but that defendants are 
seeking to deny a treatment. In McClish, the defendants accepted claimant’s work injury 
to her low back. The medical provider recommended three treatments, and defendants 
agreed to provide all but the MRI myelogram, arguing that the MRI myelogram could 
wait until a surgeon determined the necessity of it. (Id. at 16). This is not the same fact 
pattern as the case at hand. Defendants are explicitly disputing a diagnosis of CRPS, a 
sequelae injury, rather than disputing the necessity of treatment for CRPS.  
The defendants thereby lose their right to control the medical care claimant 
seeks in this proceeding and the claimant is free to choose that care on his own. Bell 
Bros. Heating & Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d 193 (Iowa 2010). The 
defendants hereby lose their authorization defense on any treatment the claimant 
receives from this point forward. 
 IT IS, THEREFORE, ORDERED that this case should be and is hereby 
dismissed without prejudice.
