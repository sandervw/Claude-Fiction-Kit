STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Skyler Terry, claimant, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from Perry’s Service, Inc., employer, and Insurance Company of 
the West, insurance carrier, and the Second Injury Fund of Iowa, as defendants. 
Hearing was held via Zoom videoconference on September 4, 2024.  Prior to the date of 
the hearing the claimant and the employer and its insurance carrier reached a 
settlement.  Therefore, the employer and insurance carrier did not participate in the 
hearing.     
The claimant and the Second Injury Fund of Iowa filed a hearing report at the 
commencement of the arbitration hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered 
into various stipulations.  All of those stipulations were accepted and are hereby 
incorporated into this arbitration decision.  The parties are now bound by their 
stipulations.  
Skyler Terry was the only witness to testify live at trial.  The evidentiary record 
also includes Joint Exhibits 1-8, Claimant’s Exhibits 9-14, and Defendant’s Exhibits AA-
EE.  Claimant objected to Defendant’s Exhibit DD, a report and C.V. from Joseph Chen, 
M.D., because it was not produced to claimant until August 20, 2024, after the 30-day 
exchange deadline.  Claimant argued he was prejudiced because he was not able to 
present the report to either of the treating physicians in this case or to claimant’s IME 
doctor for a rebuttal report.  Defendant’s Exhibit DD was admitted into the record.  
However, in an attempt to mitigate any prejudice caused to the claimant, the record was 
left open for the claimant to obtain rebuttal evidence.  (Hearing Transcript pages 10-11)  
All other exhibits were received without objection.  On October 23, 2024, claimant filed 
rebuttal evidence labeled Claimant’s Exhibit 15, p. 188, a report from John D. Kuhnlein, 
D.O.  The evidentiary record closed on October 23, 2024.       
The parties submitted post-hearing briefs on December 4, 2024, at which time 
the case was fully submitted to the undersigned.

ISSUES 
The parties submitted the following issues for resolution: 
1. Whether claimant sustained a first qualifying loss to his eyes.  If so, the extent 
of loss.   
2. Whether claimant sustained a compensable loss to his left arm on September

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based. A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause. A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable, 
rather than merely possible. George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
The question of medical causation is “essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony.” Cedar Rapids Cmty. Sch. Dist. v. Pease, 807 N.W.2d 839, 844-45 (Iowa 
2011). The commissioner, as the trier of fact, must “weigh the evidence and measure 
the credibility of witnesses.” Id. The trier of fact may accept or reject expert testimony, 
even if uncontroverted, in whole or in part. Frye, 569 N.W.2d at 156. When considering 
the weight of an expert opinion, the fact-finder may consider whether the examination 
occurred shortly after the claimant was injured, the compensation arrangement, the 
nature and extent of the examination, the expert's education, experience, training, and 
practice, and “all other factors which bear upon the weight and value” of the 
opinion. Rockwell Graphic Sys., Inc. v. Prince, 366 N.W.2d 187, 192 (Iowa 1985). 
Unrebutted expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland 
Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994).  
Claimant seeks benefits from the Second Injury Fund of Iowa.  Iowa Code 
section 85.64 governs Second Injury Fund liability.  Before liability of the Fund is 
triggered, three requirements must be met.  First, the employee must have lost or lost 
the use of a hand, arm, foot, leg, or eye.  Second, the employee must sustain a loss or 
loss of use of another specified member or organ through a compensable injury.  Third, 
permanent disability must exist as to both the initial injury and the second injury.   
The Iowa Supreme Court has explained the concept of the Second Injury Fund.  
The Court stated,

Page 8 
 
The milieu in which the second or subsequent injury fund statute operates 
is the situation of an individual within the labor force, whether or not he is 
at the moment an ‘employee’ within the meaning of the Workmen's 
Compensation Act, who is suffering from some condition of permanent 
disability that affects his employability. The source of this pre-existing 
disability is normally of no importance but it must be permanent and must 
tend to act as a hindrance to the individual's ability to obtain or retain 
effective employment. Such individuals are what society commonly refers 
to as ‘the handicapped,‘ and the primary purpose of second injury fund 
statutes is to encourage the hiring of the handicapped. 
Anderson v. Second Inj. Fund, 262 N.W.2d 789, 791 (Iowa 1978). 
The Iowa Supreme court has addressed what constitutes a first qualifying loss.  
The Court stated,  
To qualify as a first loss for purposes of section 85.64, the first loss does 
not need to be work-related. Shank, 516 N.W.2d at 812. Additionally, the 
first loss need not be a total loss or loss of use. Irish v. McCreary Saw Mill, 
175 N.W.2d 364, 368 (Iowa 1970). In 1990 the Fund again asked this 
court to hold that the first loss must be “at minimum, ‘a substantial loss 
that acts as a handicap to one's employability.’” Second Injury Fund v. 
Braden, 459 N.W.2d 467, 469 (Iowa 1990). In Braden we decided not to 
retreat from our prior position and held that a scheduled injury under 
section 85.34(2) affecting a hand, arm, foot, leg, or eye is a qualifying first 
loss. Id. at 469–70. The reason for allowing a scheduled injury affecting a 
hand, arm, foot, leg, or eye to be a qualifying first loss is because a 
scheduled injury evaluates an injured person's permanent partial disability 
by determining the loss of physiological capacity of the affected body part. 
Bergeson, 526 N.W.2d at 547. We see no reason to retreat from our long-
standing law that a scheduled injury under section 85.34(2) affecting a 
hand, arm, foot, leg, or eye is a qualifying loss. 
Second Inj. Fund of Iowa v. George, 737 N.W.2d 141, 146 (Iowa 2007)   
The first question is whether claimant lost the use of one of the enumerated 
members, in this case his right or left eye.  Claimant bears the burden of proving that 
permanent impairment resulted from the first injury, whether by a permanency rating, 
and/or by other credible evidence of permanency or work restrictions.  Haynes v. 
Second Injury Fund, 547 N.W.2d 11, 14 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996).   
Under the Iowa Workers' Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is 
compensated either for a loss or loss of use of a scheduled member under Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(a)-(u) or as an unscheduled injury pursuant to the provisions of section 
85.34(2)(v). An injury to the eye is considered a scheduled member injury under Iowa 
Code section 85.34(2)(q). The extent of scheduled member disability benefits to which 
an injured worker is entitled is determined by using the functional method. Functional 

TERRY V. SECOND INJURY FUND OF IOWA 
Page 9 
 
disability is “limited to the loss of the physiological capacity of the body or body part.” 
Mortimer v. Fruehauf Corp., 502 N.W.2d 12, 15 (Iowa 1993); Sherman v. Pella Corp., 
576 N.W.2d 312, 320 (Iowa 1998). 
The Fund contends that due to the 2017 legislative addition of subparagraph 
(2)(x) to section 85.34, an impairment rating under the Guides is required to prove a first 
qualifying loss.  The Fund argues that because the record is void of any expert 
assigning impairment that complies with the Guides, claimant failed to carry his burden 
to demonstrate he sustained a first qualifying loss.     
Iowa Code section 85.34(x) permanent disabilities states: 
x. In all cases of permanent partial disability described in paragraphs “a” 
through “u”, or paragraph “v” when determining functional disability and 
not loss of earning capacity, the extent of loss or percentage of permanent 
impairment shall be determined solely by utilizing the guides to the 
evaluation of permanent impairment, published by the American 
medical association, as adopted by the workers' compensation 
commissioner by rule pursuant to chapter 17A. Lay testimony or agency 
expertise shall not be utilized in determining loss or percentage of 
permanent impairment pursuant to paragraphs “a” through “u”, or 
paragraph “v” when determining functional disability and not loss of 
earning capacity. 
Iowa Code section 85.34 (x) (emphasis added). 
This agency has adopted the Guides to the Evaluation of Permanent Impairment, 
Fifth Edition, published by the American Medical Association for determining the extent 
of loss or percentage of impairment for permanent partial disabilities. See 876 IAC 2.4. 
This agency is bound by statute to only consider the functional disability ratings 
issued by the various medical providers. The Commissioner has addressed the 2017 
legislative changes to Iowa Code section 85.34(x). He stated, 
Though the legislature indicated only impairment ratings with a basis in 
the Guides are to be utilized for determining the extent of scheduled 
member impairment, the legislature did not set forth what can and cannot 
be considered by the agency when addressing the credibility, 
persuasiveness, and accuracy of two or more competing ratings. In other 
words, deputy commissioners still have to weigh evidence to determine 
which rating is most convincing. See Ramirez v. Arconic, Inc., File No. 
5066573 (Arb. Jan. 30, 2020) (“However, the new statute does not appear 
to prohibit using lay testimony in aiding to ascertain which of the two 
ratings in this case is more convincing or credible.”) 
Heeren v. Derby Trucking LLC, File No. 5067250, (App. Dec. 30, 2020). 

TERRY V. SECOND INJURY FUND OF IOWA 
Page 10 
 
This agency has cautioned against impairment ratings that do not comply with 
the Guides. See Hill v. Vermeer Corp., File No. 5066032 (App. January 30, 2020). In 
this case, Dr. Kuhnlein is the only medical provider who assigned any impairment to 
either of the claimant’s eyes.  Based on the above findings of fact, I conclude Dr. 
Kuhnlein’s impairment ratings for the eyes did not meet the criteria set forth in the 
AMA Guides.  Thus, claimant has failed to prove that prior to the work injury he 
sustained a scheduled injury under section 85.34(2) affecting a hand, arm, foot, leg, or 
eye.  
Claimant argues that a claimant does not need an impairment rating to establish 
a first qualifying loss.  Claimant urges that, by its own terms, Iowa Code section 
85.34(2)(x) does not apply when determining loss of earning capacity, which is what the 
agency is ultimately tasked with doing when evaluating the Fund’s liability.  In support of 
his position, he relies on this agency’s appeal decision in Hunt v. Second Injury Fund of 
Iowa.  In Hunt, the Commissioner cited George, and noted that the agency previously 
held that a medical impairment rating is not an absolute legal requirement when 
establishing qualifying first injuries.   Hunt v. Second Injury Fund of Iowa, File No. 
1650632.03 (App. March 1, 2023).  In Hunt, the claimant sought to have claimant’s 
industrial disability based, in part, on the condition of claimant’s right knee as of the date 
of the hearing, and simultaneously limit the Fund’s credit to an impairment rating which 
was based on the condition of claimant’s right knee twenty years prior.  (Id.)  However, 
ultimately in Hunt the agency relied on the AMA Guides and the minimum impairment 
rating assigned as the result of a surgical procedure for the purpose of a credit under 
Iowa Code section 85.64.  In doing so, the Commissioner stated that a “deputy 
commissioner does not act as a medical professional or utilize agency expertise when 
converting impairment ratings.”  (Hunt at p. 6)  Thus, the Commissioner still relied on an 
impairment rating under the Guides. 
The Fund argues that subsection x does apply to first alleged injuries for Fund 
purposes.  The Fund contends that the Second Injury Compensation Act is a 
subchapter of Chapter 85 and does not exist independent of other provisions within the 
chapter.  The Fund’s total potential exposure, credit, and commencement date are fixed 
using the schedule set forth in Iowa Code section 85.34.  The Fund’s liability for a 
claimant’s industrial disability is based upon a percentage of 500 weeks, pursuant to the 
compensation for unscheduled injuries set forth in Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v).  See, 
e.g., Second Injury Fund v. Neelans, 436 N.W.2d 355, 357 (Iowa 1989).  Likewise, the 
Fund’s liability for permanent and total disability benefits is assessed pursuant to Iowa 
Code section 85.34(3).  Second Injury Fund of Iowa v. Shank, 516 N.W.2d 808, 814 
(Iowa 1994).   
In this case, even if after the 2017 legislative changes an impairment rating under 
the Guides is not required to prove a first qualifying loss, I conclude claimant has still 
failed to carry his burden of proof.  Claimant contends he lost the use of one of the 
enumerated members, specifically, his eyes.  Claimant does not have any physician-
imposed restrictions related to either eye.  Further, based on the above findings of fact, I 
conclude claimant has not demonstrated that either eye has acted as a hindrance to his

ability to obtain or retain effective employment. As such, he has not proven a first 
qualifying loss.  Because claimant failed to carry his burden of proof to demonstrate by 
a preponderance of the evidence that he sustained a first qualifying loss, I conclude that 
liability of the Fund has not been triggered. As a result, all remaining issues are 
rendered moot.
