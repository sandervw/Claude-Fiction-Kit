STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Leland DeWitt, claimant, filed a petition for review-reopening against Curries 
Manufacturing, employer, and its insurer, Travelers Indemnity Company of Connecticut.  
This case came before the undersigned for an arbitration hearing on January 3, 2024, 
via Zoom. 
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the hearing.  On the 
hearing report, the parties entered into numerous stipulations.  Those stipulations were 
accepted and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be made 
or discussed.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations. 
The evidentiary record includes Joint Exhibits 1 through 3, Claimant’s Exhibits 1 
through 5, and Defendant’s Exhibits A through C.  All exhibits were received without 
objection.  Claimant testified on his own behalf.  No other witnesses were called to 
testify. 
The evidentiary record closed at the conclusion of the review-reopening hearing.  
All parties served their post-hearing briefs on February 19, 2024, at which time this case 
was deemed fully submitted to the undersigned.

The parties submitted the following disputed issues for resolution:

FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned, having considered all of the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
 
Leland Dewitt sustained an injury to his neck and right shoulder on or about June 
25, 2010, while working as a shear operator for Curries Manufacturing in Mason City, 
Iowa.  When conservative treatment measures failed to improve Mr. Dewitt’s conditions, 
David Beck, M.D., performed a C6-C7 foraminotomy and laminectomy on September 9, 
2010.  One year later, Mark Kirkland, M.D., performed a subacromial decompression, 
excision of the distal clavicle, debridement of the articular surface of the rotator cuff, and 
debridement of the biceps tendon.  Lastly, Dr. Beck performed an anterior discectomy, 
decompression, and fusion at C6-C7 on May 22, 2013.  Dr. Beck released Mr. Dewitt to 
return to work without restrictions on July 3, 2013. 
 
The initial arbitration hearing occurred on July 1, 2016.  On July 26, 2016, Deputy 
Workers’ Compensation Commissioner James Elliott issued a decision, awarding 25 
percent industrial disability.   
At the time of the July 1, 2016, hearing, Mr. Dewitt had returned to work for the 
defendant employer and was performing a variety of jobs.  He did not seek medical 
treatment for his neck between July 1, 2016, and June 2018.  He did not miss any time 
from work due to his injuries. (Hr. Tr., p. 22) 
In late May or early June of 2018, Mr. Dewitt was performing a morning 
stretching routine with some co-workers when one of the exercises produced right-sided 
neck pain. (Hr. Tr., p. 15)  Mr. Dewitt asserts his symptoms were no different from the 
symptoms he experienced at the time of the July 2016 hearing. (Id.)   
Three weeks later, Mr. Dewitt presented to Dr. Beck to discuss his recurrent 
symptoms. (JE1, p. 13)  Dr. Beck opined that Mr. Dewitt was most likely experiencing 
neck spasms and recommended conservative treatment measures including a muscle 
relaxer and physical therapy. (Id.) 
When physical therapy failed to alleviate Mr. Dewitt’s symptoms, Dr. Beck 
ordered an updated MRI of the cervical spine. (JE1, p. 21)  The September 26, 2018, 
MRI revealed severe bilateral foraminal narrowing at C4-C5 and C5-C6. (See Ex. 1, p. 
3)  After reviewing the imaging, Dr. Beck told Mr. Dewitt there was nothing dangerous 
going on and prescribed Decadron. (JE1, p. 22)  Dr. Beck released Mr. Dewitt to return 
to his regular duty job. (Id.)

On October 29, 2018, Mr. Dewitt reported worsening bilateral arm pain and neck 
pain with tingling down into the arms. (JE1, p. 23)  Given Mr. Dewitt’s ongoing 
complaints, Dr. Beck recommended a C5-C6 anterior discectomy, foraminotomy, and 
fusion with internal fixation. (Id.)  The surgery was carried out on November 16, 2018. 
(JE1, p. 27)  During surgery, Dr. Beck removed a large anterior osteophyte between C5 
and C6, and observed that the C5-C6 disc was “extremely degenerated.” (JE1, p. 28) 
Following a period of recovery, Dr. Beck placed Mr. Dewitt at maximum medical 
improvement as of January 12, 2019. (JE1, p. 35)  Utilizing the AMA Guides, Dr. Beck 
assigned an additional 8 percent whole person impairment. (Id.) 
Mr. Dewitt returned to his regular job duties as a brake press operator in January 
2019.  He continued working in this position until December 2023 when he bid into a 
new job as a single tip parts welder.  The single tip parts welder position allowed Mr. 
Dewitt to sit in a chair and handle smaller parts.  Mr. Dewitt testified that the position is 
commonly referred to as the “retirement” position at Curries. (Hr. Tr., pp. 18-19)  Mr. 
Dewitt testified that he wants to retire at age 65. (Hr. Tr., p. 19) 
Mr. Dewitt’s symptoms improved with the November 16, 2018, surgery; however, 
he continues to experience stiffness in his neck and limitations on his range of motion. 
(See Ex. 1, p. 8)  In addition, Mr. Dewitt periodically experiences numbness in his right 
hand. (Hr. Tr., p. 16) 
The main dispute in this workers’ compensation case is whether the 2018 
stretching incident is properly classified as a recurrence of a prior injury or an 
aggravation of that injury.  The dispute arises because the amount of compensation can 
vary dramatically depending on whether the condition relates back to the June 25, 2010, 
work injury, or is classified as a new injury.  The reason for the difference in the amount 
of compensation relates to the impact of the post-2017 law changes. 
Curries relies on the expert medical opinions of Dr. Beck and argues Mr. Dewitt 
sustained a new cervical spine injury on or about May 29, 2018.   
In the first of several letters regarding causation, Dr. Beck expressed to 
claimant’s counsel on December 6, 2018, that Mr. Dewitt’s issues at C5-C6 were a 
natural consequence and progression of Mr. Dewitt’s initial injury on June 25, 2010. 
(JE1, p. 29)  Dr. Beck further opined that it is not uncommon for an individual with a 
prior fusion to develop difficulties at the level above or below the prior fusion. (JE1, p. 
30) 
Just over four and a half years later, on July 10, 2023, Dr. Beck addressed 
several pre-written opinions from defense counsel. (JE1, pp. 39-40)  Dr. Beck agreed 
that “Mr. DeWitt’s C5-C6 injury was, more likely than not, caused by the exercises he 
was performing at work.” (JE1, p. 40)  He also agreed that “Had Mr. DeWitt not been 
performing the exercises at work, three weeks before June 18, 2018, his C5-C6 disc 
herniation may never have occurred.” (Id.)  However, Dr. Beck also indicated that the

Presumably to rectify any appearance of conflicting opinions, claimant’s counsel

While it is true that the exercise activities that Mr. DeWitt was performing on

Defense counsel produced one final letter to Dr. Beck on November 2, 2023. 
(JE1, pp. 44-45)  In response, Dr. Beck agreed that the cause or “identifiable factor” that 
resulted in Mr. Dewitt’s disc herniation at C5-C6, and the need for surgical intervention,

that the first two surgeries at C6-C7 were not the identifiable factor in causing Mr. 
Dewitt’s disc herniation at C5-C6. (Id.) 
 
The term “identifiable factor” as utilized by defense counsel comes directly from 
the AMA Guides, Fifth Edition.  In The Guides, causation means an identifiable factor 
(e.g., accident or exposure to hazards of a disease), that results in a medically 
identifiable condition.  While I certainly appreciate defense counsel’s attempt to bolster 
his position, The Guides to the Evaluation of Permanent Impairment, Fifth Edition, has 
been adopted by this agency as the authoritative text for determining the extent of loss 
or percentage of impairment for permanent partial disabilities, not the definition of 
causation.   
 
In addition to the expert opinions of Dr. Beck, claimant sought an updated 
independent medical evaluation with John Kuhnlein, M.D. (Exhibit 1)  In his first IME 
report, dated August 18, 2014, Dr. Kuhnlein assessed Mr. Dewitt with C6-C7 
radiculopathy and chronic cervical myofascial pain in a C6-C7 distribution.  He assigned 
27 percent whole person impairment for the neck injury and an additional 3 percent 
whole person impairment for the right shoulder injury.  At Mr. Dewitt’s request, Dr. 
Kuhnlein did not impose any permanent restrictions.   
 
Mr. Dewitt presented to Dr. Kuhnlein for an updated physical examination on 
October 11, 2023. (Ex. 1, p. 1)  Dr. Kuhnlein diagnosed Mr. Dewitt with cervical 
spondylosis, chronic neck pain, radiculopathy, and persistent intermittent myofascial 
neck pain. (Ex. 1, p. 11)  In addressing causation, Dr. Kuhnlein explained that the work-
related exercises Mr. Dewitt participated in would not have produced significant 
stressors to the neck that might have caused a disc herniation because the loading was

The Iowa Supreme Court has recognized that typically the way to recover 
benefits for any increase in functional disability resulting from aggravation of an original 
traumatic injury due to day-to-day activities in the workplace is by a review-reopening 
claim.  Mr. Dewitt sustained a compensable injury on June 25, 2010, and was awarded 
compensation for the same.  Subsequent thereto he returned to work for Curries and 
performed his normal job duties without restrictions.  Then, as a result of his work 
activities and not because of a specific job-related incident or sudden trauma, his 
condition worsened and eventually became symptomatic while performing a routine 
stretching exercise with his co-workers.  The symptoms Mr. Dewitt experienced in June 
2018 were the same symptoms he experienced following the original June 25, 2010, 
injury.  Such a recurrence of symptoms from routine stretching exercise indicates 
continuity rather than a distinct injurious event.  
As explained by Dr. Kuhnlein, the findings of a C5-C6 disc herniation and 
spondylitic changes above the previous C6-C7 fusion are consistent with adjacent 
segment disease of a fused spinal segment.  He explained that a fusion at one segment 
causes relative weakening above and below the fusion, so the C5-C6 changes were 
more likely than not related to the original C6-C7 fusion due to the adjacent segment 
issues and relative weakening, rather than the June 2018 work-related stretching 
exercises.  Dr. Beck similarly opined that the C6-C7 fusion made Mr. Dewitt more 
susceptible to adjacent disk segment failure.   
Dr. Kuhnlein convincingly explained the large anterior osteophyte between C5 
and C6 and the “extremely degenerated” condition of the C5-C6 disc could not have

developed in the five months following the June 2018 stretching incident.  In other 
words, Mr. Dewitt’s condition, while asymptomatic, was worsening prior to the May/June 
stretching incident.   
After considering all of the evidence presented, I do not find defendants’ 
argument that Mr. Dewitt sustained a new cervical spine injury on May 29, 2018, 
convincing.  I find the stretching exercise was not a new injurious event but rather a 
routine activity that triggered the recurrence of symptoms.   
Dr. Kuhnlein opined Mr. Dewitt reached maximum medical improvement on or 
about May 16, 2019, or six months after his most recent surgical procedure. (Id.)  As for 
his impairment assessment, Dr. Kuhnlein assigned two different impairment ratings to 
the cervical spine.   
First, Dr. Kuhnlein assigned permanent impairment utilizing the Range of Motion 
method as instructed by the AMA Guides, Fifth Edition. (Id.)  Dr. Kuhnlein assigned 10 
percent whole person impairment for the first C6-C7 fusion surgery, and an additional 3 
percent whole person impairment for the second surgery. (Ex. 1, p. 13)  He then 
assigned 8 percent whole person impairment for decrements in range of motion. (Id.)  
Combining these values produced a 20 percent whole person impairment. (Id.) 
Dr. Kuhnlein opined that the use of the Range of Motion method in this case 
would unfairly penalize Mr. Dewitt.  He explained that the methodology for assessing 
permanent impairment for a second fusion is one of the inconsistencies in the AMA 
Guides as an individual can have a second fusion and end up with a concomitant lesser 
impairment rating. (Id.)  As such, Dr. Kuhnlein elected to add the previously mentioned 
3 percent whole person impairment for the second fusion to the previously assigned 27 
percent whole person impairment from his August 18, 2014, report.  In total, Dr. 
Kuhnlein assigned 30 percent whole person impairment for the cervical spine injuries. 
(Id.) 
While I certainly appreciate Dr. Kuhnlein’s efforts to address an apparent 
inconsistency, I find the initial impairment rating utilizing the Range of Motion method is 
most consistent with the AMA Guides, Fifth Edition.  As such, I accept the 20 percent 
whole person impairment rating assigned by Dr. Kuhnlein. 
 
As for restrictions, Dr. Kuhnlein continued to believe that Mr. Dewitt could work 
without restrictions as long as the defendant employer continued to work with him on 
any accommodations or adjustments to his job duties. (Ex. 1, p. 14)  Nevertheless, Dr. 
Kuhnlein recommended Mr. Dewitt limit his material handling functions to 40 pounds 
occasional lifting from floor-to-shoulder height, and 10 pounds occasionally at or above 
shoulder height. (Id.) 
 
Dr. Kuhnlein’s recommendations are reasonable; however, he did not definitively 
assign any permanent restrictions.  As such, I decline the opportunity to adopt any

When considering whether claimant has carried his burden of proof to establish a 
compensable review-reopening claim, the undersigned notes that since the July 1, 
2016, hearing, Mr. Dewitt has undergone C5-C6 anterior discectomy, foraminotomy, 
and fusion with internal fixation.  He continues to experience stiffness in his neck and 
difficulties with range of motion.  He has not sought further medical care for his neck 
since January 2019.  For industrial disability purposes, both Dr. Beck and Dr. Kuhnlein 
assigned additional permanent impairment as a result of the November 16, 2018, 
fusion.  Mr. Dewitt returned to his regular job duties as a brake press operator and 
continued in this role until December 2023 when he bid into a new, less physically 
demanding position.  He plans to continue working for Curries; however, there are jobs 
within the plant he can no longer perform as a result of his injuries.   
In consideration of the above and all other appropriate factors, I find that Mr. 
Dewitt carried his burden of proving he sustained a change in condition causally related 
to the original work injury and is entitled to a review-reopening of the extent of industrial 
disability previously established by the July 2016 arbitration decision.  I find Mr. Dewitt 
has sustained 40 percent industrial disability. 
CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Mr. Dewitt seeks review and reopening of the arbitration decision.  Specifically, 
he seeks an increase in his permanent disability award.  
Upon review-reopening, claimant has the burden to show a change in condition 
related to the original injury since the original award or settlement was made. The 
change may be either economic or physical. Blacksmith v. All-American, Inc., 290 
N.W.2d 348 (Iowa 1980); Henderson v. Iles, 250 Iowa 787, 96 N.W.2d 321 (1959). A 
mere difference of opinion of experts as to the percentage of disability arising from an 
original injury is not sufficient to justify a different determination on a petition for review- 
reopening. Rather, claimant's condition must have worsened or deteriorated since the 
arbitration hearing. Bousfield v. Sisters of Mercy, 249 Iowa 64, 86 N.W.2d 109 (1957). A 
failure of a condition to improve to the extent anticipated originally may also constitute a 
change of condition. Meyers v. Holiday Inn of Cedar Falls, Iowa, 272 N.W.2d 24 (Iowa 
App. 1978). Any substantial change in condition related to the initial work injury is 
sufficient to justify an increase in a permanent disability award. The change needs to 
have been unforeseen or unexpected at the time of the arbitration hearing. Kohlhaas v. 
Hog Slat, Inc., 777 N.W.2d 387 (Iowa 2009). 
Mr. Dewitt asserts that the condition of his cervical spine has substantially 
changed since the arbitration hearing justifying an increase in his permanent disability 
award.

The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based. A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause. A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible. George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability. 
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. St. Luke's Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); 
Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995). Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted expert medical 
testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 
N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 1994). 
 
Mr. Dewitt underwent a C5-C6 anterior discectomy, foraminotomy, and fusion 
with internal fixation on November 16, 2018, which constituted a change in his condition 
since the arbitration hearing.  I conclude that Mr. Dewitt carried his burden of proving a 
substantial change in condition in his cervical spine that justifies a review reopening of 
the prior arbitration award.   
Since claimant has an impairment to the body as a whole, an industrial disability 
has been sustained. Industrial disability was defined in Diederich v. Tri-City Ry. Co., 219 
Iowa 587, 258 N.W. 899 (1935) as follows: “It is therefore plain that the Legislature 
intended the term “disability' to mean ‘industrial disability’ or loss of earning capacity and 
not a mere ‘functional disability’ to be computed in terms of percentages of the total 
physical and mental ability of a normal man.” 
Functional impairment is an element to be considered in determining industrial 
disability which is the reduction of earning capacity, but consideration must also be 
given to the injured employee's age, education, qualifications, experience, motivation, 
loss of earnings, severity and situs of the injury, work restrictions, inability to engage in 
employment for which the employee is fitted and the employer's offer of work or failure 
to so offer. McSpadden v. Big Ben Coal Co., 288 N.W.2d 181 (Iowa 1980); Olson v. 
Goodyear Service Stores, 255 Iowa 1112, 125 N.W.2d 251 (1963); Barton v. Nevada 
Poultry Co., 253 Iowa 285, 110 N.W.2d 660 (1961).

Compensation for permanent partial disability shall begin at the termination of the 
healing period. Compensation shall be paid in relation to 500 weeks as the disability 
bears to the body as a whole. Section 85.34. 
 
Having considered claimant's permanent impairment, educational background, 
employment history, continued employment, age, as well as all other factors of industrial 
disability identified by the Iowa Supreme Court, I found that Mr. Dewitt sustained a 40 
percent loss of future earning capacity as a result of the June 25, 2010, work injury.  
This is equivalent to a 40 percent industrial disability and entitles claimant to an award 
of 200 weeks of permanent partial disability benefits. Iowa Code section 85.35(2)(u) 
(2016). 
Defendants previously paid claimant 165 weeks of permanent partial disability 
benefits and are entitled to a credit for those benefits paid. (Hearing Report; Exhibit C) 
Accordingly, claimant is entitled to an additional award of 35 weeks of permanent partial 
disability benefits. 
The final disputed issue is whether costs should be assessed against either 
party. Costs are assessed at the discretion of the agency. Iowa Code section 86.40. In 
this case, claimant was successful and recovers additional permanent disability 
benefits. Exercising the agency's discretion, I conclude that it is reasonable to assess 
claimant's costs in some amount. 
Claimant requests taxation of the cost of the filing fee ($103.00).  The cost of the 
filing fee is appropriate and assessed pursuant to 876 IAC 4.33(7). 
Claimant also requests taxation of the cost associated with a September 20, 
2023, conference call with Dr. Beck. (Ex. 5, p. 30)  Agency rule 876 IAC 4.33(6) permits 
the agency to assess the reasonable cost of obtaining up to two doctors' reports. 
However, this rule does not permit assessment of the cost of a conference between the 
attorney and the physician in advance of requesting such a report.  I conclude this is not 
a taxable cost. Therefore, claimant's request to tax this cost is denied. 
ORDER 
THEREFORE, IT IS ORDERED: 
Defendants shall pay claimant an additional 35 weeks of permanent partial 
disability benefits commencing October 27, 2022. 
All benefits shall be payable at the stipulated weekly rate of five hundred 
seventy-three and 41/100 dollars ($573.41) per week. 
Defendants shall reimburse claimant's costs in the amount of one hundred three 
and 00/100 dollars ($103.00). 
Defendant shall file subsequent reports of injury (SROI) as required by this 
agency pursuant to rules 876 IAC 3.1(2) and 876 IAC 11.7.
