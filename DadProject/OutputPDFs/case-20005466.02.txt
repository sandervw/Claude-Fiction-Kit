STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
Brian Hagerman, claimant, filed a petition for arbitration against Mid-Am Building 
Supply, Inc., as the employer and Federal Mutual Insurance Company, as the insurance 
carrier. Claimant also filed a claim against the Second Injury Fund of Iowa. Claimant 
reached a settlement agreement with the Second Injury Fund of Iowa prior to the 
commencement of trial and the Second Injury Fund did not participate at this hearing.  
This case came before the undersigned for an arbitration hearing on February 14, 2024. 
   
Pursuant to an order from the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commissioner, this 
case was heard via Zoom videoconference. All participants, including the court reporter, 
appeared remotely for the hearing. 
 
The parties filed a hearing report prior to the commencement of the hearing. On 
the hearing report, the parties entered into numerous stipulations. Those stipulations 
were accepted and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be 
made or discussed. The parties are now bound by their stipulations. 
 
The evidentiary record includes Joint Exhibits 2 through 5 and 7 through 16, 
Claimant’s Exhibits 2 through 7, as well as Defendants’ Exhibit A. All exhibits offered 
were received without objection.   
 
Claimant testified on his own behalf. No other witnesses testified live at the 
hearing. The evidentiary record closed at the conclusion of the arbitration hearing.

ISSUES 
 
The parties submitted a hearing report, which contained several disputed issues.  
However, at the commencement of hearing, the parties were able to resolve most of the 
pending disputes. Specifically, claimant stipulated that this injury involves only a right 
leg. Claimant limited his request for reimbursement of his independent medical 
evaluation after defendants contested $950 of the examination fees attributable to the 
Second Injury Fund claim. The undersigned verbally ordered defendants to reimburse 
claimant for the remainder of the conceded independent medical evaluation fees 
pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.39. Claimant limited his request for reimbursement of 
costs to one filing fee and defendants conceded reimbursement for that fee. The 
undersigned verbally ordered defendants to reimburse the filing fee as a cost.  No 
additional findings of facts or conclusions of law will be entered pertaining to issues 
disputed on the hearing report but resolved at the commencement of the hearing. 
 
Ultimately, the parties submitted only one disputed issue for resolution. 
Specifically, the parties submitted the issue of the extent of claimant’s entitlement to 
permanent partial disability benefits. 
 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
The undersigned, having considered all of the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
 
Claimant, Brian Hagerman, sustained an admitted work injury to his right leg on 
April 29, 2020. Mr. Hagerman sustained two prior injuries to his right knee. These pre-
existing injuries resulted in arthroscopic surgeries on his right knee in 1995 and 2010. 
However, claimant testified that he was not experiencing symptoms or limitations in his 
right knee prior to the April 29, 2020 work injury. 
 
On April 29, 2020, claimant was performing his typical work duties as a truck 
driver for the employer. On his first delivery, claimant was climbing on pallets in his 
trailer to identify the items to be unloaded. As he climbed down, Mr. Hagerman 
experienced significant pain in his right knee. He called his employer to report the injury.  
At the employer’s request, he attempted to complete his duties and made two additional 
deliveries. However, claimant was not able to complete his work on April 29, 2020. The 
employer admits claimant sustained a work injury to his right leg on April 29, 2020. 
 
Mr. Hagerman submitted to conservative treatment, including physical therapy 
and injections into his right knee. Conservative care did not resolve claimant’s 
symptoms. Ultimately, defendants referred claimant to the University of Iowa Hospitals 
and Clinics for treatment. Orthopaedic surgeon, Robert Westermann, M.D., evaluated 
claimant and recommended surgical intervention.

Claimant consented to the recommended surgery and Dr. Westermann took 
claimant to surgery on July 31, 2020. Dr. Westermann performed an arthroscopic 
procedure and ultimately diagnosed claimant with an acute medial meniscus tear of the 
right knee. (Joint Exhibit 9, page 68-76). Dr. Westermann performed a medial 
meniscectomy on July 31, 2020. 
 
Unfortunately, the July 2020 surgery did not resolve claimant’s symptoms. With 
defendants’ authorization, claimant ultimately transferred his care to another 
orthopaedic surgeon, Michael Hendricks, M.D. Dr. Hendricks recommended another 
surgical procedure. Again, claimant consented, and Dr. Hendricks performed a second 
arthroscopic procedure on claimant’s right knee on December 15, 2022. 
 
Dr. Hendricks diagnosed claimant with a complex tear of the posterior horn 
medial meniscus of the right knee, as well as a bone contusion/subchondral fracture of 
the right medial tibial plateau. Finally, Dr. Hendricks diagnosed suprapatella and medial 
shelf plica of the patellofemoral joint in the right knee. (JE 14, pp. 279-281) 
 
Mr. Hagerman continued to struggle with symptoms, including a limited range of 
motion after the December 2022 surgery. Dr. Hendricks returned claimant to the 
operating room on March 3, 2023 and performed a manipulation of the right knee under 
anesthesia. (JE 14, p. 286) Ultimately, Dr. Hendriks declared maximum medical 
improvement on September 14, 2023. (JE 16, p. 371)  
 
The only remaining dispute in this case is the extent of claimant’s permanent 
disability. Mr. Hagerman obtained an independent medical evaluation performed by 
John D. Kuhnlein, D.O., on July 5, 2023. Dr. Kuhnlein indicates that he utilized the AMA 
Guides to the Evaluation of Permanent Impairment, Fifth Edition, in rendering his 
permanent impairment rating. 
 
Dr. Kuhnlein identified arthritis in claimant’s medial knee compartment and 
assigned 7% permanent functional impairment of the right leg as a result of that 
condition. He assigned an additional 10% permanent impairment as a result of the 
partial medial and lateral meniscectomies performed on claimant’s right knee. Dr. 
Kuhnlein also combined and awarded permanent impairment for impairment of the 
saphenous nerve in claimant’s right knee. He opined that claimant would qualify for a 
1% permanent impairment of the right lower extremity as a result of the sensory deficit. 
Finally, Dr. Kuhnlein identified and awarded 2% permanent functional impairment of the 
right lower extremity as a result of atrophy occurring as a result of a deep vein 
thrombosis (blood clot) sustained by Mr. Hagerman as a result of this work injury. In 
total, Dr. Kuhnlein combines all of the impairment ratings and opines that Mr. Hagerman 
sustained 19% permanent functional impairment of his right lower extremity as a result 
of the April 29, 2020 work injury. 
 
Defendants countered with a permanent impairment rating from the treating 
orthopaedic surgeon, Dr. Hendricks. Specifically, Dr. Hendricks issued a report on 
December 5, 2023. Dr. Hendricks also refers to the AMA Guides to the Evaluation of

Permanent Impairment, Fifth Edition, in formulating his permanent impairment rating.  
Dr. Hendricks concurred that claimant qualifies for a 10% permanent impairment of the 
right lower extremity as a result of the medial and lateral meniscectomies. He also 
offered an additional 5% permanent impairment of the right lower extremity for 
claimant’s tibial plateau fracture. In total, Dr. Hendricks opines that claimant sustained a 
15% permanent functional impairment of the right lower extremity as a result of the April 
29, 2020 work injury. 
 
Claimant contends that the opinions of Dr. Kuhnlein should be accepted as most 
credible and convincing in this case. Mr. Hagerman asserts that Dr. Kuhnlein’s 
impairment rating is more thorough because they include impairment for the nerve 
deficits claimant experienced, as well as muscle atrophy that is objectively 
demonstrated. Claimant further contends that Dr. Kuhnlein’s impairment is more 
consistent with the fact that claimant underwent a nerve block and nerve ablation as 
part of his treatment following this work injury. Therefore, claimant urges a finding that 
he sustained a 19% permanent functional impairment of his right leg as a result of the 
work injury. 
 
Defendants contend that the opinions of the treating orthopaedic surgeon, Dr. 
Hendricks are more convincing and credible. First, defendants urge that Dr. Hendricks 
has superior qualifications as an orthopaedic surgeon and that his opinions should be 
given greater weight because of his credentials. Defendants accurately point out that 
Dr. Hendricks had the benefit of examining claimant over an extended period of time, as 
well as intraoperatively. Defendants urge that Dr. Hendricks’ position is advantageous 
over the one-time evaluation performed by Dr. Kuhnlein. Finally, defendants point out 
that Dr. Hendricks offered his impairment rating more than two months after Dr. 
Kuhnlein’s impairment rating was generated. 
 
Comparing the competing impairment ratings, both appear to be generated using 
the AMA Guides, Fifth Edition. However, Dr. Kuhnlein appears to recognize additional 
deficits and impairments that claimant qualifies for as a result of the work injury. 
Specifically, he provides impairment as a result of a sensory deficit and as a result of 
atrophy resulting from the deep vein thrombosis claimant experienced. Dr. Hendricks 
does not address either of these issues in his report. He neither awards impairment for 
a sensory deficit nor atrophy. In this sense, Dr. Kuhnlein’s impairment rating appears to 
be more thorough and still consistent with the Fifth Edition of the Guides. 
 
As defendants pointed out, Dr. Hendricks offered his impairment rating two 
months after Dr. Kuhnlein. If he found errors in Dr. Kuhnlein’s analysis, he could have 
pointed out those errors or explained why claimant does not qualify for permanent 
impairment for sensory loss or atrophy due to the deep vein thrombosis. Instead, Dr. 
Hendricks is silent on these issues. It appears Dr. Kuhnlein simply considered additional 
physical and functional losses (sensory loss and atrophy) that were not considered and 
rated by Dr. Hendricks. Therefore, I find the 19% permanent functional impairment 
rating offered by Dr. Kuhnlein to be the most credible and convincing in this evidentiary 
record. I specifically find that claimant has proven a 19% permanent functional loss of 
his right light as a result of the April 20, 2020 work injury.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
The only disputed issue for resolution in this case is the extent of claimant’s 
permanent disability as a result of the April 29, 2020 work injury.   
 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based. A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause. A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible. George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996). 
 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability. 
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an expert 
opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy of the 
facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The expert 
opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 
N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); Dunlavey v. 
Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, 
Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted expert medical testimony cannot be 
summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 
1994). 
 
Under the Iowa Workers' Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is 
compensated either for a loss or loss of use of a scheduled member under Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(a)-(u) or for loss of earning capacity under section 85.34(2)(v). The 
extent of scheduled member disability benefits to which an injured worker is entitled is 
determined by using the functional method. Functional disability is "limited to the loss of 
the physiological capacity of the body or body part.”  Mortimer v. Fruehauf Corp., 502 
N.W.2d 12, 15 (Iowa 1993); Sherman v. Pella Corp., 576 N.W.2d 312 (Iowa 1998). In 
this case, the parties stipulate that claimant’s injury resulted in a scheduled member 
injury to the right leg. The only dispute is the extent of permanent disability resulting 
from the work injury. 
 
Permanent disability payable for an injury to the leg is compensated on a 220-
week schedule. Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(p). If the injury results in less than total 
disability of the scheduled member, then permanent disability benefits are paid 
proportionally to the percentage of loss of function to the maximum number of weeks to 
which the scheduled member is assigned under Iowa Code section 85.34(2). Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(w); Blizek v. Eagle Signal Company, 164 N.W.2d 84 (Iowa 1969).

In this case, I considered the permanent impairment ratings offered by each 
party. I considered the cogent arguments of counsel. Ultimately, I found the opinion of 
Dr. Kuhnlein to be more convincing and credible under the facts and evidence in this 
case. Therefore, I found claimant proved a 19% permanent functional loss of the right 
leg as a result of the April 29, 2020 work injury. 
 
As noted above, claimant’s entitlement to permanent partial disability benefits is 
proportional to the 220-week schedule assigned to the leg. Nineteen percent of 220 
weeks totals 41.8 weeks. Therefore, I conclude claimant is entitled to an award of 41.8 
weeks of permanent partial disability benefits in this case. 
 
ORDER 
 
THEREFORE, IT IS ORDERED: 
 
Defendants shall pay claimant forty-one point eight (41.8) weeks of permanent 
partial disability benefits commencing on September 14, 2023.  
 
All benefits shall be payable at the rate of six hundred thirteen and 82/100 dollars 
($613.82) per week. 
 
Defendants are entitled to credit for all permanent partial disability benefits paid 
to date. 
 
Interest shall be payable on any past due weekly benefits at an annual rate equal 
to the one-year treasury constant maturity published by the federal reserve in the most 
recent H15 report settled as of the date of injury, plus two percent. See Gamble v. AG 
Leader Technology File No. 5054686 (App. Apr. 24, 2018). 
 
Defendants shall reimburse claimant’s independent medical evaluation expense 
and costs as verbally ordered at the time of the arbitration hearing. 
 
Defendants shall reimburse claimant for one filing fee pursuant to the verbal 
order at the commencement of the arbitration hearing. 
 
Defendants shall file subsequent reports of injury (SROI) as required by this 
agency pursuant to rules 876 IAC 3.1(2) and 876 IAC 11.7.
