CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 
employment.  Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 150 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. 
Dial Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309, 311 (Iowa 1996).  The words “arising out of” refer to the 
cause or source of the injury.  The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 
circumstances of the injury.  2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124, 128 (Iowa 
1995).  An injury arises out of employment when a causal relationship exists between 
the injury and the employment.  Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311.  The injury must be a 
rational consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely 
incidental to the employment.  Koehler Electric v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1, 3 (Iowa 2000); 
Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311.  An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it 
happens within a period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may 
be when performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties 
or doing an activity incidental to them.  Ciha, 552 N.W.2d at 150. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only

The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony.  Expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question.  The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances.  The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410, 420 
(Iowa 2001); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995).  Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994).  Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected.  Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-92 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
When an expert's opinion is based upon an incomplete or incorrect history, it is 
not necessarily binding on the commissioner or the court. It is then to be weighed, 
together with other facts and circumstances, the ultimate conclusion being for the finder 
of the fact.  Musselman v. Central Telephone Company, 261 Iowa 352, 360,154 N.W.2d 
128, 133 (Iowa 1967); Bodish v. Fischer, Inc., 257 Iowa 516, 133 N.W.2d 867, 870 
(1965).  The commissioner as trier of fact has the duty to determine the credibility of the 
witnesses and to weigh the evidence, together with the other disclosed facts and 
circumstances, and then to accept or reject the opinion.  Dunlavey, 526 N.W.2d at 853. 
Under the Iowa Workers' Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is 
compensated either for a loss or loss of use of a scheduled member under Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(a)-(u) or for loss of earning capacity under section 85.34(2)(v). The 
extent of scheduled member disability benefits to which an injured worker is entitled is 
determined by using the functional method. Functional disability is “limited to the loss of 
the physiological capacity of the body or body part.” Mortimer v. Fruehauf Corp., 502 
N.W.2d 12, 15 (Iowa 1993); Sherman v. Pella Corp., 576 N.W.2d 312, 322 (Iowa 1998).  
At the start of the hearing, both parties stipulated that Rojas’ injury was a scheduled 
member injury to the left shoulder.  (See Hearing Report).  Thus, his permanent 
impairment is determined under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(n).  Iowa Code section 
85.34(2)(n) limits compensation for a shoulder injury to four hundred weeks.  Iowa Code 
§ 85.34(2)(n) (2021).   
In all cases of permanent partial disability described in paragraphs “a” through 
“u”, or paragraph “v” when determining functional disability and not loss of earning 
capacity, the extent of loss or percentage of permanent impairment shall be determined 
solely by utilizing the guides to the evaluation of permanent impairment, published by 
the American medical association, as adopted by the workers' compensation 
commissioner by rule pursuant to chapter 17A. Iowa Code § 85.34(2)(x).  The Workers'

Ratings for injuries to the shoulders are covered by Chapter 16 of the AMA 
Guides, Fifth Edition.  See AMA Guides, p. 433.  The process for assessing shoulder 
impairment under Chapter 16 requires using a goniometer to measure flexion, 
extension, abduction, adduction, and internal and external rotation. Id. at 474.  Under 
Chapter 16, “The shoulder has three functional units of motion, each contributing a 
relative value to its function.” Id. Flexion and extension make up a unit, abduction and 
adduction another, and internal and external rotation is the third unit. See id.; see also 
Reeves v. Plymouth County Solid Waste, File No. 21006846.02, 2023 WL 6953980 
(Arb. Decision, Oct. 16, 2023).  Dr. Bansal cited to AMA Guides Figures 16-40 through 
16-46 for his proposed rating. (Cl Ex. 1, p. 8).  Dr. Harrer did not cite to specific Figures 
in the AMA Guides, but did reference AMA Guides, pages 476-477 in his opinion. (Ex. 
C, p. 12).  Pages 476 and 477 contain Figures 16-40 through 16-43. See AMA Guides, 
pp. 476-477. Figure 16-40 provides impairment figures for lack of shoulder flexion and 
extension. Id. at 476. Figure 16-43 articulates impairment for lack of shoulder abduction 
and adduction. Id. at 477.  Figure 16-46 gives impairment for lack of internal and 
external rotation of the shoulder joint. Id. at 479.  Dr. Bansal’s report contains 
measurements for all six functional units of shoulder motion. (Cl Ex. 1, p. 6). Dr. Harrer’s 
report does not, nor does his final treatment note. (Ex. C, p. 12; JE 2, p. 23).  “The 
upper extremity impairment resulting from abnormal shoulder motion is calculated from 
the pie charts by adding directly the upper extremity impairment values contributed by 
each motion unit.” AMA Guides, p. 474 (emphasis in original). Failure to measure and 
include each range of motion unit results in an incomplete assessment of the shoulder’s 
motion, making it impossible to correctly utilize the charts in the Guides for determining 
the functional impairment in that shoulder. See id. at 475-79.  Dr. Harrer did not include 
measurements for extension, adduction, nor internal or external rotation. (Cl Ex. C, p. 
12; JE 2, p. 23).  It is not in compliance with the AMA Guides.  Dr. Bansal’s report 
contains all six of the required measurements.  (Cl Ex. 1, p. 6).  It is in compliance with 
the AMA Guides.

Rojas asserts a claim for penalty benefits.  Specifically, Rojas claims he is 
entitled to penalty benefits because Tyson failed to timely solicit a permanent 
impairment rating from Dr. Harrer. (Claimant’s Post-Hearing Brief, pp. 2-3).  Rojas’ last 
appointment with Dr. Harrer took place on December 7, 2022. (JE 2, p. 23).  Dr. Harrer, 
however, did not issue Tyson a permanent impairment rating until September 26, 2024. 
(Ex. C, p. 12).   
Penalty benefits are governed by Iowa Code section 10A.315, which provides 
two clear prerequisites before penalty benefits can be imposed: (1) “a delay in 
commencement or termination of benefits” that occurs (2) “without reasonable or 
probable cause or excuse.” Iowa Code § 10A.315. When the prerequisites have been 
met, the Iowa Code instructs the commissioner “shall award” penalty benefits “up to fifty 
percent of the amount of benefits that were unreasonably delayed or denied.” Id.  “To

receive a penalty benefit award under section 86.13,4 the claimant must first establish a 
delay in the payment of benefits.” Schadendorf v. Snap–On Tools Corp., 757 N.W.2d 
330, 334 (Iowa 2008).  “The burden then shifts to the employer to prove a reasonable 
cause or excuse for the delay.” Id. at 334–35.  Weekly compensation payments are due 
at the end of the compensation week. Robbennolt v. Snap-on Tools Corp., 555 N.W.2d 
229, 237-238 (Iowa 1996).  Under Iowa Code section 85.34(2), permanent partial 
disability payments are due “when it is medically indicated that maximum medical 
improvement from the injury has been reached and that the extent of loss or percentage 
of permanent impairment can be determined by use of the guides to the evaluation of 
permanent impairment. . . .”  According to Tyson, Dr. Harrer placed Rojas at MMI on 
December 7, 2022, via a work status report. (See Ex. C, p. 12). Given this, under the 
statutory language, Rojas’ permanency benefits were due on December 8, 2022.  
Those benefits, however, were not paid until October 18, 2024, almost two years after 
Dr. Harrer placed him at MMI for the work injury. (Ex. F, p. 18).  Therefore, Rojas has 
met his burden to prove a delay in payment under Iowa Code section 10A.315; the 
burden now shifts to Tyson to prove there was a reasonable cause or excuse for the 
delay.    
In Christensen v. Snap-on Tools Corp., 554 N.W.2d 254, 260 (Iowa 1996), the 
Iowa Supreme Court declared, 
A reasonable cause or excuse exists if either (1) the delay was necessary 
for the insurer to investigate the claim or (2) the employer had a reasonable 
basis to contest the employee's entitlement to benefits. A “reasonable 
basis” for denial of the claim exists if the claim is “fairly debatable.”  
Id.   The reasonableness of the employer's actions “does not turn on whether the 
employer was right. The issue is whether there was a reasonable basis for the 
employer's position that no benefits were owing.” Keystone Nursing Care Ctr. v. 
Craddock, 705 N.W.2d 299, 307-308 (Iowa 2005).  In City of Madrid v. Blasnitz, 742 
N.W.2d 77, 84 (Iowa 2007), the Iowa Supreme Court stated that when there are facts 
that create a genuine dispute between the parties, the claimant’s case is fairly 
debatable as a matter of law, and therefore the employer’s denial basis reasonable.

Id.  In Pettengill v. American Blue-Ribbon Holdings. L.L.C., 875 N.W.2d 740, 747 (Iowa 
App. 2015), the Iowa Court of Appeals held that Iowa Code section 10A.315(4) “creates 
a mandatory timeline for the employer to follow in showing it had a ‘reasonable or 
probable cause or excuse’ for the termination of benefits.” Id. The Pettengill Court 
explained that timeline: 
First, 
the 
employer's 
excuse 
for 
the 
termination 
must 
have 
been preceded by an investigation, Id. § 86.13(4)(c)(1). Second, the results 
of the investigation were “the actual basis . . . contemporaneously” relied on 
by the employer in terminating the benefits. Third, the employer 
“contemporaneously conveyed the basis for the . . . termination of 
benefits  to the employee at the time of the . . . termination.” Id. § 
86.13(4)(c)(3).  
Id. at 747 (emphasis in original). It is defendant’s burden to show compliance with this 
statutory provision in order to avoid the mandatory penalty assessment. Id. at 745.    
 
Rojas asserts he is entitled to penalty benefits because Tyson did not solicit a 
rating from Dr. Harrer in a timely manner. (Claimant’s Post-Hearing Brief, p. 2).  In 
response, Tyson argues that it was not under an obligation to pay Rojas permanent 
benefits in December 2022, because the work status report sent by Dr. Harrer stated 
“no further treatment or impairment anticipated.” (Defendant’s Post-Hearing Brief, pp. 5-
6).  Tyson interpreted this note to mean that Rojas did not have any permanent 
impairment from the work injury, and thus, did not volunteer any permanency benefits at 
that time. (Id.).  Defendant’s Post-Hearing Brief states, 
In reliance on [Dr. Harrer’s] opinion, Tyson did not pay permanency 
benefits.  Later, when Dr. Harrer was asked to confirm no impairment, he 
provided a 4% upper extremity impairment rating for Claimant’s left 
shoulder. Tyson timely paid PPD. . . pursuant to Dr. Harrer’s 4% impairment 
rating.  
Based upon this factual record, it is Tyson’s position that Claimant 
cannot prove a delay in PPD.  More specifically, Dr. Harrer completed 
Tyson’s return to work form indicating “Discharged – No Further Treatment 
or Impairment Anticipated.” Only later, when asked to confirm his prior 
opinion of no impairment, did Dr. Harrer issue an opinion finding permanent 
impairment. Dr. Harrer’s impairment rating to Tyson was dated 9/26/2024.  
Tyson timely paid permanency after receipt of Dr. Harrer’s rating.

Page 11 
 
(Id. at 5-6) (citations omitted).  Tyson did not submit Dr. Harrer’s December 7, 2022 
work status note into the hearing record.  Thus, the only portion of it available to the 
undersigned is the small part reproduced in Tyson’s May 10, 2024 letter to Dr. Harrer.  
The undersigned questions whether it was reasonable for Tyson to rely upon Dr. 
Harrer’s check-the-box work status note in this instance.  As part of the accepted work 
injury Rojas underwent arthroscopic repair of his left shoulder, a surgery that included a 
distal clavicle excision, a procedure that carries a mandatory impairment rating under 
AMA Guides, Table 16-27. See AMA Guides, p. 506.  However, even if Tyson’s 
decision not to seek clarification from Dr. Harrer in December 2022 appeared entirely 
reasonable, there is no evidence in the hearing record that they contemporaneously 
conveyed this information to Rojas.  The record does not contain a letter from Tyson to 
Rojas in December 2022, indicating that Dr. Harrer had opined Rojas did not have any 
permanent impairment from the December 13, 2021 work injury, and therefore, Tyson 
was not paying permanency benefits. Under the Pettengill decision, that is a mandatory 
requirement to prove reasonable excuse for not paying benefits timely. Pettengill, 875 
N.W.2d at 747.  Rojas has proven entitlement to penalty benefits for Tyson’s failure to 
timely pay permanency benefits.   
The purpose of penalty benefits is both punishment for unreasonable conduct 
and deterrence of similar conduct in future cases. Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 237.  The 
agency has discretion to determine the amount of the penalty imposed with a maximum 
penalty of 50 percent of the amount of the delayed or denied benefits. Christensen, 554 
N.W.2d at 261.  In determining the amount of the penalty, the agency shall consider 
such factors as the length of the delay, the number of the delays, the information 
available to the employer regarding the employee's injuries and wages, and the prior 
penalties imposed against the employer under section 10A.315.  Meyers v. Holiday 
Express Corp., 557 N.W.2d 502, 505 (Iowa 1996).  Taking into consideration the above 
factors, I find a penalty of $5,000.00 is appropriate for Tyson’s failure to timely pay 
permanency benefits.
