STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Douglas Triplett, claimant, received a prior award of worker’s compensation 
benefits in an underlying arbitration proceeding.  Specifically, in an arbitration decision 
filed on November 1, 2017, the undersigned awarded Mr. Triplett permanent partial 
disability benefits equivalent to 50 percent of the right leg.  Claimant filed a timely 
review-reopening petition and the review-reopening proceeding came before the 
undersigned on August 28, 2020. 
The parties filed a hearing report before the scheduled hearing.  On the hearing 
report, the parties entered into numerous stipulations.  Those stipulations were 
accepted and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be made 
or discussed.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations. 
The evidentiary record includes Joint Exhibit 1 as well as Claimant’s Exhibits 1 
through 3.  For clarity sake, Claimant’s Exhibit 3 was filed after the live hearing occurred 
based upon a ruling suspending the evidentiary hearing at the close of the live hearing.  
Claimant’s Exhibit 3 is received into the evidentiary record given the ruling at the time of 
the live hearing.  The evidentiary record closed upon receipt of Claimant’s Exhibit 3. 
Defendant also filed Exhibit A with their post-hearing brief.  Defendant filed the 
exhibit because claimant’s supplemental exhibit (Claimant’s Exhibit 3) had not been 
filed yet.  Defendant’s Exhibit A duplicates some of the records contained in Claimant’s 
Exhibit 3.  Therefore, it is duplicative and unnecessary.  Having received Claimant’s 
Exhibit 3, I exclude Defendant’s Exhibit A.   
Claimant testified on his own behalf.  No other witnesses testified at trial.  
However, counsel for the parties requested an opportunity to file post-hearing briefs.  
This request was granted and both parties filed briefs simultaneously on October 9, 
2020.  The case was considered fully submitted to the undersigned on that date.

ISSUES 
The parties submitted the following disputed issues for resolution: 
1. 
Whether claimant has experienced a significant change in condition since 
the 2015 arbitration hearing. 
2. 
The extent of claimant’s entitlement to additional permanent disability 
benefits, if any. 
3. 
Whether claimant’s costs should be assessed and, if so, in what amount. 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned, having considered all of the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
Douglas Triplett, claimant, sustained a work related, cumulative injury of his right 
knee, which manifested on May 10, 2016.  That injury resulted in claimant submitting to 
a total right knee replacement prior to the June 29, 2017 arbitration hearing.  Following 
an appropriate period of time, claimant’s treating physician (Mark Fish, D.O.) declared 
maximum medical improvement and assigned claimant a permanent impairment rating 
equivalent to 50 percent of the right leg as a result of the May 10, 2016 work injury. 
The undersigned issued an arbitration decision on November 1, 2017.  In that 
decision, I found Dr. Fish’s medical opinion to be the most convincing and credible 
evidence in the record.  Relying upon Dr. Fish’s causation opinion and impairment rating, 
I awarded Mr. Triplett permanent partial disability benefits equivalent to 50 percent of the 
right leg.  That decision was not appealed and became final agency action. 
Unfortunately, claimant’s symptoms continued after the arbitration hearing.  He 
returned for further care with Dr. Fish.  Dr. Fish recommended and performed a revision 
surgery of the total right knee arthroplasty he had performed before the arbitration 
hearing.  The revision surgery occurred on July 3, 2018.  (Joint Ex. 1, pages 1-3) 
Dr. Fish remained claimant’s treating physician after surgery and ultimately 
declared maximum medical improvement on January 3, 2019.  Dr. Fish performed 
appropriate range of motion measurements and physical testing to provide an impairment 
rating.  Utilizing the AMA Guides to the Evaluation of Permanent Impairment, Fifth Edition, 
Dr. Fish opined that Mr. Triplett still qualifies as having a fair result from his right total 
knee replacement.  Dr. Fish opined that claimant’s permanent impairment rating did not 
change after the revision surgery and that claimant still demonstrates 50 percent 
impairment of the right lower extremity.  (Joint Ex. 1, p. 5)  
Mr. Triplett sought an independent medical evaluation performed by orthopaedic 
surgeon, Scott B. Neff, D.O. on September 9, 2019.  (Claimant’s Ex. 1)  Dr. Neff notes 
that claimant exhibited extension to negative 5 degrees and flexion of the right knee to 65 
degrees.  Dr. Neff notes that claimant walks with a limp and walks stairs one at a time.

Dr. Neff opines that claimant suffered a “fairly poor result following knee arthroplasty with 
required revision.”  He assigns a 75 percent permanent impairment of the right lower 
extremity, but does not refer to the AMA Guides to the Evaluation of Permanent 
Impairment, Fifth Edition.  (Claimant’s Ex. 1, p. 3) 
The AMA Guides provide specific criteria to assess the result of claimant’s right 
total knee replacement.  Table 17-25 on page 549 of the AMA Guides, Fifth Edition, 
require assessment of claimant’s pain, range of motion, the stability of his knee joint, 
flexion contractures, extension lags, and alignment of the knee joint.  Dr. Neff’s report 
does not refer to some of these factors and does not explain how he assigned points or 
determined his finding of a poor result after the revision of claimant’s right knee 
replacement. 
By contrast, Dr. Fish’s May 6, 2019 report specifically notes claimant had zero to 
90 degrees of functional motion in his right knee, exhibited a stable knee joint to both 
varus and valgus stress, and 4+/5 strength.  Dr. Fish also noted normal alignment of the 
replaced knee joint and reports by claimant of mild pain with activities.  Dr. Fish clearly 
references and was clearly trying to document each of the necessary factors to assess 
the criteria needed to determine claimant’s right knee replacement outcome.  In fact, in a 
supplemental report, Dr. Fish reviewed Dr. Neff’s additional, or differing, physical 
examination findings, put those factors back into the AMA Guides’ assessment, and 
opined that claimant still qualified for a fair result after his knee replacement.   
I find that Dr. Fish’s analysis and opinions pertaining to claimant’s current 
permanent impairment are more thorough, more clearly expressed, more closely follow 
the AMA Guides’ requirements, and are more convincing and credible.  I note that Dr. 
Fish is the treating surgeon.  He evaluated claimant before and after the right knee 
revision surgery.  He knows the condition of claimant’s knee prior to the arbitration hearing 
and at the present time.   
Defendant also points out in their brief that claimant’s pain reports to his treating 
physician remain relatively stable since the prior arbitration hearing.  Although claimant 
testified that he has experienced significant worsening of his condition since the 
arbitration hearing and even since his evaluation with Dr. Neff, his medical records do not 
objectively support this report of increased symptoms.  In fact, claimant reported relatively 
the same pain levels and symptoms over time through the date of the review-reopening 
hearing.  (Claimant’s Ex. 3) 
At the end of my analysis, I find that the revision surgery on claimant’s right total 
knee replacement could be a sufficient basis for a review-reopening and could constitute 
a substantial change in condition.  However, in this instance, claimant has not proven that 
he actually experienced a substantial change in condition since the arbitration hearing.  
Although he underwent a revision surgery, I accept the opinions of Dr. Fish as the most 
credible and convincing.  I find that claimant continues to have a fair result after his right 
knee replacement and that he continues to demonstrate a 50 percent permanent 
impairment of the right leg as a result of his work injury.  Therefore, I conclude that

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
This case involves a claim for review reopening and an increase in permanent 
disability benefits by claimant.  Specifically, Mr. Triplett asserts that his condition has 
worsened and he has experienced a significant change in his condition since the 2017 
arbitration hearing.  Defendant disputes whether claimant has proven a change in 
condition that is casually related to the work injury and challenges the extent of 
claimant’s entitlement to additional permanent disability benefits, if any. 
Upon review reopening, claimant has the burden to show a change in condition 
related to the original injury since the original ward or settlement was made.  The 
change may be either economic or physical.  Blacksmith v. All-American, Inc., 290 
N.W.2d 348 (Iowa 1980); Henderson v. Iles, 250 Iowa 787, 96 N.W.2d 321 (1959).  A 
mere difference of opinion of experts as to the percentage of disability arising from an 
original injury is not sufficient to justify a difference determination on a petition for 
review-reopening.  Rather, claimant’s condition must have worsened or deteriorated in a 
substantial and discernible manner since the initial award or settlement.  Bousfield v. 
Sisters of Mercy, 249 Iowa 64, 86 N.W.2d 109 (1957).  However, it makes no difference 
whether the change in condition since the arbitration hearing was anticipated or 
unanticipated.  Kohlhaas v. Hog Slat, Inc., 777 N.W.2d 387, 391-392 (Iowa 2009).  
Instead, the question in this review-reopening proceeding is whether there has been a 
substantial change in condition since the arbitration hearing that remains causally 
related to the initial work injury.  Id. at 392.  Of course, claimant may not re-litigate facts 
that were known or knowable at the time of the arbitration hearing.  Id. at 393. 
In this case, I considered claimant’s testimony, his past and ongoing medical 
records since the 2017 arbitration hearing, as well as the competing medical opinions of 
Dr. Fish and Dr. Neff.  Ultimately, I accepted the opinions of Dr. Fish as most credible 
and convincing in this record.  Having reached that finding, I accepted Dr. Fish’s 
permanent impairment rating as the most accurate and found that claimant continues to 
demonstrate a fair result from his right knee replacement.  Similarly, I found that 
claimant continues to have a 50 percent permanent functional impairment of the right 
leg as a result of his work injury.   
Having reached these factual findings, I conclude that claimant failed to prove a 
substantial change in condition since the 2017 arbitration hearing.  I further conclude 
that claimant failed to prove that his condition warrants an increase in his permanent 
disability benefits.  Therefore, I conclude claimant failed to carry his burden of proof to 
establish a substantial change in condition that warrants an increase in his permanent 
disability award.  Iowa Code section 86.14(2). 
Finally, claimant seeks assessment of his costs.  Costs are assessed at the 
discretion of the agency.  Iowa Code section 86.40.  In this case, the employer prevailed 
on the underlying merits of this review-reopening proceeding.  Claimant failed to prove a
