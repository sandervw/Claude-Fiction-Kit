This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A. The 
expedited procedures of rule 876 IAC 4.48, the “alternate medical care” rule, are 
invoked by claimant, Elaine McDaniels.  
This alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on October 1, 2024. The 
proceedings were recorded digitally and constitute the official record of the hearing. By 
an order filed by the Workers’ Compensation Commissioner, this decision is designated 
final agency action. Any appeal would be by petition for judicial review under Iowa Code 
section 17A.15(1).  
The record in this case consists of Claimant’s Exhibits 1-2, Defendants’ Exhibits  
A-B, and the testimony of claimant. 
ISSUE 
The issue presented for resolution in this case is whether claimant is entitled to 
alternate medical care consisting of authorization for physical therapy. 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
Defendants admit liability for an injury to claimant’s right ankle. 
On September 6, 2024, Beau Shay, DPM, wrote a prescription for claimant to 
receive physical therapy for her Achilles tendon. (Claimant’s Exhibit 2)

On September 12, 2024, claimant’s counsel requested the physical therapy 
ordered by Dr. Shay be approved by defendants. (Ex. 1) 
In a September 21, 2024, email, a staff member from Dr. Shay’s office indicated 
the September 6, 2024, prescription for physical therapy was made following a call from 
claimant requesting physical therapy.  Claimant was not seen in the office by Dr. Shay 
for the prescription.  (Defendants’ Exhibit A, pages 1-2) 
In an email that appears to be from defendant insurer, dated September 30, 
2024, claimant is scheduled for an appointment with Dr. Shay on October 14, 2024. (Ex. 
B) 
Claimant testified Dr. Shay is an authorized provider.  Claimant has been 
receiving physical therapy based on referrals made by Dr. Shay.  Claimant testified the 
physical therapist told her that physical therapy appointments would end soon, and that 
claimant needed to request a referral for more physical therapy from Dr. Shay.  
Claimant said she called Dr. Shay’s office, spoke to a nurse, and based on the physical 
therapist’s recommendations, requested a referral for more physical therapy.  Dr. Shay 
issued a prescription for physical therapy which claimant got and gave to her physical 
therapist. 
Defendants contend they are denying the requested physical therapy prescribed 
by Dr. Shay because claimant requested the additional physical therapy and was not 
recently examined by Dr. Shay. 
The docket in this matter indicates seven petitions for alternate medical care 
have been filed in this case.  In a professional statement, claimant’s counsel indicated 
he has had to file four petitions for alternate medical care for physical therapy for 
claimant.  In his professional statement, claimant’s counsel indicated defendants 
routinely delay claimant’s request for physical therapy. 
CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e).  
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Section 85.27.  Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening, October 1975).   
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See Iowa

R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e); Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995). 
Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.  Id.  The 
employer’s obligation turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.  Id.; 
Harned v. Farmland Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983).  In Pirelli-Armstrong Tire 
Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 433 (Iowa 1997), the court approvingly quoted Bowles v. 
Los Lunas Schools, 109 N.M. 100, 781 P.2d 1178 (App. 1989):   
[T]he words “reasonable” and “adequate” appear to describe the same 
standard.   
[The New Mexico rule] requires the employer to provide a certain 
standard of care and excuses the employer from any obligation to provide 
other services only if that standard is met.  We construe the terms 
"reasonable” and “adequate” as describing care that is both appropriate to 
the injury and sufficient to bring the worker to maximum recovery.   
An employer’s right to select the provider of medical treatment to an injured 
worker does not include the right to determine how an injured worker should be 
diagnosed, evaluated, treated, or other matters of professional medical judgment. 
Assman v. Blue Star Foods, declaratory ruling, File No. 866389 (May 19, 1988).   
The employer is not entitled to interpose its judgment in contravention of the 
recommendation of the authorized treating physician.  The employer’s failure to follow 
recommendations of an authorized physician is a failure to provide reasonable 
treatment pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.27.  Boggs v. Cargill, Inc., File No. 1050396 
(Alt. Care, January 31, 1994).   
Dr. Shay is the authorized provider for care for claimant in this case.  Dr. Shay 
issued a prescription for additional physical therapy for claimant’s work-related injury.  
Claimant requested that additional physical therapy as recommended by an authorized 
physical therapist.  Claimant testified that physical therapy has improved her condition. 
Defendants contest the request for additional physical therapy as claimant was 
not recently examined by Dr. Shay, and because claimant requested the physical 
therapy.   
The record is clear Dr. Shay prescribed additional physical therapy for claimant.  
The record is clear claimant requested the additional physical therapy based on a 
recommendation from the authorized physical therapist.   
Defendants did not cite any rule, statute, agency precedent or case law that 
would support their denial of care in this case.  In a professional statement, claimant’s 
counsel indicated he has had to file four petitions for alternate medical care based on 
defendants’ delay and refusal to authorize physical therapy. 
Defendants’ denial of care in this case borders on bad faith.  It is also a waste of 
agency resources.  Dr. Shay prescribed additional physical therapy for claimant. There

is no precedent or legal rationale for defendants’ denial of the prescribed care.  Given 
the record as detailed above, claimant has carried her burden of proof that defendants’ 
denial of care in this case is unreasonable.  Defendants shall authorize and pay for the 
physical therapy as prescribed by Dr. Shay.   
ORDER 
Therefore, it is ordered that claimant’s petition for alternate medical care is 
granted.  Defendants shall authorize and pay for the physical therapy prescribed by Dr. 
Shay.
