ISSUES & STIPULATIONS 
The parties submitted the following stipulations and disputed issues for 
determination. 
Claimant contends he sustained two separate work injuries for Brockway; the first 
on February 22, 2022, and the second on February 15, 2023.  The employer and 
carrier, WBM deny that an injury occurred on February 22, 2022, and further assert a 
notice defense.  The employer and carrier MBC, stipulate that a work injury occurred on 
or about February 15, 2023, however, assert that the injury is not a cause of any 
permanent disability.  Defendant employer and MBC paid some temporary benefits to 
the claimant and assert that his shoulder condition returned to baseline.  Claimant 
seeks temporary disability benefits and permanent partial benefits.  The defendants in 
both claims deny that they are responsible for any such benefits. 
The claimant seeks medical expenses set forth in Claimant’s Exhibit 1.  All 
defendants deny they are responsible for such expenses.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The primary fighting issue in this case is whether either of the claimant’s alleged 
work injuries were a substantial causal or material aggravating factor to the 
development of his left shoulder condition.

Page 10 
 
 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
A possibility of causation is not sufficient; a probability is necessary. Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996) (citing Holmes v. Bruce 
Motor Freight, Inc., 215 N.W.2d 296, 297 (Iowa 1974)). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 
621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 
(Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994).   
It has long been the law of Iowa that Iowa employers take an employee subject 
to any active or dormant health problems and must exercise care to avoid injury to both 
the weak and infirm and the strong and healthy. Hanson v. Dickinson, 188 Iowa 728, 
176 N.W. 823 (1920). A material aggravation, worsening, lighting up or acceleration of 
any prior condition has been a viewed as a compensable event ever since initial 
enactment of our workers’ compensation statutes. Ziegler v. United States Gypsum Co., 
252 Iowa 613; 106 N.W.2d 591 (1961). While a claimant must show that the injury 
proximately caused the medical condition sought to be compensable, it is well 
established in Iowa that a cause is “proximate” when it is a substantial factor in bringing 
about that condition. It need not be the only causative factor, or even the primary or the 
most substantial cause to be compensable under the Iowa workers’ compensation 
system. Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994); Blacksmith v. All-
American, Inc., 290 N.W.2d 348 (Iowa 1980). 
Having reviewed all of the evidence in the record, I find that the claimant has 
failed to carry his burden of proof that either of his alleged work injuries were a 
substantial causal or aggravating factor to his condition.  A total of five physicians have 
provided opinions that claimant’s right shoulder condition is not causally related to either 
of his work injuries.  Dr. Milani, Dr. Patterson, Dr. Gleason, Dr. Mooney and Dr. Fields 
were all unable to hook up medical causation to either of claimant’s alleged injuries.

The claimant argues that the flaw in each of these opinions is the underlying 
assumption that Mr. Francis had any ongoing symptoms in his right shoulder prior to the 
work injuries. 
The claimant has provided a well-reasoned expert medical opinion from Dr. 
Rondinelli.  The problem is, for this opinion to be accepted, I must rely upon claimant’s 
credibility.  Dr. Rondinelli’s theory of causation is essentially that Mr. Francis was 
predisposed to injury and disability because of his underlying right shoulder condition.  If 
his shoulder was essentially fully functioning and pain free at the time of his work 
injuries, I could easily find it more likely than not that one or both of his work injuries 
substantially aggravated or lit up the underlying shoulder condition.  For that theory to 
be likely, or more likely than not, I would have to be able to rely upon the claimant’s 
description of his right shoulder condition prior to the accidents.  This is problematic in 
this record. 
Mr. Francis reported right shoulder pain to the V.A. Hospital in 2020 and 2021.  
Based upon these records it appears the medical providers at the V.A. believed this 
problem to be chronic.  In fact, at his deposition, he denied ever having any pain in his 
right shoulder prior to the work injuries at all.  While it is certainly possible that Mr. 
Francis simply forgot about the right shoulder pain he was having in 2020 and 2021, I 
find his ultimate explanation for these records at hearing was unconvincing.  For this 
reason, I am left to wonder what his actual right shoulder condition was at the time of 
the alleged injuries.  It seems likely in this record that he was actually having some level 
of chronic right shoulder pain at the time of the alleged work injuries.  Contemporaneous 
medical reports further support this conclusion.  (Jt. Ex. 5, p. 62)  This is also consistent 
with what his treating physicians, Dr. Milani, Dr. Patterson and Dr. Gleason all believed 
during the time they treated him.  Thus while it is possible one or both of Mr. Francis’s 
alleged work injuries is a cause of his right shoulder disability, the evidence does not 
support a finding of probability. 
For these reasons, I find that Mr. Francis is unable to meet his burden of proof for 
the threshold question of medical causation.  I further find that all other issues are 
moot.1 
ORDER 
THEREFORE IT IS ORDERED 
Claimant shall take nothing further on either file.
