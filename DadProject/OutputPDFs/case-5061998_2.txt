RULING ON JUDICIAL REVIEW 
 
Summary of Issues Presented 
 
This is a judicial review action in which Petitioner challenges the 
decision of the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commissioner 
(“Commissioner”) awarding Respondent benefits.  A hearing was held on 
August 14, 2020.  Petitioners were represented by counsel, Patrick J. 
O’Connell.  Respondent was represented by counsel, Charles Showalter.  
The issues before the Court in this case are pretty straightforward.  
Neither party disputes that on November 10, 2015, Respondent, Chad 
Simons, suffered a work related injury to his right quadriceps tendon, 
requiring surgical repair and extensive physical therapy.

In this appeal, there are two issues for the Court to decide – a 
factual dispute and a legal dispute.  Petitioner contends that substantial 
evidence does not support the factual determination made by the 
Commissioner that Respondent’s November 10, 2015 right quadriceps 
tendon injury also caused an injury to Respondent’s right hip in the form 
of loss of range of motion and strength.  Petitioner also contends that the 
Commissioner erred as a matter of law in determining that Respondent’s 
injury was compensable under Iowa Code § 85.34(2)(v) as an injury to the 
body as a whole.  Instead, Petitioners argue that the Commissioner 
should have found that Respondent’s injury was only compensable under 
Iowa Code § 85.34(2)(p) as an injury to a scheduled member (right leg) 
because the anatomical situs of the injury (quadriceps tendon) was 
located entirely within the leg.   
In response, Respondent urges the Court to affirm the holding of 
the Commissioner.  Respondent contends that the Commissioner 
properly concluded that Respondent’s quadriceps tendon injury caused 
impairment to Respondent’s hip, turning his leg injury into a body as a 
whole injury.  Contrary to Petitioner’s assertion, Respondent contends 
that it is the situs of the impairment, not the situs of the injury, that 
controls whether the injury is compensable as a body as a whole or 
scheduled member injury.  Respondent therefore contends that the

Commissioner did not err as a matter of law and the Commissioner’s 
decision is supported by substantial evidence.   
Having carefully examined the record, the briefs and legal 
arguments of counsel, for the reasons set forth herein, the Court 
concludes that the decision of the Commissioner must be affirmed. 
Findings of Fact 
 
Respondent began working at Omega in 1997. Omega manufactures 
cabinets and cabinet accessories. As of the date of his injury, Respondent 
worked as a lead person. He was required to move cabinets off the line. 
Respondent sustained a work-related injury to his right knee on 
November 10, 2015. (Arb. Tr. 14–17). 
 
The same day, Dr. Arnold Delbridge, M.D., evaluated Respondent 
for a swollen right knee. Dr. Delbridge aspirated the knee. (Joint Ex. 1, 1). 
On November 30, 2015, Dr. Matthew Smith, M.D., evaluated Respondent

diabetes, and was overweight. (Joint Ex. 2, 4). On December 9, 2015, Dr. 
Benjamin Torrez, D.O., saw Respondent for right knee pain. Dr. Torrez 
assessed that Respondent had a right knee partial quad tear and 
recommended that he get an MRI. (Joint Ex. 3, 5–6). 
 
Respondent took an MRI on December 16, 2015, which showed 
evidence of a distal quadriceps tendon tear. (Joint Ex. 4, 9). On February

Respondent initiated this claim by filing an Original Notice and 
Petition at the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commission. He alleged he 
had a right knee and hip injury that occurred on November 10, 2015 
from an alleged workplace injury. On February 19, 2019, the parties 
presented arguments at the Arbitration Hearing before Iowa Deputy 
Workers’ Compensation Commissioner James F. Christenson. The Deputy 
Commissioner issued the Arbitration Decision on April 8, 2019, 
concluding that Respondent carried his burden of proof that he sustained 
an injury to his right knee and hip that arose out of and in the course of 
employment. On April 7, 2020, the Commissioner filed the Appeal 
Decision in which he summarily affirmed the Arbitration Decision in its 
entirety. Petitioners filed the present Petition for Judicial Review of the 
Commissioner’s Appeal Decision on May 1, 2020. Petitioners allege the 
Commissioner made errors of law and the Commissioner’s findings of 
fact were not supported by substantial evidence. 
Standard of Review 
 
The Iowa Administrative Procedure Act, Chapter 17A of the Iowa 
Code, governs judicial review of administrative agency decisions. The 
court shall reverse, modify, or grant other appropriate relief from final 
E-FILED  2020 NOV 10 2:11 PM POLK - CLERK OF DISTRICT COURT

8 
 
agency action if it determines the substantial rights of a petitioner have 
been prejudiced by any of the means set forth in Iowa Code section 
17A.19(10)(a)-(n). Review of agency action is at law, not de novo, and is 
limited to the record made before the agency. Taylor v. Iowa Dep’t of Job 
Serv., 362 N.W.2d 534, 537 (Iowa 1985). Additional evidence or issues not 
considered by the agency cannot be considered by the court. Iowa Code § 
17A.19(7)(2020); Meads v. Iowa Dep’t of Social Servs., 366 N.W.2d 555, 
559 (Iowa 1985). The court may not substitute its judgment for that of 
the agency. Mercy Health Center v. State Health Facilities Council, 360 
N.W.2d 808, 809 (Iowa 1985). The court may not usurp the agency's 
function of making factual findings. McSpadden v. Big Ben Coal Co., 288 
N.W.2d 181, 186 (Iowa 1980). 
The court shall reverse, modify, or grant other appropriate relief 
from agency action if the agency action was based upon a determination 
of fact clearly vested by a provision of law in the discretion of the agency 
that is not supported by substantial evidence in the record before the 
court when that record is viewed as a whole. Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(f). 
“Record viewed as a whole” means that the adequacy of the evidence in 
the record before the court to support a particular finding of fact must 
be judged in light of all the relevant evidence in the record cited by any 
party that detracts from the findings, as well as all of the relevant 
E-FILED  2020 NOV 10 2:11 PM POLK - CLERK OF DISTRICT COURT

9 
 
evidence in the record cited by any party that supports it. § 
17A.19(10)(f)(3). This includes any determinations of veracity by the 
presiding officer who personally observed the demeanor of the witnesses 
and the agency's explanation of why the relevant evidence in the record 
supports its material findings of fact. Id.  
The evidence need not amount to a preponderance in order to be 
substantial evidence, but a mere scintilla will not suffice. Elliot v. Iowa 
Dep’t of Transp., 377 N.W.2d 250, 256 (Iowa Ct. App. 1985). Substantial 
evidence means the quantity and quality of evidence that would be 
deemed sufficient by a neutral, detached and reasonable person, to 
establish the fact at issue when the consequences resulting from the 
establishment of that fact are understood to be serious and of great 
importance. Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(f)(1). The fact that two inconsistent 
conclusions can be drawn from the evidence does not mean that one of 
those conclusions is unsupported by substantial evidence. Moore v. Iowa 
Dep’t of Transp., 473 N.W.2d 230, 232 (Iowa Ct. App. 1991). The relevant 
inquiry is not whether the evidence might support a different finding, but 
whether the evidence supports the findings actually made. Id. 
The commissioner has a duty to state the evidence relied upon and 
detail the reasons for any conclusions. Pitzer v. Rowley Interstate, 507 
N.W.2d 389, 392 (Iowa 1993) (citing Catalfo v. Firestone Tire & Rubber 
E-FILED  2020 NOV 10 2:11 PM POLK - CLERK OF DISTRICT COURT

10 
 
Co., 213 N.W.2d 506, 510 (Iowa 1973)). This requirement is satisfied if the 
reviewing court is able to determine with reasonable certainty the factual 
basis on which the administrative officer acted. Id. At 393. It is 
understood that an administrative agency “cannot in its decision set out 
verbatim all testimony in a case.” Id. at 392 (citing McDowell v. Town of 
Clarksville, 241 N.W.2d 904, 908 (Iowa 1976)) “Nor, when the agency 
specifically refers to some of the evidence, should the losing party be 
able, ipso facto, to urge successfully that the agency did not weigh all the 
other evidence.” Id. An agency decision is final if supported by 
substantial evidence. Robbennolt v. Snap-On Tools Corp., 555 N.W.2d 229, 
234 (Iowa 1996). 
The court shall also reverse, modify, or grant other appropriate 
relief from agency action if such action was based upon an erroneous 
interpretation of a provision of law whose interpretation has not clearly 
been vested by a provision of law in the discretion of the agency. Iowa 
Code § 17A.19(10)(c). The court shall not give deference to the view of 
the agency with respect to particular matters that have not been vested 
by a provision of law in the discretion of the agency. Iowa Code § 
17A.19(11)(b). However, appropriate deference is given when the contrary 
is true. Iowa Code § 17A.19(11)(c). The agency's findings are binding on 
E-FILED  2020 NOV 10 2:11 PM POLK - CLERK OF DISTRICT COURT

11 
 
appeal unless a contrary result is compelled as a matter of law. Ward v. 
Iowa Dep’t of Transp., 304 N.W.2d 236, 238 (Iowa 1981). 
Additionally, a reviewing court must also reverse, modify, or grant 
other appropriate relief when the agency's decision is “[b]ased upon an 
irrational, illogical, or wholly unjustifiable application of law to fact that 
has clearly been vested by a provision of law in the discretion of the 
agency.” Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(m). “In order to determine an 
employee's right to benefits, which is the agency's responsibility, the 
agency, out of necessity, must apply the law to the facts.” Mycogen Seeds 
v. Sands, 686 N.W.2d 457, 465 (Iowa 2004). Because the agency has been 
entrusted with the responsibility of applying the law to the facts, the 
“agency's application of the law to the facts can only be reversed if we 
determine such an application was ‘irrational, illogical, or wholly 
unjustifiable.’” Id. (citing Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(m)). 
“The findings of the commissioner are akin to a jury verdict, and we 
broadly apply them to uphold the commissioner's decision.” Quaker Oats 
Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 150 (Iowa 1996) (quoting Second Inj. Fund v. 
Shank, 516 N.W.2d 808, 812 (Iowa 1994) (citation omitted)). “We may 
reverse, modify, affirm or remand the case to the commissioner for 
further proceedings if we conclude the agency's action is affected by an 
error at law or if it is not supported by substantial evidence.” Id. at 150.

Conclusions of Law 
 
Petitioners contend that the Commissioner’s Appeal Decision was 
erroneous and must be reversed on the grounds set forth in Iowa Code 
§§ 17A.19(c) and (f). They allege that the Commissioner made an error of 
law by classifying Respondent’s injury to the quadriceps tendon and hip 
as an unscheduled hip injury pursuant to § 85.34(2)(v), rather than a 
scheduled leg injury pursuant to § 85.34(2)(p). Petitioners further allege 
that the Commissioner’s determination that Respondent’s November 10, 
2015 right quadriceps tendon injury also caused an injury to 
Respondent’s right hip in the form of loss of range of motion and 
strength was not supported by substantial evidence. 
A. Error of Law 
 
Petitioners allege the Commissioner made an error of law 
interpreting Iowa Code § 85.34. “The interpretation of the workers’ 
compensation statute has not been clearly vested by a provision of law in 
the discretion of the commissioner. Accordingly, we will reverse the 
agency’s decision if it is based on ‘an erroneous interpretation’ of the 
law.” Second Inj. Fund v. Kratzer, 778 N.W.2d 42, 45 (Iowa 2010) (citing 
Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(c)). 
 
Petitioners argue that any injury from the knee to just below the 
hip joint, including the quadriceps tendon, is compensable as a 
E-FILED  2020 NOV 10 2:11 PM POLK - CLERK OF DISTRICT COURT

13 
 
scheduled leg injury under § 85.34(2)(p). They argue that an injury only 
becomes an unscheduled injury to the body as a whole under § 
85.34(2)(v) if the injury is to the SI joint, the hip joint, or the low back, 
not to the hip flexion. Petitioners believe the Commissioner made an 
error of law by characterizing Respondent’s injury as a hip injury, 
because no doctor who examined Respondent specifically identified 
injuries to the SI joint, hip joint, or low back, so the Commissioner must 
have concluded Respondent had a hip injury based on the injury to his 
quadriceps tendon. Petitioners base their arguments on a series of cases 
that hold the Commission awards benefits based on the anatomical situs 
of injury, and the situs of an injury to the quadriceps tendon is in the 
leg.1 According to Petitioners, Respondent’s injury was a scheduled leg 
injury because his injury was to the quadriceps tendon. 
 
Petitioners point out the differences between the wording in the 
AMA Guides and the workers’ compensation statute. They argue that the 
doctors relied on the AMA Guides’ reference to impairments to the “lower 
extremity,” which includes the feet, hindfeet, ankles, legs, knees, hips, 
and pelvis, while the workers’ compensation statute differentiates

The Court concludes that the Commissioner did not make an error 
of law interpreting § 85.34. Petitioners are correct that the Commission 
awards benefits based on the anatomical situs of injury, and an injury 
that solely impairs the quadriceps tendon would be a leg injury. However, 
the scope of the anatomical situs is broader than Petitioners describe:

unscheduled, we look beyond the situs of the original injury

the body, a disability has been sustained to the body as a 
whole.

disability is scheduled or unscheduled.” Sullivan v. Standard Forwarding 
Co., File No. 5006696, 2004 WL 101642, at *9 (Arb. Dec. Jan. 12, 2004).

B. Substantial Evidence 
Petitioners also allege the Commissioner did not have substantial 
evidence to find that Respondent’s right quadriceps tendon injury also 
caused an injury to Respondent’s right hip in the form of loss of range of 
motion and strength.  Of course, Respondent bears the burden of proving 
by a preponderance of the evidence that his hip injury both arose out of 
and in the course of his employment. Quaker Oats Co., 552 N.W.2d at 
150. A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection 
is probable rather than merely possible. Sherman v. Pella Corp., 576 
N.W.2d 312, 321 (Iowa 1998). The question of medical causation is 
essentially within the domain of expert testimony. Cedar Rapids Cmty. 
Sch. Dist. v. Pease, 807 N.W.2d 839, 845 (Iowa 2011). Unrebutted medical 
testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911–12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). The weight 
given to an expert opinion is within the discretion of the fact finder and 
E-FILED  2020 NOV 10 2:11 PM POLK - CLERK OF DISTRICT COURT

17 
 
is impacted by the accuracy of the facts relied upon by the expert and 
other surrounding circumstances. Id. 
 
The Commissioner’s determination that Respondent met his burden 
to show proximate causation for his hip injury is supported by 
substantial evidence. Three out of the four doctors who examined 
Respondent stated that he had a hip injury and that the November 10, 
2015 work injury caused his hip injury. Dr. Delbridge opined that 
Respondent had loss of range of motion and strength in the right hip 
caused by the November 15, 2015 work injury. (Resp’t’s Ex. 1, 1–4). Dr. 
Smith, Respondent’s family doctor, also opined that Respondent had an 
impairment to his right hip caused by the November 10, 2015 work 
injury. (Resp’t’s Ex. 2, 7–8). Dr. Westpheling, Petitioners’ expert, also 
found that Respondent’s right hip injury was caused by Respondent’s 
right quadriceps tendon injury. Dr. Westpheling stated, “Although the hip 
joint itself is not directly involved, the quadriceps tendon crosses the hip 
joint and is a powerful hip flexor. Therefore, any weakness of the right 
hip flexion would be related to the original work injury.” (Pet’rs’ Ex. A, 1–
2). 
 
Dr. Stanford, the one doctor that did not find Respondent’s hip 
injury was caused by the November 10, 2015 work injury, stated that 
Respondent’s November 10, 2015 injury was only to the lower extremity 
E-FILED  2020 NOV 10 2:11 PM POLK - CLERK OF DISTRICT COURT

18 
 
and did not extend to the hip. His reason for this opinion was that there 
was no indication in his medical records that Respondent complained of 
right hip pain during his course of treatment in 2016. (Pet’rs’ Ex. G, 2). 
Respondent testified that he did not complain of right hip pain to Dr. 
Stanford because he did not start having hip pain until late 2017, and last 
saw Dr. Stanford on October 27, 2016. He testified that he did not seek 
follow-up care for his hip with Dr. Stanford because Dr. Stanford told him 
that, upon reaching MMI, his leg was as good as it was going to get. 
Respondent further testified that he did not seek treatment for his hip 
because he found it to be a hassle to get the workers’ compensation 
insurer to approve medical treatment. (Arb. Tr. 25–26). Dr. Stanford never 
examined Respondent’s hip and did not re-examine Respondent prior to 
providing his cursory opinion in March 2018 that Respondent did not 
have a hip impairment. 
 
Because three out of four doctors opined that Respondent’s 
impairment extended into the hip and the Commissioner had reason to 
give Dr. Stanford’s opinion less weight, the Commissioner had substantial 
evidence to conclude that Respondent had a hip impairment and that the 
November 10, 2015 work injury proximately caused Respondent’s hip 
impairment.
