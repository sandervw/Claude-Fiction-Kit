On March 21, 2024, the undersigned filed an arbitration decision in this case.  On 
April 9, 2024, the claimant filed a motion for rehearing. 
In the arbitration decision, the claimant sought an award of certain medical bills.  
An award was made of two hundred forty-four thousand one hundred thirty-three and 
02/100 dollars ($244,133.02) in medical bills.  Certain bills were excluded based upon 
reasoning outlined in the arbitration decision.  The claimant requests that the 
undersigned specifically list which medical bills were excluded and also explain why 
said bills were denied.   
The claimant requests that the undersigned place the award for payment of 
medical expenses in the order section of the arbitration decision, “so there is no 
confusion this is the deputy’s determination.”   
A motion for rehearing is made pursuant to 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.24.  
Any party may file an application for rehearing of a proposed decision in a contested 
case within 20 days after the issuance of the decision.  The motion for rehearing was 
filed within 20 days of the issuance of the arbitration decision. 
To the extent that the motion for rehearing requests an explanation for the 
undersigned’s ruling as to exclusion of certain medical bills, the motion is denied.  The 
undersigned provided an explanation in the arbitration decision as to why certain 
medical bills were excluded from the award.

A more appropriate vehicle for correcting the foregoing requests is an order nunc 
pro tunc.   
The phrase, “nunc pro tunc” means “now for then.”  See e.g. Black’s Law 
Dictionary, page 1218 (Revised 4th Edition 1968).  The definition in Black’s Law 
Dictionary further provides: “A phrase applied to acts allowed to be done after the time 
when they should be done, with a retroactive effect, i.e., with the same effect as if 
regularly done.”  Black’s at 1218.  A nunc pro tunc order “is not for the purpose of 
correcting judicial thinking, a judicial conclusion, or a mistake of law.”  Headley v. 
Headley, 172 N.W.2d 104, 108 (Iowa 1969).  The nunc pro tunc order can be employed 
to correct obvious errors or to make an order conform to the judge’s original intent.  
Graber v. District Court for Washington Cty, 410 N.W.2d 224, 229 (Iowa 1987); Brinson 
v. Spee Dee Delivery Service, No. 8-754/06-2074 (Iowa App. November 13, 2008).   
In this instance, it is appropriate to clarify the specific medical bills excluded from 
an award and add the amount of the award to the order section of the arbitration 
decision. 
IT IS THEREFORE ORDERED: 
Claimant’s motion for rehearing is granted in part and denied in part. 
That an order nunc pro tunc is hereby issued. 
The fourth paragraph of page 62 of the arbitration decision shall be modified to 
state: 
However, I question the claimant’s continual emergency room visits, 
at least one of which appeared to be at the urging of claimant’s counsel. 
There is no indication that Mr. Wooff or his counsel reached out to the 
employer prior to these allegedly emergent visits. Additionally, there is no 
indication that these visits produced a more favorable medical outcome than 
would have been achieved by care authorized by the employer. While the 
claimant will argue that the employer was not authorizing care at this time, 
the fact is that there is not sufficient evidence to show that the claimant 
contacted the employer before going to the emergency room on multiple 
occasions. Additionally, the arguably favorable medical outcome provided 
by these visits was a temporary relief of the claimant’s pain. No substantive 
procedures were performed on the claimant during these visits in order to 
provide him with lasting medical benefits. Furthermore, he was under the 
care of several physicians during this time. Therefore, I excluded several 
emergency room visits. These include visits on June 27, 2022, September 
14, 2022, and October 6, 2022, along with their associated physician fees.  
I also excluded several dates of treatment which I concluded were not 
reasonable and necessary.  These include billing records for which there 
were no corresponding medical records from June 8, 2020, August 31, 
2022, June 6, 2023, and August 15, 2023.
