STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
  
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A. The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Eric Coley. Claimant 
appeared through his attorney, Nicholas Shaull. Defendants appeared through their 
attorney, Stephen Spencer and company representative Kelli Cloud.  
  
The alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on October 6, 2025. The 
proceedings were digitally recorded. That recording constitutes the official record of this 
proceeding. Pursuant to the Commissioner’s July 21, 2023, Order, the undersigned has 
been delegated authority to issue a final agency decision in this alternate medical care 
proceeding. Therefore, this ruling is designated final agency action and any appeal of 
the decision would be to the Iowa District Court pursuant to Iowa Code section 17A. 
 
The record consists of Claimant’s Exhibit 1, pages 1-8, testimony of claimant and 
Kathy Coley, claimant’s spouse. Defendants did not present testimony nor offer exhibits. 
Counsel were permitted an opportunity to argue their cases and the record closed on 
October 6, 2025. 
 
ISSUE 
  
Claimant alleged he sustained a spinal cord injury leaving him paralyzed from the 
waist down. He seeks an order that would allow him to obtain a van with modifications 
that would permit him to drive.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
  
The undersigned, having considered all the evidence in the record, finds: 
  
Claimant Eric Coley suffered a spinal cord injury on or about March 5, 2025. This 
injury left claimant paralyzed from the waist down and he is confined to a motorized 
wheelchair. (CE 1) On or about April 7, 2025, the authorized occupational therapist, 
Natalie Uyeno, recommended a wheelchair-accessible van with modifications. (CE 3-5) 
This order was agreed to by authorized physician Jeffrey Berliner, D.O.  (CE 7)  
Occupational Therapist Uyeno noted that claimant is currently not ready to begin 
a driving evaluation, but it is anticipated that claimant will be a candidate for a driving 
evaluation in the future. (CE 5) Due the nature of claimant’s functional status, it is 
recommended that claimant remain seated in his personal mobility device to maintain 
positioning and skin protection. (Id.) Claimant is not able to transfer to a standard 
vehicle. (Id.) Because of the foregoing, a vehicle with modifications is deemed medically 
necessary by Ms. Uyeno and Dr. Berliner to function in his home and community 
environments. (CE 3, 6, 7) Claimant’s personal mobility device weighs over 400 pounds.  
The modifications recommended by Ms. Uyeno were to accommodate both 
dependent-passenger transport with the ability to add additional driving equipment when 
appropriate for the goal of true independent driving. (CE 5)  
The modifications include: 
Lowered Floor 
Allows for adequate head clearance for vehicle access while seated in a 
wheelchair. 
 
Power Doors 
Allow for independent vehicle access due to lack of hand function, sitting 
balance, and strength to manage a standard door. 
 
Power Kneel System 
Allows for decreased ramp angle, enabling safe and independent vehicle 
entry and exit as well as overall shorter ramp length to accommodate 
standard loading zone available in a handicapped parking space. 
 
Power Ramp 
Allows for independent vehicle access while seated in a wheelchair due to 
inability to perform a standard vehicle transfer.

Removable Front Driver Seat 
Allows for wheelchair access to driver's position to allow for independent 
driving while seated in a wheelchair when ready. 
 
Removable Front Passenger Seat 
Allows for wheelchair access to the front passenger position to allow for

Prior to his injury, claimant was working as an over-the-road truck driver. While at 
home, he would often opt not to drive, asking his wife to do so.  
Defendants argued at hearing that they were willing to provide the modifications 
but would not provide the vehicle to which the modifications would be applied. 
Defendants have also expressed that they are willing to cover the costs of any 
transportation for claimant’s medical treatment and services. Defendants work with a 
company called Paradigm to provide those vehicle modifications.  
Claimant testified that a vehicle from an authorized provider would have a base 
cost of around $45,000 to $50,000 plus an additional $50,000 for the modifications for a 
total of $100,000 to $108,000.  A used van would cost approximately $8,000 less, but 
would require an extended warranty which may drive the cost above a new van. 
Claimant does not have the funds to purchase either a new or used van.  
Claimant is currently living at QLI Rehabilitation in Omaha, Nebraska. His family 
lives in Southwest Missouri. Claimant owns a 2015 Chevy Silverado driven over 
153,000 miles which he purchased around July 2021. His son is the primary user of the 
truck both prior to the work injury and after. His wife owns a GMC Acadia. Claimant was 
told that his truck was too high to lift the chair into, and the GMC Acadia’s interior space 
was too low to accommodate him in the personal mobility device.  
Other than transfers from one medical institution to another, most of which was 
done via air, claimant has left his care facilities twice to go fishing and a few times for 
meals and other activities. The Craig facility in Colorado provided a class C motor home

REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
   
Under Iowa law, the employer is required to provide care to an injured employee 
and is permitted to choose the care. Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 
433 (Iowa 1997). 
 
[T]he employer is obliged to furnish reasonable services and supplies to 
treat an injured employee, and has the right to choose the care .... The 
treatment must be offered promptly and be reasonably suited to treat the 
injury without undue inconvenience to the employee. If the employee has 
reason to be dissatisfied with the care offered, the employee should 
communicate the basis of such dissatisfaction to the employer, in writing if 
requested, following which the employer and the employee may agree to 
alternate care reasonably suited to treat the injury. If the employer and 
employee cannot agree on such alternate care, the commissioner may, 
upon application and reasonable proofs of the necessity therefor, allow 
and order other care. 
  
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment - and seeking alternate care - 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable. See Iowa 
R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e); Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995). 
Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact. Id. The 
employer’s obligation turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability. Id.; 
Harned v. Farmland Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983). 
  
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving. Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care. Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant. Long, 528 N.W.2d at 122. 
  
An employer's right to select the provider of medical treatment to an injured 
worker does not include the right to determine how an injured worker should be 
diagnosed, evaluated, treated, or other matters of professional medical judgment. 
Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 866389 (Declaratory Ruling, May 18, 1988).

Reasonable care includes care necessary to diagnose the condition and 
defendants are not entitled to interfere with the medical judgment of its own treating 
physician. Pote v. Mickow Corp., File No. 694639 (Review-Reopening, June 17, 1986). 
The issue in the present case is whether defendants or claimant should bear the 
burden of purchasing a van that is capable of being modified to accommodate claimant 
as either a passenger and/or a driver.  
Claimant is paralyzed from the waist down. He is unable to move his legs and he 
is currently confined to his care facility with opportunities to leave for outings dependent 
on the availability of transportation from the care facility. Transportation for medical 
visits is paid for by the defendants. Claimant seeks a finding that defendants be ordered 
to purchase a vehicle that is capable of being modified to accommodate his personal 
mobility device.  
Claimant is not currently able to drive. He is unlicensed and is not capable of 
driving nor capable of transferring himself from even the bed to a personal mobility 
device. The claimant has not made a showing that a modified van is reasonable care at 
this time.  
The order for a modified van, however, also addresses claimant as a passenger. 
Specifically, the order states “these vehicle modifications will be utilized for dependent-
passenger transport…” (CE 5) Defendants argue that it is the claimant’s responsibility to 
provide the van to be modified.  
Whether the modified van is reasonable medical care, appliance or transportation 
is a question of fact. Manpower Temporary Services v. Sioson, 529 N.W.2d 259, 263 
(Iowa, 1995) In Sioson, the Iowa Court of Appeals found that in the case of a worker 
rendered quadriplegic, a wheelchair accessible van was found to be an extension of 
claimant’s wheelchair, providing function to her otherwise immobile state. (Id.) The 
injured worker in Siosin was a single person who did not own or desire a motor vehicle 
prior to being injured. (Id. at 262.) There was evidence that the van was not for family 
use or in accordance with the pre-injury lifestyle. (Id.)   
Similarly in this case, the claimant was not a van user. In fact, he often did not 
drive while at home due to his occupation as an over-the-road trucker. He and his wife 
own two vehicles, neither of which is a van.  
In Siosin, the injured worker had all her transportation care provided by the 
employer and insurer at no cost to her. Sioson, 529 N.W.2d at 261. When she was not 
being transported by vehicles provided and paid for by the employer, the injured worker 
was reliant on friends, relatives and even primary care givers. (Id.)  City services were 
available to her but not for trips outside of her metropolitan area or at times when 
scheduling was difficult or impossible. (Id.)

Based on precedential case law, claimant in the present case is in need of a 
modified van to transport him outside of the care facility. Current means of 
transportation are limited and in some cases not available to him due to scheduling 
conflicts. A modified van does no fulfill the role of a family vehicle that would be used for 
individuals outside of the claimant. Claimant’s wife has her own vehicle and claimant’s 
son uses the Chevy Silverado pickup truck.  
The modified van is ordered by the authorized occupational therapist and by the 
authorized treating physician as a reasonable medical necessity, and there is no 
contrary medical evidence. Claimant does not have a vehicle that is capable of being 
modified. Thus the offer to modify a vehicle that does not exist is essentially an offer of 
no care which is not reasonable but rather unduly burdensome to claimant.  
Therefore it is found that claimant has carried his burden of proof that the 
modified van to allow dependent-passenger transport is a medical necessity and 
reasonable within the set of facts provided herein. It may be that claimant would be 
entitled to additional modifications in the future that would allow him to drive if claimant 
is cleared to drive. However, whether he will be able to drive is only speculative at this 
point.  
Defendant shall furnish to claimant, without cost, a vehicle or van specially 
equipped in the manner set forth in the report of the CC Craig Hospital, dated April 7, 
2025, to the extent that the modifications are needed for a passenger-dependent van. 
Other details such as title, license, and specific model, year, and type are the choice of 
employer so long as the vehicle provided is suitable for the necessary modifications. 
Maintenance, fuel, title, license and insurance for any such vehicle shall be the 
responsibility of claimant. This specially-equipped van may be used by claimant for any 
transportation purpose.   
 Therefore, I conclude claimant has established entitlement to alternate medical 
care. 
ORDER  
THEREFORE, IT IS ORDERED: 
  
Claimant’s petition for alternate medical care is granted. 
 
Defendants shall promptly authorize and pay for a vehicle or van specially 
equipped in the manner set forth in the report of the CC Craig Hospital, dated April 7,

Other details such as title, license, and specific model, year, and type are the choice of 
employer so long as the vehicle provided is suitable for the necessary modifications. 
Maintenance, fuel, title, license and insurance for any such vehicle shall be the 
responsibility of claimant. This specially-equipped van may be used by claimant for any 
transportation purpose.
