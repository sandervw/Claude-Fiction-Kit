                                                    STANDARD OF REVIEW 
The Iowa Administrative Procedure Act (IAPA), Iowa Code chapter 17A, governs the 
scope of a district court’s appellate review in workers’ compensation cases.4  Under the IAPA, a 
district court may reverse or modify a final agency action if it determines that action was 
erroneous under one or more of the grounds enumerated in that statute and a party's substantial 
rights have been prejudiced.5   
 
In several cases decided over the past decade involving a number of different provisions 
in the workers’ compensation statutes, the Iowa Supreme Court (the Court) concluded that the 
Legislature has not clearly vested the Commissioner with interpretive authority.6  Only if this 
court finds the Commissioner’s interpretation of a statute is erroneous may it substitute its own 
interpretation for that of the agency.7

In reviewing a statute, the court must be “guided by what the legislature actually said, 
rather than that which it might or should have said.”8  Coupled with this plain language principle 
of statutory construction is the principle that the workers’ compensation statutes were enacted by 
the Legislature “primarily for the benefit of the worker and the worker’s dependents” and should 
therefore be interpreted “broadly and liberally in keeping with the humanitarian objective of the 
statute.”9  “We will not defeat the statute’s beneficent purpose by reading something into it that 
is not there, or by a narrow and strained construction.”10  The Legislature provided the 
Commissioner with the authority to make and enforce “rules necessary to implement [the 
workers’ compensation statutes].”11  The Commissioner’s interpretation of his own agency rules 
is entitled to more deference.   
In addition, the Court has concluded the Commissioner has the authority to make factual 
findings.12  The factual findings contained in the agency’s decision regarding the instant issues 
are therefore examined for substantial evidence.13  When the claim of error relates to the 
agency’s fact finding, the question on review is whether the evidence “supports the findings 
actually made.”14  In deciding this question, the court recognizes the agency’s findings of fact 
have the effect of a jury verdict:

(Iowa 2012).  We do not ask “whether the evidence would support a different

supports the findings actually made.”  Schutjer v. Algona Manor Care Ctr., 780

A. 
Whether the Commissioner’s determination of the calculation method used 
to determine the present value of Respondent’s partial commutation is correct.  Petitioner 
urges the Commissioner’s ruling that Petitioner is not entitled to a second discount when the 
commuted weeks are taken from the back end, as opposed to the front end, of remaining lifetime 
benefits is not a correct interpretation of Iowa Code section 85.48.  The most relevant ground for 
review of the Commissioner’s interpretation of section 85.48 is found under Iowa Code section 
17A.19(10)(c):  Whether the agency action was “[b]ased upon an erroneous interpretation of a 
provision of law whose interpretation has not clearly been vested by a provision of law in the 
discretion of the agency.”  Id. 
Only if the court finds the Commissioner’s interpretation of section 85.48 regarding the 
appropriate method to be used in arriving at the present value in Respondent’s case is erroneous 
may the court substitute its own interpretation for that of the Commissioner.24

However, the Court has concluded the proper analysis in determining if a commutation plan is in 
the claimant’s best interest is whether the plan is reasonable: 
Claimant’s plans may not develop as profitably as he hopes but they are not

Respondent’s plans to pay off his high-interest debt, make necessary repairs to his home,

plan based upon his consultation with Mr. Lodden which he intends to follow through with.  The 
risk that Respondent’s plan may not work out or that he would spend unwisely—which the 
Diamond Court noted is present in every commutation circumstance—does not outweigh the 
benefits to Respondent achieved by eliminating his debt, repairing his home and acquiring 
investments if given the opportunity.

constant maturity published by the federal reserve in the H15 report settled 
immediately prior to the date of the appeal decision (1.49) plus 2 percent equals 
3.49 percent.  I therefore find the correct discount rate in this case is 3.49

The goal of statutory interpretation is determining legislative intent.  When statutory 
language is clear and its meaning is plain, the court must apply the express terms of the statute.  
Denison Mun. Utils. v. Iowa Workers’ Comp. Comm’r, 857 N.W.2d 230, 235 (Iowa 2014).  The 
statute should be read as a whole instead of considering specific words or phrases in isolation.  
Iowa Ins. Inst. v. Core Group of Iowa Ass’n for Justice, 867 N.W.2d 58, 72 (Iowa 2015). 
 
Under section 668.13(3), the rate shall be equal to that set forth in the “H15 report settled 
immediately prior to the date of the judgment” plus two percent.46  This section further provides 
that the State Court Administrator shall distribute notice monthly “of that rate.”   
It seems clear that the Legislature intended the monthly announcement by the State Court 
Administrator “of that rate” in the H15 report to be outcome determinative on the issue 
presented.  Any other conclusion would render the reference in section 668.13(3) to monthly 
publication of notice by the State Court Administrator superfluous.  It would mandate daily 
publication of the relevant rate by the State Court Administrator to all district courts under the 
plain language at the end of section 668.13(3) requiring the State Court Administrator to notify 
all district courts of “any changes to that rate.”47  It would require the court, as Respondent 
invites the court to do, to imply impose additional language on the existing statute that isn’t 
there.
