STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Shawn Geyssens, filed a petition in arbitration for workers’ 
compensation benefits against Arconic, Inc., as employer, and Indemnity Insurance 
Company of North America, as insurance carrier.  The undersigned heard this case on 
February 21, 2025, via Zoom.   
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the hearing.  On the 
hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  Those stipulations were 
accepted and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be made 
or discussed.  The parties are bound by their stipulations.   
The evidentiary record includes Joint Exhibits 1 through 7, Claimant’s Exhibits 1 
through 3, and Defendants’ Exhibits A through F.  Claimant testified on his own behalf.  
Defendants did not call any witnesses to testify.  The evidentiary record closed at the 
conclusion of the arbitration hearing.   
All parties filed their post-hearing briefs on March 28, 2025, at which time the 
case was deemed fully submitted.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned, having considered all of the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
The instant case stems from a stipulated right knee injury occurring on April 26, 
2022.  The claimant at the center of this case is Shawn Geyssens.  At the time of the 
evidentiary hearing that brought forth this claim, Geyssens was a 50-year-old individual.  
Geyssens began working as a thermal equipment operator for Arconic, Inc. in 2004.  As 
a thermal equipment operator, he was required to climb into forklifts and other mobile 
equipment. 
Geyssens’ relevant medical history includes a left knee injury that occurred while 
he was playing basketball.  On May 17, 2017, Geyssens presented to Charles Cassel, 
M.D., reporting intermittent left knee pain since shifting his knee while coming down 
from a jump shot during a basketball game 20 years ago. (Joint Exhibit 4, page 30)  The 
right knee was stable and demonstrated full range of motion on examination. (JE4, p. 
31)  Dr. Cassel assessed Geyssens with a chronic ACL tear and moderately unstable 
left knee. (Id.)  A May 22, 2017, MRI confirmed chronic tears of the ACL and medial 
meniscus. (JE4, p. 32)   
Geyssens also experienced issues with his right knee prior to the April 26, 2022, 
work injury.  On June 6, 2019, Geyssens reported right calf pain with no known injury. 
(JE4, p. 33)  X-rays of the right knee showed mild primary osteoarthritis. (Id.)  Thomas 
VonGillern, M.D. assessed right calf pain, right knee mild primary osteoarthritis, and 
possible right lateral head gastrocnemius tear. (Id.)   
A June 12, 2019, MRI revealed right low-grade medial soleus and/or epimysial 
strain about the medial soleus and medial head gastrocnemius, right low-grade partial 
or nearly resolved sequela of partial plantaris tendon tear, and right knee mild primary 
osteoarthritis. (JE4, pp. 35-36; see JE4, p. 37)   
Geyssens sustained a stipulated work injury on April 26, 2022.  Geyssens was

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence. Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury actually occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 
employment.  Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 150 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. 
Dial Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309, 311 (Iowa 1996). The words “arising out of” refer to the 
cause or source of the injury. The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 

GEYSSENS V. ARCONIC, INC. 
Page 8 
 
circumstances of the injury. 2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124, 128 (Iowa 
1995). An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists 
between the injury and the employment. Miedema, 551 N.W.2d 311. The injury must be 
a rational consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely 
incidental to the employment. Koehler Electric v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1, 3 (Iowa 2000); 
Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it 
happens within a period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may 
be when performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties 
or doing an activity incidental to them. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d at 150.  
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability. 
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. St. Luke's Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410, 420 
(Iowa 2001); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
While a claimant is not entitled to compensation for the results of a preexisting 
injury or disease, its mere existence at the time of a subsequent injury is not a defense. 
Rose v. John Deere Ottumwa Works, 247 Iowa 900, 908, 76 N.W.2d 756, 760-61 
(1956). If the claimant had a preexisting condition or disability that is materially 
aggravated, accelerated, worsened or lighted up so that it results in disability, claimant 
is entitled to recover. Nicks v. Davenport Produce Co., 254 Iowa 130, 135, 115 N.W.2d 
812, 815 (1962); Yeager v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co., 253 Iowa 369, 374-75,112 
N.W.2d 299, 302 (1961). 
The first issue to be addressed is whether Geyssens is entitled to healing period 
benefits.  Geyssens asserts entitlement to healing period benefits from January 23, 
2023, through March 10, 2023, while he was recovering from surgery. 
Healing period compensation describes temporary workers' compensation 
weekly benefits that precede an allowance of permanent partial disability benefits. 
Ellingson v. Fleetguard, Inc., 599 N.W.2d 440 (Iowa 1999). Section 85.34(1) provides 
that healing period benefits are payable to an injured worker who has suffered 
permanent partial disability until (1) the worker has returned to work; (2) the worker is 
medically capable of returning to substantially similar employment; or (3) the worker has 
achieved maximum medical recovery. The healing period can be considered the period 
during which there is a reasonable expectation of improvement of the disabling 
condition. See Armstrong Tire & Rubber Co. v. Kubli, 312 N.W.2d 60 (Iowa App. 1981). 

GEYSSENS V. ARCONIC, INC. 
Page 9 
 
Healing period benefits can be interrupted or intermittent. Teel v. McCord, 394 N.W.2d 
405 (Iowa 1986).  
I find claimant was off work, recovering from a surgical procedure causally 
related to the original work injury, between January 23, 2023, and March 10, 2023.  As 
such, I find claimant is entitled to healing period benefits between January 23, 2023, 
and March 10, 2023, less defendants’ credit for short-term disability benefits. 
 
The second issue to be addressed is whether the stipulated April 26, 2022, work 
injury, resulted in permanent disability.  In this regard, I found the causation opinions 
and impairment ratings of Dr. Hussain and Dr. Rondinelli to be the most convincing and 
consistent with Iowa’s workers’ compensation laws.  The weight of the evidence shows 
that Geyssens had pre-existing primary osteoarthritis in his right knee, which was 
aggravated, accelerated, worsened, or lighted up by the stipulated work injury on April 
26, 2022.  The need for surgical intervention naturally and proximately flows from the 
accident.  The accident was a significant contributing factor to the need for the 
procedure.  Claimant met his burden of proving a causal connection between the 
stipulated work injury to his right knee and the right knee arthroscopically-assisted ACL 
reconstruction with hamstring autograft, possible allograft, and partial medial 
meniscectomy chondroplasty.   
Under the Iowa Workers' Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is 
compensated either for a loss, or loss of use, of a scheduled member under Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(a)-(u) or for loss of earning capacity under section 85.34(2)(v).  Mr. 
Geyssens sustained an injury to the right knee.  The knee is a scheduled member injury 
that is compensated under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(p). 
Where an injury is limited to a scheduled member the loss is measured 
functionally, not industrially. Graves v. Eagle Iron Works, 331 N.W.2d 116, 117 (Iowa 
1983). The 2017 statutory amendments also change prior case law, which held that 
functional disability was not limited to an impairment rating. As revised, Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(x) provides that when determining functional disability, the extent of 
loss or percentage of permanent impairment shall be determined solely by utilizing the 
guides to the evaluation of permanent impairment, published by the American medical 
association, as adopted by the workers' compensation commissioner by rule pursuant to 
chapter 17A. Lay testimony or agency expertise shall not be utilized in determining loss 
or percentage of permanent impairment. Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(x). 
 
Having adopted the impairment rating assigned by Dr. Rondinelli, I found 
Geyssens proved a functional loss of 44 percent of the right lower extremity.  Pursuant 
to Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(p), claimant is entitled to a proportional award equivalent 
to 44 percent of 220 weeks.  Therefore, I conclude that claimant is entitled to an award 
of 96.8 weeks of permanent partial disability benefits, commencing on December 29, 
2023.  Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(p)

At this juncture, I note that defendants listed apportionment as a disputed issue

Geyssens next seeks an award of past medical expenses.  The employer shall 
furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, chiropractic, podiatric, 
physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance, and hospital services and supplies for all 
conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law. The employer shall also 
allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred for those services. 
The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except where the employer 
has denied liability for the injury. Iowa Code section 85.27. Holbert v. Townsend 
Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial Commissioner 78 
(Review-Reopening October 1975). 
Geyssens is seeking reimbursement for the medical expenses contained in 
Exhibit 3.  The medical expenses in Claimant’s Exhibit 3 appear to be for the treatment 
of Geyssens’ work injury. I find the medical treatment he received was reasonable, 
necessary, and causally related to the April 26, 2022, work injury. I conclude the 
defendants are liable for all causally related and reasonable medical expenses outlined 
in Exhibit 3. 
The last issue to be addressed is costs. Assessment of costs is a discretionary 
function of the agency. Iowa Code § 10A.328. Because claimant was successful in his 
claim, I conclude it is appropriate to assess claimant's costs in some amount. 
Geyssens first seeks reimbursement for his filing fees ($257.50). This cost is 
appropriate and assessed pursuant to 876 IAC 4.33(7). 
Second, Geyssens seeks the cost of Dr. Hussain’s medical report ($2,875.00).  
Agency rule 4.33(6) permits the assessment of the reasonable costs of “obtaining no 
more than two doctors' or practitioners' reports.” However, the Iowa Supreme Court has 
held that only the cost of drafting the expert's report is permissible in lieu of testimony. 
Des Moines Area Regional Transit Authority v. Young, 867 N.W.2d 839, 845-846 (Iowa 
2015). The Court stated that the “underlying medical expenses associated with the 
examination do not become costs of a report needed for a hearing, just as they do not 
become costs of the testimony or deposition.” (Young, 867 N.W.2d 839, 846 (Iowa 
2015). 
Exhibit 2 provides that Dr. Hussain charged $2,875.00 for his “Narrative Report.”
