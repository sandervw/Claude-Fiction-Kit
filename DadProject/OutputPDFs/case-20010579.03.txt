STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Ricardo Ramirez, filed a petition in review-reopening seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from Arconic, Inc., employer, and Indemnity Insurance Company 
of North America, insurer, both as defendants.  This matter was heard on August 29, 
2025, with a final submission date of September 19, 2025. 
The record in this case consists of Joint Exhibits 1-3, Claimant’s Exhibits 1-5, 
Defendants’ Exhibits A-E, and the testimony of claimant. 
STIPULATIONS 
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the review-reopening 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this review-
reopening decision and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will 
be raised or discussed in this decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
An arbitration decision was filed in this matter on September 14, 2022. That 
decision found, in part, that claimant sustained an injury that arose out of and in the

filed a petition for judicial review in this matter.  The decision was affirmed at the district

Arbitration Decision. 
Claimant began his employment with Arconic in June of 2014.  On November 27, 
2019, claimant was “throwing alloy” of different weights when he experienced pain in his 
left upper extremity.  (Arbitration Decision, page 2) 
On November 11, 2020, claimant was evaluated by Jonathan Winston, M.D.  Dr. 
Winston assessed claimant as having a moderate left carpal tunnel syndrome and an 
electrodiagnostically silent cubital tunnel syndrome.  Dr. Winston recommended 
claimant have a left carpal tunnel and left cubital tunnel release for treatment.  (Arb.

On February 7, 2024, claimant was assessed by Dr. Winston.  Claimant was 
assessed as having left carpal tunnel syndrome.  Dr. Winston recommended claimant 
have an updated nerve study.  (Joint Exhibit 1, page 3) 
On March 27, 2024, claimant underwent EMG/nerve conduction velocity studies 
for the left upper extremity.  It showed evidence of a moderate left median neuropathy 
at the wrist.  (JE 2)  
Claimant returned to Dr. Winston on April 3, 2024.  He assessed claimant as 
having a moderate carpal tunnel syndrome following the recent study.  A left endoscopic 
carpal tunnel release was discussed and chosen as a treatment option.  (JE 1, p. 5) 
On December 20, 2024, claimant underwent a left endoscopic carpal tunnel 
release.  Surgery was performed by Dr. Winston.  (JE 1, pp. 20-21) 
Claimant returned to Dr. Winston on January 2, 2025.  Claimant had stiffness in 
his upper extremity.  Claimant was sent to physical therapy.  (JE 1, p. 9) 
Claimant returned to Dr. Winston on February 13, 2025.  Claimant had improved.  
Claimant still had dysesthesias in the median nerve distribution.  Claimant was put on a 
10-pound weight limitation.  (JE 1, p. 13)

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The first issue to be determined is whether claimant is entitled to permanent 
partial disability benefits under a review-reopening proceeding. 
 The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 

RAMIREZ V. ARCONIC, INC. 
Page 4 
 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
A possibility of causation is not sufficient; a probability is necessary. Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996) (citing Holmes v. Bruce 
Motor Freight, Inc., 215 N.W.2d 296, 297 (Iowa 1974)). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 
621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 
(Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
Upon review-reopening, claimant has the burden to show a change in condition 
related to the original injury since the original award or settlement was made.  The 
change may be either economic or physical.  Blacksmith v. All-American, Inc., 290 
N.W.2d 348, 350 (Iowa 1980); Henderson v. Iles, 250 Iowa 787, 793, 96 N.W.2d 321, 
324 (1959).  A mere difference of opinion of experts as to the percentage of disability 
arising from an original injury is not sufficient to justify a different determination on a 
petition for review-reopening.  Rather, claimant's condition must have worsened or 
deteriorated in a manner not contemplated at the time of the initial award or settlement 
before an award on review-reopening is appropriate.  Bousfield v. Sisters of Mercy, 249 
Iowa 64, 69-70, 86 N.W.2d 109, 113 (1957).   
Under the Iowa Workers' Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is 
compensated either for a loss or loss of use of a scheduled member under Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(a)-(u) or as an unscheduled injury pursuant to the provisions of section 
85.34(2)(v). The extent of scheduled member disability benefits to which an injured 
worker is entitled is determined by using the functional method. Functional disability is 
"limited to the loss of the physiological capacity of the body or body part.” Mortimer v. 
Fruehauf Corp., 502 N.W.2d 12, 15 (Iowa 1993); Sherman v. Pella Corp., 576 N.W.2d 
312, 320 (Iowa 1998).   
Iowa Code section 85.34(x) states:  
x. In all cases of permanent partial disability described in 
paragraphs “a” through “u”, or paragraph “v” when determining functional 
disability and not loss of earning capacity, the extent of loss or percentage 
of permanent impairment shall be determined solely by utilizing the guides

The arbitration decision found that claimant had a work-related cubital tunnel and 
carpal tunnel syndrome.  The decision also found that defendants should provide the 
left carpal tunnel and left cubital tunnel releases as recommended by Dr. Winston.  (Arb. 
Dec., p. 11) 
Defendants have provided the carpal tunnel release as ordered.  There is no 
evidence in the record as to why defendants did not authorize the cubital tunnel release. 
Claimant’s credible testimony is that he still has numbness and tingling in his left 
hand.  Claimant’s credible testimony is that he has loss of strength and range of motion 
in his left hand.  (Tr., pp. 8-9) 
Two experts have opined regarding the extent of claimant’s permanent 
impairment from his injury.  Dr. Bansal evaluated claimant two times for IMEs. In his 
most recent IME, Dr. Bansal found that claimant had a permanent impairment to his left 
upper extremity due to his carpal tunnel syndrome and his cubital tunnel syndrome.  Dr. 
Bansal’s IME indicates that claimant still has loss of range of motion and strength in his 
left upper extremity.  Dr. Bansal last evaluated claimant on July 3, 2025. 
Dr. Winston performed surgery on claimant and treated claimant for an extended 
period of time.  Dr. Winston last saw claimant on February 13, 2025.  (JE 1, p. 13)  In a 
letter, written by defendants’ attorney, Dr. Winston indicated claimant had no permanent 
impairment regarding his carpal tunnel syndrome.  Dr. Winston gave no opinion 
regarding permanent impairment due to claimant’s cubital tunnel syndrome. 
Claimant credibly testified Dr. Bansal spent a longer amount of time in his 
evaluation of claimant’s strength and range of motion of his left upper extremity.  He 
testified Dr. Bansal measured his range of motion and strength with measuring 
instruments.  He testified that Dr. Winston did not. 
Dr. Bansal’s opinion regarding claimant’s permanent impairment is more detailed 
than that of Dr. Winston.  The record indicates Dr. Bansal tested claimant’s range of 
motion and strength with instruments.  Dr. Winston’s permanent impairment evaluation

The next issue to be determined is claimant’s entitlement to reimbursement for 
Dr. Bansal’s IME under Iowa Code section 85.39. 
Iowa Code section 85.39 permits an employee to be reimbursed for subsequent 
examination by a physician of the employee's choice where an employer-retained 
physician has previously evaluated “permanent disability” and the employee believes 
that the initial evaluation is too low.   Iowa Code § 85.39(2) (2023).  The section also 
permits reimbursement for reasonably necessary transportation expenses incurred and 
for any wage loss occasioned by the employee attending the subsequent examination.  
Iowa Code § 85.39 (1), (2).   
The Iowa Supreme Court has provided a literal interpretation of the plain 
language of Iowa Code section 85.39, stating that section only allows the employee to 
obtain an independent medical evaluation at the employer’s expense if an evaluation of 
permanent disability has already been made by an employer retained physician.  Des 
Moines Area Reg’l Transit Auth. v. Young, 867 N.W.2d 839, 847 (Iowa 2015). Under 
the Young decision, an employee can only obtain an IME at the employer's expense if 
an evaluation of permanent disability has been made by an employer-retained 
physician. Id. 
An employer is only liable to reimburse an employee for the cost of an 
examination conducted pursuant to this subsection if the injury for which the employee 
is being examined is determined to be compensable.  Iowa Code § 85.39(2) (2017).  
“An employer is not liable for the cost of such examination if the injury for which the 
employee is being examined is determined not to have a compensable injury. A 
determination of the reasonableness of a fee for an examination made pursuant to this 
subsection, shall be based on the typical fee charged by a medical provider to perform 
an impairment rating in the local area where the examination is conducted.”  Iowa Code 
§ 85.39(2); Tweeten v. Tweeten, 999 N.W.2d 270, 282 (Iowa 2023).

Iowa Code section 85.39(2), as amended, allows an employee with a 
compensable injury to be reimbursed the reasonable cost of an examination. See Iowa 
Code § 85.39(2)(2017); Mid American Construction LLC v. Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d 838, 849 
(Iowa 2024). Under the statute, an examination may include reviewing medical records, 
conducting a physical exam, opining on causation, assessing permanent impairment, 
assigning restrictions, and recommending further care. Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d at 851.  
The burden is on the claimant to show the reasonableness of his or her chosen 
physician’s IME fee. Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d at 852; see also Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e) 
(stating “Ordinarily, the burden of proof on an issue is upon the party who would suffer 
loss if the issue were not established.”); Pexa v. Auto Owners Ins. Co., 686 N.W.2d 150, 
156 (Iowa 2004)(stating “the plaintiff has the burden to prove the reasonable value of 
the [medical] service rendered”).  The reasonableness of an IME fee is a question of 
fact to be decided by the commissioner. See Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 
154 (Iowa 1996). The reasonableness of an IME fee must be based upon the typical fee 
charged by a medical provider performing an impairment rating in the local area where 
the IME is conducted. Iowa Code § 85.39 (2); Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d at 852.   
In Des Moines Area Reg’l Transit Auth. v. Young, 867 N.W.2d 839, 847 (Iowa 
2015), the Supreme Court noted that in cases where Iowa Code section 85.39 IME 
reimbursement is not triggered, a claimant can still be reimbursed expenses associated 
with the preparation of a written report as a cost under administrative rule 876 IAC 4.33 
because the report is being used as evidence in lieu of the doctor’s testimony.  Id. at 
846-47.  However, only the cost of the report can be assessed, not the underlying exam 
or treatment. Id. 
Iowa Code section 85.39 limits a worker to one IME.  Larson Mfg. Co. v. 
Thorson, 763 N.W.2d 842 (Iowa 2009) 
Defendants contend that because claimant was reimbursed for an IME in the 
underlying arbitration hearing, claimant cannot be reimbursed for an IME under Iowa 
Code section 85.39 in a review-reopening proceeding.  This is incorrect.  The rule in 
Larson indicates that claimant can only be reimbursed for one IME for a proceeding.  
Claimant’s review-reopening petition is a separate claim, and evidence was taken in a 
separate proceeding.  Agency caselaw indicates that claimant is entitled to 
reimbursement for an IME in a review-reopening proceeding where claimant has 
prevailed.  Zalaznick v. John Deere Dubuque Works, File No. 5066386.02 (Appeal 
Decision Feb. 2, 2023); Gibbs v. John Deere Dubuque Works, File No. 5067271.01 
(Review-Reopening Decision Sept. 14, 2023) 
Dr. Winston indicated, in a May 15, 2025, letter written by defendants’ counsel, 
that claimant had no permanent impairment.  Dr. Bansal issued his report regarding 
permanent impairment in a July 25, 2025, report.  Given this chronology, claimant is 
entitled to reimbursement for Dr. Bansal’s IME.
