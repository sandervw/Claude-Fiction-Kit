STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
Christopher Lee, claimant, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from Walmart, employer, and New Hampshire Insurance 
Company, insurance carrier, as defendants. The hearing was held on January 11, 2024.  
Pursuant to an order from the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commissioner, this case 
was heard via videoconference using Zoom with all parties and the court reporter 
appearing remotely.   
 
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing. On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations. Those 
stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration decision and 
no factual issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised or discussed in this 
decision. The parties are now bound by their stipulations.  
 
 Christopher Lee and his mother, Linda Lee, testified live at the trial. Claimant 
also attempted to call Gabe Washington as a witness. Defendants, however, objected to  
Mr. Washington’s testimony because he was not identified in claimant’s discovery 
responses, nor was he included/identified on claimant’s witness list. Defendants were 
first informed of Mr. Washington’s existence and intention to testify as Mr. Lee’s 
caretaker on January 10, 2024, the day prior to hearing. Defendants argued they would 
be prejudiced by Mr. Washington’s testimony because they still did not know the

ISSUES 
 
 
The parties identified the following disputed issues on the hearing report: 
 
1. Whether the stipulated work-injury on August 8, 2020, entitles claimant to 
permanent partial disability benefits, and if so, the extent of claimant’s 
entitlement of permanency benefits.  
 
2. The proper commencement date for permanent partial disability benefits, if 
awarded. 
 
3. Whether claimant is entitled to temporary total or healing period benefits 
from August 9, 2020, through July 10, 2023.  
 
4. Whether claimant is entitled to payment of the medical expenses listed in 
Claimant’s Exhibit 9. 
 
5. Whether claimant is entitled to alternate medical care. 
 
6. Whether claimant is entitled to recover the cost of an independent medical 
examination pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.39.  
 
7. Assessment of costs.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence. Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 
 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 
employment. Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. Dial 
Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309 (Iowa 1996). The words “arising out of” refer to the cause or 
source of the injury. The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 
circumstances of the injury. 2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124 (Iowa 1995). An 
injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists between the injury 
and the employment. Miedema, 551 N.W.2d 309. The injury must be a rational 
consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely incidental to 
the employment. Koehler Electric v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1 (Iowa 2000); Miedema, 551 
N.W.2d 309. An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it happens within a 

LEE V. WALMART 
Page 10 
 
period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may be when 
performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties or doing 
an activity incidental to them. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143. 
 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based. A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause. A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible. George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996). 
 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability. 
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); 
Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995). Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted expert medical 
testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 
N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 1994). 
 
When an expert's opinion is based upon an incomplete or incorrect history, it is 
not necessarily binding on the commissioner or the court. It is then to be weighed, 
together with other facts and circumstances, the ultimate conclusion being for the finder 
of the fact. Musselman v. Central Telephone Company, 154 N.W.2d 128 (Iowa 
1967); Bodish v. Fischer, Inc., 257 Iowa 521, 133 N.W.2d 867 (1965). The 
commissioner as trier of fact has the duty to determine the credibility of the witnesses 
and to weigh the evidence, together with the other disclosed facts and circumstances, 
and then to accept or reject the opinion. Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Casualty Co., 
526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995). 
 
Based on the above findings of fact, I conclude Lee failed to meet his burden of 
proof to show he sustained a permanent injury to his left knee out of and in the course 
of his employment with Walmart on August 8, 2020. Lee relied upon the opinion of Dr. 
Bansal to causally relate his cyst and continued knee symptoms to the work incident on 
August 8, 2020. However, Dr. Bansal’s opinion is entitled to very little weight as it is not 
supported by the medical evidence. Lee has not proven entitlement to permanent 
disability benefits in this action.  
 
Lee seeks an award of temporary total or healing period benefits from February 
20, 2021, the date after his termination at Walmart, through July 10, 2023, the date 

LEE V. WALMART 
Page 11 
 
claimant’s IME doctor placed him at MMI. Lee did not prove entitlement to permanent 
disability benefits as a result of the August 8, 2020 date of injury, thus the benefits he 
seeks are temporary total benefits. See, e.g., Dunlap v. Action Warehouse, 824 N.W.2d 
545, 556 (Iowa Ct. App. 2012).  
 
When an injured worker has been unable to work during a period of recuperation 
from an injury that did not produce permanent disability, the worker is entitled to 
temporary total disability benefits during the time the worker is disabled by the injury. 
Those benefits are payable until the employee has returned to work or is medically 
capable of returning to work substantially similar to the work performed at the time of 
injury. Iowa Code § 85.33(1). According to Dr. Peterson, Lee was capable of returning 
to work that was substantially similar to his job at Walmart on February 12, 2021. Under 
the language of Iowa Code section 85.33, Lee is not entitled to temporary benefits after 
that date.   
 
Lee seeks payment of the medical expenses and mileage listed in Claimant’s 
Exhibit 9.  For all compensable injuries under Iowa Code chapter 85 or 85A, the 
employer must “furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, chiropractic, 
podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance, and hospital services and supplies 
therefor and shall allow reasonably necessary transportation expenses incurred for such 
services.” Iowa Code § 85.27(1). Above, I determined Lee did not suffer permanent 
impairment as a result of the work incident on August 8, 2020. He suffered a contusion 
and temporary knee pain but was placed at MMI by Dr. Aviles on October 7, 2020, and 
released to return to work without restrictions or further recommended treatment by Dr. 
Peterson on February 12, 2021. The expenses listed in Claimant’s Exhibit 9 are for 
treatment with Dr. Molis on August 10, 2020, October 26, 2020, and May 13, 2021, Dr. 
Miller on August 14, 2020, the second MRI on May 26, 2021, and physical therapy 
appointments at Unity Point in 2021, 2022, and 2023. (Cl Ex. 9, pp. 54-55, 57) As well 
as Dr. Bremner on June 28, 2022, Dr. Slayman on September 21, 2023, Dr. Bollier on 
November 10, 2023, and the third MRI on November 10, 2023. (Id. at 60-61, 63) This 
treatment was not authorized by defendants; it was arranged by Lee and his mother. By 
selecting his own care and abandoning the statutory scheme that permits the employer 
to select care, Lee has assumed a higher burden of proof to establish liability for that 
unauthorized medical care. See Bell Bros. Heating and Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 
N.W.2d 193 (Iowa 2020).   
 
If a claimant elects to pursue unauthorized medical care, he can still recover the 
expense of that care if he proves by a preponderance of the evidence that such care 
was reasonable and beneficial. Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d at 206. However, often there are 
multiple reasonable courses of treatment. Id. Therefore, to recover the cost of 
unauthorized care, a claimant must prove that the unauthorized care was beneficial in 
that it “provides a more favorable medical outcome than would likely have been 
achieved by the care authorized by the employer.” Id. In this case, defendants did not 
authorize any further care after Lee’s release by Dr. Peterson. Lee testified that the 
injection provided by Dr. Dunlay gave him temporary symptom relief but wore off. (Tr., 
p. 32). In her testimony, Lee’s mother stated that the injection did not help much with his

knee pain. (Id. at 71) The medical records also show that Lee’s pain was not affected or 
lessened by the treatment he received from Dr. Molis, Dr. Bremner, Dr. Miller, Dr. 
Slayman, and/or Dr. Bollier. He continues to report ongoing knee pain. In short, there is 
no evidence in the record that the care Lee sought out provided a more favorable 
outcome than would likely have been achieved by the care authorized by the employer. 
I conclude Lee failed to overcome his burden to establish that the care he sought out 
was reasonable, beneficial, and provided a more favorable medical outcome than would 
likely have been achieved through authorized care offered by the employer. 
Accordingly, I conclude Lee is precluded from recovering the medical expenses listed in 
Claimant’s Exhibit 9.  
 
Lee also asserts a claim for alternate medical care. Specifically, he seeks 
treatment with a pain management provider. (Claimant’s Post-Hearing Brief, p. 10) 
Before the agency can order alternate care, a claimant must establish a causal 
connection between the work injury, the current symptoms, and the need for treatment. 
See Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e). Defendants claim this causal connection is missing 
from the hearing record. They allege that based upon the opinions of Dr. Aviles, Dr. 
Bremner, Dr. Peterson, Dr. Bollier, and Dr. Dulaney, Lee only suffered a contusion and 
temporary pain from the August 8, 2020 work incident and does not require any 
additional medical care for the work injury. (Defendants’ Post-Hearing Brief, pp. 17-18) 
Above, I determined those opinions were consistent and supported by the evidence and 
adopted them. Given this, Lee has failed to carry his burden of proof that he is entitled 
to alternate medical care consisting of treatment with a pain management provider. 
  
 
Lee seeks reimbursement for his IME with Dr. Bansal. A claimant’s right to 
reimbursement for an IME exam is controlled by Iowa Code section 85.39. This section 
permits an employee to be reimbursed for a subsequent examination by a physician of 
the employee’s choice where an employer-retained physician has previously evaluated 
“permanent disability” and the employee believes that the initial evaluation is too low. 
Iowa Code section 85.39(2)(2019). However, this right to reimbursement is also 
dependent upon the claimant’s success at hearing. Iowa Code section 85.39(2), states, 
 
An employer is only liable to reimburse an employee for the cost of an 
examination conducted pursuant to this subsection if the injury for which the 
employee is being examined is determined to be compensable under this 
chapter or chapter 85A or 85B. An employer is not liable for the cost of such 
an examination if the injury for which the employee is being examined is 
determined not to be a compensable injury.  
 
Id.  According to the weight of the medical evidence in the record, defendants are not 
liable for any permanent disability to Lee’s left knee. Lee has not established the 
prerequisites for reimbursement of Dr. Bansal’s evaluation pursuant to Iowa Code 
section 85.39.
