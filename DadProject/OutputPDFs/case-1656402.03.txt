STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Brian Klein, has filed a petition for arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits against Whirlpool Corporation, a self-insured employer.  
In accordance with agency scheduling procedures and pursuant to the Order of 
the Commissioner the hearing was held on October 23, 2023, via Zoom. The case was 
considered fully submitted on November 17, 2023, upon the simultaneous filing of 
briefs.  
The record consists of Joint Exhibits 1-4, Claimant’s Exhibits 1-4, Defendant’s 
Exhibits A-B along with the testimony of the claimant. 
ISSUES 
1. 
Extent of permanent partial disability; 
 
2. 
Costs. 
STIPULATIONS 
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing. On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations. All of those 
stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration decision and 
no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised or discussed 
in this decision. The parties are now bound by their stipulations. 
The parties stipulate claimant sustained an injury arising out of and in the course 
of his employment on July 19, 2018. The parties further agree the claimant sustained a 
permanent disability, but disagree to the extent of that disability.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
Claimant was a 58-year-old person at the time of the hearing. His educational 
background includes graduation from high school in 1983. He was in the army and 
honorably discharged. 
He is currently employed with defendant employer and has been for the last 35 
years. The position he works now, and has worked for most of his career with defendant 
employer, is fab setup and operator. As a fab setup and operator his duties include 
loading copper coils onto a machine. The machine straightens the coiled tubing and 
then drops it into a cart below. Because of the way the machine was set up, claimant 
would often wedge his foot against the machine and pull the material through with his 
hands. 
Claimant testified that there were post-injury modifications to his machine 
including an automatic feeder which makes the position claimant works far less 
strenuous. Currently, after he loads the machine with tubing, he needs only to watch the 
material as it is fed into the machine. He earns $24.08 per hour with some overtime. He 
is right-handed. 
On July 19, 2018, claimant was feeding tubing through the machine and pulling it 
through when he felt pain in his right arm. He underwent conservative treatment 
including anti-inflammatory medication, activity modification and physical therapy which 
did not improve his symptoms. (Joint Exhibit 1, page 1) 
Claimant presented to Matthew Bollier, M.D., at the University of Iowa Hospitals 
& Clinics, on October 15, 2018, for evaluation of his right shoulder pain. (JE 1:1) The 
MRI showed a labral tear with associated paralabral cyst, rotator cuff tendinopathy 
without a full tear and mild AC joint degeneration. (JE 1:3) Dr. Bollier opined that 
claimant’s reported work incident was a significant factor in worsening or aggravating 
his shoulder condition and recommended claimant undergo surgical repair. (JE 1:3) 
Dr. Bollier performed arthroscopic shoulder SLAP repair on October 23, 2018. 
(JE 1:7-9) Claimant underwent a period of recovery which included around five months 
of physical therapy and injection treatment. (See e.g. JE 1:23; 1:25; 3:66)  
Around February 8, 2019, claimant was diagnosed with carpal tunnel syndrome 
on the right. (JE 1:19) Claimant wanted to undergo surgical repair but surgery was not 
scheduled until after he completed physical therapy for the right shoulder as claimant 
would not be able to exert full force with the right hand until a week after surgery. (JE 
1:21; 1:36) Right carpal tunnel surgery took place on September 11, 2019, with Thomas 
Fowler, M.D., at the UIHC. (JE 1:39)

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury actually occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 
employment.  Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. Dial 
Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309 (Iowa 1996).  The words “arising out of” refer to the cause or 
source of the injury.  The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 
circumstances of the injury.  2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124 (Iowa 1995).  
An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists between the 
injury and the employment.  Miedema, 551 N.W.2d 309.  The injury must be a rational 
consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely incidental to 
the employment.  Koehler Electric v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1 (Iowa 2000); Miedema, 551 
N.W.2d 309.  An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it happens within a 
period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may be when 
performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties or doing 
an activity incidental to them.  Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony.  The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question.  The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances.  The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); 
Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995).  Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994).  Unrebutted expert medical 
testimony cannot be summarily rejected.  Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 
N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 1994). 
The dispute at hand is whose expert testimony should be adopted. Claimant 
submits that it should be the opinion of Dr. Taylor who awarded an impairment rating for 
the distal clavicle excision and who spent more time with the claimant during the 
examination and performed multiple measurements. Defendant argues that the

appropriate opinion to rely upon is that of Dr. Bollier who specializes in the diagnosis 
and surgical treatment of shoulders. While Dr. Bollier is an expert in surgical repair of 
the shoulder, he is not an expert in functional impairment. He ceded that evaluation to 
an evaluator and then adopted the evaluator’s opinions. Thus, the fact that he 
performed claimant’s surgery and specializes in shoulder treatment does not confer 
greater weight for opinions on functional impairment. He and Dr. Broghammer took 
single range of motion measurements and Dr. Taylor took three. Neither Dr. Bollier nor 
Dr. Broghammer assigned an impairment rating for the distal clavicle excision. Claimant 
testified that Dr. Taylor took more time with him and that his measurements more 
accurately reflected his own sense of impairment. Based on the foregoing, Dr. Taylor’s 
opinion is given greater weight.  
Dr. Taylor assigned a 6 percent impairment to the right upper extremity based on 
the loss of range of motion and an additional 10 percent for the distal clavicle excision. 
Combined, the two impairments result in a 15 percent impairment.  
Defendant argues the issue regarding the impairment value of the distal clavicle 
excision should be governed by the case of Jay v. Archer Skid Loader Service, File No 
19003586.01 (App., Aug. 23, 2022).  
In Jay, the appropriate rating for a distal clavicle excision applies a 25 per 
modifier. See pp. 4 and 6-7. Claimant argues that Jay is not controlling because the use 
of the modifier was based on the opinion of Dr. Kuhnlein in another agency decision and 
that if there is a disagreement in medical determination pursuant to the Guides, the 
opinion of one side of the dispute cannot become agency precedent. Applying the 
modifier, argues claimant, is tantamount to using agency expertise which is prohibited 
according to Iowa Code § 85.34(2)(x). 
In reviewing the Jay decision, the District Court addressed this argument. Jay, at 
*6. The injured worker in Jay argued that the Commissioner acted outside the scope of 
his authority by issuing his own impairment rating applying the modifier instead of 
adopting one offered by an expert. The claimant in this case submits that Dr. Taylor’s 
uncontroverted opinion cannot be superseded or substituted by the agency’s 
“judgment.” See Claimant’s Brief, pp. 7-8. However, the District Court in Jay directs the 
agency to do exactly that. If an expert opinion is flawed, it is the agency’s duty to 
calculate the ratings in conformance with the AMA guidelines and to do otherwise would 
controvert the plain meaning of Iowa Code § 85.34(2)(x). Jay, at *6.  
While Dr. Taylor’s opinion is given more weight in this case, the appropriate 
impairment rating must include the 25 percent modifier. Thus, the impairment is 2.5 
percent plus 6 percent. In the combined chart, this is 9 percent as a rating must be a 
whole number per the Guides.  
Given that claimant has prevailed in this matter, costs of $110.82 are assessed 
against the defendant.
