STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Graciela de Maldonado, claimant, filed a petition seeking penalty benefits from 
Waterloo Community School District, employer, and United Wisconsin Insurance 
Company, insurance carrier, as defendants.  Hearing was held via Zoom 
videoconference on September 22, 2023.     
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the penalty benefits 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this penalty benefits 
decision and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised 
or discussed in this decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.  
Claimant, Graciela de Maldonado. was the only witness to testify live at trial.  The 
evidentiary record also includes Claimant’s Exhibits 1-5, and Defendants’ Exhibits A, B, 
and C, pp. 1-5, 9-41.  Defendants’ Exhibit C, pages 6-8, were deemed irrelevant and 
therefore were excluded pursuant to Iowa Code section 17A.14.  The evidentiary record 
closed at the conclusion of the penalty benefits hearing.  The parties submitted post-
hearing briefs on November 6, 2023.   
On January 5, 2024, claimant filed a motion to strike defendants’ post-hearing 
brief and for further sanctions.  On that same date defendants filed a resistance, at 
which time the case was fully submitted to the undersigned.  The motion and resistance 
are considered at this time.

Claimant seeks to strike defendants’ post-hearing brief and seeks further

not clear if IRCP 1.434 would apply.  However, even if the rule did apply, motions to

the controversy.  The claimant’s motion to strike is denied.  The briefing will be allowed 
to stand as filed.

a party fails to comply with a rule or order of this agency, then sanctions may be 
imposed. Claimant does not indicate which rule or order she believes defendants have

sanctions ordered in this decision are adequate.  I find that further sanctions are not

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 
Weekly compensation payments are due at the end of the compensation week.  
Robbennolt v. Snap-on Tools Corp., 555 N.W.2d 229, 235 (Iowa 1996). 
For purposes of determining whether an employer has delayed making 
payments, payments are “made”  either when (a) the check addressed to a claimant is 
mailed (Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 236; Kiesecker, 528 N.W.2d at 112), or when (b) 
the check is delivered personally to the claimant by the employer or its workers’ 
compensation insurer.  Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 235.   
As noted above, defendants were ordered to pay weekly benefits commencing 
on December 5, 2018 and extending through February 26, 2025.  Thus, there is no 
dispute that claimant was entitled to receive weekly benefits during said timeframe.  
Based on the above findings of fact, I conclude that claimant has carried her burden of 
proof to demonstrate by a preponderance of the evidence that defendants delayed 
payment of weekly PPD benefits totaling $5,784.60.   
We now turn to whether the defendants have shown a reasonable cause or 
excuse for the delay in the payment of weekly benefits.   
 
Iowa Code section 10A.315(4)2 provides: 
 
a. 
If a denial, a delay in payment, or a termination of benefits occurs without 
reasonable or probable cause or excuse known to the employer or 
insurance carrier at the time of the denial, delay in payment, or termination 
of benefits, the workers’ compensation commissioner shall award benefits

in addition to those benefits payable under this chapter, or chapter 85, 
85A, or 85B, up to fifty percent of the amount of benefits that were denied, 
delayed, or terminated without reasonable or probable cause or excuse. 
b. 
The workers’ compensation commissioner shall award benefits 
under this subsection if the commissioner finds both of the following 
facts: 
(1) The employee has demonstrated a denial, delay in payment, or

(2) The employer has failed to prove a reasonable or probable 
cause or excuse for the denial, delay in payment, or termination 
of benefits. 
In Christensen v. Snap-on Tools Corp., 554 N.W.2d 254 (Iowa 1996), and 
Robbennolt v. Snap-on Tools Corp., 555 N.W.2d 229 (Iowa 1996), the supreme court 
said: 
Based on the plain language of section [10A.315(4)], we hold an 
employee is entitled to penalty benefits if there has been a delay in 
payment unless the employer proves a reasonable cause or excuse.  A 
reasonable cause or excuse exists if either (1) the delay was necessary 
for the insurer to investigate the claim or (2) the employer had a 
reasonable basis to contest the employee’s entitlement to benefits.  A 
“reasonable basis” for denial of the claim exists if the claim is “fairly 
debatable.” 
Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 260. 
 
The supreme court has distilled several principles from Iowa Code section 10A.315: 
 
(1) If the employer has a reason for the delay and conveys that 
reason to the employee contemporaneously with the beginning of the 
delay, no penalty will be imposed if the reason is of such character that a 
reasonable fact-finder could conclude that it is a "reasonable or probable 
cause or excuse" under Iowa Code section [10A.315].  In that case, we 
will defer to the decision of the commissioner.  See Christensen, 554 
N.W.2d at 260 (substantial evidence found to support commissioner’s 
finding of legitimate reason for delay pending receipt of medical report); 
Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 236. 
 
(2) If no reason is given for the delay or if the “reason” is not one 
that a reasonable fact-finder could accept, we will hold that no such cause 
or excuse exists and remand to the commissioner for the sole purpose of 
assessing penalties under section [10A.315(4)].  See Christensen, 554 
N.W.2d at 261.

(3) Reasonable causes or excuses include (a) a delay for the 
employer to investigate the claim, Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 260; 
Kiesecker v. Webster City Custom Meats, Inc., 528 N.W.2d at 109, 111 
(Iowa 1995); or (b) the employer had a reasonable basis to contest the 
claimthe “fairly debatable” basis for delay.  See Christensen, 554 
N.W.2d at 260 (holding two-month delay to obtain employer’s own medical 
report reasonable under the circumstances).  
 
(4) For the purpose of applying section [10A.315], the benefits that 
are underpaid as well as late-paid benefits are subject to penalties, unless 
the employer establishes reasonable and probable cause or excuse.  
Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 237 (underpayment resulting from application 
of wrong wage base; in absence of excuse, commissioner required to 
apply penalty). 
   If we were to construe [section 10A.315] to permit the 
avoidance of penalty if any amount of compensation benefits 
are paid, the purpose of the penalty statute would be 
frustrated.  For these reasons, we conclude section 
[10A.315] is applicable when payment of compensation is 
not timely . . . or when the full amount of compensation is not 
paid. 
Id. 
Meyers v. Holiday Express Corp., 557 N.W.2d 502, 504-505 (Iowa 1996).   
Penalty is not imposed for delayed interest payments.  Davidson v. Bruce, 594 
N.W.2d 833, 840 (Iowa App. 1999).  Schadendorf v. Snap-On Tools Corp., 757 N.W.2d 
330, 338 (Iowa 2008).   
In the present case defendants had previously been ordered to pay the weekly 
benefits.  Defendants have not demonstrated that the delay in payment of weekly 
benefits was necessary for the insurer to investigate the claim, nor did the employer 
have a reasonable basis to contest the employee’s entitlement to benefits.  
Throughout discovery, claimant attempted to gain information on why her weekly 
payments were delayed.  As set forth in the undersigned’s prior ruling regarding 
discovery, defendants failed to fully respond to claimant’s discovery requests and failed 
to comply with the spirit of the discovery process in this case.  (Ruling on Claimant’s 
Motion to Find Defendants in Contempt of Court and for Sanctions, August 9, 2023)  In 
the ruling defendants were warned that failure to produce evidence within a party’s 
control can, and often does, lead to a legal, factual, and evidentiary presumption that 
the evidence withheld would be favorable to the other party.  See Phillips v. Covenant 
Clinic, 625 N.W.2d 714, 718 (Iowa 2001).

In this case defendants have offered some theories on what may have happened 
to stop the recurring payments; however, it appears defendants do not know or will not 
say why the payments were stopped.  Defendants’ allegations that the delayed 
payments were the fault of the claimant’s law firm and/or the USPS further supports the 
conclusion that defendants have not offered a reasonable explanation for why the 
delays occurred.  I conclude defendants did not offer a probable cause or excuse for the 
delays of claimant’s weekly benefits that they were ordered to pay by this agency.  I 
conclude that defendants unreasonably delayed claimant’s PPD benefits as set forth 
above.  
The purpose of Iowa Code section 10A.315 is both punishment for unreasonable 
conduct but also deterrence for future cases.  Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 237.  In this 
regard, the Commission is given discretion to determine the amount of the penalty 
imposed with a maximum penalty of 50 percent of the amount of the delayed, or denied, 
benefits.  Christensen v. Snap-On Tools Corp., 554 N.W.2d 254, 261 (Iowa 1996).   
In exercising its discretion, the agency must consider factors such as the length 
of the delays, the number of delays, the information available to the employer regarding 
the employee’s injury and wages, and the employer’s past record of penalties.  Meyers 
v. Holiday Express Corp., 557 N.W.2d 502, 505 (Iowa 1996).  Neither party put 
evidence on, or offered argument about, the employer’s past record of penalties. 
Having considered the relevant factors and the purposes of the penalty statute, I 
conclude that a section 10A.315 penalty in the amount of two thousand eight hundred 
ninety-two and 30/100 dollars ($2,892.30) is appropriate.  This amount represents one-
half of the amount that the defendants unreasonably delayed to the claimant. This 
amount is appropriate to punish the employer for delays in payment of benefits under 
these facts and should serve as a deterrent against future conduct.

On September 6, 2023, claimant’s counsel filed an affidavit in support of the 
request for attorney fees as a sanction.  The affidavit submitted 14.70 total hours at a 
rate of $250.00 per hour for a total of $3,675.00 in attorney fees.  The affidavit included 
time for issues not related to defendants’ failure to comply with the undersigned’s ruling 
on claimant’s motion to compel; for example, time spent filing the petition, propounding 
the initial interrogatories and requests for production of documents, reviewing witness 
and exhibits lists.  I find that the requested attorney fees are broader than what was 
incurred due to the defendants’ failure to comply with my ruling.  However, as set forth 
in my August 9, 2023 ruling, defendants’ deficiencies in their discovery responses and 
their failure to comply with the discovery process do warrant a sanction.   
IAC 876 4.36 provides: 
If any party to a contested case or an attorney representing such party 
shall fail to comply with these rules or any order of a deputy workers’ 
compensation commissioner or the workers’ compensation commissioner, 
the deputy workers’ compensation commissioner or workers’ 
compensation commissioner may impose sanctions which may include 
dismissing the action without prejudice, excluding or limiting evidence, 
assessing costs or expenses, and closing the record in whole or in part to 
further activity by the party. 
I find that an award of attorney fees is an appropriate sanction for defendants’ 
failure to comply with the undersigned’s order of August 9, 2023.  I further find attorney 
fees in the amount of nine hundred and no/100 dollars ($900.00) is a reasonable and 
appropriate sanction in this case.
