is Redwood Fire and Casualty Insurance Company.  Hearing was held via Zoom 
videoconference. The case originally proceeded to hearing on August 29, 2023.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
The undersigned, having considered all the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
 
Claimant, Armando Ojeda, was 60 years old at the time of the hearing.  He 
began working for the defendant-employer, Sivyer Steel Castings, LLC, in May 2015.  
At the time of the stipulated November 27, 2019 work injury Mr. Ojeda was working in 
the checkout position.  His job duties included overseeing crane operations in the 
movement of boxes.  He was responsible for grabbing boxes and using a crane and 
chains to move the box to checkout or open the box.  A crane, which was attached to 
the ceiling, moved forwards or backwards along rails.  Mr. Ojeda worked on the floor, 
while another employee worked above operating the crane.  Mr. Ojeda was to ensure 
that the box was correctly secured to the chain with a hook on the crane and help the 
crane operator connect the boxes to the machinery.  (Claimant’s Exhibit 2, page 29)    
 
On November 27, 2019, Mr. Ojeda was performing his normal work duties when 
he was struck on the left side of his head, near his ear, with a chain after it was raised 
too fast.  He had a laceration behind his left ear.  (Joint Exhibit 1, pages 4-7; Cl. Ex. 8, 
p. 66)

On August 14, 2023, Dr. Bansal issued a report in response to the IME report 
from Dr. Garrels.  Dr. Bansal stated that Dr. Garrels’ IME report did not change the 
opinions he provided in his original IME report dated June 22, 2023.  Dr. Bansal 
addressed how a total shoulder replacement procedure affects structures beyond the 
shoulder, similar to that of a reverse total shoulder replacement.  He stated: 
 
Yes. The shoulder is fundamentally the glenohumeral joint, where the 
humerus or the arm bone, interfaces with the scapula which is commonly 
known as the shoulder blade.  During a traditional total shoulder 
replacement, the humeral head (essentially the “ball” of the joint) gets 
replaced, and the glenoid (the “socket” on the scapula) is resurfaced.  This 
means that the procedure is not just limited to the humerus, but it also 
substantially engages the scapula. 
Furthermore, the scapular is deeply tethered to a collection of muscle 
groups, often termed the periscapular musculature.  These muscles 
include the serratus anterior, levator scapulae, rhomboids, and particularly 
the trapezius, and are pivotal for shoulder movements and overall stability.  
Notably, muscles like the trapezius have their origins and terminations 
spanning areas far removed from the immediate vicinity of the shoulder.  
This indicates that any procedure impinging on the scapula has a 
secondary effect on these associated muscles. 
(Cl. Ex. 3, p. 37) 
 
Dr. Bansal went on to discuss the parallels involved in the procedures of 
traditional and reverse total shoulder arthroplasty.  He stated both the traditional and 
reverse total shoulder arthroplasty “interventions stretch beyond the humerus to 
encompass the scapula and the muscles that envelop it.”  (Cl. Ex. 3, p. 37) Dr. Bansal 
noted that a traditional total shoulder replacement “extends beyond the boundaries of 
the immediate glenohumeral joint, impacting an array of structures in and around the 
shoulder.”  (Id.)   
 
Dr. Bansal was asked, in the context of both a traditional and reverse total 
shoulder replacement, if there were “structures involved in those procedures, though 
while not directly impacting the primary function of the shoulder, are still affected by the 
surgeries?”  (Cl. Ex. 3, p. 37) He replied: 
Yes.  The scapula is a key bone for the shoulder joint’s operation.  It 
provides a stable base for shoulder movement, and is integrated into a 
system that supports the upper back and connects the arm to the chest.  
Some muscles attached to the scapula, such as the rhomboids and 
trapezius, are not solely focused on moving the shoulder.  For example, 
the trapezius spans from the base of the skull to the mid-back.  It primarily 
manages neck movement and scapular positioning, with shoulder 
movement being a secondary function.  
(Cl. Ex. 3, p. 37)

Dr. Bansal stated that there are structures that do not control the shoulder joint’s 
main movement that are affected by traditional and reverse total shoulder replacements.  
He also noted that by changing a shoulder’s biomechanics, the surgeries could 
indirectly change the stress or demands on nearby structures.  (Cl. Ex. 3, pp. 37-38)  He 
summarized by stating, “while the primary aim of these surgeries is to improve shoulder 
function, their impact is broader and affects structures with roles beyond just the 
shoulder’s basic movements.”  (Cl. Ex. 3, p. 38)  Notably, Dr. Bansal’s opinions 
regarding traditional and reverse total shoulder arthroplasties were not regarding Mr. 
Ojeda.  Rather, his opinions were rendered regarding these procedures in general and 
what affect these procedures could have.  Dr. Bansal did not indicate that the 
arthroplasty performed on Mr. Ojeda affected his neck, upper back, torso, or otherwise.     
  
In light of Dr. Bansal’s August 2023 report, Dr. Garrels responded to a series of 
questions posed to him by defense counsel about the total shoulder arthroplasty that 
Mr. Ojeda underwent.  (Def. Ex. F)  With regard to Mr. Ojeda’s surgery, Dr. Garrels 
stated that the “purpose of the total shoulder arthroplasty was to improve/maintain 
function associated with the glenohumeral joint due to expected progression of

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony.  The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question.  The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances.  The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); 
Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995).  Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994).  Unrebutted expert medical 
testimony cannot be summarily rejected.  Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 
N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 1994).   
The well-established law is clear, however, that an expert's opinion is not 
necessarily binding when it is based on an incomplete or inaccurate history. Dunlavey v. 
Economy Fire & Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 1995) (citing Bodish v. Fischer. 
Inc., 133 N.W.2d 867, 870 (1965)). 
Based on the above findings of fact, I conclude that claimant failed to carry his 
burden of proof to demonstrate by a preponderance of the evidence that he sustained 
any permanent partial disability as the result of the November 27, 2019, injury.  I found 
the opinion of Dr. Frederick to carry greater weight than that of Dr. Bansal.  Thus, I 
conclude claimant has failed to demonstrate entitlement to any permanent partial 
disability benefits as the result of the November 27, 2019, injury.

We now turn to the nature and extent of permanent disability claimant sustained 
as the result of the May 6, 2020, work injury.  Claimant contends the injury extends 
beyond the shoulder and into the body as a whole and thus should be compensated 
under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v).  Defendants contend the injury is limited to the 
shoulder and should be compensated based under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(n). 
In 2017 the Iowa Legislature amended Iowa Code section 85.34.  Before the 
2017 changes, shoulder injuries were considered proximal to the arm and compensated 
as a body as a whole injury, under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(u).  Prior to the 2017 
changes, a shoulder injury was compensated as an unscheduled injury, and based on 
industrial disability.  See Alm v. Morris Barick Cattle Co., 240 Iowa 1174, 38 N.W.2d 161 
(1949).  Through the 2017 legislative changes, the legislature added the shoulder to the 
list of scheduled members.  Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(n) states: “[f]or the loss of a 
shoulder, weekly compensation during four hundred weeks.”  Iowa Code section 
85.34(2)(n).  This amendment went into effect on July 1, 2018.  The legislature did not 
define the term “shoulder.” 
The Iowa Supreme Court in Chavez v. MS Tech. LLC, 972 N.W.2d 662 (Iowa 
Apr. 1, 2022), and Deng v. Farmland Foods, Inc., 972 N.W.2d 727 (Iowa Apr. 1, 2022), 
affirmed that the shoulder was construed in a functional sense to include the 
glenohumeral joint as well as all of the muscles, tendons, and ligaments that were 
essential for the shoulder to function.  The Court stated, “[d]efining ‘shoulder’ in the 
functional sense under section 85.34(2)(n) best achieves the statues’ purpose.”  
Chavez, at 669.  In Chavez the Court also noted that throughout her medical records 
the physicians who treated or assessed the injury considered it to be a shoulder injury.  
Chavez, at 670.   
In the present case, claimant failed to demonstrate by a preponderance of the 
evidence that his May 6, 2020, injury extends beyond the right shoulder.  I conclude the 
evidence demonstrates that claimant’s injury is contained to the shoulder in the 
functional sense.  See Chavez.   
The Iowa Supreme Court stated,  
Chavez cites prior Iowa caselaw that looked to “the proximal point of the 
joint to classify an injury under the workers’ compensation statues” to treat 
“shoulder” injuries as unscheduled whole body injuries, but that caselaw 
developed before the legislature amended the statutory scheme to add 
shoulder injuries to the list of scheduled member injuries under section 
85.34(2).   
Id. at 670 (citations omitted). 
Thus, claimant’s arguments pertaining to symptoms extending into areas 
proximal to the glenohumeral joint are not persuasive.

Under Iowa law, if a denial, a delay in payment, or a termination of benefits

the workers' compensation commissioner shall award benefits in addition to those 
benefits payable under this chapter, or chapter 85, 85A, or 85B, up to fifty percent of the 
amount of benefits that were denied, delayed, or terminated without reasonable or 
probable cause or excuse.  See Iowa Code section 10A.315. 
Based on the above findings of fact, I conclude that claimant failed to carry his

delayed weekly payments to him.  Thus, I conclude penalty benefits are not appropriate
