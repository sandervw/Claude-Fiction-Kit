STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Jared Jackson, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from Gomaco Corporation (Gomaco), employer, and Old 
Republic Insurance Company, insurer, both as defendants.  This matter was heard on 
February 21, 2025, with a final submission date of April 16, 2025.   
The record in this case consists of Joint Exhibits 1-23, Claimant’s Exhibits 1-6, 
Defendants’ Exhibits A-F, and the testimony of claimant.

hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
The first issue to be determined is the extent of claimant’s entitlement to 
temporary benefits. 

JACKSON V. GOMACO CORPORATION

The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e). 
Healing period compensation describes temporary workers’ compensation 
weekly benefits that precede an allowance of permanent partial disability benefits.  
Ellingson v. Fleetguard, Inc., 599 N.W.2d 440, 446 (Iowa 1999).  Healing period benefits 
are payable to an injured worker who has suffered permanent partial disability until the 
first to occur of three events.  Iowa Code § 85.34(1) (2015).  These are:  (1) the worker 
has returned to work; (2) the worker medically is capable of returning to substantially 
similar employment; or (3) the worker has achieved maximum medical recovery.  Id.  
Maximum medical recovery is achieved when healing is complete, and the extent of 
permanent disability can be determined.  Armstrong Tire & Rubber Co. v. Kubli, 312 
N.W.2d 60, 65 (Iowa Ct. App. 1981).  Neither maintenance medical care nor an 
employee's continuing to have pain or other symptoms necessarily prolongs the healing 
period.  Id. 
When an injured worker has been unable to work during a period of recuperation 
from an injury that did not produce permanent disability, the worker is entitled to 
temporary total disability benefits during the time the worker is disabled by the injury.  
Iowa Code § 85.33(1) (2015).  Those benefits are payable until the employee has 
returned to work or is medically capable of returning to work substantially similar to the 
work performed at the time of injury.  Id.  
 
Claimant is seeking temporary disability benefits for the periods of January 26, 
2021, through February 17, 2021, and from December 5, 2021, through November 7, 
2022.  (Claimant’s Post-Hearing Brief, pages 20-22)  Claimant initially sought temporary 
benefits, for this second period, commencing on November 16, 2021.  Claimant 
indicated in his post-hearing brief that wage records and the payment of temporary 
benefits indicated that claimant stopped working on December 4, 2021.  (Claimant’s 
Post-Hearing Brief, p. 21) 
 
On January 26, 2021, claimant was evaluated by Dr. Gupta.  At that time, Dr. 
Gupta returned claimant to work.  (JE 4, p. 122)  On February 11, 2021, records 
indicated claimant returned to work with no restrictions.  On February 18, 2021, Dr. 
Martin indicated that claimant should be kept off work until his histoplasmosis was under 
control.  (JE 9, p. 192)  Dr. Gupta returned claimant to work on January 27, 2021.  Dr. 
Martin did not take claimant off work until February 18, 2021.  Based on this record, 
claimant has failed to carry his burden of proof he is due temporary benefits from 
January 26, 2021, to February 17, 2021.

The next issue to be determined is whether claimant’s injury resulted in a 
permanent disability. 
 
Regarding claimant’s histoplasmosis, a number of experts have opined regarding 
claimant’s pulmonary condition, and whether it is a temporary or permanent condition. 
 
On July 20, 2021, claimant was evaluated by Dr. Lamptey, an infectious disease 
specialist.  Dr. Lamptey opined that when claimant had finished his medications, his 
histoplasmosis would be resolved.  (JE 8, p. 158) 
 
In a December 27, 2021 report, Dr. Martin opined that claimant did not require 
continued treatment for his histoplasmosis.  (JE 9, p. 197) 
 
On September 17, 2024, claimant was evaluated by Dr. Low, a pulmonologist.  
She found that claimant had no pulmonary reason not to work.  She also found that 
claimant’s lung parenchyma did not show any changes.  (JE 22, pp. 452-454) 
 
Dr. Horne, an infectious disease specialist, evaluated claimant on August 27, 
2024.  He found that there was no clinical evidence of an active histoplasmosis 
infection.  (JE 22, p 440) 
 
Dr. Westpheling evaluated claimant once for an IME.  He found no objective 
evidence to rate claimant’s histoplasmosis.  (Ex. A, p. 7) 
 
Dr. Kuhnlein evaluated claimant once for an IME.  Dr. Kuhnlein is the only expert 
to find that claimant has a permanent impairment for his histoplasmosis. 
 
Five experts have found that claimant has no permanent impairment regarding 
his histoplasmosis, and that his condition has resolved.  Of those five experts, two are 
specialists in infectious disease and one is a pulmonologist.  Given the overwhelming 
evidence indicating claimant has no permanent impairment for his histoplasmosis, 
claimant has failed to carry his burden of proof that his histoplasmosis resulted in a 
permanent impairment.

Dr. Westpheling performed a record review.  He opined that claimant’s

There are a number of problems with Dr. Kuhnlein’s opinions regarding 
permanent impairment for the arthritic condition as a sequela of the histoplasmosis.  
First, in his July 2022 report, Dr. Kuhnlein noted that when asked about causation of 
claimant’s rheumatoid arthritis, he indicated, “I would defer to the rheumatologists 
whether this represented an exacerbation or aggravation of the underlying rheumatoid 
arthritis condition.”  (Ex. 1, p. 8)  It is unclear why Dr. Kuhnlein believes he is qualified, 
in 2025, to opine regarding permanent impairment of claimant’s arthritis, when in 2022 
he suggested he lacked qualifications to opine whether claimant had an aggravation of 
his arthritic condition.   
 
Second, Dr. Westpheling based his opinion, in part, on the lack of radiographic 
findings or imaging studies that claimant’s arthritic condition had worsened.  (Ex. A, pp. 
5-7)  Dr. Kuhnlein does not respond to this rebuttal.  In short, how can Dr. Kuhnlein 
opine that claimant aggravated his rheumatoid arthritis without the support of imaging or 
diagnostic studies indicating a worsening condition? 
 
Third, in his January of 2025 report, Dr. Kuhnlein indicated the combined values 
for impairments resulted in a 56 percent permanent impairment to the body as a whole.  
Dr. Kuhnlein went on to state, “Based on my experience, I believe this overestimates 
the impairment.  I would reduce this to a 40% whole person impairment for both 
conditions.”  (Ex. 1, p. 34) 
 
I appreciate Dr. Kuhnlein’s candor.  His honesty regarding evaluating claimants 
historically before this agency is a characteristic that generally makes his evaluations 
convincing.  However, I am unsure of any guidance in the Guides that indicates the 
evaluator can decrease or increase a permanent impairment rating based on the 
evaluator’s experience. 
 
Finally, as noted, the records indicate, and claimant agreed, that at least in 2024

The next issue to be determined is whether claimant is entitled to reimbursement 
for an IME. 
Iowa Code section 85.39 permits an employee to be reimbursed for subsequent 
examination by a physician of the employee's choice where an employer-retained 
physician has previously evaluated “permanent disability” and the employee believes 
that the initial evaluation is too low.   Iowa Code § 85.39(2) (2023).  The section also 
permits reimbursement for reasonably necessary transportation expenses incurred and 
for any wage loss occasioned by the employee attending the subsequent examination.  
Iowa Code § 85.39 (1), (2).   
The Iowa Supreme Court has provided a literal interpretation of the plain 
language of Iowa Code section 85.39, stating that section only allows the employee to 
obtain an independent medical evaluation at the employer’s expense if an evaluation of 
permanent disability has already been made by an employer retained physician.  Des 
Moines Area Reg’l Transit Auth. v. Young, 867 N.W.2d 839, 847 (Iowa 2015). Under 
the Young decision, an employee can only obtain an IME at the employer's expense if 
an evaluation of permanent disability has been made by an employer-retained 
physician. Id. 
An employer is only liable to reimburse an employee for the cost of an 
examination conducted pursuant to this subsection if the injury for which the employee 
is being examined is determined to be compensable.  Iowa Code § 85.39(2) (2017).  
“An employer is not liable for the cost of such examination if the injury for which the 
employee is being examined is determined not to have a compensable injury. A 
determination of the reasonableness of a fee for an examination made pursuant to this 
subsection, shall be based on the typical fee charged by a medical provider to perform 
an impairment rating in the local area where the examination is conducted.”  Iowa Code 
§ 85.39(2); Tweeten v. Tweeten, 999 N.W.2d 270, 282 (Iowa 2023).   
Iowa Code section 85.39(2), as amended, allows an employee with a 
compensable injury to be reimbursed the reasonable cost of an examination. See Iowa 
Code § 85.39(2)(2017); Mid American Construction LLC v. Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d 838, 849 
(Iowa 2024). Under the statute, an examination may include reviewing medical records, 
conducting a physical exam, opining on causation, assessing permanent impairment, 
assigning restrictions, and recommending further care. Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d at 851.  
The burden is on the claimant to show the reasonableness of his or her chosen 
physician’s IME fee. Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d at 852; see also Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e) 
(stating “Ordinarily, the burden of proof on an issue is upon the party who would suffer

156 (Iowa 2004)(stating “the plaintiff has the burden to prove the reasonable value of

fact to be decided by the commissioner. See Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143,

Dr. Martin, the employer-retained expert, gave his opinions of permanent
