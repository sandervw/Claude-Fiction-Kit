Claimant appeals from a September 8, 2025 arbitration decision and October 6, 
2025 Ruling on Claimant’s Motion for Rehearing.  Defendants respond to the appeal 
and urge it be fully affirmed.  The presiding deputy commissioner held an arbitration 
hearing in this case on June 24, 2025, and the case was fully submitted before the 
deputy on July 15, 2025. 
In the arbitration decision, the deputy commissioner found that claimant failed to 
prove he sustained an alleged December 17, 2023 work injury.  That decision is not the 
subject of this appeal.  However, the deputy commissioner also concluded that claimant 
sustained a cumulative work injury that manifested by at least January 24, 2024.  The 
deputy commissioner found that claimant did not give notice of the alleged injury until 
filing his petition on May 7, 2024.  The deputy concluded that the notice was beyond the 
90-day statutory requirement and barred recovery under the notice defense in Iowa 
Code section 85.23. 
On appeal, claimant contends the deputy erred in finding that claimant did not 
give notice until filing his petition.  Claimant contends that he reported a fall on ice on 
January 19, 2024 and that the employer received timely notice or had actual knowledge 
of claimant’s work injury.  Claimant further contends that the discovery rule applies and 
that his 90-day window for reporting the injury did not commence until he received a 
written report from a physician, documenting that his injury was work related.  
Specifically, claimant urges that “it is an overstatement to infer that the Tweeten opinion 
nullifies the application of the discovery rule here.”  (Claimant’s Appeal Brief, page 5)  
Instead, claimant urges a separate standard such that a “claimant cannot provide notice 
of an injury until it has both manifested and been discovered by the claimant.”

(Claimant’s Appeal Brief, p. 7) (emphasis in original)  Defendants respond to the appeal 
and urge that the arbitration decision and ruling on rehearing be affirmed in their

Pursuant to Iowa Code section 10A.321 and section 17A.15(3), I performed a de 
novo review of the evidentiary record and the detailed arguments of the parties.  Those 
portions of the proposed arbitration decision pertaining to issues not raised on appeal 
are adopted as part of this appeal decision.  With respect to the issues properly raised 
on appeal, I affirm the arbitration decision with the following analysis: 
I concur with the deputy commissioner’s findings of fact relative to the issue of 
notice.  Specifically, I concur that the claimant’s cumulative work injury manifested on or 
before January 24, 2024.  If forced to select a specific manifestation date, I conclude 
that claimant’s injury manifested on January 23, 2024.  On that date, claimant reported 
to a treating medical provider that his heavy work for this employer may have caused 
his symptoms.  (Joint Exhibit 2, p. 13)   
In 2017, the Iowa legislature amended Iowa Code section 85.23.  That statute 
now provides: 
Unless the employer or the employer’s representative shall have actual 
knowledge of the occurrence of an injury received within ninety days from 
the date of the occurrence of the injury, or unless the employee or 
someone on the employee’s behalf or a dependent or someone on the 
dependent’s behalf shall give notice thereof to the employer within ninety 
days from the date of the occurrence of the injury, no compensation shall 
be allowed.  For the purposes of this section, “date of the occurrence of 
the injury” means the date that the employee knew or should have known 
that the injury was work-related. 
I concur with the deputy commissioner’s finding that “The defendants did not 
receive formal notice of a work-related injury until May 2024 when claimant filed his 
petition.”  I also concur with the deputy commissioner that “A reasonable manager in 
defendants’ position is not required to assume that every extended medical absence is 
due to a work-related injury, particularly when the employee denied any injuries just 
days before.”  Therefore, I conclude the employer did not have actual knowledge of the 
injury and that defendants proved they did not receive notice of this injury until May 
2024. 
By January 23, 2024, claimant reported to a medical provider that his injury may 
be related to his heavy work activities.  By that date, claimant knew or should have 
known that the injury was work-related.  Iowa Code section 85.23.  Accordingly, 
claimant’s injury manifested on January 23, 2024.  Claimant was required by the clear 
and explicit language of Iowa Code section 85.23 to give notice of that injury within 90 
days of its manifestation.  In fact, claimant did not give notice until the filing of a petition 
in May 2024, after the 90-day notice period.  Therefore, I conclude that defendants

carried their burden of proof on the notice defense and that claimant is barred from 
recovery under the notice defense.  Iowa Code section 85.23. 
Claimant urges application of the discovery rule to toll his deadline for giving 
notice of his injury.  As noted above, claimant urges “it is an overstatement to infer that 
the Tweeten opinion nullifies the application of the discovery rule here.”  (Claimant’s 
Appeal Brief, page 5)  Instead, he urges a separate standard such that a “claimant 
cannot provide notice of an injury until it has both manifested and been discovered by 
the claimant.”  Claimant’s assertion is not supported by the clear language of Iowa Code 
section 85.23.   
Iowa Code section 85.23 clearly requires notice within 90 days of the date of 
manifestation.  Claimant urges the insertion of language or a requirement (that the injury 
has “been discovered by the claimant”) not included in the specific legislative enactment 
of Iowa Code section 85.23.  This agency is “bound to apply the statutes as enacted by 
the general assembly.”  Tweeten v. Tweeten, 999 N.W.2d 270, 282 (Iowa 2023).  
Claimant’s construction “would add words to the statute that the general assembly did 
not—something we cannot do.”  Id. at 281. 
The Iowa Supreme Court interpreted the language of the statute of limitations, 
Iowa Code section 85.26.  That statute includes the same language to define the “date 
of the occurrence of the injury” as Iowa Code section 85.23.  In Tweeten, the Iowa 
Supreme Court determined that the discovery rule does not apply under the specific 
language defining the date of the occurrence of the injury contained in Iowa Code 
section 85.26. 
Common sense, principles of statutory construction, and consistency all suggest 
that the same phrase and definition in Iowa Code section 85.23 would be interpreted 
consistently with the manner in which it was interpreted and applied in Tweeten.  In fact, 
this issue has come before an appellate court already.  In Tyler v. Tyson Fresh Meats, 
Inc., 4 N.W.3d 721 (Iowa Ct. App. 2024) (table of unpublished decisions) (2024 WL 
470352), the Iowa Court of Appeals considered this issue and determined that the 
discovery rule does not apply under Iowa Code section 85.23 for the exact rationale 
explained by the Iowa Supreme Court in Tweeten. 
I conclude that the discovery rule does not apply.  I conclude that the employer 
did not have actual knowledge of claimant’s cumulative injury, that claimant failed to 
give timely notice of his injury to the employer, and that defendants carried their burden 
of proof pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.23.  Therefore, I also conclude that this claim 
is barred pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.23. 
Therefore, I affirm the arbitration decision and ruling on motion for rehearing in 
their entirety.
