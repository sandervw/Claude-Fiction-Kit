STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A.  The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Linda Denton.  
Claimant filed her original notice and petition for alternate medical care on August 28, 
2020, seeking authorization of a left knee surgery recommended by her treating 
orthopaedic surgeon, Robert B. Bartelt, M.D., on July 31, 2020.  Defendants filed a 
timely answer.  Defendants admit liability for the alleged work-related knee injury, which 
occurred on June 25, 2020. 
 
The alternate medical care claim came on for a telephonic hearing on September 
9, 2020.  Claimant appeared through her attorney, Joseph Powell.  Defendants 
appeared through their attorney, Tyler Block. 
 
The alternate medical care proceedings were digitally recorded.  That recording 
constitutes the official record of this proceeding.  Pursuant to the Commissioner’s 
February 16, 2015 Order, the undersigned has been delegated authority to issue a final 
agency decision in this alternate medical care proceeding.  Therefore, this ruling is 
designated final agency action and any appeal of the decision would be to the Iowa 
District Court pursuant to Iowa Code section 17A. 
 
Claimant offered exhibits 1-2, which include a total of 3 pages.  Defendants 
offered exhibits A-B, which consisted of a total of four pages.  All exhibits were received 
into the evidentiary record without objection.   
 
No other witnesses were called to testify and the evidentiary record closed at the 
conclusion of the alternate medical care hearing.  Counsel for the parties offered 
succinct and cogent argument on the merits of this alternate medical care proceeding.

ISSUE 
 
The issue presented for resolution is whether the claimant is entitled to an order 
of alternate care and specifically authorization of medical care through Dr. Bartelt, 
including but not limited to the surgery Dr. Bartelt recommended after his July 31, 2020 
evaluation of claimant’s left knee. 
 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
The undersigned having considered all the evidence in the record finds: 
 
Claimant, Linda Denton, sustained a work-related injury to her left knee on June 
25, 2020, when she fell while working for the employer, Pillar of Cedar Valley.  (Original 
Notice and Petition Concerning Application for Alternate Care; Answer; Defendants’ 
Exhibit A)  Defendants conceded at hearing that they authorized medical care for 
claimant through Allen Occupational Health after the injury.  According to argument 
offered by counsel, Allen Occupational Health recommended an MRI of her left knee 
and subsequently made a referral to an orthopaedic surgeon, Dr. Bartelt.   
 
Defendants conceded at hearing that they authorized an evaluation to be 
performed by Dr. Bartelt.  That evaluation occurred on July 31, 2020.  (Defendants’ Ex. 
A)  Dr. Bartelt reviewed claimant’s left knee MRI, performed an evaluation, and noted, 
“there is some irregularity of the lateral meniscus but it is not 100% definitive for tear.”  
(Defendants’ Ex. A, page 2)  According to his evaluation note, Dr. Bartelt discussed 
various treatment options with claimant “including a trial of therapy versus diagnostic 
arthroscopy with possible partial lateral meniscectomy.”  (Defendants’ Ex. A, p. 2)  
Claimant expressed her desire to proceed with surgical intervention and Dr. Bartelt 
indicated that his office would make arrangements to proceed with the recommended 
surgery.  (Defendants’ Ex. A, p. 2)  Again, through this alternate medical care 
proceeding, claimant asserts her desire to proceed with the surgical recommendations 
of Dr. Bartelt. 
 
Defendants refused to authorize the surgical procedure recommended by Dr. 
Bartelt.  Instead, on August 26, 2020, defendants gave notice of their intention to 
redirect medical care to a different orthopaedic surgeon, Matthew Bollier, M.D., at the 
University of Iowa Hospitals and Clinics.  Defendants obtained and relayed the 
appointment they secured for claimant to be evaluated by Dr. Bollier two days later on 
August 28, 2020.  (Defendants’ Ex. B) 
 
Claimant promptly responded, through counsel, and objected to the redirection of 
her medical care.  Claimant gave notice that she did not want a new orthopaedic 
specialist and gave notice of her dissatisfaction for purposes of filing an alternate 
medical care petition.  (Claimant’s Ex. A; Defendants’ Ex. B) 
 
A colloquy ensued between counsel with claimant asserting her preference to 
proceed with surgery through Dr. Bartelt and her argument that defendants cannot 
interfere with the recommendations of the authorized surgeon.  Defense counsel 
responded, noting that further care was not authorized through Dr. Bartelt and asserting

that defendants had the right to direct claimant’s medical care, including a transfer of 
care to Dr. Bollier. 
 
Defendants have rescheduled claimant’s evaluation with Dr. Bollier to occur on 
September 11, 2020.  (Claimant’s Ex. 2)  Claimant resists the transfer of care and seeks 
an order authorizing the recommended surgery through Dr. Bartelt. 
 
I find that Dr. Bartelt is an orthopaedic surgeon and that he was an authorized 
medical provider when he evaluated claimant and provided his surgical 
recommendation on July 31, 2020.  I find that Dr. Bollier is also an orthopaedic surgeon, 
qualified to evaluate and treat claimant’s left knee injury. 
 
 
Defendants provide no evidence to support their decision to transfer care from 
Dr. Bartelt to Dr. Bollier.  Defense counsel urged some potential factual bases for the 
decision, but defendants did not prove a specific reason or basis for rejecting the 
surgery recommended by Dr. Bartelt.  The undersigned notes the timing of the potential 
transfer of care as occurring immediately after Dr. Bartelt recommended surgical 
intervention.  While defense counsel argues to the contrary at hearing, this timing can 
be concerning and potentially suggests that defendants are interfering with the medical 
recommendations of Dr. Bartelt simply because they do not like the surgical 
recommendation he made. 
 
On the other hand, defendants now offer a similarly qualified orthopaedic 
surgeon, Dr. Bollier, to provide ongoing care for claimant.  Defense counsel clarified and 
confirmed at the alternate medical care hearing that the appointment scheduled with Dr. 
Bollier is for treatment purposes and not an independent medical evaluation.  Defense 
counsel also stated at the time of hearing that defendants would authorize surgery 
through Dr. Bollier, if Dr. Bollier recommends surgical intervention after his evaluation. 
 
Claimant seeks medical care through an orthopaedic surgeon, Dr. Bartelt.  
Defendants offer orthopaedic medical care through an orthopaedic surgeon but select 
Dr. Bollier instead of Dr. Bartelt.  Ultimately, I find that the care being offered by 
defendants is at least equivalent to the care requested through Dr. Bartelt.  At least at 
this juncture, claimant cannot prove that the care proposed to be offered by Dr. Bollier is 
inferior to or less extensive than the care being recommended by Dr. Bartelt.  I 
specifically find that the medical care offered by defendants, specifically care through 
Dr. Bollier, is being offered promptly and is reasonably suited to treat claimant’s injury 
without undue inconvenience to Ms. Denton. 
 
REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
The employer has a statutory duty to furnish reasonable surgical, medical, 
dental, osteopathic, chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance 
and hospital services and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' 
compensation law.  The employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary 
transportation expenses incurred for those services.  The employer has the right to 
choose the provider of care, except where the employer has denied liability for the 
injury.  Section 85.27.  Holbert v. Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial 
Report of the Industrial Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening October 16, 1975).

By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See Iowa 
R. App. P 14(f)(5); Bell Bros. Heating and Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d 193, 
209 (Iowa 2010); Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  Determining 
what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.  Long v. Roberts Dairy 
Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  The employer’s obligation turns on the question of 
reasonable necessity, not desirability.  Id.; Harned v. Farmland Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 
98 (Iowa 1983).   
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care she has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).   
On the other hand, an employer’s right to select the provider of medical treatment 
to an injured worker does not include the right to determine how an injured worker 
should be diagnosed, evaluated, treated, or other matters of professional medical 
judgment.   Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 866389 (Declaratory Ruling, May 19, 
1988).  In other words, defendants are not entitled to interfere with the medical 
judgment of their own treating physician.  Pote v. Mickow Corp., File No. 694639 
(Review-Reopening June 17, 1986).  
In Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 433, 437 (Iowa 1997), the 
supreme court held that “when evidence is presented to the commissioner that the 
employer-authorized medical care has not been effective and that such care is ‘inferior 
or less extensive’ than other available care requested by the employee, . . . the 
commissioner is justified by section 85.27 to order the alternate care.” 
 
 
In this case, I found that there is concern the defendants are attempting to 
interfere with or supersede the medical care recommended by Dr. Bartelt.  However, the 
the orthopaedic care defendants offer through Dr. Bollier is also at least equivalent to 
the care being sought by claimant.  It may come to pass that Dr. Bollier recommends 
care, other than surgical intervention, that is either not as extensive or that provides 
inferior results.  Claimant may at that juncture be able to prove that the care 
recommended by Dr. Bartelt is superior or more extensive and prevail.  In other words, 
defendants may pay for more medical care and ultimately may still be obligated to 
provide surgery through Dr. Bartelt.  See Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 
N.W.2d 433, 437 (Iowa 1997).  However, it would be speculative to assume or make 
any such findings at this time. 
 
Rather, I found that the current evidence demonstrates that the defendants are 
offering reasonable medical care that is prompt and not unduly inconvenient for 
claimant.  This is the statutory standard required of defendants as set forth in Iowa 
Code section 85.27(4).  Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  In 
fact, in the Long case, the Iowa Supreme Court permitted the employer to withdraw an 
authorization for care at the Mayo Clinic and transfer care to the University of Iowa

Hospitals and Clinics, even though claimant preferred the initial authorization of medical 
care through Mayo Clinic.  Id. 
 
In Long, the Court emphasized, “The ultimate question is whether … the 
employer met its obligation under Iowa Code section 85.27 ‘to furnish reasonable 
services’ for the treatment of [claimant’s] injuries.”  Id. at 123.  The Court further noted 
that the employer “is permitted to choose the care” and that it is claimant’s “burden of 
proving that the authorized care is unreasonable.”  Id. 
 
In this case, defendants offer medical care with an orthopaedic surgeon, Dr. 
Bollier.  Claimant seeks medical care through a different orthopaedic surgeon.  There is 
no evidence in this record that demonstrates the care to be offered by Dr. Bollier is 
inferior or less extensive than that recommended by Dr. Bartelt.  Claimant failed to 
prove that the care recommended by defendants through Dr. Bollier is unreasonable.  
Id.  Therefore, having found that the care by defendants through Dr. Bollier is 
reasonable, prompt, and not unduly inconvenient for claimant, I conclude that 
defendants retain the statutory right to select the medical provider they desire to provide 
claimant’s care.  Iowa Code section 85.27; Long, 528 N.W.2d at 123. 
 
ORDER 
 
THEREFORE IT IS ORDERED: 
 
The claimant's petition for alternate medical care is denied.
