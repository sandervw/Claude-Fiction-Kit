STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Fernando Mejia, claimant, filed 2 petitions in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from Andersen Window & Door, (“Andersen”) employer and Old 
Republic Insurance Co., insurance carrier, as defendants.  Hearing was held via Zoom 
videoconference on October 24, 2024.     
The parties filed a hearing report for each date of injury. On each hearing report, 
the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of those stipulations were accepted and 
are hereby incorporated into this arbitration decision.  The parties are now bound by 
their stipulations.  
Fernando Mejia was the only witness to testify live at trial.  The evidentiary record 
also includes joint exhibits 1-10, claimant’s exhibits 1-5, and defendant’s exhibits A-J.   
Defendants objected to claimant’s exhibit 5, page 54, a report from Dr. Sassman 
dated October 3, 2024 because it was not produced to defendants until after the 30-day 
exchange deadline.  Defendants argued that admission of the report would be 
prejudicial to defendants as the report attempts to introduce an alleged exam performed 
by Dr. Sassman, a non-treating physician, after the case preparation deadline had been 
completed.  (Def. Objection to Claimant’s Exhibit; Transcript p. 14)  Claimant contended 
the exam did take place before the deadline, but the report was not completed until after 
the deadline.  Claimant argues that the report is not prejudicial to defendants because 
the report “is simply a supplement to the previous report and exam that was done by 
Doctor Sassman back in 2022.”  (Tr. p. 15)  Dr. Sassman’s October 3, 2024 report is the

first time she placed the claimant at maximum medical improvement.  (Tr. p. 15) 
Claimant asserts they provided the report to defendants upon receipt.  (Tr. pp. 14-15)  
On November 14, 2023, the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commissioner issued 
a Hearing Assignment Order (HAO).  The HAO states in pertinent part,  
Witness and Exhibit Lists. At least 30 days prior to hearing, all parties shall 
serve a witness and exhibit list on all opposing parties and exchange all 
intended exhibits that were not previously required to be served. The 
exchange of these documents shall be served outside of WCES. Parties 
may depose any witness pursuant to Iowa Code section 10A.314(2) at any 
time before the hearing in lieu of the witness testifying at hearing. 
(HAO, p. 2) 
There is no dispute that claimant’s exhibit 5, p. 54 was served after the 30-day 
exchange deadline.  I find that the late production of claimant’s exhibit 5, p. 54 was in 
violation of the Commissioner’s HAO.  I find receipt of the evidence would be unfairly 
prejudicial to the defendants.  Receipt of the late evidence would allow claimant to serve 
a supplemental IME report after the exchange deadline, thus, allowing claimant’s expert 
to have the last word.  I find defendants have demonstrated at least some prejudice as 
a result of the late report.  Allowing the report into evidence and keeping the record 
open for defendants to obtain rebuttal evidence would also be prejudicial to defendants 
because that would require defendants to incur additional time and expense.  
Defendants demonstrated that receipt of the evidence would be unfairly prejudicial as 
required by 876 IAC 4.19(3)(e). The Supreme Court found that the agency should use 
its discretion in determining whether unfair prejudice exists in the exclusion or allowance 
of late testimony. Hagen v. Serta/Nat'l Bedding Co., LLC, 1 N.W.3d 1, 8 (Iowa 2024). 
Therefore, I exercised my discretion and sustained defendants’ objection; claimant’s 
exhibit 5, page 54 was excluded.  All other exhibits were received without objection.  
The evidentiary record closed at the conclusion of the arbitration hearing.

changes which take many years to develop and were not caused by a work injury or

The AMA Guides to the Evaluation of Disease and Injury Causation, 2nd 
Edition, on page 757 explains in broad terms that “The assumption that 
injury to 1 limb (upper or lower) can result in an overuse condition in the 
opposite limb is widespread but unproved.  Laypeople and some 
physicians believe that pain or impairment in 1 limb can stress the other 
and produce symptoms in the uninjured limb.  This belief has led to the 
concept termed favoring.  The impact of these speculative concepts is 
persuasive in spite of quality scientific investigations suggesting otherwise.  
It is important that popular conceptions {beliefs} of causation be kept in 
line with the best available scientific evidence.”  Regarding shoulders in 
particular the AMA Guides, states clearly on page 766 “the articles 
reviewed do not support ‘favoring’ as a reasonable cause for development 
of symptoms in the contralateral shoulder.  Rotator cuff lesions are seen 
with aging and are often asymptomatic.  Typically, activity is beneficial, not 
detrimental.”  It goes on to state that “even if symptoms in the second limb 
develop after symptoms are present in the first limb (a temporal 
relationship), there is no scientific support for the concept that having 
symptoms in the first limb causes an increased rate of disease in the 
second limb.” 
(JE5, p. 101)

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996). 
An employer covered by the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Act must “provide, 
secure, and pay compensation according to the provisions of this chapter for any and all 
personal injuries sustained by an employee arising out of and in the course of the 
employment, and in such cases, the employer shall be relieved from other liability for 
recovery of damages or other compensation for such personal injury.” Iowa Code § 
85.3(1). “[W]here an accident occurs to an employee in the usual course of his 
employment, the employer is liable for all consequences that naturally and proximately 
flow from the accident.” Oldham v. Scofield & Welch, 266 N.W. 480, 482, opinion 
modified on denial of reh'g, 222 Iowa 764, 269 N.W. 925 (Iowa 1936). This includes, but 
is not limited to, a mental health condition caused by a work injury. See Coghlan v. 
Quinn Wire & Iron Works, 164 N.W.2d 848, 852–53 (Iowa 1969). 
 While a claimant is not entitled to compensation for the results of a preexisting 
injury or disease, its mere existence at the time of a subsequent injury is not a 

MEJIA V. ANDERSEN WINDOW & DOOR 
Page 16 
 
defense. Rose v. John Deere Ottumwa Works, 247 Iowa 900, 908, 76 N.W.2d 756, 760-
61 (1956). If the claimant had a preexisting condition or disability that is materially 
aggravated, accelerated, worsened or lighted up so that it results in disability, claimant 
is entitled to recover. Nicks v. Davenport Produce Co., 254 Iowa 130, 135, 115 N.W.2d 
812, 815 (1962); Yeager v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co., 253 Iowa 369, 374-75, 112 
N.W.2d 299, 302 (1961). 
“[T]he burden of proof is on the claimant to prove some employment incident or 
activity was a proximate cause of the health impairment on which he bases his claim.” 
Anderson v. Oscar Mayer & Co., 217 N.W.2d 531, 535 (Iowa 1974). “[A] possibility is 
insufficient; a probability is necessary.” Id. The claimant must prove causation by a 
preponderance of the evidence. See, e.g., St. Luke's Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 
652 (Iowa 2000) (citing Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 150 (Iowa 1996)). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony.  The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question.  The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances.  The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); 
Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995).  Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994).  Unrebutted expert medical 
testimony cannot be summarily rejected.  Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 
N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 1994). 
In a decision similar to the facts of this case, the agency has previously found the 
opinion of Dr. Bollier and the AMA Guides to the Evaluation of Disease and Injury 
Causation, 2nd Edition to be persuasive.  See Rema v. Transco Railway Products, Inc., 
File No. 21001855.01, 21012426.01 (App. September 12, 2023). 
 
Based on the above findings of fact, I conclude claimant has failed to 
demonstrate that he sustained an injury on April 7, 2021 that extends beyond his right 
shoulder.  Under the Iowa Workers' Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is 
compensated either for a loss or loss of use of a scheduled member under Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(a)-(u) or as an unscheduled injury pursuant to the provisions 
of section 85.34(2)(v). The extent of scheduled member disability benefits to which an 
injured worker is entitled is determined by using the functional method. Functional 
disability is “limited to the loss of the physiological capacity of the body or body 
part.” Mortimer v. Fruehauf Corp., 502 N.W.2d 12, 15 (Iowa 1993); Sherman v. Pella 
Corp., 576 N.W.2d 312 (Iowa 1998). 
Iowa Code section 85.34(x) permanent disabilities states:

Claimant is seeking reimbursement for an IME.  Iowa Code section 85.39 permits 
an employee to be reimbursed for subsequent examination by a physician of the 
employee's choice where an employer-retained physician has previously evaluated 
“permanent disability” and the employee believes that the initial evaluation is too low.   
Iowa Code § 85.39(2) (2023).  The section also permits reimbursement for reasonably 
necessary transportation expenses incurred and for any wage loss occasioned by the 
employee attending the subsequent examination.  Iowa Code § 85.39 (1), (2).   
The Iowa Supreme Court has provided a literal interpretation of the plain 
language of Iowa Code section 85.39, stating that section only allows the employee to 
obtain an independent medical evaluation at the employer’s expense if an evaluation of 
permanent disability has already been made by an employer retained physician.  Des 
Moines Area Reg’l Transit Auth. v. Young, 867 N.W.2d 839, 847 (Iowa 2015). Under 
the Young decision, an employee can only obtain an IME at the employer's expense if

an evaluation of permanent disability has been made by an employer-retained 
physician. Id. 
An employer is only liable to reimburse an employee for the cost of an 
examination conducted pursuant to this subsection if the injury for which the employee 
is being examined is determined to be compensable.  Iowa Code § 85.39(2) (2017).  
“An employer is not liable for the cost of such examination if the injury for which the 
employee is being examined is determined not to have a compensable injury. A 
determination of the reasonableness of a fee for an examination made pursuant to this 
subsection, shall be based on the typical fee charged by a medical provider to perform 
an impairment rating in the local area where the examination is conducted.”  Iowa Code 
§ 85.39(2); Tweeten v. Tweeten, 999 N.W.2d 270, 282 (Iowa 2023).
