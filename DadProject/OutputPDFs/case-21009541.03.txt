STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Molly Camarigg, claimant, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from Fresenius Medical Care Holdings, employer and American 
Casualty Company of Reading, Pennsylvania, insurance carrier, as defendants.  
Hearing was held via Zoom videoconference on October 7, 2024.     
The parties filed a hearing report in each file at the commencement of the 
arbitration hearing.  On the hearing reports, the parties entered into various stipulations.  
All of those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised 
or discussed in this decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.  
Molly Camarigg was the only witness to testify live at trial.  The evidentiary record 
also includes joint exhibits 1-6, claimant’s exhibits 1-2, and defendant’s exhibits A-F.  All 
exhibits were received without objection.  The exhibits for the two agency file numbers 
are identical.  The evidentiary record closed at the conclusion of the arbitration hearing.  
The parties submitted post-hearing briefs on November 7, 2024, at which time the case 
was fully submitted to the undersigned.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 

CAMARIGG V. FRESENIUS MEDICAL CARE HOLDINGS 
Page 6 
 
 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony.  The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question.  The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances.  The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); 
Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995).  Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994).  Unrebutted expert medical 
testimony cannot be summarily rejected.  Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 
N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 1994). 
Under the Iowa Workers' Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is 
compensated either for a loss or loss of use of a scheduled member under Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(a)-(u) or as an unscheduled injury pursuant to the provisions 
of section 85.34(2)(v). The extent of scheduled member disability benefits to which an 
injured worker is entitled is determined by using the functional method. Functional 
disability is “limited to the loss of the physiological capacity of the body or body 
part.” Mortimer v. Fruehauf Corp., 502 N.W.2d 12, 15 (Iowa 1993); Sherman v. Pella 
Corp., 576 N.W.2d 312 (Iowa 1998). 
Iowa Code section 85.34(x) permanent disabilities states: 
x. In all cases of permanent partial disability described in paragraphs “a” 
through “u”, or paragraph “v” when determining functional disability and 
not loss of earning capacity, the extent of loss or percentage of permanent 
impairment shall be determined solely by utilizing the guides to the 
evaluation of permanent impairment, published by the American medical 
association, as adopted by the workers' compensation commissioner by 
rule pursuant to chapter 17A. Lay testimony or agency expertise shall not 
be utilized in determining loss or percentage of permanent impairment 
pursuant to paragraphs “a” through “u”, or paragraph “v” when determining 
functional disability and not loss of earning capacity. 
 
Iowa Code section 85.34 (x) (emphasis added).

This agency has adopted The Guides to the Evaluation of Permanent 
Impairment, Fifth Edition, published by the American Medical Association for 
determining the extent of loss or percentage of impairment for permanent partial 
disabilities.  See 876 IAC 2.4. 
The parties have stipulated that claimant’s injury should be compensated as a 
shoulder under section 85.34(2)(n).  For the loss of a shoulder, weekly compensation 
during four hundred weeks.  Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(n). The Commissioner has 
concluded that it is appropriate to apply the upper extremity impairment rating for a 
shoulder injury. See Deng v. Farmland Foods, Inc., File No. 5061883 (App., September 
29, 2020). See also; Chavez v. MS Technology, LLC, File No. 5066270 (App., 
September 30, 2020); Smidt v. JKB Restaurants, LC, File No. 5067766 (App., 
December 11, 2020). 
Based on the above findings of fact, I conclude that the opinion of Dr. Bansal 
carries more weight than that of Dr. Johnson.  As the result of the November 16, 2020 
work injury Dr. Bansal opined that claimant sustained 7 percent impairment of her left 
upper extremity due to range of motion.  (Cl. Ex. 1, p. 18)  
Dr. Bansal cited Table 16-27 of The Guides and assigned 10 percent upper 
extremity impairment for her distal clavicle resection.  (Cl. Ex. 1, p. 19) However, the 
Commissioner has stated that under The Guides, Fifth Edition, a rating physician must 
apply the 25 percent multiplier contained in Table 16-18 to any rating for a distal clavicle 
resection.  Jay v. Archer Skid Loader Service, File No. 19003586.01 (App., Aug. 23, 
2022).  That decision was affirmed by the district court on judicial review. Jay v. Archer 
Skid Loader Service, L.L.C., Case No. CVCV064318 (Iowa District Court for Polk 
County, May 22, 2023).  The Commissioner indicated that Table 16-27 of the Guides 
governs impairment of the upper extremities after arthroplasty of specific bones and 
joints.  Under Table 16-27, a distal clavicle excision is assigned ten percent impairment.     
Page 498 of the AMA Guides directs the examining physician as follows: 
Conditions not previously described that can contribute to impairments of the 
hand and upper extremity include bone and joint disorders (Section 16.7a), presence of 
resection or implant arthroplasty (Section 16.7b), musculotendinous disorders (Section 
16.7c), tendinitis (Section 16.7d), and loss of strength (Section 16.8). The severity of the 
impairment due to these disorders is rated separately according to Tables 16-19 
through 16-30 and then multiplied by the relative maximum value of the unit involved as 
specified in Table 16-18 .... 
Under Table 16-18, the appropriate multiplier for the acromioclavicular joint is 25 
percent. 
Dr. Johnson performed a distal clavicle resection in this case.  The Guides 
instruct the physician to assign a rating for a distal clavicle resection.  The Guides 
require application of a 25 percent multiplier.  This results in a 2.5 percent impairment 
for a distal clavicle resection. See Jay v. Archer Skid Loader Service, File No. 

CAMARIGG V. FRESENIUS MEDICAL CARE HOLDINGS 
Page 8 
 
 
19003586.01 (App., Aug. 23, 2022).  According to the The Guides, this rating must be 
rounded to the nearest whole number.  Utilizing the combined values chart on page 
604, Dr. Bansal’s individual shoulder ratings result in a combined rating of 10 percent of 
the left upper extremity.   
Dr. Bansal also assigned claimant 2 percent upper extremity impairment for 
elbow flexion strength loss from the biceps tenotomy.  (Cl. Ex. 1, pp. 19-20) Under Iowa 
Code section 85.34(x), claims of functional disability are to be determined solely by 
applying the AMA Guides to the Evaluation of Permanent Impairment, Fifth Edition. 
Iowa Code § 85.34(2)(x). This Agency has cautioned against impairment ratings that do 
not comply with the AMA Guides. See Hill v. Vermeer Corporation, File No. 5066032 
(App., Jan. 30, 2020). The Hill decision states, 
[T]he AMA Guides caution physicians against assigning impairment for loss of 
strength. Section 16.8 on page 507 provides the AMA Guides do not assign a large role 
to strength measurements due to the fact strength measurements are functional tests 
influenced by subjective factors that are difficult to control. Review of Section 16.8a of 
the AMA Guides to the Evaluation of Permanent Impairment, Fifth Edition, page 508, 
indicates: 
In a rare case, if the examiner believes the individual's loss of strength 
represents an impairing factor that has not been considered adequately by 
other methods in the Guides, the loss of strength may be rated separately. 
An example of this situation would be loss of strength due to a severe 
muscle tear that healed leaving a palpable muscle defect. If the examiner 
judges that loss of strength should be rated separately in an extremity that 
presents other impairments, the impairment due to loss of strength could 
be combined with the other impairments, only if based on unrelated       
etiologic or pathomechanical causes. Otherwise, the impairment ratings 
based on objective anatomic findings take precedence. Decreased 
strength cannot be rated in the presence of decreased motion, painful 
conditions, deformities, or absence of parts (e.g., thumb amputation) that 
prevent effective application of maximal force in the region being 
evaluated. 
See Hill, at p. 4 (Emphasis added). Dr. Bansal's loss-of-strength rating is not based on 
etiologic or pathomechanical causes unrelated to the shoulder surgery. According to Dr. 
Bansal's report, it is a direct cause of her bicep tendinopathy which was a documented 
part of her shoulder surgery. It is also clear that claimant has decreased motion in her 
shoulder joint. Given this, Dr. Bansal's strength loss rating is not consistent with the 
AMA Guides and cannot be adopted. 
Based on the above, I conclude that claimant has demonstrated that she 
sustained 10 percent impairment of the left upper extremity.  Pursuant to Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(n) this amounts to 40 weeks of permanent partial disability benefits.  
These benefits shall be paid at the stipulated weekly rate of eight hundred seventy-nine

We now turn to the right shoulder.  Based on the above findings of fact, I 
conclude that Dr. Bansal’s opinion carries greater weight than Dr. Johnson’s.  As such, I 
find claimant has demonstrated by a preponderance of the evidence that she sustained 
10 percent impairment of the right upper extremity as the result of the January 4, 2024 
work injury.   
Defendants argue that claimant had a preexisting 3 percent permanent disability 
as the result of a work injury with an employer that is not the defendant employer.  (JE6, 
p. 49; Tr. p. 20) Defendants contend that under Iowa Code section 85.34(7) defendants 
are only liable for permanent partial disability to the right shoulder if same is greater 
than the preexisting disability.  Regarding successive disabilities the Iowa Code states,  
An employer is liable for compensating only that portion of an employee's 
disability that arises out of and in the course of the employee's 
employment with the employer and that relates to the injury that serves as 
the basis for the employee's claim for compensation under chapter 10A, 
subchapter III, this chapter, or chapter 85A or 85B. An employer is not 
liable for compensating an employee's preexisting disability that arose out 
of and in the course of employment from a prior injury with the employer, 
to the extent that the employee's preexisting disability has already been 
compensated under chapter 10A, subchapter III, this chapter, or chapter 
85A or 85B. An employer is not liable for compensating an employee's 
preexisting disability that arose out of and in the course of employment 
with a different employer or from causes unrelated to employment. 
Iowa Code § 85.34 (7). 
In the present case, Dr. Bansal was asked to perform an IME and provide his 
opinions regarding the claimant and the November 16, 2020 and January 4, 2021 work 
injuries.  Dr. Bansal opined that the claimant sustained 10 percent impairment of her 
right upper extremity.  Neither Dr. Johnson nor Dr. Bansal assigned any portion of the 
impairment to a work injury not incurred with the defendant employer. I find that 
defendants failed to demonstrate that any of the impairment Dr. Bansal assigned is due 
to a preexisting condition.       
Thus, I conclude that claimant has demonstrated that she sustained 10 percent 
impairment of her right upper extremity as the result of the January 4, 2021 work injury.  
As such she is entitled to 40 weeks (400 weeks x 10 percent) of weekly benefits at the 
stipulated weekly rate of $879.90.  These benefits shall commence on the stipulated 
date of November 9, 2023.

Iowa Code section 85.39 permits an employee to be reimbursed for subsequent 
examination by a physician of the employee's choice where an employer-retained 
physician has previously evaluated “permanent disability” and the employee believes 
that the initial evaluation is too low.   Iowa Code § 85.39(2) (2023).  The section also 
permits reimbursement for reasonably necessary transportation expenses incurred and 
for any wage loss occasioned by the employee attending the subsequent examination.  
Iowa Code § 85.39 (1), (2).   
First, we will address the IME of the left shoulder.  Dr. Johnson is a physician 
retained by the defendant employer in this case.  He issued an evaluation of permanent 
disability on December 15, 2023.  (Def. Ex. E, p. 33)  Claimant underwent an IME with 
Dr. Bansal on February 24, 2024.  (Cl. Ex. 1, p. 1)  I find that the requirements of Iowa 
Code section 85.39 were met and that claimant is entitled to reimbursement for an IME 
of her left shoulder.   
Second, we will address the IME of the right shoulder.  Dr. Johnson is a 
physician retained by the defendant employer in this case.  He issued an evaluation of 
permanent disability on September 6, 2024.  (Def. Ex. E, p. 34) Claimant underwent an 
IME with Dr. Bansal on February 24, 2024.  (Cl. Ex. 1, p. 1)  I find that at the time of the 
IME with Dr. Bansal an evaluation of permanent disability had not yet been made by a 
physician retained by the employer.  Thus, the requirements of Iowa Code section 85.39 
were not met and that claimant is not entitled to reimbursement for an IME of her right 
shoulder.   
Unfortunately, the invoice from Dr. Bansal’s office lists the description of services 
as, “Total cost for Independent Medical Evaluation Camarigg, Molly” and one amount of 
$4,643.00.  (Cl. Ex. 1, p. 23)  I find that claimant has demonstrated entitlement to 
reimbursement for the IME for the November 16, 2020 date of injury, but not for the 
January 4, 2021 date of injury.  As such, I find that claimant is entitled to reimbursement 
of one-half the cost of the IME.  Thus, defendants shall reimburse claimant in the 
amount of $2,321.50.
