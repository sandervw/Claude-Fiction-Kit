Obed Mendoza, claimant, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from Lennox Industries, Inc., employer, and Indemnity Insurance 
Company of North America, insurance carrier, as defendants.  The hearing was held on 
March 28, 2024.  Pursuant to an order from the Iowa Workers’ Compensation 
Commissioner, this case was heard via videoconference using Zoom with all parties 
and the court reporter appearing remotely.   
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  Those 
stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration decision and 
no factual issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised or discussed in this 
decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.  
Obed Mendoza was the only witness to testify live at trial.  The evidentiary record 
also includes joint exhibits 1-7, claimant’s exhibits 1-6, and defendants’ exhibits A-I.  All 
exhibits were received into the record without objection.  
The parties submitted post-hearing briefs on May 3, 2024, at which time the case 
was fully submitted to the undersigned.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned, having considered all the evidence and testimony in the

At the time of the hearing the claimant, Obed Mendoza (hereinafter “Mendoza”) 
was 58 years old and residing in Marshalltown, Iowa. (Hearing Transcript, pp. 13-14).  
On March 30, 2010, Lennox Industries, Inc. (hereinafter “Lennox”) hired Mendoza to 
work as an assembler. (Claimant’s Exhibit 2, p. 18).  While he was an assembler at 
Lennox, Mendoza performed two different positions. (See Cl Ex. 1, p. 1).  The first was 
named the best rail job and involved screwing parts onto heating and cooling units, and 
the second required him to attach parts onto a collector/burner box. (Id.).  Both positions 
took place on the assembly line. (Id.).  At the time of the hearing, Mendoza was still 
employed by Lennox in the parts packaging department. (Defendants’ Exhibit B, depo p. 
6).  He transferred to this position after the work injury at issue in this case. (Ex. B, depo 
p. 9).  
On November 9, 2021, Mendoza injured his right knee while working on the 
assembly line. (Hearing Report; Tr., p. 15).  At the hearing, Mendoza testified that he 
twisted around to pick up a part behind him, when his foot got stuck on the floor mat and 
he heard a pop in his knee and felt immediate pain. (Tr., pp. 14-15; Joint Exhibit 3, p. 
57).  He attempted to report the incident to an assistant right away but was instructed to 
wait until the next morning. (Tr., pp. 15-16).  Mendoza reported the injury to his 
supervisor the next day. (Id. at 16).  He, however, did not receive medical treatment 
until approximately a week later. (Id. at 17).  According to the medical records, Mendoza 
presented to the nurse at Lennox on November 23, 2021, complaining of right knee 
pain. (JE 2, p. 56).  The treatment notes from this visit, however, are not in the hearing 
record. (Id.).  The nurse at Lennox Occupational Health referred him to Sherman Jew, 
D.O., at the Occupational Medicine Clinic in Nevada, Iowa. (JE 3, p. 58).

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 
employment.  Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 150 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. 
Dial Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309, 311 (Iowa 1996).  The words “arising out of” refer to the 
cause or source of the injury.  The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 
circumstances of the injury.  2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124, 128 (Iowa 
1995).  An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists 
between the injury and the employment.  Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311.  The injury must 
be a rational consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely 
incidental to the employment.  Koehler Electric v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1, 3 (Iowa 2000); 
Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311.  An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it 
happens within a period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may 
be when performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties 
or doing an activity incidental to them.  Ciha, 552 N.W.2d at 150. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 

MENDOZA V. LENNOX INDUSTRIES, INC. 
Page 9 
 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony.  The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question.  The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances.  The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410, 420 
(Iowa 2001); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995).  Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994).  Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected.  Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-92 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
When an expert's opinion is based upon an incomplete or incorrect history, it is 
not necessarily binding on the commissioner or the court. It is then to be weighed, 
together with other facts and circumstances, the ultimate conclusion being for the finder 
of the fact.  Musselman v. Central Telephone Company, 154 N.W.2d 128 (Iowa 
1967); Bodish v. Fischer, Inc., 257 Iowa 516, 133 N.W.2d 867 (1965).  The 
commissioner as trier of fact has the duty to determine the credibility of the witnesses 
and to weigh the evidence, together with the other disclosed facts and circumstances, 
and then to accept or reject the opinion.  Dunlavey, 526 N.W.2d 845. 
While a claimant is not entitled to compensation for the results of a preexisting 
injury or disease, its mere existence at the time of a subsequent injury is not a defense. 
Rose v. John Deere Ottumwa Works, 247 Iowa 900, 908, 76 N.W.2d 756, 760-61 
(1956). If the claimant had a preexisting condition or disability that is materially 
aggravated, accelerated, worsened or lighted up so that it results in disability, claimant 
is entitled to recover. Nicks v. Davenport Produce Co., 254 Iowa 130, 135, 115 N.W.2d 
812, 815 (1962); Yeager v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co., 253 Iowa 369, 374-75, 112 
N.W.2d 299, 302 (1961). 
Defendants admit that Mendoza sustained a work-related injury to his right knee 
on November 9, 2021, and that he has suffered permanent impairment due to that 
injury. (See Hearing Report).  The parties’ main disagreement is whether Mendoza’s 
second knee surgery with Dr. White was necessary because of that accepted work 
injury.  Defendants maintain the surgery was to treat a pre-existing degenerative 
condition. (Defendants’ Post-Hearing Brief, p. 9).  Mendoza argues the work incident 
caused the tear repaired during his second knee surgery, as well as his current knee 
pain. (Claimant’s Post-Hearing Brief, pp. 7-9).  He claims he did not have any knee 
problems until after the work incident. (Id.).   
There are five separate causation opinions in the hearing record—Dr. Vinyard, 
Dr. White, Dr. Vincent, Dr. Kuhnlein, and Dr.  Fields.  Dr. Vinyard opined that Mendoza’s 

MENDOZA V. LENNOX INDUSTRIES, INC.

meniscal tear was caused by the work incident, but that his ongoing knee symptoms 
were from pre-existing medial compartment osteoarthritis, not the work injury.  In 
contrast, Dr. White thought that Mendoza’s second knee surgery was necessitated by

surgery.  Dr. Vincent provided his opinion after reviewing both of Mendoza’s MRIs.  He 
believed the second MRI showed a horizontal tear in his medial meniscus which was 
degenerative and not caused by the work injury.  He also indicated that the tear repaired 
by Dr. White was an oblique tear, not a horizontal one.  Dr. Kuhnlein performed an IME 
for Mendoza.  He opined that the tear repaired by Dr. White was a recurrent tear and a 
sequela of the work injury.  He indicated the tear was caused by Mendoza’s work 
activities right after the initial surgery, which exceeded his restrictions and required 
twisting.  Finally, Dr. Fields has indicated that Mendoza’s second knee surgery was not 
necessitated by the work injury but rather caused by his pre-existing degenerative 
condition.   
Out of the opinions presented, I find those of Dr. Vinyard and Dr. Kuhnlein to be 
the most convincing.  Dr. Vinyard sent defendants a report on March 26, 2022. (See JE 
1, pp. 9-10).  In that report, he noted that Mendoza’s January 2022 MRI showed “a 
horizontal and oblique tear of the posterior horn of his medial meniscus.” (Id. at 
9)(emphasis added).  Dr. Vinyard recommended surgery and opined Mendoza’s twisting 
injury at work was “the cause of his symptoms and need for further treatment.” (Id.).  
Thus, according to Dr. Vinyard, the horizontal meniscus tear was present prior to the 
first surgery, and he causally related it to the work injury, not a degenerative condition. 
(Id.).  This contradicts the opinions of Dr. Vincent and Dr. Fields, who opined that the 
horizontal tear developed after the first surgery, that it was degenerative in origin, and 
not actually the source of Mendoza’s knee pain.  The first MRI clearly demonstrates a 
meniscus tear with a horizontal component; thus a horizontal tear was present in some 
form prior to the first surgery.  Additionally, Mendoza’s follow-up records show that the 
first surgery did not relieve his knee pain. (See JE 2, pp. 51-55).  He complained of 
ongoing pain to Lennox Occupational Health shortly after the surgery. (Id.).   
Now, whether the tear was not repaired during Mendoza’s first surgery or 
whether it re-tore after the surgery is not completely clear.  There is some support for Dr. 
Kuhnlein’s theory of a recurrent tear occurring at work after the surgery with Dr. Vinyard.  
In a treatment note dated May 25, 2022, PA Hedrick notes that Mendoza requested an 
earlier follow-up appointment with Dr. Vinyard due to “increased pain and swelling from 
overdoing it at work.” (JE 1, p. 16).  The medical records show that Mendoza’s 
complaints of ongoing knee pain were pretty consistent after that appointment. (See id. 
at 19-48).  Those complaints were not relieved by injections or physical therapy. (Id.).  
Only the second surgery to repair the horizontal tear seemed to provide him with any 
relief. (See Tr., p. 30).   
Defendants contest whether the surgery actually helped with Mendoza’s 
symptoms and pain levels.  (Defendants’ Post-Hearing Brief, pp. 12-13). They argue 
that Mendoza cannot prove the second surgery was reasonable and beneficial, and 
therefore cannot meet the test for reimbursement of unauthorized medical care. See

The parties have a disagreement about Mendoza’s level of permanent 
impairment from the November 9, 2021 work injury.  Under the Iowa Workers' 
Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is compensated either for a loss or loss 
of use of a scheduled member under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(a)-(u) or for loss of 
earning capacity under section 85.34(2)(v). The extent of scheduled member disability 
benefits to which an injured worker is entitled is determined by using the functional 
method. Functional disability is “limited to the loss of the physiological capacity of the 
body or body part.” Mortimer v. Fruehauf Corp., 502 N.W.2d 12, 15 (Iowa 1993); 
Sherman v. Pella Corp., 576 N.W.2d 312, 320 (Iowa 1998).  On the hearing report, the 
parties stipulated that Mendoza’s compensable injury is limited to his right lower 
extremity. (See Hearing Report).  Thus, his permanent impairment for that injury is 
determined under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(p).  Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(p) limits 
compensation for a leg to two hundred twenty weeks.  Iowa Code § 85.34(2)(p) (2021).   
In all cases of permanent partial disability described in paragraphs “a” through 
“u”, or paragraph “v” when determining functional disability and not loss of earning 
capacity, the extent of loss or percentage of permanent impairment shall be determined 
solely by utilizing the guides to the evaluation of permanent impairment, published by 
the American medical association, as adopted by the workers' compensation 
commissioner by rule pursuant to chapter 17A. Lay testimony or agency expertise shall 
not be utilized in determining loss or percentage of permanent impairment pursuant to 
paragraphs “a” through “u”, or paragraph “v” when determining functional disability and 
not loss of earning capacity.  Iowa Code § 85.34(2)(x).   
Ratings for knee injuries such as Mendoza’s are covered by Chapter 17 of the 
AMA Guides.  See AMA Guides, p. 523. Chapter 17 provides three different methods 
for assessing permanency in the lower extremity: (1) anatomic, (2) functional, and (3) 
diagnosis based. Id. at 525.  The Guides instructs the evaluator to select the most

clinically appropriate or specific method of assigning impairment.3 Id. at 526.   Dr. 
Vinyard, Dr. Fields, and Dr. Kuhnlein all assigned Mendoza 2 percent permanent 
impairment for his medial meniscectomy surgery/surgeries per Table 17-33.  However, 
Dr. Kuhnlein also assigned him an additional 1 percent permanent impairment to the 
right lower extremity for a sensory deficit in his saphenous nerve distribution. (See Cl 
Ex. 1, p. 11).  This additional impairment is not supported by the hearing record.  Dr.

The parties dispute Mendoza’s correct weekly rate.  The disagreement originates 
from a difference of opinion about his average weekly earnings.  The language of Iowa 
Code section 85.36(6) states,

and then rounded to the nearest dollar:

the closest previous week with earnings that fairly represent the employee’s

Id.  The focus of section 85.36 is on whether the employee's “earnings” are “customary.” 
As the court in Jacobson Transportation Co. v. Harris, 778 N.W.2d 192, 199 (Iowa 2010), 
explained:   
“Customary” means “based on or established by custom”; “commonly 
practiced, used or observed”; or “usual.” Merriam-Webster's Collegiate 
Dictionary 285 (10th ed.2002). We have previously defined “customary” as 
“typical.” . . .  Ascertainment of an employee's customary earnings does not

Id.: see also Griffin Pipe Products Co. v. Guarino, 663 N.W.2d 862, 866 (Iowa 2003) 
(stating pertinent question is what hours the employee customarily worked); Weishaar v.

and therefore the weeks of August 1, 2021; August 8, 2021; October 3, 2021; and 
October 24, 2021, should be included in his rate calculation.  Given these findings, 
Mendoza’s average weekly wage is $842.25. He was single and entitled to one 
exemption.  Mendoza’s weekly rate for the November 9, 2021 date of injury is $533.10.

in claimant’s exhibit 5, he is entitled to reimbursement of those expenses.  If Mendoza 
has not paid those expenses, defendants are ordered to pay the listed providers directly 
and/or satisfy any liens asserted for payment of those expenses. Krohn v. State of Iowa,

Mendoza requests reimbursement of the IME performed by Dr. Kuhnlein.  Iowa 
Code section 85.39 permits an employee to be reimbursed for a subsequent 

MENDOZA V. LENNOX INDUSTRIES, INC. 
Page 15 
 
examination by a physician of the employee's choice where an employer-retained 
physician has previously evaluated “permanent disability” and the employee believes 
that the initial evaluation is too low. The Iowa Supreme Court has provided a literal 
interpretation of the plain language of Iowa Code section 85.39, stating that section only 
allows the employee to obtain an independent medical evaluation at the employer’s 
expense if an evaluation of permanent disability has already been made by an employer 
retained physician.  Des Moines Area Reg’l Transit Auth. v. Young, 867 N.W.2d 839, 
847 (Iowa 2015). Dr. Vinyard, an authorized treating physician, rated Mendoza’s right 
knee impairment in August 2022.  Dr. Kuhnlein’s IME report was issued in February 
2024.  Given this, Mendoza is entitled to reimbursement for some portion of Dr. 
Kuhnlein’s report.   
Defendants are responsible only for reasonable fees associated with claimant's 
independent medical examination. Claimant has the burden of proving the 
reasonableness of the expenses incurred for the examination. See Schintgen v. 
Economy Fire & Casualty Co., File No. 855298 (App. April 26, 1991).  It is the agency’s 
duty to parse out whether an IME expense is reimbursable under the revised language 
of 85.39(2). Fuller v. Bimbo Bakeries, U.S.A., File No.  20012896.01 (App. Sept. 12, 
2023) (citing to P.M. Lattner Manufacturing Co. v. Rife, 2023 WL 3862594 (Iowa Ct. 
App. June 7, 2023) (unpublished decision).  Dr. Kuhnlein charged $4,581.50 for his 
services. (Cl Ex. 6, p. 52).  Dr. Kuhnlein’s invoice breaks this down into several 
categories.  He charges $4,040.00 for preparation of the impairment rating report, 
$190.00 for the impairment rating exam, $190.00 for providing restrictions and 
recommending future medical care, and $161.50 for new x-rays. (Id.).  In their post-
hearing brief, defendants argue that they are not responsible for the x-rays Dr. Kuhnlein 
ordered because he did not utilize them to assign any impairment. (Defendants’ Post-
Hearing Brief, p. 19).  Mendoza’s post-hearing brief does not address the issue.  
The Iowa Supreme Court recently stated that under the revised language of 
85.39, an employee can be reimbursed for the cost of an expert’s examination, 
including costs to review medical records, conduct a physical exam, opine on causation, 
assess impairment, assign restrictions, and suggest future treatment. Mid American 
Construction, LLC v. Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d 838, 851-852 (Iowa 2024).  The Court did not 
include the cost of obtaining new medical tests in their list of reimbursable expenses.  
Given this, I find the cost of obtaining new x-rays is not reimbursable in this case.  The 
Court has not specifically articulated it as a reimbursable cost, and Dr. Kuhnlein did not 
utilize it to assign Mendoza permanent impairment.  
In his report, Dr. Kuhnlein attests to the reasonableness of his IME fees. (Cl Ex. 
1, p. 14).  Defendants did not submit any hearing evidence addressing the 
reasonableness of the remainder of Dr. Kuhnlein’s IME fees. On this record, I find that 
Mendoza is entitled to reimbursement of $4,420.00 from defendants pursuant to Iowa 
Code section 85.39(2).
