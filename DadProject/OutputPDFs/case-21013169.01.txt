STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Thomas Gibbs, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from the Second Injury Fund of Iowa (Fund) as defendant.  This 
matter was heard on December 16, 2024, with a final submission date of January 21, 
2025. 
The record in this case consists of Joint Exhibits 1-9, Claimant’s Exhibits 1-3 and 
6-8, Fund Exhibits AA-BB, and the testimony of claimant. 
STIPULATIONS 
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised 
or discussed in this decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.  
ISSUES 
1. Did claimant sustain a qualifying first injury for the purposes of Fund benefits? 
 
2. The extent of claimant’s entitlement to Fund benefits. 
 
3. The commencement date for Fund benefits. 
 
4. Credit due to the Fund.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The first issue to be determined is whether claimant sustained a first qualifying 
loss for the purposes of qualifying for Fund benefits. 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e). 
Iowa Code section 85.64 governs Second Injury Fund liability.  Before liability of 
the Fund is triggered, three requirements must be met.  First, the employee must have 
lost or lost the use of a hand, arm, foot, leg, or eye.  Second, the employee must sustain 
a loss or loss of use of another specified member or organ through a compensable 
injury.  Third, permanent disability must exist as to both the initial injury and the second 
injury.   
The Second Injury Fund Act exists to encourage the hiring of handicapped 
persons by making a current employer responsible only for the amount of disability 
related to an injury occurring while that employer employed the handicapped individual 
as if the individual had had no preexisting disability.  See Anderson v. Second Injury

Fund, 262 N.W.2d 789, 792 (Iowa 1978); 15 Iowa Practice, Workers’ Compensation, 
Lawyer, Section 17:1, p. 211 (2014-2015). 
The Fund is responsible for the industrial disability present after the second injury 
that exceeds the disability attributable to the first and second injuries.  Section 85.64.  
Second Injury Fund of Iowa v. Braden, 459 N.W.2d 467, 471 (Iowa 1990); Second Injury 
Fund v. Neelans, 436 N.W.2d 355, 358 (Iowa 1989); Second Injury Fund v. Mich Coal 
Co., 274 N.W.2d 300, 304 (Iowa 1979). 
Claimant contends he has a qualifying first injury for the purposes of Fund 
benefits due to his bilateral upper extremities.  (Claimant’s Brief, pages 5-7)  Defendant 
Fund argues that claimant’s alleged first injury is to his bilateral shoulders, and is not a 
qualifying first injury for the purposes of Fund benefits.  (Fund’s Post-Hearing Brief, 
pages 16-22) 
As detailed in the Findings of Fact, claimant was evaluated by Dr. Switzer in 
August 2017 for right shoulder problems.  There is no mention of right arm or elbow 
problems at that visit.  (JE 1, pp. 5-6) 
Claimant returned to Dr. Switzer in October and November 2018 for shoulder 
pain.  There is no mention of arm or elbow problems at those visits.  (JE 1, pp. 11-17)  
Claimant returned to Dr. Switzer in May of 2019 with complaints of right shoulder 
pain.  There is no mention of arm or elbow pain in this visit.  (JE 1, p. 20) 
Claimant had a right total shoulder arthroplasty in September 2019.  There is no 
mention of treatment of the elbow or arm in these records.  (Ex. 2, pp. 74-76) 
Claimant returned in follow-up with Dr. Switzer in December of 2019, October of 
2020, and December of 2020, for the shoulder.  There is no mention of any problems to 
the elbows or arms in these records.  (JE 1, pp. 27-28, 29-30, 32-33) 
Claimant had a left shoulder surgery in February 2021.  There is no mention of 
any treatment for the elbows regarding this surgery.  (JE 1, p. 42) 
Claimant had follow-up care for his left shoulder in March of 2021, May of 2021, 
and July of 2021.  There is no mention of any treatment or care for claimant’s upper 
extremities or elbows in these records.  (JE 1, pp. 44, 46, 48) 
As noted in the Findings of Fact, claimant has also had numerous fitness for duty 
exams during that period.  All exams found claimant’s elbows had normal range of 
motion and normal strength.  (JE 6, pp. 166, 180-186, 197) 
Claimant testified he had no treatment after his August 2011 event for his arms.  
He testified he has not had any diagnosis by any treating physicians for his elbows or 
arms.  (Tr., pp. 47-48)

In brief, claimant has had numerous exams for his shoulders between 2017 and 
2021.  Not one of those records indicates that claimant had any loss of strength or 
range of motion in his elbows.   
Dr. Taylor evaluated claimant once for an IME.  He found that claimant had 
permanent impairment in both elbows due to an alleged loss of range of motion.  (Ex. 7, 
pp. 74-76) 
Dr. Taylor’s opinions regarding permanent impairment to the bilateral elbows is 
problematic for several reasons.  As detailed above, claimant received treatment for his 
bilateral shoulders from 2017 through 2021.  There is no evidence in any of these 
records that claimant had any problems with his elbows or arms during this period of 
time.  Given this record, Dr. Taylor offers no rationale as to why claimant has a work-
related injury to his elbows given the medical records that do not refer to any work-
related elbow injury. 
Second, Dr. Taylor does not give a causation opinion that claimant’s alleged 
2011 bilateral shoulder injury caused permanent impairment to claimant’s elbows.   
Third, Dr. Taylor also does not indicate that claimant’s elbow condition preceded 
claimant’s 2021 leg injury.   
Given these concerns, Dr. Taylor’s opinions regarding claimant having a 
qualifying first injury for the bilateral elbows is found not convincing.  
The law regarding the Fund also indicates that claimant’s elbow condition is not a 
qualifying injury for the purposes of Fund benefits. 
In Second Injury Fund of Iowa v. Strable, 14 N.W.3d 742, 749-750 (Iowa 2024), 
the Iowa Supreme Court considered the Fund’s liability when a worker with a pre-
existing disability had a subsequent injury causing both enumerated and non-
enumerated injuries. The Court indicated that the Fund is liable under Iowa Code 
section 85.64 if the first and second qualifying injuries caused a compensable injury to 
an enumerated member, regardless of whether they also caused other non-enumerated 
or unscheduled injuries.   
The court in Strable reached its decision, in part, based on the law in the Second 
Injury Fund v. Nelson, 544 N.W.2d 258 (Iowa 1995)  The court in Strable held, in part,  
Thus, in Nelson, we held that a shoulder injury was not a qualifying second 
injury even though it “affect[ed]” the employee’s arm.  The commissioner’s 
contrary conclusion was “inconsistent with the clear language of section 
85.64,” where that section explicitly listed an arm but not a shoulder.  (“[T]he 
word ‘arm’ as used in section 85.64 simply does not include the shoulder.”).  
Nelson simply means that the qualifying injury must result in the loss of use 
of an enumerated member – it is not enough that the injury merely affected,

without disabling, an enumerated member.  Strable meets that requirement 
here.   
Strable, 14 N.W.3d at 749 (citations omitted) 
Claimant’s claim of a qualifying first injury, a bilateral shoulder injury, allegedly 
occurred in 2011.  Claimant did not have a bilateral elbow injury in 2011.  Accepting 
claimant’s argument that his bilateral shoulder injury resulted in an elbow condition, 
claimant still fails to qualify for Fund benefits.  This is because his alleged bilateral 
shoulder injury in 2011 did not result in any elbow condition, but allegedly merely 
affected his elbow condition. 
Claimant alleges bilateral shoulder injuries in 2011 as an arguable first injury.  
Claimant’s shoulders are non-enumerated members for the purposes of Fund benefits.  
Claimant had treatment for his bilateral shoulders from 2017 through 2021.  There is no 
evidence in those records of an elbow condition.  Dr. Taylor’s opinions regarding 
claimant’s elbow injuries are found not convincing.  Case law under Strable also 
indicates that claimant does not have a qualifying first injury for the purposes of Fund 
benefits.  Based on this record, claimant has failed to carry his burden of proof that his 
bilateral shoulder injuries are a qualifying injury for the purposes of Fund benefits.  
As claimant failed to carry his burden of proof that his alleged bilateral elbow 
injuries are a qualifying first injury for the purposes of Fund benefits, all other issues are 
moot.   
ORDER 
THEREFORE IT IS ORDERED: 
That claimant shall take nothing in the way of Fund benefits. 
Both parties shall pay their own costs.
