STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A.  The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Denise McCoy.  Claimant 
appeared telephonically and through her attorney, Richard Schmidt.  Defendants 
appeared through their attorney, Gregory Taylor.   
The alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on September 8, 2020.  
The proceedings were digitally recorded.  That recording constitutes the official record 
of this proceeding.  Pursuant to the Commissioner’s February 16, 2015 Order, the 
undersigned has been delegated authority to issue a final agency decision in this 
alternate medical care proceeding.  Therefore, this ruling is designated final agency 
action and any appeal of the decision would be to the Iowa District Court pursuant to 
Iowa Code section 17A. 
The record consists of claimant’s exhibits 1 and 2, and defendants’ exhibits A 
and B.  Claimant provided testimony.  No other witnessed were called.  Counsel offered 
oral arguments to support their positions. 
ISSUE 
The issue presented for resolution is whether the claimant is entitled to alternate 
medical care consisting of authorization and scheduling of a right proximal row 
carpectomy surgery with Tobias Mann, M.D., who is the authorized treating physician.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned having considered all of the testimony and evidence in the 
record finds: 
Claimant is a 64-year old woman who is currently employed at a Casey’s 
General Store in Davenport, Iowa.  She works in the bakery/kitchen area, preparing 
pizza and donuts, among other items.  Claimant sustained an injury to her right upper 
extremity on April 20, 2019, while working for defendant employer.  Defendants 
admitted liability for this injury and the current right wrist/hand condition for which 
claimant seeks alternate medical care. 
After the work injury, defendants authorized medical treatment with Cheryl 
Benson, PA-C, Rick Garrels, M.D., and Tobias Mann, M.D. (Claimant’s Testimony; See 
also Defendants’ Addendum to Answer)  Dr. Mann performed right wrist surgery, 
described as “right wrist arthroscopy with TFCC and scapholunate ligament 
debridement” on February 20, 2020. (Exhibit 2, p. 3)   
Claimant continued to see Dr. Mann in follow up after surgery.  On June 26, 
2020, she reported ongoing pain over the dorsal ulnar aspect of her wrist. (Exhibit 2, p. 
3)  Dr. Mann recommended a wrist injection, which was performed that day, and 
reduced claimant’s work restrictions to 10 pounds as needed.  He noted that if claimant 
was not any better by her next visit “we will probably have to consider a proximal row 
carpectomy.”  Id. 
Claimant returned to Dr. Mann on July 27, 2020. (Ex. 2, p. 1-2)  She reported that 
the injection she received at the last visit had helped for three weeks, and that she had 
been wearing her wrist brace.  (Ex. 2, p. 1)  However, she was also having pain in the 
dorsal wrist radiating up her forearm. Id.  Following his physical examination, Dr. Mann 
discussed claimant’s options, which included “getting a second opinion, a smaller 
surgery to remove some of the nerves, versus proceeding with proximal row 
carpectomy.” Id.  After consideration, claimant decided that she wanted to proceed with 
the proximal row carpectomy surgery.  Dr. Mann then further explained that he could not 
guarantee that her radiating pain would resolve with surgery, and that “she will lose 
significant motion and there is a risk of no relief of her symptoms and she could still 
have pain.”  He also explained that the proximal row carpectomy is a larger surgery that 
involves removing bones from the wrist.  Finally, he noted that since claimant had good 
relief from the injection, he hoped the surgery would resolve her symptoms. Id. 
After receiving Dr. Mann’s July 27, 2020 record, defendants arranged for a 
second opinion appointment to take place with Joseph Buckwalter, M.D., PhD. (Ex. A, 
p. 1; See also Defendants’ Addendum to Answer)  The appointment was scheduled to 
take place on September 1, 2020.  Id.  On August 24, 2020, claimant, through her 
attorney, expressed her dissatisfaction with the care being offered, and asked for the 
surgery Dr. Mann recommended to be authorized and scheduled immediately. (Ex. 1)  
Claimant’s petition for alternate medical care was filed on August 25, 2020.  Defendants

move on with her life.  Claimant acknowledged that Dr. Mann explained the risk of 
losing motion in her wrist, and the risk that the surgery might not improve her pain.  She 
agreed that those are valid reasons to seek a second opinion.  Claimant also agreed 
that if Dr. Buckwalter has other viable treatment options to offer, she would want to 
know.  However, claimant again reiterated that she would prefer to have the surgery 
authorized and scheduled now, in order to prevent further delay. 
REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Iowa Code section 85.27(4) provides, in relevant part: 
For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish 
reasonable services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has 
the right to choose the care. . . .  The treatment must be offered promptly 
and be reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience 
to the employee.  If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the 
care offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such 
dissatisfaction to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the 
employer and the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited 
to treat the injury.  If the employer and employee cannot agree on such 
alternate care, the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable 
proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
Defendants’ “obligation under the statute is confined to reasonable care for the 
diagnosis and treatment of work-related injuries.” Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 
N.W.2d 122, 124 (Iowa 1995) (emphasis in original).  In other words, the “obligation 
under the statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.”  Id.

Similarly, an application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained 
because claimant is dissatisfied with the care she has been receiving.  Mere 
dissatisfaction with the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for 
alternate medical care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered 
promptly, was not reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly 
inconvenient for the claimant.  See Iowa Code § 85.27(4).  Thus, by challenging the 
employer’s choice of treatment and seeking alternate care, claimant assumes the 
burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See Iowa R. App. P 14(f)(5); 
Long, 528 N.W.2d at 124.   
The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except where the 
employer has denied liability for the injury. Section 85.27; Holbert v. Townsend 
Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial Commissioner, 78 
(Review-Reopening 1975). 
An employer's right to select the provider of medical treatment to an injured 
worker does not include the right to determine how an injured worker should be 
diagnosed, evaluated, treated, or other matters of professional medical 
judgment. Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 866389 (Declaratory Ruling, May 19, 
1988). 
Reasonable care includes care necessary to diagnose the condition and 
defendants are not entitled to interfere with the medical judgment of its own treating 
physician. Pote v. Mickow Corp,, File No. 694639 (Review-Reopening June 17, 1986).  
Ultimately, determining whether care is reasonable under the statute is a question of 
fact.  Long, 528 N.W.2d at 123. 
In this case the claimant has not proven that the care offered by defendants is 
unreasonable.  Defendants acted promptly in scheduling the second opinion 
appointment with Dr. Buckwalter after receiving Dr. Mann’s July 27, 2020 medical 
record.  The appointment was initially scheduled to take place on September 1, 2020, 
about 5 weeks after the appointment with Dr. Mann.  The fact that it had to be 
rescheduled to September 29 is unfortunate, but is not due to any action or inaction of 
defendants.  There is no claim that an appointment with Dr. Buckwalter is unduly 
inconvenient to claimant, as she testified that she had planned to attend on September 
1, and still plans to attend on September 29.   
Finally, I find that under the circumstances of this case, getting a second opinion 
prior to surgery is not unreasonable.  Dr. Mann noted that a second opinion was one of 
the three options available to claimant. (Ex. 2, p. 1)  Dr. Mann has also outlined the risks 
inherent with the proximal row carpectomy, including loss of motion in the wrist and the 
potential that it will not resolve claimant’s pain.  He described it as a “bigger surgery that 
involves removing bones from the wrist.” Id.  Defendants have not, at this time, denied 
the surgery with Dr. Mann, but simply seek to have a second opinion performed by 
another qualified orthopedic surgeon before authorizing it.  Claimant testified that she

wants the surgery so she can get on with her life, but also agreed that if Dr. Buckwalter 
has other viable treatment options to offer, she would want to know.   
While both parties have presented valid arguments in this case, I find that 
defendants have offered reasonable care to date.  Seeking a second opinion, given the 
nature of the surgery and inherent risks involved, is reasonable, and was scheduled 
promptly.  Treatment with both Dr. Garrels and Dr. Mann remains authorized pending 
the second opinion appointment.  In the event the surgery with Dr. Mann is not 
authorized following the second opinion with Dr. Buckwalter, claimant might be entitled 
to an award of alternate care at a later date.  However, at this time, based on the facts 
presented, claimant’s petition for alternate medical care is denied. 
ORDER 
THEREFORE, IT IS ORDERED: 
The claimant's petition for alternate medical care is denied.
