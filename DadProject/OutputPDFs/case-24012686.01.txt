STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A.  The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Luis Arreola-Villa.  
Claimant filed his original notice and petition for alternate medical care on February 18, 
2025 and requested a telephonic hearing.  Pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.27(4), this 
case is required to be heard and decided within ten business days of the filing of the 
petition.   
 
The alternate medical care claim came on for telephonic hearing on February 27, 
2025.  The proceedings were digitally recorded.  That recording constitutes the official 
record of this proceeding.  Pursuant to the Iowa Workers’ Compensation 
Commissioner’s July 21, 2023 Order, the undersigned has been delegated authority to 
issue a final agency decision in this alternate medical care proceeding.  Therefore, this 
ruling is designated final agency action and any appeal of the decision would be to the 
Iowa District Court pursuant to Iowa Code section 17A. 
 
Claimant’s original notice and petition for alternate medical care contains a proof 
of service, indicating that defendants were served via certified mail, return receipt on  
February 18, 2025.  Claimant’s counsel certified that the individual signing the proof of 
service is his legal assistant.  Claimant’s proof of service is accepted and it is found that 
defendants were properly served via Iowa Code section 17A.12(1) and 876 IAC 4.48(5).

ISSUE 
 
The issue presented for resolution is whether the claimant is entitled to alternate 
medical care, including an order authorizing further treatment for his low back injury. 
 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
The undersigned having considered claimant’s undisputed petition, as well as all 
the evidence in the record finds: 
 
Claimant sustained a back injury arising out of and in the course of his 
employment with the employer on November 13, 2024.  That injury occurred in North 
Liberty, Iowa, and has caused the need for treatment of claimant’s back.  At this time, 
defendants are not authorizing medical care that is reasonable and necessary for the 
treatment of claimant’s back.  (Claimant’s Petition) 
 
Claimant expressed dissatisfaction with the defendants’ lack of authorized 
medical care in written correspondence on February 11, 2025.  (Claimant’s Exhibit 1)  
Claimant’s dissatisfaction is justified and I find that defendants are not currently 
authorizing reasonable and necessary medical care for claimant’s back.  Claimant 
specifically seeks authorization for further treatment of his back with a physician 
selected by defendants.  Claimant’s counsel noted that claimant was previously treated 
at the University of Iowa Hospitals and Clinics, but no evidence was introduced to

REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Section 85.27.  Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening October 16, 1975). 
 
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See Iowa 
R. App. P 14(f)(5); Bell Bros. Heating and Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d 193, 
209 (Iowa 2010); Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  Determining 
what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.  Long v. Roberts Dairy 
Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  The employer’s obligation turns on the question of 
reasonable necessity, not desirability.  Id.; Harned v. Farmland Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 
98 (Iowa 1983).   
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).   
 
At this time, defendants are not offering any medical care for claimant’s low back 
injury.  I conclude that claimant has clearly established that the treatment he requests, 
specifically further treatment of his back injury, is reasonable.  Having found that 
defendants offered no alternative treatment, I conclude that claimant has established 
entitlement to an order directing defendants to authorize a physician for further care of 
claimant’s November 13, 2024, back injury. 
 
ORDER 
 
THEREFORE, IT IS ORDERED: 
 
The claimant's petition for alternate medical care is granted.
