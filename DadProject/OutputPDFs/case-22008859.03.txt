STATEMENT OF THE CASE   
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A.  The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Jessica Padilla. 
Claimant appeared personally and through her attorney, Mark Chipokas.  Defendant, 
Mercy Medical Center, self-insured employer, appeared through their attorney, Thomas 
Wolle.   
The alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on November 5, 2024.  
The proceedings were digitally recorded.  That recording constitutes the official record 
of this proceeding.  Pursuant to the Commissioner’s July 21, 2023 Order, the 
undersigned has been delegated authority to issue a final agency decision in this 
alternate medical care proceeding.  Therefore, this ruling is designated final agency 
action and any appeal of the decision would be to the Iowa District Court pursuant to 
Iowa Code section 17A.   
The evidentiary record consists of Claimant’s Exhibits 1-4 and Defendant’s 
Exhibit A.  The exhibits in file numbers 22008859.03 and 24700113.03 are identical.  
Claimant testified live during the telephonic hearing.  Prior to the hearing, the defendant 
filed answers which accepted liability for the July 28, 2022 and December 4, 2022 work 
injuries and for the conditions that for which claimant is seeking treatment.            
ISSUE   
The issue for resolution is whether the claimant is entitled to alternate medical 
care. 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
Defendant admits that claimant, Jessica Padilla, sustained an injury on July 28, 
2022 and December 4, 2022, which arose out of and in the course of her employment.

REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW   
Under Iowa law, the employer is required to provide care to an injured employee 
and is permitted to choose the care.  Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 
562 N.W.2d 433, 435 (Iowa 1997). 
[T]he employer is obliged to furnish reasonable services and supplies to 
treat an injured employee, and has the right to choose the care.  The 
treatment must be offered promptly and be reasonably suited to treat the 
injury without undue inconvenience to the employee.  If the employee has 
reason to be dissatisfied with the care offered, the employee should 
communicate the basis of such dissatisfaction to the employer, in writing if 
requested, following which the employer and the employee may agree to 
alternate care reasonably suited to treat the injury.  If the employer and 
employee cannot agree on such alternate care, the commissioner may, 
upon application and reasonable proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and 
order other care.  Id. 
 
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  Long v. 
Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122, 123 (Iowa 1995).  Determining what care is 
reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.  Id.  The employer’s obligation turns 
on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.  Id. at 124; Harned v. Farmland 
Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98, 101 (Iowa 1983).  In Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d 
at 437, the court approvingly quoted Bowles v. Los Lunas Schools, 109 N.M. 100, 106, 
781 P.2d 1178, 1184 (N.M. Ct. App. 1989): 
[T]he words “reasonable” and “adequate” appear to describe the same 
standard. 
[The New Mexico rule] requires the employer to provide a certain standard 
of care and excuses the employer from any obligation to provide other 
services only if that standard is met.  We construe the terms "reasonable” 
and “adequate” as describing care that is both appropriate to the injury and 
sufficient to bring the worker to maximum recovery. 
The commissioner is justified in ordering alternate care when employer-authorized care 
has not been effective, and evidence shows that such care is “inferior or less extensive” 
care than other available care requested by the employee.  Long, 528 N.W.2d at 124; 
Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 437. 
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance, and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Section 85.27.  Holbert v.

Reasonable care includes care necessary to diagnose the condition and 
defendants are not entitled to interfere with the medical judgment of its own treating 
physician.  Pote v. Mickow Corp., File No. 694639 (Review-Reopening Decision June 
17, 1986).  
Claimant is seeking pain management treatment in Cedar Rapids.  Defendant is 
willing to provide pain management treatment in Iowa City, but no appointment date has 
been provided.  On September 11, 2023, Dr. Struthers recommended seeing a pain 
management doctor, possibly Dr. Mathew.  (Cl. Ex. 1, p. 1)  Although defendant is 
willing to provide the recommended treatment, the treatment they are willing to provide 
is not being offered in a prompt manner.  It has been just shy of two years since the 
treatment recommendation has been made, yet defendant still has not offered an 
appointment date.  I find claimant has demonstrated that there are at least two qualified 
doctors in Cedar Rapids who can see her in a more timely fashion than what the 
defendant is offering.  I find the pain management treatment offered by defendant is not 
reasonable.  As such, I find claimant’s petition for alternate medical care as it relates to 
Dr. Struther’s recommendation to see a pain management doctor is granted.  Therefore, 
claimant may seek reasonable and necessary pain management treatment with a 
doctor of her choosing at the expense of the defendant.     
Claimant is also seeking psychotherapy treatment and EDMR therapy.  Based on 
the above findings of fact, I conclude that given the current circumstances, the 
treatment that defendant is currently offering is reasonable.  However, defendant shall 
continue to diligently work to secure the recommended treatment for Jessica as soon as 
possible.  As noted above, in the meantime, if claimant were able to demonstrate that 
there are appropriate providers who could provide the recommended treatment in a 
more timely fashion, then this issue could be revisited.   
ORDER   
THEREFORE IT IS ORDERED:   
Claimant’s petition for alternate medical care is granted in part and denied in 
part.   
Claimant’s petition for alternate medical care as it relates to Dr. Struther’s 
recommendation to see a pain management doctor is granted.   
The remainder of claimant’s petition for alternate medical care is denied.
