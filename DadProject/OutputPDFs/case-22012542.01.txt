STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Luis Cisneros, claimant, filed a petition in arbitration and seeks workers’ 
compensation benefits from defendants, Uncommon Ground of the Quad Cities, Inc. 
(“Uncommon Ground”), as the employer, and Brickstreet Mutual Insurance Company, 
as the insurance carrier.  The case proceeded to an arbitration hearing on June 21, 
2024 via Zoom conference call.   
The parties filed a hearing report prior to the arbitration hearing.  On the hearing 
report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of those stipulations were 
accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration decision and no factual or 
legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised or discussed in this 
decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations. 
The evidentiary record includes Joint Exhibits 1 through 6, Claimant’s Exhibits 1 
through 3, and Employer’s Exhibits A through D.  All exhibits were received into the 
evidentiary record without objection.   Claimant testified on his own behalf.  No other 
witnesses testified live. 
The evidentiary record closed at the conclusion of the arbitration hearing. 
However, the parties requested the opportunity to file post-hearing briefs.  Their request 
was granted, and the parties filed their briefs on August 5, 2024.  The case was 
considered fully submitted to the undersigned on that date.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned, having considered all of the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
Luis Cisneros, claimant, is a 52-year-old gentleman who lives in Moline, Illinois. 
Mr. Cisneros is a native of Mexico, where he obtained 8 years of education before 
moving to the United States in 2000.  Claimant speaks very little English. 
Prior to beginning his employment at Uncommon Ground, claimant worked in the 
construction industry and performed factory work.  On April 23, 2019, Mr. Cisneros 
began his employment at Uncommon Ground.  He started his employment as a 
landscaper.  In that position, claimant was required to lift a fairly significant amount, 
including 60-70 pound cement blocks. 
On October 10, 2022, claimant sustained an admitted low back injury while 
performing his duties as a landscaper.  Specifically, he testified that about an hour after 
he arrived at his jobsite, his supervisor instructed him to begin moving cement blocks.  
While moving those large cement blocks, claimant experienced low back pain.   
Claimant reported the injury to his supervisor.  Claimant was unable to complete 
his shift, and his supervisor drove him home that day.  Claimant testified that he 
required assistance getting into and out of the truck after his injury, and that he 
remained home for three days after the injury due to symptoms in his low back. 
The employer admitted liability for the low back injury and directed claimant for 
medical care.  Unfortunately, conservative care did not prove beneficial and claimant 
was referred to an orthopaedic surgeon, Michael R. Berry, M.D.  Dr. Berry attempted 
further conservative care but ultimately took claimant to surgery on February 14, 2023.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The primary dispute in this case is the extent of claimant’s entitlement to 
permanent disability benefits.  The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not 
established has the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the 
evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e). 
All parties stipulate claimant sustained a low back injury, which required surgical 
intervention.  The parties also stipulated in the hearing report that the injury caused 
permanent disability.  However, there is a dispute about the extent of claimant’s 
permanent disability. 
Mr. Cisneros asserts he is permanently and totally disabled as a result of the 
October 10, 2022 injury.  Total disability does not mean a state of absolute 
helplessness.  Permanent total disability occurs where the injury wholly disables the 
employee from performing work that the employee's experience, training, education, 
intelligence, and physical capacities would otherwise permit the employee to perform.  
See McSpadden v. Big Ben Coal Co., 288 N.W.2d 181, 192 (Iowa 1980); Diederich v. 
Tri-City Ry. Co. of Iowa, 219 Iowa 587, 258 N.W. 899 (1935). 
A finding that claimant could perform some work despite claimant's physical and 
educational limitations does not foreclose a finding of permanent total disability, 
however.  See Chamberlin v. Ralston Purina, File No. 661698 (App. October 1987); 
Eastman v. Westway Trading Corp., II Iowa Industrial Commissioner Report 134 (App. 
May 1982). 
I found that claimant remains capable of performing work in the heavy work 
category pursuant to the FCE recommendations, as adopted by Dr. Berry and 
concurred by Dr. Ridenour.  Given that claimant continues to remain employed by the 
employer, he is likely physically capable of returning to his former work as a landscaper, 
as well as performing work in a factory.  Given these findings, I also found that claimant 
remains capable of performing work within his experience, training, education, 
intelligence, and physical capabilities within the general labor market in claimant’s 
residential area.  Therefore, I conclude claimant failed to prove he is permanently and 
totally disabled. 
Nevertheless, the parties appropriately stipulated that claimant sustained 
permanent disability in some amount.  Claimant’s injury is a low back injury, which is an 
unscheduled injury that is compensated pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v).  
The parties stipulated that the injury should be compensated with industrial disability 
benefits. 
Since claimant has an impairment to the body as a whole, an industrial disability 
has been sustained.  Industrial disability was defined in Diederich v. Tri-City Ry. Co. of

Iowa, 219 Iowa 587, 591, 258 N.W. 899, 902 (1935) as follows:  "It is therefore plain that 
the Legislature intended the term 'disability' to mean 'industrial disability' or loss of 
earning capacity and not a mere 'functional disability' to be computed in terms of 
percentages of the total physical and mental ability of a normal man." 
Functional impairment is an element to be considered in determining industrial 
disability which is the reduction of earning capacity, but consideration must also be 
given to the injured employee's age, education, qualifications, experience, motivation, 
loss of earnings, severity and situs of the injury, work restrictions, inability to engage in 
employment for which the employee is fitted and the employer's offer of work or failure 
to so offer.  McSpadden v. Big Ben Coal Co., 288 N.W.2d 181, 192 (Iowa 1980); Olson 
v. Goodyear Service Stores, 255 Iowa 1112, 1120, 125 N.W.2d 251, 256 (1963); Barton 
v. Nevada Poultry Co., 253 Iowa 285, 110 N.W.2d 660, 663 (1961). 
Compensation for permanent partial disability shall begin when it is medically 
indicated that maximum medical improvement from the injury has been reached and 
that the extent of loss or percentage of permanent impairment can be determined by the 
use of the guides to the evaluation of permanent impairment, published by the American 
Medical Association, as adopted by the Commissioner. Iowa Code section 85.34(2)  
Pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v), industrial disability is also determined by 
considering “the number of years in the future it was reasonably anticipated that the 
employee would work at the time of the injury.” 
Having considered the situs and severity of claimant’s injury, his age, permanent 
restrictions, permanent impairment, education, ability to retrain, motivation, proximity to 
retirement, as well as all other factors of industrial disability outlined by the Iowa 
Supreme Court, I found claimant proved a 25 percent loss of future earning capacity as 
a result of the October 10, 2022 work injury.  This is equivalent to a 25 percent industrial 
disability.  Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v).   
Industrial disability is compensated based on a 500-week basis.  Twenty-five 
percent of 500 weeks is 125 weeks.  Therefore, I conclude claimant proved entitlement 
to 125 weeks of permanent partial disability benefits.  Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v). 
Claimant also asserts a claim for penalty benefits for unreasonable delay in 
payment of permanent disability benefits.  In Christensen v. Snap-on Tools Corp., 554 
N.W.2d 254 (Iowa 1996) and Robbennolt v. Snap-on Tools Corp, 555 N.W.2d 229 (Iowa 
1996) the supreme court said: 
Based on the plain language of section 86.13,1 we hold an employee 
is entitled to penalty benefits if there has been a delay in payment unless 
the employer proves a reasonable cause or excuse.  [Iowa Code § 86.13 
(2015).]  A reasonable cause or excuse exists if either (1) the delay was 
necessary for the insurer to investigate the claim or (2) the employer had a

reasonable basis to contest the employee’s entitlement to benefits.  A 
“reasonable basis” for denial of the claim exists if the claim is “fairly 
debatable.”  Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 260. 
The supreme court has stated: 
(1) If the employer has a reason for the delay and conveys that 
reason to the employee contemporaneously with the beginning of the delay, 
no penalty will be imposed if the reason is of such character that a 
reasonable fact finder could conclude that it is a "reasonable or probable 
cause or excuse" under Iowa Code section 86.13 [2015].  In that case, we 
will defer to the decision of the commissioner.  See Christensen, 554 
N.W.2d at 260 (substantial evidence found to support commissioner’s 
finding of legitimate reason for delay pending receipt of medical report); 
Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 236. 
(2) If no reason is given for the delay or if the “reason” is not one that 
a reasonable fact finder could accept, we will hold that no such cause or 
excuse exists and remand to the commissioner for the sole purpose of 
assessing penalties under section 86.13.  See Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 
261. 
(3) Reasonable causes or excuses include (a) a delay for the 
employer to investigate the claim, Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 260; 
Kiesecker v. Webster City Custom Meats, Inc., 528 N.W.2d at 109, 111 
(Iowa 1995); or (b) the employer had a reasonable basis to contest the 
claimthe “fairly debatable” basis for delay.  See Christensen, 554 N.W.2d 
at 260 (holding two-month delay to obtain employer’s own medical report 
reasonable under the circumstances).  
(4) For the purpose of applying section 86.13, the benefits that are 
underpaid as well as late-paid benefits are subject to penalties, unless the 
employer establishes reasonable and probable cause or excuse. [Iowa 
Code § 86.13 (2015);] Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 237 (underpayment 
resulting from application of wrong wage base; in absence of excuse, 
commissioner required to apply penalty). 
If we were to construe [Iowa Code § 86.13 (2015)] to 
permit the avoidance of penalty if any amount of 
compensation benefits are paid, the purpose of the penalty 
statute would be frustrated.  For these reasons, we conclude 
section 86.13 is applicable when payment of compensation is 
not timely . . . or when the full amount of compensation is not 
paid.  Id. 
(5) For purposes of determining whether there has been a delay, 
payments are “made” when (a) the check addressed to a claimant is mailed 
(Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 236; Kiesecker, 528 N.W.2d at 112), or (b) the

check is delivered personally to the claimant by the employer or its workers’ 
compensation insurer.  Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 235.   
(6) In determining the amount of the penalty, the commissioner is to 
consider factors such as the length of the delay, the number of delays, the 
information available to the employer regarding the employee’s injury and 
wages, and the employer’s past record of penalties.  Robbennolt, 555 
N.W.2d at 238. 
(7) An employer’s bare assertion that a claim is “fairly debatable” 
does not make it so.  A fair reading of Christensen and Robbennolt, makes 
it clear that the employer must assert facts upon which the commissioner 
could reasonably find that the claim was “fairly debatable.”  See 
Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 260. 
Meyers v. Holiday Express Corp., 557 N.W.2d 502, 504-505 (Iowa 1996).   
Weekly compensation payments are due at the end of the compensation week.  
Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 235. 
Penalty is not imposed for delayed interest payments.  Davidson v. Bruce, 594 
N.W.2d 833, 840 (Iowa Ct. App. 1999); Schadendorf v. Snap-On Tools Corp., 757 
N.W.2d 330, 338 (Iowa 2008).   
When an employee’s claim for benefits is fairly debatable based on a good faith 
dispute over the employee’s factual or legal entitlement to benefits, an award of penalty 
benefits is not appropriate under the statute.  Gilbert v. USF Holland, Inc., 637 N.W.2d 
194, 199 (Iowa 2001).  Whether the issue was fairly debatable turns on whether there 
was a disputed factual dispute that, if resolved in favor of the employer, would have 
supported the employer's denial of compensability.  Id. 
An employer may not unilaterally decide to deny, delay, or terminate benefits 
without adhering to section 10A.315. Iowa Code § 10A.315 (2023).  Section 
10A.315(4)(c) creates a mandatory timeline for the employer to follow, to show the 
employer met its burden of a “reasonable or probable cause or excuse” for denial, 
delay, or termination of benefits.  Iowa Code § 10A.315(4)(c)(1)-(3).2   First, the 
employer’s excuse must be preceded by a reasonable investigation and evaluation into 
whether the benefits were owed.  Iowa Code § 10A.315(4)(c)(1); Baker v. 
Bridgestone/Firestone & Old Republic Inc., 872 N.W.2d 672, 678 (Iowa 2015).  Second, 
the results of the reasonable investigation were “the actual basis . . . 
contemporaneously” relied on by the employer for the denial, delay or termination of the 
benefits.  Iowa Code § 10A.315(4)(c)(2).   Third, the employer “contemporaneously 
conveyed the basis for the denial, delay in payment or termination of benefits to the 
employee at the time of their denial, delay or termination.”  Iowa Code §

10A.315(4)(c)(3).  An employer cannot unilaterally decide to terminate benefits without 
adhering to section 10A.315.   Iowa Code § 10A.315.  
If weekly compensation benefits are not fully paid when due, section 10A.315(4) 
requires that additional benefits be awarded unless the employer shows reasonable 
cause or excuse for the delay or denial.  Iowa Code § 10A.315(4) (2023); Robbennolt v. 
Snap-on Tools Corp., 555 N.W.2d 229, 236-37 (Iowa 1996).  
“Benefits may be delayed, without penalty, during such time as an employer’s 
liability is “fairly debatable”. Kiesecker v. Webster City Custom Meats, Inc., 528 N.W.2d 
109, 111 (Iowa 1995).  A delay attributable to the time required to perform a reasonable 
investigation is not unreasonable.  Id.  
It also is not unreasonable to deny a claim when a good faith issue of law or fact 
makes the employer’s liability fairly debatable.  Rodda v. Vermeer Mfg., 734 N.W.2d 
480, 483 (Iowa 2007).  An issue of law is fairly debatable if viable arguments exist in 
favor of each party.  Covia v. Robinson, 507 N.W.2d 411, 412 (Iowa 1993).  An issue of 
fact is fairly debatable if substantial evidence exists which would support a finding 
favorable to the employer.  Gilbert v. USF Holland, Inc., 637 N.W.2d 194, 199 (Iowa 
2001).  
“An employer’s bare assertion that a claim is ‘fairly debatable’ does not make it 
so.”  Meyers v. Holiday Express Corp., 557 N.W.2d 502, 505 (Iowa 1996).  The 
employer must assert facts upon which the commissioner could reasonably find that the 
claim was “fairly debatable.”  Id.   
If the employer fails to show reasonable cause or excuse for the delay or denial, 
the commissioner shall impose a penalty in an amount up to 50 percent of the amount 
unreasonably delayed or denied.  Christensen v. Snap-on Tools Corp., 554 N.W.2d 
254, 259 (Iowa 1996).  The factors to be considered in determining the amount of the 
penalty include the length of the delay, the number of delays, the information available 
to the employer and the employer’s past record of penalties.  Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d 
at 238. 
The parties stipulate in the hearing report that permanent disability benefits 
commence on August 21, 2023.  This is the point in time where claimant has achieved 
maximum medical improvement and the extent of permanent impairment can be 
determined.  Iowa Code section 85.34(2).  Defendants did not commence payment of 
permanent disability benefits at this point in time and did not commence permanent 
disability benefits until November 2023.  Therefore, claimant proved a delay in payment 
of benefits. 
Once claimant proved the delay in payment of benefits, the burden shifted to 
defendants to come forward with evidence to establish their delay was reasonable.  
Iowa Code section 10A.315.  Defendants did not introduce evidence at hearing to 
demonstrate a reasonable investigation occurred or to establish the basis for their delay 
in payment of permanent disability.  Defendants also failed to introduce evidence at the 
time of hearing to demonstrate they contemporaneously conveyed the basis of their 

CISNEROS V. UNCOMMON GROUND OF THE QUAD CITIES, INC. 
Page 10 
 
 
delay to claimant.  Under this set of facts, a penalty in some amount is mandatory.  Iowa 
Code section 10A.315. 
The purpose of Iowa Code section 10A.315 is both punishment for unreasonable 
conduct but also deterrence for future cases.  Id. at 237.  In this regard, the Commission 
is given discretion to determine the amount of the penalty imposed with a maximum 
penalty of 50 percent of the amount of the delayed, or denied, benefits.  Christensen v. 
Snap-On Tools Corp., 554 N.W.2d 254, 261 (Iowa 1996).   
In exercising its discretion, the agency must consider factors such as the length 
of the delays, the number of delays, the information available to the employer regarding 
the employee’s injury and wages, and the employer’s past record of penalties.  Meyers 
v. Holiday Express Corp., 557 N.W.2d 502, 505 (Iowa 1996).   
The delay was not egregious in this case.  Having considered the relevant factors 
and the purposes of the penalty statute, I conclude that a section 10A.315 penalty in the 
amount of $500.00 is appropriate in this case.  Such an amount is appropriate to punish 
the employer for delays in payment of benefits under these facts and should serve as a 
deterrent against future conduct.  However, the facts of this case are not of such an 
egregious nature that an additional penalty is warranted.  
Finally, claimant seeks assessment of his filing fee as costs.  Costs are assessed 
at the discretion of the agency.  Iowa Code section 10A.328.  Given that claimant 
received an award of benefits in this case, I conclude it is reasonable to assess 
claimant’s filing fee as a cost in this proceeding.  876 IAC 4.33(7).
