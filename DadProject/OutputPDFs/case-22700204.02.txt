STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
 
Claimant Lester Sanchez Cabada filed petitions in arbitration seeking worker’s 
compensation benefits against Tyson Fresh Meats, Inc., self-insured employer, for 
stipulated work injury dates of June 26, 2020 (File No. 21701096.02) and October 20, 
2020 (File No. 22700204.02). The case came before the undersigned for an arbitration 
hearing on August 20, 2024. The case proceeded to a live video hearing via Zoom, with 
all participants appearing remotely. The hearing proceeded without significant 
difficulties. 
 
The parties filed a hearing report prior to the commencement of the hearing. On 
the hearing report, the parties entered into numerous stipulations. Those stipulations 
were accepted and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be 
made or discussed. The parties are now bound by their stipulations. 
 
The evidentiary record includes Joint Exhibits 1 through 8, Claimant’s Exhibits 1 
through 9, and Defendants’ Exhibits A through N.  
 
Claimant testified on his own behalf, through sworn interpreter, Alina Salvat. The 
evidentiary record closed at the conclusion of the evidentiary hearing on August 20, 
2024. The parties submitted post-hearing briefs on October 14, 2024, and the case was 
considered fully submitted on that date.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
The undersigned, having considered all of the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
 
Claimant’s testimony was consistent as compared to the evidentiary record, and 
his demeanor at the time of hearing gave the undersigned no reason to doubt his 
veracity. Claimant is found credible.

14) He was married with two young children. Claimant attended school through ninth 
grade in Cuba, after which he attended the Polytechnical school in Cuba. (Tr., pp. 15-
16) At the polytechnical school, his classes essentially involved machine operation. (Tr.,

impairment. As such, Dr. Bansal’s 25 percent impairment rating is accepted as the most 
accurate with respect to claimant’s cervical spine.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
The first issue to determine is the extent of permanent partial disability. The 
parties stipulated that October 20, 2020 injury caused permanent disability, and that it is 
an industrial disability. Claimant conceded that the June 26, 2020 injury did not result in 
permanent disability. As such, the issue is the extent of industrial disability to which 
claimant is entitled due to the October 20, 2020 injury. 
 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence. Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the 
evidence that the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is 
based. A cause is proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it 
need not be the only cause. A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal 
connection is probable rather than merely possible. George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 
569 N.W.2d 148, 153 (Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 
156 (Iowa Ct. App. 1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa 
Ct. App. 1996). A possibility of causation is not sufficient; a probability is necessary. 
Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996) (citing 
Holmes v. Bruce Motor Freight, Inc., 215 N.W.2d 296, 297 (Iowa 1974)). 
 
Industrial disability was defined in Diederich v. Tri-City Ry. Co. of Iowa, 258 N.W. 
899, 902 (Iowa 1935) as follows:  "It is therefore plain that the Legislature intended the 
term 'disability' to mean 'industrial disability' or loss of earning capacity and not a mere 
'functional disability' to be computed in terms of percentages of the total physical and 
mental ability of a normal man." 

SANCHEZ CABADA V. TYSON FRESH MEATS, INC. 
Page 13 
Functional impairment is an element to be considered in determining industrial 
disability which is the reduction of earning capacity, but consideration must also be 
given to the injured employee's age, education, qualifications, experience, motivation, 
loss of earnings, severity and situs of the injury, work restrictions, inability to engage in 
employment for which the employee is fitted and the employer's offer of work or failure 
to so offer.  McSpadden v. Big Ben Coal Co., 288 N.W.2d 181, 192 (Iowa 1980); Olson 
v. Goodyear Service Stores, 255 Iowa 1112, 1120, 125 N.W.2d 251, 256 (1963); Barton 
v. Nevada Poultry Co., 253 Iowa 285, 110 N.W.2d 660, 663 (1961). 
I found claimant to be a credible witness. He testified as to his ongoing physical 
difficulties and continuing symptoms from both the left shoulder and neck injuries. He 
has been able to secure work since his departure from Tyson, first at his step-
daughter’s store and later at King’s Point Resort. He currently earns $14.00 per hour 
and works part-time, although he is hoping to get full-time hours soon. He has limited 
education, and speaks and understands little English. His past employment primarily 
involves physical labor, which he testified he is no longer able to do without pain. While 
the treating physicians have not imposed permanent restrictions, claimant credibly 
testified regarding his limitations and ongoing symptoms, and Dr. Bansal recommended 
restrictions. Having considered all the factors of industrial disability, I find claimant is 
entitled to 35 percent industrial disability, or 175 weeks of permanent partial disability 
benefits. Benefits commence on the stipulated date of July 8, 2022. 
The next issue to determine is the proper rate of compensation. For the June 26, 
2020 date of injury, Claimant believes the weekly rate should be $555.04, based on 
average gross earnings of $853.10. (See Hearing Report; Cl. Ex. 5, p. 36) Defendant 
believes the rate should be $533.79, based on gross earnings of $790.56. (See Hearing 
Report; Def. Ex. F, p. 31) For the October 20, 2020 date of injury, claimant believes the 
weekly rate should be $595.85, based on average gross earnings of $918.46. (See 
Hearing Report; Cl. Ex. 5, p. 61) Defendant believes the rate should be $519.11, based 
on average gross earnings of $764.43. (See Hearing Report; Def. Ex. F, p. 51) 
Iowa Code section 85.36 states the basis of compensation is the weekly earnings 
of the employee at the time of the injury. The section defines weekly earnings as the 
gross salary, wages, or earnings to which an employee would have been entitled had 
the employee worked the customary hours for the full pay period in which the employee 
was injured as the employer regularly required for the work or employment. The various 
subsections of section 85.36 set forth methods of computing weekly earnings 
depending upon the type of earnings and employment.  
Both parties used section 85.36(6) to calculate claimant’s rate. That section 
states that if the employee is paid on a daily or hourly basis or by output, weekly 
earnings are computed by dividing by 13 the earnings, including shift differential pay but 
not including overtime or premium pay, over the 13-week period immediately preceding 
the injury. However, any week that does not fairly reflect the employee’s customary 
earnings is excluded, and replaced with the closest previous week with earnings that 
fairly represent the employee’s customary earnings.

Page 14 
The main difference in the rate calculations comes from the inclusion/exclusion of 
the “we care” bonus, which claimant received during some weeks due to the COVID-19 
pandemic, as well as defendant’s inclusion of some weeks in which claimant worked 
less than 40 hours. Defendant argues the “we care” bonus was an irregular bonus that 
should be excluded, and the weeks with less than 40 hours were still customary for 
claimant and should be included. Claimant disagrees. 
It is claimant’s burden to prove that a bonus should be included in the calculation 
of the average weekly wage. See Olson v. Christensen Farms, File No. 5039510 (Arb. 
Feb. 19, 2014). Irregular bonuses are not included in “gross earnings” for purposes of 
calculating the workers' compensation rate. Whether a bonus is “regular” is determined 
on a case-by-case basis and is dependent upon a variety of factors. These factors 
include how often the bonus is received, how consistently it has been paid to the 
employee over the course of his or her employment, whether it is subject to a condition 
precedent, whether it is voluntarily paid, and whether it had vested at the time of the 
injury. See Noel v. Rolscreen Co., 475 N.W.2d 666 (Iowa 1991) The Iowa Supreme 
Court has suggested that these factors are neither an exclusive nor exhaustive list. 
Burton v. Hilltop Care Ctr., 813 N.W.2d 250, 264-65 (Iowa 2012) 
There is no testimony about the “we care” bonus in the record, but claimant 
asserts in his brief that the bonus was paid by the employer during the COVID-19 
pandemic to incentivize employees to continue coming to work. (Cl. Brief, p. 16) In 
reviewing the wage records provided by the parties, it appears claimant did not receive 
this bonus every week. (Cl. Ex. 5, pp. 36, 61) There is no evidence to establish the 
criteria upon which receipt of the bonus was based. It does appear, however, that the 
bonus was temporary and tied to a specific event – the pandemic. This suggests the 
bonus payments were irregular in nature. They were paid inconsistently, and were not 
an ongoing part of claimant’s compensation history over the duration of his employment. 
The “temporary and situational” nature of the “we care” bonuses distinguishes them 
from a regular bonus. See Peska v. Graphic Packaging International, File No. 
23009765.01 (Arb. March 10, 2025). I find the “we care” bonuses were irregular, and 
should not be included in the rate calculation.  
With respect to the weeks defendant excluded in which claimant worked less 
than 40 hours, when a claimant’s hours vary, weeks with fewer hours are not 
automatically excluded if they fall within the range of customary weeks for the claimant. 
See Speakar v. Wal-Mart Stores, File No. 5039558 (Arb. July 23, 2013); Acosta v. 
Tyson Fresh Foods, Inc., File No. 1663215.01 (Arb. Sept. 15, 2022). Given the number 
of weeks claimant worked less than 40 hours, I find those weeks defendant included do 
fall within the range of customary weeks for the claimant. As such, I find defendant’s 
calculations of gross wages to be correct. 4
