STATEMENT OF THE CASE   
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A.  The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Susan Toth.  Claimant 
appeared personally and through her attorney, James Neal.  Defendants appeared 
through their attorney, Lindsey Mills.   
The alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on December 12, 
2025.  The proceedings were digitally recorded.  That recording constitutes the official 
record of this proceeding.  Pursuant to the Interim Workers’ Compensation 
Commissioner William H. Grell’s December 12, 2025 Order, the undersigned has been 
delegated authority to issue a final agency decision in this alternate medical care 
proceeding.  Therefore, this ruling is designated final agency action and any appeal of 
the decision would be to the Iowa District Court pursuant to Iowa Code section 17A.   
The evidentiary record consists of Claimant’s Exhibit 1, pages 1-4, and claimant’s 
testimony during the telephonic hearing.  During the course of the hearing, defendants 
accepted liability for the September 28, 2022, work injury and for the left knee condition 
which claimant is seeking treatment.

ISSUE   
The issue for resolution is whether the claimant is entitled to alternate medical 
care. 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
Claimant, Susan Toth, was involved in a work-related accident wherein she 
sustained an injury to her left knee.  Defendants have accepted the September 28, 
2022, left knee injury and have authorized and paid for the treatment Ms. Toth has 
received to date.  (Testimony)  Most recently, Ms. Toth has been treating at Steindler 
Orthopedics with Mark C. Mysnyk, M.D., for her left knee.  (Testimony; Claimant’s 
Exhibit 1, pp. 1-2)  On November 14, 2025, Dr. Mysnyk recommended manipulation 
under anesthesia and a cortisone injection for her left knee. (CE1, p. 1)  Dr. Mysnyk had 
previously performed a manipulation under anesthesia and she found it to be beneficial.  
(Testimony)  Ms. Toth wants to proceed with the manipulation and injection.  (CE1, p. 1; 
Testimony)   
At the November 14, 2025, appointment Dr. Mysnyk advised Ms. Toth that he 
was retiring and not available to do the recommended procedure. (Testimony)  Ms. Toth 
would have liked to have had this procedure performed already.  (Testimony)  In light of 
Dr. Mysnyk’s retirement, Ms. Toth will need to see a different orthopedic surgeon for the 
procedure.  (Testimony)  It is her understanding that she needs to see the new surgeon 
prior to scheduling the procedure. (Testimony)  Ms. Toth testified that Steindler told her 
on the phone that Dr. Mysnyk referred her to another orthopedic surgeon that deals with 
workers’ compensation. (Testimony)  However, she later testified that there are two 
different orthopedic surgeons at Steindler that she could see.  (Testimony)  The medical 
records in this case do not contain a referral to another surgeon.  (CE1, pp. 1-2)  I find 
that it is unclear from claimant’s testimony if a specific referral was made or if Steindler 
Clinic simply offered to have one of their other surgeons see Ms. Toth.    
Due to prior personal commitments, Ms. Toth would like to have the first 
appointment with the new orthopedic surgeon before she leaves for a trip on December 
19, 2025.  (Testimony)  Additionally, she wants the appointment for the manipulation 
and injection to occur the first week of January, after she returns on January 3, 2026.  
(Testimony) She is still employed with the school and classes resume on January 5, 
2026. (Testimony)  She teaches physical education, health, and driver’s education. Ms. 
Toth testified that it is difficult to find substitute teachers. (Testimony) There is a retired 
teacher that could cover her physical education and health teaching duties if it is before 
that teacher undergoes knee replacement surgery in mid-January.  While the 
undersigned appreciates why Ms. Toth prefers the timing of the appointments to occur 
in this manner, the undersigned has no authority to order any physician to perform any 
services within a certain timeframe.   
In their answer to the application for alternate medical care, defendants stated, 
“Defendants’ authorized provider, Dr. Mysnyk, is retiring and not available to perform 
the surgery for Claimant.  Defendants have authorized Physicians’ Clinic of Iowa to take

Ms. Toth does not want her care switched to Physicians’ Clinic of Iowa (PCI).

REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW   
Under Iowa law, the employer is required to provide care to an injured employee 
and is permitted to choose the care.  Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 
N.W.2d 433 (Iowa 1997).   
[T]he employer is obliged to furnish reasonable services and supplies to 
treat an injured employee, and has the right to choose the care. . . .  The 
treatment must be offered promptly and be reasonably suited to treat the 
injury without undue inconvenience to the employee.  If the employee has 
reason to be dissatisfied with the care offered, the employee should 
communicate the basis of such dissatisfaction to the employer, in writing if 
requested, following which the employer and the employee may agree to 
alternate care reasonably suited to treat the injury.  If the employer and 
employee cannot agree on such alternate care, the commissioner may, 
upon application and reasonable proofs of the necessity therefor, allow 
and order other care.   
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See Iowa 
R. App. P. 5.904(3)(e); Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 
1995).  Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of 
fact.  Id.  The employer’s obligation turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not 
desirability.  Id.; Harned v. Farmland Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983).  In Pirelli-
Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 433, the court approvingly quoted Bowles v. Los 
Lunas Schools, 109 N.M. 100, 781 P.2d 1178 (App. 1989):   
[T]he words “reasonable” and “adequate” appear to describe the same 
standard.

[The New Mexico rule] requires the employer to provide a certain 
standard of care and excuses the employer from any obligation to provide 
other services only if that standard is met.  We construe the terms 
"reasonable” and “adequate” as describing care that is both appropriate to 
the injury and sufficient to bring the worker to maximum recovery.   
The commissioner is justified in ordering alternate care when employer-
authorized care has not been effective and evidence shows that such care is “inferior or 
less extensive” care than other available care requested by the employee.  Long; 528 
N.W.2d at 124; Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co.; 562 N.W.2d at 437.   
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance, and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Section 85.27.  Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening October 16, 1975).   
Reasonable care includes care necessary to diagnose the condition and 
defendants are not entitled to interfere with the medical judgment of its own treating 
physician.  Pote v. Mickow Corp., File No. 694639 (Review-Reopening Decision June 
17, 1986).  
Based on the above findings of fact, I conclude claimant has failed to 
demonstrate the authorized care is unreasonable.  Additionally, I conclude claimant has 
failed to prove the care she is receiving is “inferior or less extensive” care than other 
available care requested by the employee.  While claimant may desire to continue her 
care at Steindler, the preponderance of the evidence does not demonstrate that 
defendants’ authorized care is unreasonable.  As such, claimant’s application for 
alternate medical care is denied.   
ORDER   
THEREFORE IT IS ORDERED:   
Claimant’s petition for alternate medical care is denied.
