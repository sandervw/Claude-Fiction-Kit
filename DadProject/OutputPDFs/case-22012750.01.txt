This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A. The 
expedited procedures of rule 876 IAC 4.48, the “alternate medical care” rule, are 
invoked by claimant, William Musus-Alonzo. 
 
This alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on January 11, 2024. The 
proceedings were recorded digitally and constitute the official record of the hearing. By 
an order filed by the Workers’ Compensation Commissioner, this decision is designated 
final agency action. Any appeal would be by petition for judicial review under Iowa Code 
section 17A.19. 
 
The record in this case consists of Claimant’s Exhibit 1-2, Defendants’ Exhibit A, 
and the testimony of claimant.   
ISSUE 
 
The issue presented for resolution in this case is whether claimant is entitled to 
alternate medical care consisting of authorization for medications prescribed by 
claimant’s primary care provider as shown in Exhibit 2. 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
Defendants accept liability for a work-related injury to claimant occurring on 
November 7, 2022.

In a December 21, 2023, letter, Shelly Weiland, PA-C, noted claimant was 
evaluated on December 19, 2023, to discuss low back pain.  Claimant had fallen while 
working on a roof and had trauma and vertebral fractures.  Claimant was prescribed 
meloxicam and gabapentin for pain.  Physician’s Assistant Weiland indicated claimant’s 
MRI from October of 2023 gave “rationale” for claimant’s pain complaints.  (Exhibit 2) 
 
In a December 21, 2023, email, claimant’s attorney asked Jessica Sniadach if 
defendant insurer would authorize the medications prescribed by Physician’s Assistant 
Weiland.  Claimant had found the medications helpful with his symptoms.  Ms. Sniadach 
is a senior claims representative with defendant insurer.  (Ex. 1) 
 
On December 28, 2023, emails were exchanged between claimant’s attorney 
and Ms. Sniadach.  Emails suggest the authorized treating physician is Ashar Afzal, 
M.D.  Dr. Afzal prescribed medications for claimant’s pain.  Claimant believed Dr. Afzal 
had discontinued the medications.  Defendant insurer was unclear if that occurred.  
Claimant was scheduled to meet with Dr. Afzal on January 12, 2024, regarding 
medication for claimant’s symptoms.  (Ex. A) 
 
Claimant testified he was prescribed pain medications by Dr. Afzal but they did 
not help his pain.  He said he went to Physician’s Assistant Weiland after the 
medications prescribed by Dr. Afzal did not work.  He said the medications prescribed 
by Physician’s Assistant Weiland have helped with this pain.  He said he wants 
defendants to authorize Physician’s Assistant Weiland to continue to prescribe pain 
medications for him. 
CONCLUSION OF LAW  
Iowa Code section 85.27(4) provides, in relevant part:  
For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish 
reasonable services and supplies to treat an injured employee and has the 
right to choose the care. . .  The treatment must be offered promptly and 
be reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the 
employee. If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the care 
offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such 
dissatisfaction to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the 
employer and the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited 
to treat the injury. If the employer and employee cannot agree on such 
alternate care, the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable 
proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and order other care.  
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See Iowa 
R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e); Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 
1995).  Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of 
fact.  Id.  The employer’s obligation turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not 
desirability.  Id.; Harned v. Farmland Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983).  In Pirelli-
Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d at 433, the court approvingly quoted 
Bowles v. Los Lunas Schools, 109 N.M. 100, 781 P.2d 1178 (App. 1989):

     [T]he words “reasonable” and “adequate” appear to describe the same 
standard.  
     [The New Mexico rule] requires the employer to provide a certain 
standard of care and excuses the employer from any obligation to provide 
other services only if that standard is met. We construe the terms 
"reasonable” and “adequate” as describing care that is both appropriate to 
the injury and sufficient to bring the worker to maximum recovery.  
The record indicates Dr. Afzal is an authorized treating physician.  Dr. Afzal 
specializes in pain management.  The record indicates Dr. Afzal prescribed pain 
medications for claimant in the past.  Claimant testified the medications prescribed by 
Dr. Afzal did not help him with his pain.  Claimant’s family practitioner, who is not an 
authorized provider, recently prescribed pain medications for claimant related to his 
work injury.  Claimant wants defendants to authorize those medications.  The record 
indicates claimant is returning to Dr. Afzal the day after the hearing to discuss 
medications.   
Defendants have authorized a pain management specialist for claimant who, has 
in the past, authorized pain medications for claimant.  Claimant is returning to the 
authorized treating physician the day after the hearing.  Claimant can talk with Dr. Afzal 
regarding the benefit he has received from taking the medications prescribed by 
Physician’s Assistant Weiland. 
Given the record as detailed above, claimant has failed to carry his burden of 
proof the care given by the defendants, in this case, is unreasonable.  Because claimant 
has failed to carry his burden of proof the care given by defendants is unreasonable, 
claimant’s petition for alternate medical care is denied.  
ORDER  
Claimant’s petition for alternate medical care is denied.
