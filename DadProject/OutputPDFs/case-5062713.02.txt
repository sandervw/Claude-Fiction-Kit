STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
On September 23, 2020, claimant filed an original notice and petition for alternate 
medical care under Iowa Code section 85.27, invoking the provisions of rule 876 IAC 
4.48.  Defendants filed an answer accepting that claimant sustained an injury on or 
about March 23, 2015, which arose out of and in the course of his employment.   
This alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on October 2, 2020, at 
8:30 a.m.  The proceedings were recorded digitally.  The recording constitutes the 
official record of the proceeding.   
The record consists of Claimant’s Exhibits 1 through 10, which include a total of 
10 pages.  Defendants offered Exhibit A, which includes a total of 10 pages.  Mr. 
Dunham was the only witness to provide testimony.  Counsel for both parties provided 
argument.  The record closed at the end of the alternate medical care telephonic 
hearing.   
ISSUE 
The issue presented for resolution is whether claimant is entitled to alternate 
medical care in the form of an order authorizing care from Karen Harmon, M.D.   
FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned having considered all of the testimony and evidence in the 
record finds as follows:

Claimant sustained a work injury on March 23, 2015.  He has undergone two low 
back surgeries, one in 2012 and the other in 2015. (See Ex. 7)  Claimant does not feel 
as though either surgery was beneficial. (See id.)  He has received epidural steroid 
injections and physical therapy.  He does not feel as though these treatments have

Claimant presented to authorized treating pain specialist, Julie Saddler, M.D., on 
October 21, 2019. (Ex. A, p. 1)  Following her examination of claimant, Dr. Saddler

claimant’s right leg pain persisted, he would be a candidate for a trial run of spinal cord 
stimulation, “Otherwise, no further pain interventional injections [because] no benefit 
from prior.” (Id.)   
Claimant received physical therapy through Athletico in Cedar Rapids, Iowa. 
(See Ex. A, p. 2)  His physical therapy records note improvement with physical therapy, 
including improved range of motion and core/hip strength. (Ex. A, p. 2)  However, 
claimant continued to note limitations with prolonged standing and walking. (Id.)  
Claimant rated his overall improvement at 75 percent in February 2020. (Id.)   
Claimant filed an alternate medical care petition in February 2020, requesting 
that the massage therapy recommended by Dr. Saddler be authorized. A decision was 
rendered on February 28, 2020, which provided, in part, “Defendants shall … follow and 
pay for treatments that Dr. Luke recommends.” (Alternate Care Decision, page 8).   
Defendants referred claimant to Cassim Igram, M.D., at University of Iowa 
Hospitals and Clinics, on Dr. Saddler’s recommendation that claimant see an orthopedic 
specialist. (See Ex. A, p. 3)  Claimant presented to Dr. Igram for his evaluation on 
March 11, 2020. (Ex. A, p. 3)  Claimant complained of low back pain and right lower 
extremity radiculopathy. (Id.)  Dr. Igram diagnosed claimant with chronic back pain with 
chronic right-sided radicular pain. (Ex. A, p. 6)  After his examination, Dr. Igram 
discussed his recommendations with claimant.  On the topic of surgically removing 
claimant’s scar tissue, Dr. Igram opined that removing scar tissue around nerves is not 
predictable for pain relief because the scar tissue may grow back. (Ex. A, p. 4)  Dr. 
Igram opined that surgical intervention is not recommended. (Id.)  Dr. Igram further 
opined that conservative care, “such as physical therapy, massage, acupuncture, or 
chiropractic [care] may be reasonable unless he does not display functional 
improvement after three months of treatment.” (Id.)  Dr. Igram opined he was reluctant 
to recommend a spinal cord stimulator. (Ex. A, p. 6)   
According to defendants’ Exhibit A, pages 5 through 7, claimant regularly 
presented to Dr. Luke for chiropractic care between January and September 2020.  
Claimant consistently reported pain levels between 4 and 5 out of 10 at these visits.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence. Iowa R. App. P. 6.14(6). 
Iowa Code section 85.27(4) provides, in relevant part: 
For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish 
reasonable services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has 
the right to choose the care. . . . The treatment must be offered promptly 
and be reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience 
to the employee. If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the 
care offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such 
dissatisfaction to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the 
employer and the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited 
to treat the injury. If the employer and employee cannot agree on such 
alternate care, the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable 
proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving. Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care. Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant. Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995). 
The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except where the 
employer has denied liability for the injury. Section 85.27; Holbert v. Townsend 
Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial Commissioner, 78 
(Review-Reopening 1975). 
An employer’s right to select the provider of medical treatment to an injured 
worker does not include the right to determine how an injured worker should be 
diagnosed, evaluated, treated, or other matters of professional medical judgment. 
Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 866389 (Declaratory Ruling, May 19, 1988). 
Reasonable care includes care necessary to diagnose the condition and 
defendants are not entitled to interfere with the medical judgment of its own treating 
physician. Pote v. Mickow Corp., File No. 694639 (Review-Reopening June 17, 1986). 
When a designated physician refers a patient to another physician, that physician 
acts as the defendant employer’s agent. Permission for the referral from defendant is 
not necessary. Kittrell v. Allen Memorial Hospital, Thirty-fourth Biennial Report of the 
Industrial Commissioner, 164 (Arb. November 1, 1979) (aff’d by industrial

commissioner). See also Limoges v. Meier Auto Salvage, I Iowa Industrial 
Commissioner Reports 207 (1981). 
The defendants argue that they maintain the right to choose the medical provider 
who treats the claimant. While the defendant does have the right to choose care they 
must do so in a timely and prompt fashion less they be deemed to have abandoned the 
care. Further, referrals from authorized treating physicians do not need permission or 
approval from the defendants. 
Defendants assert that Dr. Luke is not authorized to make referrals on their 
behalf.  Defendants assert that the authorization of chiropractic care through Dr. Luke 
does not necessarily equate to an authorization of Dr. Luke as an authorized treating 
physician.   
The alternate medical care procedure requires the claimant to express 
dissatisfaction with the care provided. This was done via email on June 29, 2020, with a 
follow-up on September 1, 2020. Defendants first replied to the expression of 
dissatisfaction via e-mail on or about July 16, 2020.  It appears from the record that the 
parties were participating in settlement discussions when the initial request was made. I 
do not find that defendants abandoned care during this period of time. 
While the employer has the right to choose the provider of care, the right to 
select the provider does not include the right to determine how an injured worker should 
be diagnosed, evaluated, treated, or other matters of professional judgment.  
In this particular case the defendants point out that Dr. Luke’s referral was not 
done based on any medical justification, or for any specific treatment. Rather, the 
referral was made due to the fact that the claimant requested to receive ongoing care 
with his primary physician.  
Defendants argue that claimant has been seen by a pain management specialist 
and an orthopedic surgeon, who both believe claimant’s condition requires only 
ongoing, conservative treatment.  Defendants further argue that claimant’s condition 
has not changed significantly since presenting to Drs. Saddler and Igram.  Claimant is 
hopeful that he will regain functionality within his low back with additional treatment.  At 
this time, no specific treatment recommendations exist.  Again, the referral to Dr. 
Harmon is a general referral.  
It is the claimant’s burden to show that the care offered by defendants is not 
reasonable or presents an undue inconvenience to the claimant.  
Defendants have provided claimant with reasonable and necessary care for a 
significant period of time.  That treatment has included medications, physical therapy, 
diagnostic imaging, injections, and chiropractic adjustments.

In this case, Dr. Luke is a chiropractor. Dr. Luke asserts that “medical treatment 
along with chiropractic, acupuncture, massage, and cold laser therapy will be of benefit” 
to claimant.  These are similar to the opinions of Dr. Igram.  Dr. Luke’s 
recommendations appear to be for maintenance of claimant’s ongoing complaints.  
Again, it appears Dr. Luke referred claimant to Dr. Harmon primarily because of 
claimant’s suggestion and/or preference.  Such a general referral could just as easily be 
carried out by an occupational medicine physician chosen by defendants.   
To find that referrals based on an injured worker’s request or suggestion should 
automatically be deemed approved would diminish the employer’s right to choose the 
medical provider. In this case, based on the evidence it cannot be found that Dr. Luke 
chose Dr. Harmon based on some medical reason or judgment. Therefore, it cannot be 
said that Dr. Luke was acting as an agent of the defendants. Rather, Dr. Luke was 
acting as the agent of the claimant with respect to who the referral was to. See Lacina v. 
Fansteel, Inc., File No. 5031505 (Alt. Care January 19, 2011) 
Defendants have offered claimant another appointment with Dr. Igram regarding 
recommendations for additional treatment.  This offer appears to fulfill the general 
referral of Dr. Luke.  I find defendants’ offer to be reasonable.   
Claimant did not carry his burden to show that the offer to return claimant to Dr. 
Igram for recommendations regarding additional medical treatment is unreasonable and 
unduly inconvenient.  Claimant has failed to prove he is entitled to the alternate medical 
care he seeks.   
ORDER 
THEREFORE IT IS ORDERED: 
Claimant’s petition for alternate medical care is denied.
