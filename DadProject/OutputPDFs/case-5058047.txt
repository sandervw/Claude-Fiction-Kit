STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
The claimant, John Oppelt, filed a petition for arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from employer Klockner USA Holding, Inc. (hereinafter 
“Klockner”), and its insurer, Travelers Property Casualty Company of America.  Nick 
Avgerinos appeared on behalf of the claimant.  Julie Burger appeared on behalf of the 
defendants.   
 
The matter came on for hearing on September 3, 2020, before deputy workers’ 
compensation commissioner Andrew M. Phillips in Des Moines, Iowa.  An order issued 
on March 13, 2020, and updated June 1, 2020, by the Iowa Workers’ Compensation 
Commissioner, In the Matter of Coronavirus/COVID-19 Impact on Hearings (Available 
online at: https://www.iowaworkcomp.gov/order-coronavirus-covid-19 (last viewed July 
29, 2020)) amended the hearing assignment order in each case before the 
Commissioner scheduled for an in-person regular proceeding hearing between March 
18, 2020, and November 20, 2020.  The amendment makes it so that such hearings will 
be held by Internet-based video, using CourtCall.  The parties appeared electronically, 
and the hearing proceeded without significant difficulties.  The matter was fully 
submitted on October 2, 2020, after briefing by the parties.     
The record in this case consists of Joint Exhibits 1-13, Claimant’s Exhibits 1-6, 
and Defendants’ Exhibits A-E.  Testimony under oath was also taken from the claimant, 
John Oppelt.  Amy Pedersen was appointed the official reporter and custodian of the 
notes of the proceeding.  The exhibits were accepted without objection.

STIPULATIONS 
 
Through the hearing report, as reviewed at the commencement of the hearing, 
the parties stipulated and/or established the following: 
1. There was an employer-employee relationship at the time of the alleged injury. 
  
2. The claimant sustained an injury, which arose out of and in the scope of 
employment, on January 5, 2015.     
 
3. The alleged injury is a cause of temporary disability during a period of recovery. 
 
4. The alleged injury is a cause of permanent disability. 
 
5. The disability is an industrial disability. 
 
6. The commencement date for permanent partial disability benefits, if any are 
awarded, is July 15, 2019.   
 
7. The claimant was married, entitled to 5 exemptions and had gross earnings of 
$1,315.63 per week, resulting in a weekly compensation rate of $838.64. 
 
8. The claimant was paid 43 weeks of compensation at the rate of $838.64 per 
week.   
Additionally, there is no dispute as to the entitlement for temporary disability and/or 
hearing period benefits.  There also is no longer a dispute regarding credits against any 
award.  Defendants waived their affirmative defenses. 
The parties are now bound by their stipulations. 
ISSUES 
The parties submitted the following issues for determination: 
1. Whether the claimant is permanently and totally disabled under the odd-lot 
doctrine. 
 
2. Whether the claimant is entitled to reimbursement for an independent medical 
examination (IME) under Iowa Code 85.39.   
 
3. Whether costs should be assessed against any party, and if so, in what amount.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned, having considered all of the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
John Oppelt, the claimant, was 54 years old at the time of the hearing.  He 
resides in Dubuque, Iowa.  (Testimony).  He is married.  (Testimony).  He attended high 
school in Dubuque, Iowa, and graduated in 1984.  (Testimony).  He admitted to being 
an average student.  (Testimony).  After high school, he obtained a certification in auto 
mechanics and truck driving from NICC.  (Testimony). 
From 1986 to 1988, Mr. Oppelt drove a dump truck for Addocco, Inc., in Peosta, 
Iowa.  (Claimant’s Exhibit 2:40).  He earned $9.00 per hour.  (CE 2:40).  He hauled 
firewood and other materials.  (CE 2:40; Testimony).  He also hauled sawdust, made 
pallets, and performed all around maintenance. (Testimony).  Mr. Oppelt felt that this 
was medium work, wherein he had to lift about 40 pounds throughout a typical day. 
(Testimony).  Mr. Oppelt left Addocco, Inc., voluntarily to work for a new employer.  
(Testimony).   
From 1988 to 1994, Mr. Oppelt worked for Loras Kalb Trucking as a tractor trailer 
driver.  (CE 2:39; Testimony).  The trailer was a dump trailer.  (Testimony).  He earned 
25 percent of the cost of the load.  (CE 2:39).  He hauled dirt, rock, coal, and corn.  (CE 
2:39).  This position was considered to be in the medium category of physical demands.  
(CE 2:39).  Mr. Oppelt felt that his job with Loras Kalb was less physically demanding 
than his job with Addocco, Inc.  (Testimony).  Mr. Oppelt voluntarily left Loras Kalb for a 
position at Klockner.  (Testimony).   
From 1994 to June of 2020, Mr. Oppelt drove a flatbed tractor trailer for Klockner 
Metals/Namasco.  (Claimant’s Exhibit 2:38-39; Testimony).  This position was 
considered to be medium in physical demands.  (CE 2:39).  Mr. Oppelt traveled 
between Iowa, Minnesota, South Dakota, Indiana and Illinois, hauling steel of various 
sizes.  (CE 2:39; Testimony).  He reported handling three to four loads per week, and 
spent two to three nights on the road.  (CE 2:39; Testimony).  He worked 50 to 80 hours 
per week.  (Testimony).  Depending on the location, Mr. Oppelt may have cargo to bring 
back to Klockner.  (CE 2:39; Testimony).  Mr. Oppelt had to chain down loads of steel, 
and place tarps over them prior to driving to a delivery location.  (Testimony).  In order 
to do so, he needed to throw a chain over the cargo, place a chain binder, and pull the 
chains tight.  (Testimony).  Sometimes, this used ratchets.  (Testimony).  The chains 
weight from 25 to 30 pounds.  (Testimony).  The tarps weighed upwards of 75 pounds.  
(Testimony).  Mr. Oppelt earned between $22.00 and $23.00 per hour. (CE 2:39; 
Testimony).  Klockner Metals Corporation terminated Mr. Oppelt on June 8, 2020, due 
to “partial/total disability.”  (Joint Exhibit 13:108).  Mr. Oppelt indicated that he enjoyed 
his job at Klockner.  (Testimony). 
As a Klockner employee, Mr. Oppelt was disciplined on several occasions.  
(Defendants’ Exhibit B).  On June 2, 2009, Mr. Oppelt received an employee warning for 
unexcused tardiness.  (DE B:1).  In July of 2009, Klockner terminated Mr. Oppelt’s

On January 5, 2015, Mr. Oppelt reported for work, and punched in on the time 
clock.  (Testimony).  He approached his load, and began to tighten it down with a 
cheater pipe.  (Testimony).  He began to crank down the load, and encountered some

he came back the next morning, his pain increased.  (Testimony).  He told Klockner,

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa Rule of 
Appellate Procedure 6.14(6)(e).   
Odd-Lot Permanent and Total Disability 
John Oppelt alleges he is permanently and totally disabled under the common 
law odd-lot doctrine.   
 
In Iowa, a claimant may establish permanent total disability under the statute, or 
through the common law odd-lot doctrine.  Michael Eberhart Const. v. Curtin, 674 
N.W.2d 123, 126 (Iowa 2004)(discussing both theories of permanent total disability 
under Idaho law and concluding the deputy’s ruling was not based on both theories 
rather, it was only based on the odd-lot doctrine).  Under the statute, the claimant may 
establish that they are totally and permanently disabled if the claimant’s medical 
impairment, taken together with nonmedical factors totals 100-percent.  Id.  The odd-lot 
doctrine applies when the claimant has established the claimant has sustained 
something less than 100-percent disability, but is so injured that the claimant is “unable 
to perform services other than ‘those which are so limited in quality, dependability or 
quantity that a reasonably stable market for them does not exist.’”  Id.  (quoting Boley v. 
Indus. Special Indem. Fund, 130 Idaho 278, 281, 939 P.2d 854, 857 (1997)).   
 
“Total disability does not mean a state of absolute helplessness.”  Walmart 
Stores, Inc. v. Caselman, 657 N.W.2d 493, 501 (Iowa 2003)(quoting IBP, Inc. v. Al-
Gharib, 604 N.W.2d 621, 633 (Iowa 2000)).  Total disability occurs “when the injury 
wholly disables the employee from performing work that the employee’s experience, 
training, intelligence, and physical capacities would otherwise permit the employee to 
perform.”  IBP, Inc., 604 N.W.2d at 633. However, finding that the claimant could 
perform some work despite claimant’s physical and educational limitations does not 
foreclose a finding of permanent total disability.   See Chamberlin v. Ralston Purina, File 
No. 661698 (App. October 1987); Eastman v. Westway Trading Corp., II Iowa Industrial 
Commissioner Report 134 (App. May 1982).   
 
In Guyton v. Irving Jensen, Co., the Iowa Supreme Court formally adopted the 
“odd-lot doctrine.”  373 N.W.2d 101 (Iowa 1985).  Under that doctrine, a worker 
becomes an odd-lot employee when an injury makes the worker incapable of obtaining 
employment in any well-known branch of the labor market.  An odd-lot worker is thus 
totally disabled if the only services the worker can perform are “so limited in quality, 
dependability, or quantity that a reasonably stable market for them does not exist.”  Id., 
at 105.

Under the odd-lot doctrine, the burden of persuasion on the issue of industrial 
disability always remains with the worker.  Nevertheless, when a worker makes a prima 
facie case of total disability by producing substantial evidence that the worker is not 
employable in the competitive labor market, the burden to provide evidence showing 
availability of suitable employment shifts to the employer.  If the employer fails to 
produce such evidence and the trier of fact finds the worker does fall in the odd-lot 
category, then the worker is entitled to a finding of total disability.  Guyton, 373 N.W.2d 
at 106.  Factors to be considered in determining whether a worker is an odd-lot 
employee include: the worker’s reasonable but unsuccessful effort to find steady 
employment, vocational or other expert evidence demonstrating suitable work is not 
available for the worker, the extent of the worker’s physical impairment, intelligence, 
education, age, training, and potential for retraining.  No factor is necessarily dispositive 
on the issue.  Second Injury Fund of Iowa v. Nelson, 544 N.W.2d 258 (Iowa 1995).  
Even under the odd-lot doctrine, the trier of fact is free to determine the weight and 
credibility of evidence in determining whether the worker’s burden of persuasion has 
been carried, and only in an exceptional case would evidence be sufficiently strong as 
to compel a finding of total disability as a matter of law.  Guyton, 373 N.W.2d at 106.   
 
The burden of proof is on the claimant to prove, via substantial evidence, that Mr. 
Oppelt is not employable in the competitive labor market.  Mr. Oppelt’s initial injury 
involved a sprain/strain type of injury to his neck.  Mr. Oppelt utilized Biofreeze to treat 
this injury, which resulted in an infected area in his neck.  Doctors debrided his neck 
wound on several occasions, and treated the open wound.  Mr. Oppelt had an IME in 
2016 with Dr. Carrier, in which Dr. Carrier noted significant concerns about Mr. Oppelt’s 
movements.  For example, Dr. Carrier observed Mr. Oppelt moving freely in certain 
directions with his neck that did not comport with the issues which Mr. Oppelt claimed 
during his examination.  Dr. Carrier gave Mr. Oppelt a rating, but noted the examination 
and rating were invalid due to his observations of the claimant.  In 2017, Mr. Oppelt had 
an IME with Dr. Garrels.  Dr. Garrels gave Mr. Oppelt a zero percent impairment rating 
to his neck, as Dr. Garrels opined that the neck issues were related to degenerative 
issues and the claimant’s morbid obesity.  Mr. Oppelt had another IME with Dr. Bansal 
in May of 2017, in which Dr. Bansal opined that Mr. Oppelt had disc issues in his neck, 
and recommended an MRI.  Mr. Oppelt had the MRI in September of 2017, which 
showed mild disc bulging at C3-4 and C5-6 without significant central canal narrowing 
along with mild degenerative foraminal narrowing on the right at C5-6.  Dr. Garrels 
reviewed the MRI and opined that the mild degenerative changes correlated with his 
IME findings.  Dr. Bansal recommended intermittent epidural injections based upon the 
MRI results, and otherwise stood by his IME findings.   
 
In early 2018, Mr. Oppelt felt his pain increased, so he returned to one of his

opined that Mr. Oppelt was too young for total reverse shoulder replacements.  In June 
of 2018, Dr. Nepola provided the following restrictions for Mr. Oppelt: lifting only 10

IME under Iowa Code 85.39 
Iowa Code section 85.39 states, in pertinent part: 
If an evaluation of permanent disability has been made by a physician 
retained by the employer and the employee believes this evaluation to be 
too low, the employee shall, upon application to the commissioner and 
upon delivery of a copy of the application to the employer and its 
insurance carrier, be reimbursed by the employer the reasonable fee for a 
subsequent examination by a physician of the employee’s own choice, 
and reasonably necessary transportation expenses incurred for the 
examination.   
 
Defendants are responsible only for reasonable fees associated with claimant’s 
independent medical examination.  Claimant has the burden of proving the 
reasonableness of the expenses incurred for the examination.  See Schintgen v. 
Economy Fire & Casualty Co., File No. 855298 (App. April 26, 1991).  Claimant need 
not prove the injury arose out of and in the course of employment to qualify for 
reimbursement under section 85.39.  See Dodd v. Fleetguard, Inc., 759 N.W.2d 133, 
140 (Iowa App. 2008).   
 
The claimant requests that the undersigned order payment to the claimant of the 
fee for Dr. Bansal’s June 30, 2020, IME.  Dr. Field provided an impairment rating for Mr. 
Oppelt’s shoulders.  Dr. Bansal provided a subsequent examination and rating.  
Therefore, under applicable Iowa law, the claimant is entitled to reimbursement for Dr. 
Bansal’s IME in the amount of $3,381.00.
