STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
Claimant, Jessica Wagner, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers 
compensation benefits from IH Mississippi Valley Credit Union, employer, and 
Employers Insurance, insurer, both as defendants. The case was heard on May 21, 
2025 at 8:30 a.m. remotely via Zoom with a final submission date of August 29, 2025.  
 
The record consists of Joint Exhibits 1-9, Claimant’s Exhibits 1-10, Defendants’ 
Exhibits A-I, and the testimony of claimant and Morgan Dunn.  
 
STIPULATIONS 
 
 
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised 
or discussed in this decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.  
 
The parties agree that claimant sustained an injury on March 23, 2019, which 
arose out of and in the course of her employment. They further agree that if the injury 
resulted in a permanent disability, that disability is industrial in nature.

ISSUES 
 
1. Whether claimant’s current psychological condition, including PTSD, is 
related to the March 23, 2019, work incident;  
 
2. Whether claimant is entitled to past-due temporary total disability and/or 
healing period benefits1;  
 
3. Whether claimant has reached maximum medical improvement related to 
the work injury, and if so, the extent of permanent disability benefits, if 
any;  
 
4. If claimant is found to have not yet reached maximum medical 
improvement, whether she is entitled to a running award of temporary total 
disability and/or healing period benefits;  
 
5. Whether defendants are entitled to apportionment;  
 
6. Whether claimant is entitled to further medical care for conditions related 
to the work injury;  
 
7. Whether claimant is entitled to penalty benefits; and  
 
8. Whether claimant is entitled to costs.

Dr. Wadle’s assessment of Dr. Tranel’s May 24, 2023, report is not entirely

In the deposition, Dr. Wadle took exception to Dr. Tranel’s opinions because 
claimant did not meet the threshold score to qualify for PTSD. (DE C:23) In the May 24, 
2023, assessment claimant’s PCL score was below the clinically significant range. (JE 
4:85) However, Dr. Wadle admitted in his deposition that the PCL is only helpful, not 
diagnostic. (DE C:27) The PCL is merely a tool that must be used with clinical 
observations. (DE C:27) In response to a question of what symptoms are seen with a 
diagnosis of PTSD, Dr. Wadle states:  
 
The first one has to be the witnessing or experiencing a life-threatening 
event or aware of that with a friend or family. And then you get into themes 
of arousability, hyperarousal, jumpy. There can be reminders via 
flashbacks, nightmares.  
 
There can be avoidance of situations, places, people that were related 
to whatever that incident was.  
 
They could have this negative cognition, kind of blaming themselves,

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
  
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury actually occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 

WAGNER V. IH MISSISSIPPI VALLEY CREDIT UNION 
Page 17 
 
employment. Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 150 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. 
Dial Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309, 311 (Iowa 1996). The words “arising out of” refer to the 
cause or source of the injury. The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 
circumstances of the injury. 2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124, 128 (Iowa 
1995).  An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists 
between the injury and the employment. Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. The injury must 
be a rational consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely 
incidental to the employment. Koehler Elec. v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1, 3 (Iowa 2000); 
Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it 
happens within a period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may 
be when performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties 
or doing an activity incidental to them. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d at 150. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
 
A possibility of causation is not sufficient; a probability is necessary. Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996) (citing Holmes v. Bruce 
Motor Freight, Inc., 215 N.W.2d 296, 297 (Iowa 1974)). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 
621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 
(Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
 
Nontraumatically caused mental injuries are compensable under Iowa Code 
section 85.3(1).  Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 855 (Iowa 
1995).  
 
Under Dunlavey, mental injuries caused by work-related stress are compensable 
if, after demonstrating medical causation, the employee shows that the mental injury 
was caused by workplace stress of greater magnitude than the day-to-day mental 

WAGNER V. IH MISSISSIPPI VALLEY CREDIT UNION 
Page 18 
 
stresses experienced by other workers employed in the same or similar jobs, regardless 
of their employer.  (Id.) at 857. 
 
Both medical and legal causation must be resolved in claimant’s favor before an 
injury arising out of and in the course of the employment can be established.  To 
establish medical causation, the employee must show that the stresses and tensions 
arising from the work environment are a proximate cause of the employee’s mental 
difficulties.  If the medical causation issue is resolved in favor of the employee, legal 
causation is examined.  Legal causation involves a determination of whether the work 
stresses and tensions the employee experienced, when viewed objectively and not as 
the employee perceived them, were of greater magnitude than the day-to-day mental 
stresses workers employed in the same or similar jobs experience routinely regardless 
of their employer. 
 
The employee has the burden to establish the requisite legal causation.  
Evidence of stresses experienced by workers with similar jobs employed by a different 
employer is relevant; evidence of the stresses of other workers employed by the same 
employer in the same or similar jobs will usually be most persuasive and determinative 
on the issue.  (Id.) at 858. 
 
The primary question is whether claimant sustained a mental injury arising out of 
the March 23, 2019, attempted robbery at her place of employment. Based on the 
opinion of Dr. Tranel, claimant did suffer from PTSD and general anxiety disorder as a 
result of the March 23, 2019 robbery incident. While defendants point out that the 
security footage does not match claimant’s account, particularly that there was no direct 
confrontation with the robber nor was claimant threatened with a gun directly, Dr. Tranel 
did not rely on those facts to come to his conclusion. In the report, he stated “she was 
unable to give any substantive details of this incident as any attempt to ask about it 
resulted in her becoming very tearful and developing panic-like symptoms.” (JE 4:80) 
Another factor in support of Dr. Tranel’s diagnosis was that claimant was able to 
respond to evidence-based treatment for PTSD with notable reduction in her symptoms. 
 
Dr. Wadle’s opinion that PTSD is not the only cause of the claimant’s condition 
does not rule out claimant’s entitlement to benefits as legal causation requires the 
March 23, 2019, incident to be a substantial cause, not the only cause. Further, a strict 
application of the DSM-5 is not necessary for medical or legal causation to be met. See 
Renaud V. Warren County/Second Injury Fund of Iowa, File no. 23008425.01 (App. 
Dec. 19, 2025)  In Renaud, the Interim Commissioner noted that admissibility of expert 
evidence is within the agency’s discretion and that there is no mandate that PTSD 
diagnoses be made by licensed psychologists or psychiatrists using DSM-based 
methodologies. Renaud at page 11.  
 
The greater weight of the evidence supports a finding that claimant suffered 
PTSD following the robbery of the bank where she worked.

Iowa Code section 85.39 permits an employee to be reimbursed for subsequent

The Iowa Supreme Court has provided a literal interpretation of the plain

permanent disability has already been made by an employer retained physician.  Des

an evaluation of permanent disability has been made by an employer-retained

“reasonable basis” for denial of the claim exists if the claim is “fairly 
debatable.”  Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 260. 
 
The supreme court has stated: 
(1) If the employer has a reason for the delay and conveys that 
reason to the employee contemporaneously with the beginning of the delay, 
no penalty will be imposed if the reason is of such character that a 
reasonable fact finder could conclude that it is a "reasonable or probable 
cause or excuse" under Iowa Code section 86.13 [2015].  In that case, we 
will defer to the decision of the commissioner.  See Christensen, 554

(4) For the purpose of applying section 86.13, the benefits that are

(5) For purposes of determining whether there has been a delay, 
payments are “made” when (a) the check addressed to a claimant is mailed 
(Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 236; Kiesecker, 528 N.W.2d at 112), or (b) the 
check is delivered personally to the claimant by the employer or its workers’ 
compensation insurer.  Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 235.   
 
(6) In determining the amount of the penalty, the commissioner is to

wages, and the employer’s past record of penalties.  Robbennolt, 555 
N.W.2d at 238.
