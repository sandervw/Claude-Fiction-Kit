STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
On September 27, 2024, the claimant filed a petition for alternate medical care 
pursuant to Iowa Code 85.27(4) and 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.48.  The 
defendant filed an answer admitting liability for injuries related to the claimant’s left 
shoulder, but contend that the claimant only sustained a temporary injury to their left 
shoulder. 
The undersigned presided over the hearing held via telephone and recorded 
digitally on October 8, 2024.  That recording constitutes the official record of the 
proceeding pursuant to 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.48(12).  Claimant participated 
personally and through his attorney, Benjamin Roth.  The defendant participated 
through their attorney, Stephanie Techau.   
Prior to the hearing, the claimant submitted six pages of exhibits, marked as 
Exhibits 1-2.  The defendant submitted two pages of unlabeled exhibits.  The 
evidentiary record consists of Claimant’s Exhibits 1-2 and Defendant’s Exhibit.   
 
On July 21, 2023, the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commissioner issued an 
order delegating authority to deputy workers’ compensation commissioners, such as the 
undersigned, to issue final agency decisions on applications for alternate care.  
Consequently, this decision constitutes final agency action, and there is no appeal to 
the commissioner.  Judicial review in a district court pursuant to Iowa Code Chapter 17A 
is the avenue for an appeal. 
ISSUE 
The issue under consideration is whether the defendant should be ordered to 
authorize and provide for left shoulder care with Dr. Benjamin Torrez.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
Claimant, David Bowles, alleges that he sustained an injury to his left shoulder, 
arm, and body as a whole, on September 13, 2022, while working for John Deere 
Waterloo Works (“John Deere”).  John Deere denied liability for the workers’ 
compensation claim on, or about, October 19, 2022.  Thus, the claimant sought care 
with a physician of his own choosing.  He established care with Benajmin Torrez, D.O., 
an orthopedic physician.  In response to the claimant filing an original notice and petition 
for arbitration on June 28, 2024, John Deere filed an answer on July 1, 2024, again 
denying liability.  John Deere later amended their answer on July 16, 2024, admitting 
liability for the injury, but denying that the claimant sustained a permanent injury.   
 
Mr. Bowles visited Dr. Torrez on July 29, 2024, in order to review the results of a 
left shoulder MRI.  (Claimant’s Exhibit 2:5-6).  Mr. Bowles also wanted to discuss the 
surgical process with Dr. Torrez.  (CE 2:5).  Dr. Torrez noted that the MRI was from 
November of 2022, and showed a “near complete tear of the rotator cuff at the insertion 
site at that time with a prominent SLAP tear and subacromial impingement.”  (CE 2:5).  
The MRI also showed “scarring in the axillary recess indicating adhesive capsulitis.”  
(CE 2:5).  Dr. Torrez recommended a new MRI.  (CE 2:5).  Dr. Torrez proceeded to 
examine Mr. Bowles, including measuring the range of motion in the claimant’s left 
shoulder.  (CE 2:5-6).   
 
On August 2, 2024, counsel for the claimant contacted counsel for the defendant 
indicating that the claimant attempted to obtain treatment for his work injuries.  (CE 1:4).   
However, the claimant’s chosen physician would not treat the claimant without a formal 
written denial.  (CE 1:4).  Claimant’s counsel requested that the defendant provide a 
letter denying liability so that the claimant could obtain his chosen treatment.  (CE 1:4).   
 
After no response from counsel for the defendant, claimant’s counsel e-mailed 
defendant’s counsel again on August 19, 2024, again requesting a “denial letter” so that 
the claimant could proceed with treatment.  (CE 1:3-4).  Claimant’s counsel noted in this 
e-mail that they would file an alternate medical care petition if the defendant did not 
provide a formal denial letter.  (CE 1:4).   
 
On August 27, 2024, counsel for the defendant e-mailed claimant’s counsel 
indicating that their client admitted a left shoulder injury while denying permanency of 
said injury.  (CE 1:3).  Defendant’s counsel further noted that they wished to arrange for 
an appointment with Dr. Bartelt.  (CE 1:3).  Defendant’s counsel clarified during the 
hearing that this appointment would be an independent medical examination (“IME”) 
pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.39(1).   
 
Claimant’s counsel responded to the defendant’s e-mail on August 27, 2024, 
asking if the defendant would authorize treatment with Dr. Torrez, as “Deere uses 
Torrez sometimes and he’s already established with Torrez.”  (CE 1:2-3).   
 
Counsel for the defendant responded to claimant’s counsel on August 28, 2024, 
indicating that John Deere wanted “…to keep the 85.39(1) appointment with Dr. Bartelt

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Iowa Code 85.27(4) provides, in relevant part: 
For purposes of this section, the employer is obligated to furnish reasonable 
services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has the right to 
choose the care….  The treatment must be offered promptly and be 
reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the 
employee.  If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the care 
offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such dissatisfaction 
to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the employer and 
the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited to treat the 
injury.  If the employer and employee cannot agree on such alternate care, 
the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable proofs of the 
necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
Iowa Code 85.27(4). See Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 433 (Iowa 
1997).   
“Iowa Code section 85.27(4) affords an employer who does not contest the 
compensability of a workplace injury a qualified statutory right to control the medical 
care provided to an injured employee.”  Ramirez-Trujillo v. Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 
N.W.2d 759, 769 (Iowa 2016) (citing R.R. Donnelly & Sons v. Barnett, 670 N.W.2d 190, 
195, 197 (Iowa 2003)).  “In enacting the right-to-choose provision in section 85.27(4), 
our legislature sought to balance the interests of injured employees against the

competing interests of their employers.”  Ramirez-Trujillo, 878 N.W.2d at 770-71 (citing 
Bell Bros., 779 N.W.2d at 202, 207; IBP, Inc. v. Harker, 633 N.W.2d 322, 326-27 (Iowa 
2001)).   
The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except where the 
employer has denied liability for the injury.  Iowa Code section 85.27; Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening, October 16, 1975).  An employer’s right to select 
the provider of medical treatment to an injured worker does not include the right to 
determine how an injured worker should be diagnosed, evaluated, treated, or other 
matters of professional medical judgment.  Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 
866389 (Declaratory Ruling, May 18, 1988).  Reasonable care includes care necessary 
to diagnose the condition, and defendants are not entitled to interfere with the medical 
judgment of its own treating physician.  Pote v. Mickow Corp., File No. 694639 (Review-
Reopening Decision, June 17, 1986).   
The employer must furnish “reasonable medical services and supplies and 
reasonable and necessary appliances to treat an injured employee.”  Stone Container 
Corp. v. Castle, 657 N.W.2d 485, 490 (Iowa 2003)(emphasis in original).  Such 
employer-provided care “must be offered promptly and be reasonably suited to treat the 
injury without undue inconvenience to the employee.”  Iowa Code section 85.27(4).   
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment - and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See e.g. 
Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(3); Bell Bros. Heating and Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 
N.W.2d 193, 209 (Iowa 2010); Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  
An injured employee dissatisfied with the employer-furnished care (or lack thereof) may 
share the employee’s discontent with the employer and if the parties cannot reach an 
agreement on alternate care, “the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable 
proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and order the care.”  Id.  “Determining what care 
is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.”  Long, 528 N.W.2d at 123; Pirelli-
Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 436.  As the party seeking relief in the form of 
alternate care, the employee bears the burden of proving that the authorized care is 
unreasonable.  Id. at 124; Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d at 209; Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co., 562 
N.W.2d at 436.  Because “the employer’s obligation under the statute turns on the 
question of reasonable necessity, not desirability,” an injured employee’s dissatisfaction 
with employer-provided care, standing alone, is not enough to find such care 
unreasonable.  Id.   
 
The claimant is correct, in this case, there is a question of the reasonableness of 
the care offered (or in this case not offered).  The defendant in this case took a stance 
of denying liability for the claimant’s left shoulder injury.  During this time, the claimant 
appears to have sought out care on his own, including with Dr. Torrez.  This established 
a doctor-patient relationship with Dr. Torrez.  After several years of maintaining their 
denial position, the defendant changed their position to admit liability for the injury, while 
denying permanency of the left shoulder injury.  Had the defendant wanted to examine 
the claimant with a provider of their own choosing pursuant to Iowa Code section

85.39(1), they had the option to do so prior to the need for an alternate care hearing.  
Deciding to send the claimant for an IME only after he expressed dissatisfaction with the 
lack of action by the defendant is not reasonable.   
Continuing to delay care after the claimant has already engaged Dr. Torrez 
during a period of denial is unreasonable.  Failing to promptly authorize care is 
unreasonable considering the defendant’s change in position on liability effective July 
16, 2024.  The defendant can still have the claimant examined by Dr. Bartelt pursuant to 
Iowa Code section 85.39(1).  Certainly, the defendant may later change their position, 
depending on the results of Dr. Bartelt’s examination; however, the evidence in the 
record indicates that the defendant’s actions to date in failing to authorize treatment 
have been unreasonable.   
 
IT IS THEREFORE ORDERED: 
1. The claimant’s petition for alternate care is granted.  
  
2. The defendant shall authorize care with Dr. Torrez.
