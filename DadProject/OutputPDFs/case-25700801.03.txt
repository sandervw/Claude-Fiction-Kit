STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
On December 29, 2025, the claimant filed a petition for alternate medical care 
pursuant to Iowa Code 85.27(4) and 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.48.  The 
defendants filed an answer admitting liability for the injuries to the claimant’s left 
shoulder and left hip. 
The undersigned presided over the hearing held electronically and recorded 
digitally on January 8, 2026.  That recording constitutes the official record of the 
proceeding pursuant to 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.48(12).  The claimant 
participated through his attorney, Andrew Giller.  The defendants participated through 
their attorney, Caitlin Kilburg.   
Prior to the hearing, the claimant submitted four pages of exhibits, marked as 
Exhibits 1 and 2.  The defendants submitted two pages of exhibits, marked as Exhibit A.  
The exhibits were received into the record without objection. 
 
On December 12, 2025, the Interim Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commissioner 
issued an order delegating authority to deputy workers’ compensation commissioners, 
such as the undersigned, to issue final agency decisions on applications for alternate 
care.  Consequently, this decision constitutes final agency action, and there is no 
appeal to the commissioner.  Judicial review in a district court pursuant to Iowa Code 
Chapter 17A is the avenue for an appeal.

ISSUE 
The issue under consideration is whether the defendants should be ordered to 
authorize and provide the claimant with a left hip replacement surgery as recommended 
by Dr. Nassif. 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
 On December 2, 2025, counsel for the claimant sent defendants’ counsel a letter 
outlining their dissatisfaction with the medical care authorized by the defendants.  The 
claimant represented in their letter as follows: 
1. Claimant was sent by your client to Dr. Jeffrey Nassif at Physicians’ Clinic of 
Iowa for an appointment on November 11, 2025. 
  
2. At the November 11, 2025, appointment, Dr. Nassif recommended a total hip

3. On November 12, 2025, [claimant’s counsel] informed [defendants’ counsel] 
via email that Dr. Nassif had recommended a hip replacement surgery and 
requested authorization of the same. I sent another email on November 14

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Iowa Code 85.27(4) provides, in relevant part: 
For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish reasonable 
services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has the right to 
choose the care….  The treatment must be offered promptly and be 
reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the 
employee.  If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the care 
offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such dissatisfaction 
to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the employer and 
the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited to treat the 
injury.  If the employer and employee cannot agree on such alternate care, 
the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable proofs of the 
necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
Iowa Code 85.27(4). See Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 433 (Iowa 
1997).   
“Iowa Code section 85.27(4) affords an employer who does not contest the 
compensability of a workplace injury a qualified statutory right to control the medical 
care provided to an injured employee.”  Ramirez-Trujillo v. Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 
N.W.2d 759, 769 (Iowa 2016) (citing R.R. Donnelly & Sons v. Barnett, 670 N.W.2d 190, 
195, 197 (Iowa 2003)).  “In enacting the right-to-choose provision in section 85.27(4), 
our legislature sought to balance the interests of injured employees against the 
competing interests of their employers.”  Ramirez-Trujillo, 878 N.W.2d at 770-71 (citing 
Bell Bros., 779 N.W.2d at 202, 207; IBP, Inc. v. Harker, 633 N.W.2d 322, 326-27 (Iowa 
2001)).   
The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except where the 
employer has denied liability for the injury.  Iowa Code section 85.27; Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening, October 16, 1975).  An employer’s right to select 
the provider of medical treatment to an injured worker does not include the right to 
determine how an injured worker should be diagnosed, evaluated, treated, or other 
matters of professional medical judgment.  Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 
866389 (Declaratory Ruling, May 19, 1988).  Reasonable care includes care necessary 
to diagnose the condition, and defendants are not entitled to interfere with the medical 
judgment of its own treating physician.  Pote v. Mickow Corp., File No. 694639 (Review-
Reopening Decision, June 17, 1986).

The employer must furnish “reasonable medical services and supplies and 
reasonable and necessary appliances to treat an injured employee.”  Stone Container 
Corp. v. Castle, 657 N.W.2d 485, 490 (Iowa 2003)(emphasis in original).  Such 
employer-provided care “must be offered promptly and be reasonably suited to treat the 
injury without undue inconvenience to the employee.”  Iowa Code section 85.27(4).  
More specifically in Long, the court noted that “the employer’s obligation under the 
statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.”  Long v. Roberts 
Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122, 128 (Iowa 1995).   
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment - and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See e.g. 
Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3); Bell Bros. Heating and Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d 
193, 209 (Iowa 2010); Long, 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  More specifically in Long, 
the court noted that “the employer’s obligation under the statute turns on the question of 
reasonable necessity, not desirability.”  Long, 528 N.W.2d at 128.   
“Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.”  
Long, 528 N.W.2d at 123; Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 436.  As the party 
seeking relief in the form of alternate care, the employee bears the burden of proving 
that the authorized care is unreasonable.  Id. at 124; Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d at 209; Pirelli-
Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 436.  Because “the employer’s obligation under the 
statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability,” an injured 
employee’s dissatisfaction with employer-provided care, standing alone, is not enough 
to find such care unreasonable.  Id.  However, if substantial evidence exists that the 
care authorized by the employer was ineffective, it may be found that the care was not 
reasonable.  Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 437.   
The parties agree that the authorized treating physician, Dr. Nassif, 
recommended a left hip replacement surgery for James Raney.  While the defendants 
admitted liability in their answer and verbally at the outset of the hearing, it became 
apparent from listening to the defendants’ arguments on and off the record that the 
defendants were not prepared to take a position as to causation and/or compensability 
for the recommended left hip replacement surgery.  Thus, the defendants were not 
willing to authorize treatment recommended by Dr. Nassif.  The defendants noted that 
Dr. Nassif had yet to make any opinion as to causation for the requested left hip 
replacement surgery.  Based on this, the defendants were unwilling to authorize the 
requested surgery, as they claimed they continued to investigate the necessity of the 
proposed surgery.   
The defendants argued that they accepted liability, and thus, the question is 
whether the authorized care is unreasonable.  It became apparent from listening to the 
defendants’ arguments that, while they admitted liability in their answer, they were not 
willing to accept causation for the surgery recommended by Dr. Nassif.  The claimant 
argued that this should result in a dismissal.   
As noted above, alternate medical care proceedings are:

…limited to determining the reasonableness and necessity of medical care 
sought by an employee as an alternative to the care furnished by the 
employer.  Thus, if a compensability issue arises in the course of an 
alternate care dispute, the commissioner cannot order that the alternate 
care sought by the employee be furnished by the employer prior to a 
determination of the compensability of the injury in a contested case 
proceeding or some other proceeding. 
See R.R. Donnelly & Sons v. Barnett, 670 N.W.2d 190, 195, 197 (Iowa 2003).  While 
the defendants admitted liability in their answer, it became obvious during the hearing 
that causation and/or compensability was still an issue.  This was illustrated by a 
number of arguments made by the defendants’ including that they were not prepared to 
authorize the treatment recommended by Dr. Nassif because he had not issued any 
opinions on causation.   
 
As there are issues related to compensability and/or causation, this matter 
should be dismissed.  An alternate care proceeding cannot be used to determine 
causation and/or compensability. 
By challenging causation and/or compensability, the defendants’ constructively 
denied liability, even if their answer states differently.  Therefore, the claimant may 
obtain reasonable medical care from any provider for this treatment, at claimant’s 
expense, and seek reimbursement for such care using regular claim proceedings before 
this agency.  Haack v. Von Hoffman Graphics, File No. 1268172 (App. July 31, 2002); 
Kindhart v. Fort Des Moines Hotel, I Iowa Industrial Comm’r Decisions No. 3, 611 (App. 
March 27, 1985).  “[T]he employer has no right to choose the medical care when 
compensability is contested.”  Bell Bros. Heating and Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 
N.W.2d 193, 204 (Iowa 2010).  The defendants are precluded from asserting an 
authorization defense as to any future treatment during their period of denial.  Brewer-
Strong v. HNI Corp., 913 N.W.2d 235 (Iowa 2018).   
IT IS THEREFORE ORDERED: 
1. Claimant’s application for alternate medical care regarding the above named 
defendants is dismissed without prejudice. 
  
2. If claimant seeks to recover the charges incurred in obtaining care for the 
condition for which defendants constructively denied liability, defendants are 
barred from asserting lack of authorization as a defense to those charges 
during the period of their denial.
