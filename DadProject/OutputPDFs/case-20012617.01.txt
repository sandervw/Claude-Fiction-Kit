STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Jose Isaac Ortega, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits against Joy Cone Company, a/k/a BoDeans, employer, and 
Travelers Indemnity Co. of CT, insurer, both as defendants for an accepted work injury 
dated October 10, 2020. This case was heard on January 24, 2024, and considered 
fully submitted on February 14, 2024, upon the simultaneous filing of briefs.   
The record consists of Joint Exhibits 1-7, Claimant’s Exhibits 1-6, and 
Defendants’ Exhibits A-B, along with the testimony of claimant, via Interpreter Alina 
Salvat, and Leslie Gilmore, a witness for the defendants.   
ISSUES 
1. Whether claimant has sustained a whole body injury or a scheduled member 
injury; 
 
2. The extent of the injury; 
 
3. Whether claimant is entitled to permanent total disability;  
 
4. Whether claimant is entitled to permanent total disability under the odd-lot 
doctrine.

STIPULATIONS 
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised 
or discussed in this decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations. 
The parties agree claimant sustained a work-related injury which arose out of 
and in the course of employment on October 10, 2020. They further agree that the injury 
was the cause of temporary disability, entitlement to which is no longer in dispute, and a 
permanent disability.  
At the time of the injury, claimant’s gross earnings were $770.39 per week, and 
he was married and entitled to four exemptions. Based on the foregoing, the weekly 
benefit rate is $529.631.

At the time of the hearing, claimant Jose Isaac Ortega was a 72-year-old person. 
His educational background consisted of completing a ninth-grade education in Mexico. 
He is not fluent in English. His past work history included serving as a taxi driver and 
some manufacturing-type jobs while living in Mexico. He immigrated to the U.S. in 2019. 
Once in the U.S., he initially obtained employment at a pallet assembly company as a

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Defendants maintain that the situs of the injury controls. Claimant argues that the 
injury was caused in part by a brachial plexus injury combined with a left humerus 
fracture and therefore a whole-body injury.   
Claimant argues that brachial plexus injuries have been considered whole body 
injuries in the past.  Hoskey v. Kinze Mfg. Inc., File No. 5056163 (Arb. March 6, 2018); 
Galvan v. City of Des Moines, File No. 5063402 (Arb. July 9, 2019); Matthew Birke v. 
Wabash Valley Transportation, File No. 5021256 (Arb. February 20, 2008); Larry 
Bettmeng v. Bryan Construction, File No. 1004986 (Arb. March 17, 1994). When a 
scheduled member injury is combined with an unscheduled member injury the entire 
disability is measured by the industrial loss standard.  Mortimer v. Fruehauf Corp., 502 
N.W.2d 12, 16 (Iowa 1993). In this case, claimant argues that along with the left 
humerus fracture, claimant sustained a brachial plexus injury. According to Dr. 
Sassman the brachial plexus nerve is located in the neck and extends into the axillary 
area down the arm and therefore is an injury to the body.  (CE 1:015). 
The cases cited by claimant as support are brachial plexus injuries that involve 
pre-2017 shoulder injuries. Pre-2017 shoulder injuries were considered to be industrial 
in nature. There was no specific finding that a brachial plexus injury was a whole-body 
injury. It should be noted that in Galvan v. City of Des Moines, Dr. Sassman assigned 
an impairment rating for the brachial plexus injury separate from a cervical spine and 
right shoulder rating, but did not give a separate brachial plexus impairment rating in the 
present case.  
Defendants assert that Dr. Sassman is the only medical expert that opines 
claimant sustained a neck injury and that claimant’s loss of function is limited to his arm 
by his own testimony. Both arguments are true, but Dr. Samuelson and Dr. Gallant both 
diagnosed claimant with brachial plexus injuries, and Dr. Samuelson was concerned 
about a cervical nerve injury, but it does not appear that concern was given follow up.  
It is possible that Dr. Sassman does not provide a neck impairment rating due to 
the brachial plexus injury and the AMA Guides, 5th Edition, which instructs physicians to 
rate deficits caused by brachial plexus injuries as upper extremity injuries.  
Even though the mechanism of injury may have included “jerking and tugging of 
the arm into the machine” which could have caused strain in the neck, there is no 
evidence that claimant sustained a neck injury. He does not complain of neck pain, nor 
does he report any reduced function in the neck. He was not treated for any neck injury.  
Despite claimant sustaining a brachial plexus injury, it does not appear that the 
brachial plexus is causing pain in the neck or shoulder. The deficits, both in range of

motion and in sensation, are limited to the upper extremity by Dr. Sassman’s 
examination and in claimant’s own testimony. “It is the anatomical situs of the 
permanent injury or impairment which determines whether the schedules in section 
85.34(2)(a) - (t) are applied.”  Lauhoff Grain Co. v. McIntosh, 395 N.W.2d 834 (Iowa 
1986).  
 It is found claimant sustained an upper extremity and shoulder injury but not a 
whole-body injury.   
Alternatively, claimant argues that a brachial plexus injury limited to the shoulder 
with additional injuries such as a fractured humerus, a carpal tunnel injury or an ulnar 
nerve injury qualifies as a body as a whole injury.  Claimant cited Anderson v. 
Bridgestone Americas, File No. 5067475 (Arb. Sept. 2, 2021) (Affirmed on Appeal); 
however, that case has been reversed on appeal to the Supreme Court. The Supreme 
Court determined in Anderson v. Bridgestone Americas, Inc., 4 N.W.3d 676 (Iowa 2024)  
that a shoulder injury is not compensated under 85.34(2)(v) but under 85.34(2)(n). In 
Anderson, the injured worker suffered impairment to the right shoulder and right arm, 
and the Supreme Court found that those injuries should be separately compensated as 
a shoulder injury and an arm injury.  Following the guideline of Anderson, claimant 
should be compensated for two scheduled injuries. 
Dr. Sassman’s impairment ratings reflect claimant’s consistent reports of pain 
and loss of function and are based on the AMA Guides, 5th Edition. (CE 1:14)  The 
breakdown between the shoulder and left upper extremity are as follows: 
Shoulder:  
 9 percent upper extremity impairment (for the axillary motor deficit) 
 4 percent upper extremity impairment (for the suprascapular motor deficit) 
For the shoulder, claimant is entitled to 52 weeks of compensation  
Upper extremity:  
A combined 53 percent; however, as noted by defendants, the 9 percent rating to 
the ulnar nerve is for claimant’s deformation of his right pinky which is a congenital 
defect and unrelated to the work injury.  
For the arm, claimant is entitled to 110 weeks of compensation. 
Because claimant’s injuries are scheduled member injuries, claimant is not 
entitled to a permanent total disability finding nor benefits under the odd-lot doctrine.

qualifier “medical” and thus the agency’s position has been that the reasonable costs of 
a vocational evaluation fall under 4.33 and can be assessed as a practitioner report
