CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The first issue to be determined is whether claimant’s work injury resulted in a 
permanent disability. 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury actually occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 
employment. Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 150 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. 
Dial Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309, 311 (Iowa 1996). The words “arising out of” refer to the 
cause or source of the injury. The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 
circumstances of the injury. 2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124, 128 (Iowa 
1995).  An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists 
between the injury and the employment. Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. The injury must 
be a rational consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely 
incidental to the employment. Koehler Elec. v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1, 3 (Iowa 2000); 
Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it 
happens within a period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may 
be when performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties 
or doing an activity incidental to them. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d at 150. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
A possibility of causation is not sufficient; a probability is necessary. Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996) (citing Holmes v. Bruce 
Motor Freight, Inc., 215 N.W.2d 296, 297 (Iowa 1974)). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole,

621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 
(Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
Claimant contends the work injury of November 28, 2022, caused a permanent 
disability.  Defendant argues claimant’s November 28, 2022, injury resulted in a 
temporary disability, and that claimant’s injury at home is what ultimately caused 
claimant’s permanent disability.  (Defendant’s Post-Hearing Brief, pages 10-13) 
Claimant testified he injured his arm at work on November 28, 2022, while getting 
a box of powder paint off a shelf.  He said when he pulled the box, he felt and heard 
something give in his right shoulder.  (Tr., pp. 15-18) 
Claimant was initially treated conservatively.  He was placed on light-duty jobs 
that did not require a lot of repetitive activity at or above shoulder level.

Claimant indicated at that time the shoulder was 95 percent improved.  (JE 1, pp. 52-53;

hand and had dropped his paint gun.  (Id.)  Claimant was later subsequently assessed

Claimant had an EMG/nerve conduction study in April 2023, indicating carpal

Besides Nurse Practitioner Kalita, four other experts have opined regarding the 
permanent nature of claimant’s work injury. 
Dr. Foad evaluated claimant once for an IME.  Dr. Foad opined the November 
2022 work injury was temporary in nature and was an aggravation of a pre-existing 
condition.  (Ex. A, p. 8) 
In a letter written by claimant’s counsel, Dr. Hoffman indicated claimant’s biceps 
injury and rotator cuff tear probably occurred over time due to his repetitive activities at 
Hon and the “long head biceps tendon eventually tore while he was picking up boxes at 
Hon on 11/28/2022.”  (Ex. 6, p. 23) 
Dr. Hoffman’s opinion contains a few discrepancies.  First, the evidence indicates 
that claimant actually tore his long head biceps tendon at home; not while working at 
Hon.  The record indicates claimant did not have a “Popeye deformity” bicep until after 
his incident at home.  (JE 5, p. 89)  There is no evidence claimant developed his biceps 
tear on November 28, 2022.  Dr. Hoffman’s letter also does not refer to the self-reported 
incident that happened to claimant at home in January 2023.  Given these 
discrepancies, Dr. Hoffman’s opinion regarding causation of claimant’s permanent 
impairment are found not convincing. 
Dr. Darmafall provided care for claimant and performed shoulder surgery.  Dr. 
Darmafall gave her opinions of causation of claimant’s permanent impairment in a letter 
written by claimant’s counsel.  Dr. Darmafall indicated claimant’s tendon tear and the 
rotator cuff tears likely occurred over time due to repetitive use.  (Ex. 7, p. 25)  In brief, 
Dr. Darmafall opines claimant’s work at Hon suggests an overuse injury.  (Ex. 7, p. 25) 
There are several concerns with Dr. Darmafall’s opinion regarding the cause of 
claimant’s permanent impairment.  Dr. Darmafall indicates claimant’s shoulder and 
biceps injury occurred over time due to overuse.  However, claimant testified at hearing 
that while pulling a box of paint off a shelf, he felt and heard something in his right 
shoulder.  This would suggest a traumatic cause of claimant’s shoulder injury, and not a 
repetitive cumulative injury.  There is no evidence in the medical records claimant had 
problems with his biceps tendon after his injury at work.  All evidence indicates the 
biceps tear occurred after the incident at home.  Dr. Darmafall does not address how 
claimant’s condition could have improved to be 95 percent better on January 12, 2023, 
and yet his need for surgery to the tendon and shoulder were caused by the November 
2022 work injury at Hon.  Given these problems with Dr. Darmafall’s opinion, her 
opinions regarding permanent impairment are found not convincing. 
Dr. Crites evaluated claimant once for an IME.  Dr. Crites opined that claimant’s 
work at Hon was a significant factor in causing claimant’s rotator cuff tendinopathy and 
biceps tendinopathy in a cumulative fashion over time.  (Ex. 9, p. 36)

His opinions regarding causation are found not convincing. 
Claimant testified at hearing that he had a traumatic injury at work on November 
28, 2022, while lifting a box of paint from a shelf.  The records of claimant’s treatment 
indicated he reported his symptoms had improved after approximately a month and a 
half of conservative treatment.  Part of claimant’s improvement was due to his 
placement on a light-duty job at Hon.  However, the record indicates that claimant 
returned to work at full-duty with no restrictions to his paint job for two weeks.  After two 
weeks of doing his regular job, medical records note claimant still had slight 
improvement in his symptoms.  There is no indication that claimant required surgical 
intervention at the time of the January 12, 2023, evaluation by Nurse Practitioner Kalita.  
It was not until claimant had an incident at home, where his biceps tendon ruptured, that 
claimant’s symptoms regarding his right upper extremity flared and claimant ultimately 
required surgery.  The causation opinions of Dr. Hoffman, Dr. Crites, and Dr. Darmafall 
are not convincing regarding permanent impairment of the work injury.  The opinions of 
Nurse Practitioner Kalita and Dr. Foad concerning the causation of permanent 
impairment are found more convincing.  Based on this, claimant has failed to carry his 
burden of proof that he sustained a permanent disability caused by the November 28, 
2022, injury. 
I recognize that claimant had a job requiring he regularly work at or above 
shoulder level.  I also recognize that his injury at home sounds as if it involved light or 
relatively easy lifting of an object.  However, the majority of claimant’s experts opine that 
claimant had a cumulative injury.  Claimant’s testimony is that his injury of November

resulted in a permanent impairment, the issues of claimant’s entitlement to permanent 
partial disability benefits, temporary disability benefits, and alternate medical care, are 
all found to be moot. 
The next issue to be determined is if claimant is entitled to reimbursement of his 
IME. 
Iowa Code section 85.39 permits an employee to be reimbursed for subsequent 
examination by a physician of the employee's choice where an employer-retained 
physician has previously evaluated “permanent disability” and the employee believes 
that the initial evaluation is too low.   Iowa Code § 85.39(2) (2023).  The section also 
permits reimbursement for reasonably necessary transportation expenses incurred and 
for any wage loss occasioned by the employee attending the subsequent examination.  
Iowa Code § 85.39 (1), (2).   
The Iowa Supreme Court has provided a literal interpretation of the plain 
language of Iowa Code section 85.39, stating that section only allows the employee to 
obtain an independent medical evaluation at the employer’s expense if an evaluation of 
permanent disability has already been made by an employer retained physician.  Des 
Moines Area Reg’l Transit Auth. v. Young, 867 N.W.2d 839, 847 (Iowa 2015). Under 
the Young decision, an employee can only obtain an IME at the employer's expense if 
an evaluation of permanent disability has been made by an employer-retained 
physician. Id. 
An employer is only liable to reimburse an employee for the cost of an 
examination conducted pursuant to this subsection if the injury for which the employee 
is being examined is determined to be compensable.  Iowa Code § 85.39(2) (2017).  
“An employer is not liable for the cost of such examination if the injury for which the 
employee is being examined is determined not to have a compensable injury. A 
determination of the reasonableness of a fee for an examination made pursuant to this 
subsection, shall be based on the typical fee charged by a medical provider to perform 
an impairment rating in the local area where the examination is conducted.”  Iowa Code 
§ 85.39(2); Tweeten v. Tweeten, 999 N.W.2d 270, 282 (Iowa 2023).   
Iowa Code section 85.39(2), as amended, allows an employee with a 
compensable injury to be reimbursed the reasonable cost of an examination. See Iowa 
Code § 85.39(2)(2017); Mid American Construction LLC v. Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d 838, 849

(Iowa 2024). Under the statute, an examination may include reviewing medical records, 
conducting a physical exam, opining on causation, assessing permanent impairment, 
assigning restrictions, and recommending further care. Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d at 851.  
The burden is on the claimant to show the reasonableness of his or her chosen 
physician’s IME fee. Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d at 852; see also Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e)(stating “Ordinarily, the burden of proof on an issue is upon the party who 
would suffer loss if the issue were not established.”); Pexa v. Auto Owners Ins. Co., 686 
N.W.2d 150, 156 (Iowa 2004)(stating “the plaintiff has the burden to prove the 
reasonable value of the [medical] service rendered”).  The reasonableness of an IME 
fee is a question of fact to be decided by the commissioner. See Quaker Oats Co. v. 
Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 154 (Iowa 1996). The reasonableness of an IME fee must be 
based upon the typical fee charged by a medical provider performing an impairment 
rating in the local area where the IME is conducted. Iowa Code § 85.39 (2); Sandlin, 2 
N.W.3d at 852.   
Dr. Foad, the employer-retained expert, issued his opinions regarding permanent 
impairment in an August 31, 2023 report.  Dr. Crites, the employee-retained expert, 
gave his opinion of permanent impairment in a September 20, 2024 report.  Dr. Crites 
opined that his costs for his report were reasonable for this area.  (Ex. 9, p. 39)  Based 
on this record, it is found claimant is due reimbursement for Dr. Crites’ report. 
The next issue to be determined is whether there is a causal connection between 
the injury and the claimed medical expenses.  As noted, claimant failed to carry his 
burden of proof that he sustained a permanent impairment as a result of the November 
28, 2022, work injury.  Dr. Foad found that claimant had reached maximum medical 
improvement as of January 26, 2023.  Defendant is liable for all medical expenses 
related to claimant’s shoulder and upper extremity injury occurring before January 26, 
2023.  Defendant is not liable for medical expenses after this date. 
ORDER 
THEREFORE IT IS ORDERED: 
That claimant shall take nothing in the way of permanent partial disability benefits 
or temporary benefits from this proceeding. 
That defendant shall reimburse claimant for the costs associated with Dr. Crites’ 
IME. 
That defendant shall pay medical expenses up to January 26, 2023.  Defendant 
is not liable for medical expenses after that date. 
That both parties shall pay their own costs. 
That defendant shall file subsequent reports of injury as required by Rule 876 
IAC 3.1(2).
