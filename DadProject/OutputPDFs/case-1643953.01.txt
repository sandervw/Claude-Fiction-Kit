JUDICIAL REVIEW RULING 
 
 
On March 5, 2021, Carolyn Sallis (“Sallis”) filed a petition seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits for an injury she sustained while working for the City of Waterloo 
(“Waterloo”) at the Waterloo Public Library.  On May 4, 2022, Deputy Worker’s 
Compensation Commissioner William Grell entered an arbitration decision that Carolyn 
Sallis sustained an injury to her right shoulder and right arm which resulted in a sequelae 
injury of her left shoulder and left arm and the combined loss resulted in a 35 percent 
industrial disability. On August 29, 2022, Workers’ Compensation Commissioner Joseph 
S. Cortese II entered an appeal decision which affirmed the central holdings of the 
underlying decision but evaluated that Sallis sustained only a 20 percent industrial 
disability. On September 23, 2022, the City of Waterloo filed a Petition for Judicial review 
of the Iowa Division of Workers’ Compensation (DWC) actions as previously described. 
Through no fault of the parties, a filing error resulted in a substantial delay in the Court’s 
notice of this petition and the Court is regretful of any inconvenience or discomfort this 
has caused for the parties. The Court has now reviewed the briefs filed and analyzed the 
underlying case law. On this day, the Court now finds the Iowa Workers’ Compensation 
Commissioner’s decision is supported by substantial evidence and is affirmed.  
Findings of Fact 
 
Sallis, an employee of the City of Waterloo, suffered an injury on February 4, 2018 
on her way to work at the Waterloo Public Library by falling and landing on her right arm 
which caused a displaced fracture in her shoulder. Sallis received medical attention and 
her right shoulder was placed in an immobilizer. The immobilizer in addition to subsequent

Standard of Review 
 
The Iowa Administrative Procedure Act governs judicial review of an administrative 
agency’s action.1 Judicial review of an administrative agency is appellate and limited to 
deciding issues of law.2  A court must reverse the decision of an administrative agency 
when the agency’s decision has prejudiced the substantial rights of the petitioner and the 
decision is not supported by substantial evidence or is based on errors of law.3  
The Administrative Procedure Act defines substantial evidence as “the quantity 
and quality of evidence that would be deemed sufficient by a neutral, detached, and 
reasonable person, to establish the fact at issue when the consequences resulting from 
the establishment of that fact are understood to be serious and of great importance”.4  
This statute has been interpreted very broadly to mean that an agency decision is based 
on substantial evidence if the finding was gathered from any part of the evidence.5  The 
proper inquiry is not whether there was substantial evidence to warrant the decision that 
the administrative agency did not make but whether there is substantial evidence to 
support the decision that was made.6

In administrative review, the court must not reassess the weight of evidence as 
this determination is exclusively within the administrative agency’s domain.7 The 
administrative agency has the sole authority to determine the credibility of witnesses and 
may rely on the testimony of “witnesses whom the [agency] believed”.8 The Court may 
not interfere with agency findings where there is a conflict in the evidence or when 
reasonable minds may disagree about the inferences drawn.9 The Legislature has not 
clearly vested the interpretation of workers’ compensation statutes and related case law 
with the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commission however, the Commission has the 
discretion to make factual determinations.10 If this authority has been delegated to the 
administrative agency, the application of law may only be reversed if the decision is 
“irrational, illogical, or wholly unjustifiable”.11  
Applicable Law 
When an employee has suffered an injury causing permanent partial disability in 
the scope of their employment, they are entitled to compensation for a healing period 
following the injury.12 The Iowa Legislature has set out 21 specific types of scheduled 
injuries for which they have assigned a specific number of weeks of compensation.13 If 
an injury does not fit the description of a scheduled injury, it is an unscheduled injury and 
the compensation is determined by reducing a 500-week assessment by the employee’s 
earning capacity.14 If an employee who is eligible for compensation based on an 
unscheduled injury is offered work by their employer for the same or greater salary or 
returns to work, the compensation is determined based on functional impairment rather 
than earning capacity.15 When determining the employee’s earning capacity, the Iowa 
Workers’ Compensation Commission should evaluate several factors, such as the

Analysis 
The City of Waterloo brings this petition to review the final decision of the Worker’s 
Compensation Commissioner’s affirming the arbitration decision with modification, in part.  
In the Appeal Decision filed on August 29, 2022, the Iowa Workers’ Compensation 
Commissioner denied the City of Waterloo’s claims and affirmed the underlying decision 
only making a reduction in the determination of Sallis’ compensation assessment. The 
City of Waterloo now asserts that both the appeal decision and the underlying decision 
are based on mistaken interpretations of Iowa Law and additionally that the application of 
law based on the facts presented is irrational, illogical, or wholly unjustifiable.  Waterloo 
raises two challenges in this request for judicial review.  It first argues that the 
Commissioner’s finding that Sallis the combined effect of the right arm and shoulder injury 
and the left arm and should injury should be compensated as an unscheduled injury under 
Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v), is an erroneous interpretation of law and there is not 
substantial evidence to support the finding.  Waterloo second argues that the 
Commissioner should have used a functional impairment rating in the calculating the 
permanent partial disability benefits and not the industrial disability analysis under Iowa 
Code section 85.34(2)(v) when calculating permanent partial disability benefits. 
1. 
Injury Schedule: 
 
The Court first reviews Waterloo’s assertion that Sallis sustained two scheduled member 
injuries on her right side and her left side. The Workers’ Compensation Commissioner 
affirmed the determination in the arbitration decision that the Respondent’s combined loss 
to her right shoulder, right upper arm, left shoulder, and left upper arm constituted an 
unscheduled injury as such an injury is not otherwise described as a scheduled injury. 
Waterloo asserts that the Commissioner’s conclusion that the Respondent sustained an 
injury to both her right arm and her right shoulder is an erroneous interpretation of Iowa 
law because a loss of function of a shoulder would necessarily result in a loss of function 
of the upper arm.  The Iowa Supreme Court defined shoulder under section 85.34(2)(n),

workers’ compensation law evidencing the Legislature’s belief that the combined effect of 
injuries to multiple scheduled members is more disabling than an injury to only one 
scheduled member and is therefore deserving of increased compensation.  The two 
scheduled member provision evidences this intent because it assigns a greater number 
of weeks of compensation for injuries to two scheduled members than if the injuries were 
considered in isolation and it allows an injured worker to be compensated for the rest of 
their life in the event that the injury causes permanent and total disability.  The law clearly 
reflects the Legislature’s belief that the combined effect of multiple scheduled injuries is 
far more devastating to a worker than a singular injury and that shoulders are the most 
disabling scheduled injuries. Construing section 85.34(2)(n) to include injuries to both 
shoulders would therefore run afoul of the Legislature’s intent because the Legislature 
cannot have intended to treat injuries to both shoulders less favorably and award fewer 
benefits than for less disabling injuries to two arms, two hands, etc.  The Deputy’s decision 
that the right arm injury affected the claimant’s right shoulder and caused reduced range 
of motion in that shoulder, as well as a distal clavicle excision was based on a factual 
finding based on which medical expert he found to be more credible. Further the Deputy

2. Calculation of Permanent Partial Disability Benefits – Functional Impairment 
vs. Industrial Disability: 
Waterloo also disputes the awarding of industrial disability benefits to Sallis for the 
combined loss to her rights shoulder, right upper extremity, left shoulder, and left upper 
extremity.  It argues the plain reading of Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v) does not support 
the finding in the Arbitration Decision, which was affirmed by the Worker’s Compensation 
Commissioner, that Sallis was entitled to use an industrial disability analysis in which to 
determine her entitlement to permanent disability benefits.  Under the facts of this case, 
Sallis voluntarily retired from her position with the City of Waterloo.  This was not a 
situation where Sallis returned to work and was subsequently terminated from 
employment by that employer.  Sallis argues that at the time of the hearing before the 
Worker’s Compensation Deputy Commissioner, she was no longer employed by 
Waterloo.  Therefore, she asks that the industrial disability award be affirmed.   
The Court in reviewed Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v) finds that the legislature intended 
the use of the industrial disability benefits calculation in situations where an injured 
employee is subsequently terminated after returning to work as a deterrent to employers.  
Or said alternatively, it provides an incentive to employers to stick with injured workers as 
they get back to work.  The Court finds the Commissioner erred in its conclusion that 
Sallis’ benefits should be calculated according to an industrial disability analysis.  
Waterloo did not terminate Sallis; she voluntarily retired from her position, albeit sooner 
than she had wanted to.  Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v) authorizes the use of an industrial 
disability analysis only in situations where an employer terminates an injured worker after 
they have returned to work.  Therefore, the Commissioner ruling is modified and the
