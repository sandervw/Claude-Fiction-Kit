The Court heard this matter on August 8, 2025, on Petitioner’s Petition for Judicial 
Review of the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commissioner’s March 10, 2025 Appeal Decision. 
Having reviewed the parties’ briefs, the certified agency record, and the applicable law, the 
Court enters the following Order. 
I. PROCEDURAL HISTORY 
 
Petitioner Caroline Toe filed an Iowa Civil Rights Act (ICRA) complaint with the Iowa 
Civil Rights Commission (ICRC) on January 9, 2023, alleging sex discrimination arising from 
her employment with Defendant Kraft Heinz Food Company. The ICRC administratively closed 
her complaint on May 3, 2023, determining she was not entitled to relief under Iowa Code § 
216.6. Petitioner subsequently requested formal withdrawal of her ICRA complaint. 
 
On November 6, 2023, Petitioner filed a workers' compensation claim seeking benefits 
for mental-mental and mental-physical injuries allegedly arising from the same employment 
incidents. On August 19, 2024, Defendants moved for summary judgment, arguing preemption.

II. STANDARD OF REVIEW 
 
Judicial review of an agency decision is governed by the Iowa Administrative Procedure 
Act, Iowa Code Chapter 17A. Iowa Code § 10A.322 (formerly § 86.26); Meyer v. IBP, Inc., 710 
N.W.2d 213, 218 (Iowa 2006). Although the Commissioner resolved this case through summary 
judgment, the resulting ruling constitutes final agency action and is reviewed under Chapter 17A, 
not under the de novo standard applicable to summary judgment in district court. See generally 
Renda v. Iowa Civil Rights Commission, 784 N.W.2d 8, 10-11 (Iowa 2010). 
 
Pursuant to Iowa Code § 17A.19(10), a reviewing court may reverse, modify, or grant 
other appropriate relief if the agency's action is legally erroneous, unsupported by substantial 
evidence, or otherwise unreasonable, arbitrary, or capricious. A court may only interfere with the 
commissioner's decision if it is erroneous under one of the grounds enumerated in the statute, 
and a party's substantial rights have been prejudiced. Id. The district court acts in an appellate 
capacity to correct errors of law by the agency. Grundmeyer v. Weyerhaeuser Co., 649 N.W.2d 
744, 748 (Iowa 2002). The party challenging an agency decision bears the burden of 
demonstrating the action's invalidity and resulting prejudice. Iowa Code § 17A.19(8)(a).

III. PETITIONER'S ARGUMENTS 
 
Petitioner argues the agency erred in four ways: 
 
First, Petitioner argues the dismissal was arbitrary and capricious under Iowa Code § 
17A.19(10)(n) because she never pursued civil remedies under the ICRA, and the two-year 
statutory period to request a right-to-sue letter has now expired. 
 
Second, Petitioner contends the agency's interpretation of Ottumwa Housing Authority v. 
State Farm Fire & Casualty Co., 495 N.W.2d 723 (Iowa 1993), is irrational under Iowa Code § 
17A.19(10)(l) because her case differs from Ottumwa where plaintiffs actively pursued civil 
litigation. 
 
Third, Petitioner asserts the agency's application of law defies logic under Iowa Code § 
17A.19(10)(m) because the agency relied on a theoretical possibility rather than actual facts. 
 
Fourth, Petitioner maintains the agency failed to consider that both Defendants and the 
ICRC determined her facts did not support an ICRA sex discrimination claim under Iowa Code § 
17A.19(10)(j). 
IV. ANALYSIS 
A. Threshold Issue: Preemption as a Subject Matter Jurisdiction Bar 
 
As a threshold matter, the preemption doctrine applied by the agency operates as a 
limitation on the workers' compensation agency's subject matter jurisdiction. See generally 
Ottumwa Housing Authority v. State Farm Fire & Casualty Co., 495 N.W.2d 723, 729 (Iowa 
1993); Greenland v. Fairtron Corp., 500 N.W.2d 36, 37 (Iowa 1993), Channon v. United Parcel 
Serv., Inc., 629 N.W.2d 835, 857 (Iowa 2001). When comprehensive civil rights remedies are 
available for the same conduct alleged in a workers' compensation claim, the statutory quid pro 
quo that gives the workers' compensation system its exclusive jurisdiction is extinguished. See

Ottumwa Housing Authority, 495 N.W.2d at 729: Karen D. Gillespie, File No. 1042214, 1994 
WL 16029584, at *2 (Iowa Indus. Comm’r Oct. 24, 1994), Barbara D. Odell, File No. 1134607, 
1999 WL 33619868, at *1 (Iowa Indus. Comm’r Mar. 9, 1999), and Gary L. Louvar, File 
No.504837, 2018 WL 1952780, at *14 (Iowa Indus. Comm’r Apr. 9, 2018). This is not a merits 
determination but rather a jurisdictional prerequisite to the agency's authority to adjudicate the 
claim. 
B. The Controlling Legal Standard: Availability, Not Pursuit 
 
Before addressing Petitioner's four arguments, the Court must clarify the controlling legal 
standard that governs all of them. The Iowa Supreme Court in Ottumwa Housing Authority held 
that the “quid pro quo is gone when a sex discrimination claim under the state or federal statutes 
is available for the same acts alleged in the workers' compensation proceeding.” Baird v. 
Ottumwa Cmty. Sch. Dist., 551 N.W.2d 874, 876 (Iowa 1996) citing Ottumwa Housing 
Authority, 495 N.W.2d at 729. 
 
The critical term is "available." The preemption analysis turns on whether alternative 
statutory remedies are legally available to the claimant, not on whether the claimant actually 
pursues those remedies, intends to pursue them, or would likely succeed in obtaining them. This 
distinction is fundamental to all four of Petitioner's arguments. 
 
Three principles follow from this standard: First, the existence of a statutory right to 
pursue civil remedies establishes "availability" even if that right has not been exercised. A 
statutory mechanism is not "theoretical" merely because the claimant chooses not to use it. Iowa 
Code § 216.16(3)(a)(4) created an actual, enforceable legal right for Petitioner to request a right-
to-sue letter within two years of the ICRC's administrative closure. This right existed as a matter 
of law throughout the agency's consideration of this case. Second, jurisdictional determinations

based on availability are assessed at the time of the agency's ruling, not at some later date when 
statutory periods may have expired. At all relevant times, September 24, 2024, October 30, 2024, 
and March 10, 2025, the two-year statutory window remained open (the ICRC closed Petitioner's 
complaint on May 3, 2023, making the right-to-sue letter available until May 3, 2025). Third, 
subjective statements of intent not to pursue available remedies cannot override the objective 
existence of those remedies. Jurisdictional rules cannot rest on representations that may change 
at any time. Otherwise, claimants could manipulate jurisdictional requirements by temporarily 
disclaiming intent to pursue available remedies. 
 
These principles, derived from Ottumwa Housing Authority and applied by the agency, 
provide the framework for analyzing each of Petitioner's arguments. 
C. Substantial Evidence Supports the Agency's Factual Findings 
 
Respondents correctly argue that substantial evidence supports the agency's factual 
findings. The undisputed facts establish: (1) Petitioner filed an ICRA complaint on January 9, 
2023, based on sexual harassment allegations; (2) Petitioner's workers' compensation claim, filed 
November 6, 2023, arose from the same incidents; (3) at the time of all agency rulings, Petitioner 
retained the statutory right to request a right-to-sue letter; and (4) Petitioner neither requested 
such a letter nor pursued civil remedies. 
 
While Petitioner characterizes her appeal as challenging pure legal conclusions under §§ 
17A.19(10)(j), (l), (m), and (n), the substantial evidence standard applies to the agency’s factual 
determinations underlying those legal conclusions. Even applying the legal error standards 
Petitioner advocates, however, the agency’s decision withstands scrutiny.

D. Petitioner’s First Argument: The Agency’s Decision Was Not Arbitrary or Capricious 
Under Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(n) 
 
 
Petitioner argues the agency's dismissal was arbitrary and capricious because she never 
pursued civil remedies under the ICRA and the two-year statutory period has now expired. This 
argument fails because it misapplies the controlling legal standard explained above. 
1. Jurisdictional Determinations Are Made at the Time of the Agency's Ruling 
The subsequent expiration of the two-year statutory window on May 3, 2025, after all agency 
rulings were issued, cannot retroactively confer jurisdiction that did not exist when the agency 
ruled. This principle follows directly from the availability standard: if the remedies were 
available when the agency ruled, preemption applies regardless of later developments. 
 
Accepting Petitioner’s argument would create perverse incentives, allowing claimants to 
file ICRA complaints, simultaneously file workers’ compensation claims, strategically delay 
proceedings, and then argue that the passage of time eliminated the preemption bar. This 
approach would undermine both the workers’ compensation system’s exclusivity and the ICRA’s 
comprehensive remedial scheme. 
2. Failure to Pursue Available Remedies Does Not Confer Jurisdiction 
 
Respondents correctly argue that Petitioner's failure to request a right-to-sue letter from 
the Iowa Civil Rights Commission and pursue her civil remedies in state or federal court does 
not give the workers' compensation agency subject matter jurisdiction over claims she made on 
the basis of sexual harassment, since such claims are compensable under the Iowa Civil Rights 
Act. 
 
The critical question is not whether Petitioner actually pursued ICRA remedies, but 
whether those remedies were available to her. During the entire time the agency considered this 
matter, Petitioner had the statutory right to request a right-to-sue letter. Iowa Code § 
E-FILED                    CVCV068847 - 2025 NOV 09 06:56 PM             POLK    
CLERK OF DISTRICT COURT                    Page 6 of 17

7 
 
216.16(3)(a)(4). Petitioner's voluntary choice not to exercise this right does not transform the 
jurisdictional analysis. 
 
The Deputy Commissioner correctly observed that counsel’s representation of 
Petitioner’s intent “does not” bind the claimant, and “there is nothing preventing the claimant 
from seeking alternate counsel and pursuing her civil remedies under the ICRA during the two-
year period.” (10/30/2024 Ruling at 3). This reflects the reality that jurisdictional determinations 
must rest on objective legal availability, not subjective and changeable intent. Circumstances 
change, counsel changes, and litigation strategies evolve. 
3. Administrative Closure and Formal Withdrawal Do Not Eliminate Statutory Rights 
 
Petitioner emphasizes that she formally withdrew her ICRA complaint and that the ICRC 
administratively closed it. Neither action eliminates the statutory availability of civil remedies 
during the two-year period provided by Iowa Code § 216.16(3)(a)(4). 
 
The statute expressly provides that a complainant may request a right-to-sue letter within 
two years of the ICRC's notice that it will not issue a finding. This right exists independently of 
whether the complaint remains pending before the ICRC. Administrative closure is explicitly not 
an adjudication on the merits and does not bar pursuit of civil remedies through the right-to-sue 
process. See generally Petro v. Palmer College of Chiropractic, 945 N.W.2d 763 (2020). 
 
Petitioner's formal withdrawal reflects her decision not to participate in the ICRC's 
administrative process, but the statute does not condition the right-to-sue mechanism on 
maintaining an active administrative complaint. During the entire two-year statutory period, 
Petitioner could have requested, and the ICRC would have been statutorily obligated to issue—a 
right-to-sue letter authorizing civil litigation.

4. The Agency’s Decision Was Rational and Consistent with the Workers’ Compensation 
Quid Pro Quo 
 
 
The agency's decision serves the legitimate purpose of maintaining the exclusivity of the 
workers' compensation system where the Legislature has provided comprehensive alternative 
remedies. Iowa's workers' compensation scheme is premised on a fundamental exchange: 
employees surrender common law tort rights in exchange for prompt, no-fault benefits. Tripp v. 
Scott Emergency Commc'n Ctr., 977 N.W.2d 459, 464 (Iowa 2022). 
 
When alternative statutory schemes provide comprehensive remedies for the same 
conduct, as the ICRA does for sex discrimination, and those remedies remain available, the 
employee has not truly surrendered common law rights. Rather, the employee retains those rights 
in modified statutory form through the ICRA. 
 
Petitioner's situation results from her own litigation choices. She filed an ICRA 
complaint, chose not to pursue it administratively, chose not to request a right-to-sue letter 
during the statutory period, and chose not to file civil litigation. These choices prevented her 
from obtaining ICRA remedies, but they were choices made when those remedies remained 
legally available. 
 
For these reasons, the agency’s dismissal was not arbitrary or capricious under Iowa 
Code § 17A.19(10)(n). 
E. Petitioner’s Second Argument: The Agency’s Interpretation of Ottumwa Housing 
Authority Was Not Irrational Under Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(l) 
 
 
Petitioner contends the agency's interpretation of Ottumwa Housing Authority is irrational 
because her case differs from Ottumwa, where plaintiffs actively pursued civil litigation. This 
argument misconstrues Ottumwa's holding and improperly attempts to limit its scope.

1. Ottumwa Established the Availability Standard Applied Here 
 
In Ottumwa, the plaintiffs were actively litigating in state and federal courts, which made 
the preemption issue obvious. However, the Court did not limit its holding to cases of active 
litigation. Rather, the Court articulated a broader principle focused on "availability." As stated 
previously, the Iowa Supreme Court in Ottumwa Housing Authority held that the “quid pro quo 
is gone when a sex discrimination claim under the state or federal statutes is available for the 
same acts alleged in the workers' compensation proceeding.” Baird v. Ottumwa Cmty. Sch. Dist., 
551 N.W.2d 874, 876 (Iowa 1996) citing Ottumwa Housing Authority, 495 N.W.2d at 729. 
 
The agency's interpretation, that availability, rather than active pursuit, triggers 
preemption, is entirely consistent with both the language and rationale of Ottumwa Housing 
Authority. Limiting preemption to cases of active litigation would create an irrational loophole: 
claimants could avoid preemption by delaying civil litigation until after obtaining workers' 
compensation benefits, then pursuing ICRA claims for the same conduct. Such a result would 
eviscerate the exclusivity principle and permit the exact double recovery that the preemption 
doctrine prevents. 
2. Baird Is Distinguishable Because the ICRA Claim Had Been Resolved 
 
Petitioner argues her case is analogous to Baird v. Ottumwa Community School District, 
551 N.W.2d 874 (Iowa 1996). In Baird, the plaintiff had settled and resolved her ICRA claim 
before filing her workers' compensation claim. The Court held that a genuine issue of material 
fact existed regarding whether the workers' compensation claim involved different conduct. The 
Court noted that the settlement "may be viewed as a method by which Baird's employer bought 
its peace on the civil rights claim, leaving the pending workers' compensation proceeding to run 
its course." Id. at 876.

The critical distinction is that in Baird, the plaintiff had already pursued and resolved her 
ICRA claim through settlement, thereby extinguishing the availability of future ICRA remedies. 
The only remaining question was whether the two claims involved the same conduct, a factual 
dispute requiring trial. Id. 
 
Here, Petitioner has neither pursued nor resolved her ICRA claim. She received no 
settlement, no damages, and no consideration for releasing her civil rights claims. ICRA 
remedies remained legally available throughout the agency proceedings. This case, therefore, 
falls squarely within Ottumwa Housing Authority, not Baird. 
3. Both Claims Arise from Identical Conduct 
 
The Baird court noted that "not all circumstances that would create a compensable claim 
for emotional distress benefits under our decision in Dunlavey... would give rise to a sexual 
discrimination claim under section 601A.6 [now Iowa Code § 216.6]." 551 N.W.2d at 876. This 
observation addresses whether the two claims involve the same underlying conduct. 
 
Here, there is no factual dispute about the identity of conduct. Petitioner's ICRA 
complaint alleged that male coworkers exposed themselves to her and engaged in sexually 
harassing conduct. Her workers' compensation claim alleges mental-mental and mental-physical 
injuries arising from these same incidents of sexual harassment.  
 
Because the same factual conduct underlies both claims, the Baird analysis is 
inapplicable. The agency correctly determined that Ottumwa Housing Authority governs this 
case. For these reasons, the agency's interpretation of Ottumwa Housing Authority was not 
irrational under Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(l).

F. Petitioner's Third Argument: The Agency's Application of Law Was Not Illogical Under 
Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(m) 
 
 
Petitioner asserts the agency's application of law defies logic because the agency relied 
on a "theoretical possibility" rather than actual facts, would lead to absurd results, and leaves 
Petitioner without a remedy. Each contention fails. 
1. Legal Availability Is an Actual Fact, Not a Theory 
 
Petitioner characterizes the agency's analysis as based on "theoretical" availability. This 
characterization misunderstands the nature of statutory rights. The availability of a right-to-sue 
letter under Iowa Code § 216.16(3)(a)(4) during the two-year statutory period was not 
theoretical; it was an actual, concrete legal right established by statute. A statutory right is not 
"theoretical" merely because it has not been exercised. Had Petitioner requested a right-to-sue 
letter during the statutory period, the ICRC would have been legally obligated to issue it. The 
existence of this enforceable legal right is a factual reality governed by the availability standard 
explained in Section B above. 
2. The Agency's Analysis Does Not Lead to Absurd Results 
 
Petitioner argues the agency's logic would permit dismissal of any workers' compensation 
claim based on the theoretical possibility of filing a tort action, even where Iowa's exclusivity 
doctrine (Iowa Code § 85.20) bars such claims. This argument misapprehends the distinction 
between hypothetical actions that would fail as a matter of law and actual statutory remedies 
created by the Legislature. For instance, an employee cannot file a viable negligence action 
against an employer for work-related injuries because such claims are barred by statute. (See 
Mehmedovic v. Tyson Foods Inc., 21 N.W.3d 412, 426 (2025). By contrast, the ICRA provides 
an actual, viable statutory remedy, with its own procedural mechanisms, that the Legislature 
E-FILED                    CVCV068847 - 2025 NOV 09 06:56 PM             POLK

12 
 
specifically designed to address employment discrimination. The distinction is between remedies 
the law prohibits and remedies the law affirmatively creates. 
3. Sexual Harassment Claims Are Compensable Under the ICRA 
 
Respondents correctly emphasize that sexual harassment claims of the type Petitioner 
alleged are compensable under the Iowa Civil Rights Act. Iowa Code § 216.6 prohibits sex 
discrimination in employment, including sexual harassment. The ICRA provides comprehensive 
remedies, including compensatory damages, back pay, front pay, emotional distress damages, 
and attorney fees. Iowa Code § 216.16(1). 
 
Petitioner argues that because the ICRC administratively closed her complaint and 
Defendants denied her allegations, the claims are not "compensable." This conflates 
compensability with the likelihood of success. A claim is compensable if it falls within the scope 
of remedies provided by statute, not based on whether the claimant would ultimately prevail. The 
record establishes that Petitioner alleged sexual harassment by male coworkers. Such allegations, 
if proven, would constitute sex discrimination compensable under Iowa Code § 216.6. Whether 
Petitioner could prove her allegations is a merits question irrelevant to the compensability and 
availability analysis. 
4. Petitioner’s Lack of Remedy Results from Her Own Litigation Choices 
 
Petitioner argues the agency's decision leaves her without any remedy. The Iowa 
Legislature provided comprehensive remedies for sex discrimination through the ICRA. When 
the Legislature provides such alternative remedies, and those remedies remain available to the 
claimant, the workers' compensation system need not provide duplicative relief. 
 
Petitioner's situation results from her litigation choices. She filed an ICRA complaint, 
chose not to pursue it administratively, chose not to request a right-to-sue letter during the 
E-FILED                    CVCV068847 - 2025 NOV 09 06:56 PM             POLK

statutory period, and chose not to file civil litigation. These choices prevented her from obtaining 
ICRA remedies, but they were choices made when those remedies remained legally available. 
The agency properly applied the preemption doctrine to prevent circumvention of the exclusivity 
system. For these reasons, the agency’s application of law to the facts was not illogical under 
Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(m). 
G. Petitioner’s Fourth Argument: The Agency Did Not Fail to Consider Relevant Evidence 
Under Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(j) 
 
 
Petitioner maintains the agency failed to consider that both Defendants and the ICRC 
determined her facts did not support an ICRA sex discrimination claim. This argument 
mischaracterizes both the record and the preemption analysis. 
1. The ICRC's Administrative Closure Is Not a Merits Determination 
 
The ICRC’s administrative closure reflects a preliminary determination that the agency 
would not pursue the matter administratively, not a merits determination that the allegations 
would fail to establish sex discrimination. The ICRC issues closure determinations for various 
reasons, including resource constraints, evidentiary insufficiency at the preliminary stage, and 
strategic priorities. Critically, administrative closure does not affect the availability of the right-
to-sue mechanism. Iowa Code § 216.16(3)(a)(4) expressly contemplates that complainants may 
request right-to-sue letters after the ICRC declines to pursue administrative relief. 
2. Defendants’ Denial Does Not Affect Compensability or Availability 
 
Defendants' denial of Petitioner's allegations reflects their litigation posture and factual 
defenses, not a concession regarding the compensability of such claims under Iowa law. Every 
contested case involves disputes about underlying facts. Sexual harassment allegations of the 
type Petitioner made are unquestionably compensable under Iowa Code § 216.6, regardless of 
whether Defendants contest the facts.

Petitioner conflates two distinct concepts: (1) whether a claim, if proven, would be 
compensable under the statute; and (2) whether the claimant can prove the necessary facts. 
Defendants' denial addresses only the latter. 
3. Petitioner's Clarification Does Not Alter the Availability Analysis 
 
In her Reply Brief, Petitioner clarifies that she is not arguing there are 
"additional/different facts" in her workers' compensation claim versus her ICRA complaint. 
Rather, she argues the agency failed to consider that both Defendants and the ICRC expressly 
stated the facts underlying her workers' compensation claim did not support a sex discrimination 
claim under Iowa Code § 216.6. This clarification does not change the analysis. Even accepting 
Petitioner's characterization, that Defendants denied liability and the ICRC found her allegations 
insufficient, this does not eliminate the availability of the right-to-sue mechanism. The statute 
provides that a complainant may request a right-to-sue letter within two years of the ICRC's 
determination not to issue a finding, regardless of the reason for that determination. Iowa Code § 
216.16(3)(a)(4). Whether the ICRC closed the case due to insufficient evidence, lack of probable 
cause, or any other reason, the statutory pathway to civil litigation remained open. The 
preemption analysis does not turn on whether preliminary evaluations by the ICRC or denials by 
Defendants suggest the claim lacks merit. It turns on whether the statutory mechanism for 
pursuing civil remedies remained available, and here, it unquestionably did throughout the entire 
time period relevant to the agency's rulings. 
4. The Preemption Analysis Does Not Require Merits Assessment 
 
The agency was not required to assess the likelihood that Petitioner would prevail in civil 
litigation. The relevant questions for preemption are: (1) whether ICRA remedies were available; 
(2) whether those remedies would address the same conduct alleged in the workers'

compensation claim; and (3) whether Petitioner had the statutory right to pursue those remedies 
during the relevant time period. The answer to all three questions is yes. The merits of the 
underlying discrimination claim, including credibility, proof, and affirmative defenses, are 
irrelevant to the preemption analysis. Ottumwa Housing Authority does not require an evaluation 
of claim strength. The case holds that when civil rights remedies are available for the same 
conduct, preemption applies. 495 N.W.2d at 729. Availability is a question of statutory scope 
and procedure, not factual or legal merit. 
V. CONCLUSION 
 
The Iowa Workers' Compensation Commissioner correctly determined that Petitioner's 
workers' compensation claim was preempted by the availability of civil remedies under the Iowa 
Civil Rights Act. At all times relevant to the agency's rulings, Petitioner retained the statutory 
right to pursue comprehensive remedies through the ICRA's right-to-sue mechanism. 
 
The agency properly applied the availability standard articulated in Ottumwa Housing 
Authority v. State Farm Fire & Casualty Co.: when civil rights remedies are available for the 
same conduct alleged in a workers' compensation claim, the statutory quid pro quo is 
extinguished and preemption applies. The agency's interpretation of this standard was not 
irrational. The agency's application of law was logical and consistent with statutory purposes. 
The agency properly considered all relevant evidence and correctly focused on legal availability 
rather than speculative merits assessments. The agency's decision was not arbitrary or capricious. 
 
Substantial evidence supports the agency's factual findings. Respondents correctly argue 
that Petitioner's failure to request a right-to-sue letter and pursue civil remedies does not confer 
subject matter jurisdiction on the workers' compensation agency over sexual harassment claims 
compensable under the Iowa Civil Rights Act.
