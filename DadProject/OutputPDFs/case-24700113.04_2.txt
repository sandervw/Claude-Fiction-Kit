This matter comes before the Court on Petitioner Mercy Medical Center's ("Mercy") 
Petition for Judicial Review of a final agency decision by the Iowa Workers' Compensation 
Commissioner dated January 27, 2025. A hearing was held on the appeal on June 27, 2025. At 
the time of the hearing, attorney Jeff Margolin appeared on behalf of the Petitioner/Employer, 
Mercy, and attorney Mark Chipokas appeared on behalf of the Respondent/Padilla, Jessica 
Padilla (“Padilla”). 
`After reviewing the briefs and administrative record, and considering the parties' arguments, the 
Court rules as follows.   
PROCEDURAL HISTORY 
 
Padilla sustained work-related injuries on July 28, 2022, and December 4, 2022, while 
employed by Mercy. Both injuries resulted from patient assaults. Padilla filed three sequential 
petitions for alternate medical care under Iowa Code section 85.27. 
 
In the first petition filed June 12, 2024, Mercy admitted liability for Padilla's "knee, neck, 
and mental" injuries. D0006 at 31, 6/18/24 (Answer). Following a hearing, the Deputy 
Commissioner granted the petition and ordered Mercy to provide pain management treatment. 
D0006 at 10, 6/21/24 (Alt. Care Dec'n., p. 3). This decision was not appealed.

ARGUMENTS OF THE PARTIES 
A. Petitioner Mercy's Arguments 
 
Mercy's primary argument challenges the Commissioner’s application of judicial estoppel 
on the grounds that the required legal elements are not present. Mercy contends it consistently 
acknowledged the compensable neck injury while disputing whether ongoing pain management 
treatment was causally related to the work injuries (Mercy Brief, p. 10). This distinction carries 
legal significance because Iowa law permits an employer to admit to an injury while reserving 
the right to contest causation or necessity of future care, as established in McGowan v. Brandt 
Construction Co. (Mercy Brief, p. 10; Mercy Reply, pp. 3-4). 
 
Mercy also distinguishes this case from the seminal Winnebago Industries v. Haverly 
case, arguing that unlike the employer in Haverly who initially admitted liability to maintain 
control over care and then denied liability to avoid payment, Mercy consistently acknowledged

B. Respondent Padilla's Arguments 
 
In her brief, Padilla argues that the Commissioner was correct in his determination. 
Specifically, Padilla emphasizes Mercy's pattern of consistent liability admissions across three 
separate alternate medical care proceedings before attempting to deny causation in the third 
proceeding filed January 10, 2025 (Padilla Brief, pp. 10-13). Padilla argues that Mercy faces a 
high burden under Iowa Code section 17A.19(10), which requires demonstrating substantial 
prejudice through legally erroneous or unreasonable agency action. Factual determinations are 
reviewed under the substantial evidence standard, and legal applications are reviewed for abuse

Padilla in her brief relies heavily on Hormel Foods Corp. v. Yunior Tamayo-Perez, 2

STANDARDS OF REVIEW 
Judicial review of agency action is governed by Iowa Code section 17A.19(10). The Court may 
reverse, modify, or grant other appropriate relief if it determines that substantial rights have been 
prejudiced because the agency action is: 
1. Unconstitutional on its face or as applied or is based upon a provision of law that is 
unconstitutional on its face or as applied.  
 
2.  Beyond the authority delegated to the agency by any provision of law or in violation 
of any provision of law. 
 
3.  Based upon an erroneous interpretation of a provision of law whose interpretation has 
not clearly been vested by a provision of law in the discretion of the agency. 
 
4.  Based upon a procedure or decision-making process prohibited by law or was taken 
without following the prescribed procedure or decision-making process. 
 
5.  The product of decision making undertaken by persons who were improperly 
constituted as a decision-making body, were motivated by an improper purpose, or were 
subject to disqualification. 
 
6.  Based upon a determination of fact clearly vested by a provision of law in the 
discretion of the agency that is not supported by substantial evidence in the record before 
the court when that record is viewed as a whole.  
 
Iowa Code § 17A.19(10). 
 
The burden of demonstrating the required prejudice and invalidity of agency action rests 
on the party asserting invalidity. Iowa Code § 17A.19(8)(a). For factual determinations, the

ANALYSIS 
I. THE AGENCY PROPERLY APPLIED THE DOCTRINE OF JUDICIAL ESTOPPEL 
 
In this case, Mercy asserts that the Commission’s application of judicial estoppel is 
legally flawed. Mercy claims that the elements necessary to invoke estoppel are not present in 
this case, and its application improperly overrides the employer’s statutory right to select care. 
Given this argument, this Court must then evaluate the Commission’s application of the law to 
determine if it is erroneous.    
A. Legal Framework for Judicial Estoppel 
 
“[J]udicial estoppel is a ‘commonsense doctrine’ that ‘prohibits a party who has 
successfully and unequivocally asserted a position in one proceeding from asserting an 
inconsistent position in a subsequent proceeding.’” Tyson Foods v. Hedlund, 740 N.W.2d 192, 
195 (Iowa 2007)(quoting Vennerberg Farms, Inc. v. IGF Ins. Co., 405 N.W.2d 810, 814 (Iowa 
1987)). It is “designed to protect the integrity of the judicial process.” Id. The doctrine applies to 
administrative proceedings as well as judicial proceedings. See Winnebago Indus. v. Haverly, 
E-FILED                    CVCV068614 - 2025 AUG 31 11:42 AM             POLK    
CLERK OF DISTRICT COURT                    Page 8 of 15

 
9 
 
727 N.W.2d 567, 573–74 (Iowa 2006). If there is a significant change in the facts after an initial 
position, then a change in position does not violate the doctrine of judicial estoppel. Id. at 575.  
 
The requirements for the doctrine's application are (1) “the successful assertion of [an] 
inconsistent position in a prior action” and (2) “judicial acceptance of the inconsistent position.” 
Hormel Foods Corp. v. Tamayo-Perez, 2 N.W.3d 66 (Iowa Ct. App. 2023). “[W]here there is no 
risk of inconsistent or misleading results, the rationale underlying the judicial estoppel doctrine is 
not advanced and there is no reason to apply the doctrine.” Id. (quoting Tyson Foods, Inc., 740 
N.W.2d at 198). 
 
The doctrine serves a critical function in workers' compensation proceedings, where the 
Supreme Court has recognized that it is not unheard of for certain employers and insurance 
carriers to “admit” liability when there is something to gain thereby (the right to control medical 
treatment) yet thereafter attempt to deny liability as to the case in chief. Winnebago Industries v. 
Haverly, 727 N.W.2d at 570-71. This practice undermines the integrity of the workers' 
compensation system and creates precisely the type of unfair advantage judicial estoppel is 
designed to prevent. 
B. The Commission's Application of the Doctrine 
 
The record at the agency level establishes that Mercy took clearly inconsistent positions 
across the three alternate medical care proceedings. The first admission occurred on June 18, 
2024. In that situation, Padilla filed her initial petition for alternate medical care on June 12, 
2024, Mercy formally admitted liability for Padilla's injuries to her "knee, neck, and mental" 
health in its Answer. At the hearing, Mercy presented Dr. Iverson's January 2024 report as 
evidence but argued the neurologist's recommendation for care was insufficient to justify 
alternate medical care. The Agency disagreed, finding it unreasonable for Mercy to fail to offer

The second admission occurred on November 5, 2024. In that situation, Mercy had failed

II. MERCY FAILED TO ESTABLISH A SIGNIFICANT CHANGE IN FACTS 
 
The question then becomes whether the Commissioner’s decision is based upon 
substantial evidence in the light of Mercy’s claim that there was a significant change in facts 
justifying a denial. Mercy bases its argument on the holding of the Iowa Supreme Court in 
Winnebago Industries. In that case, the court recognized a narrow exception to judicial estoppel, 
stating that "[t]here might, in some cases, be a significant change in the facts after the admission 
of liability that could justify a change of position by the employer." Winnebago Industries, 727 
N.W.2d at 575. As stated previously, evidence is substantial when a "neutral, detached, and 
reasonable person" would find it sufficient "to establish the fact at issue when the consequences 
resulting from the establishment of that fact are understood to be serious and of great 
importance." Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(f)(1). 
 
In reviewing the Commission’s record, this Court concludes there was substantial 
evidence supporting the agency’s conclusion. Two authorized treating physicians, Dr. Stanley 
Mathew and Dr. Sunny Kim, recommended continued treatment. Dr. Mathew's December 3, 
2024 recommendations included massage therapy, chiropractic care, HBOT therapy, Botox 
injections, and other treatments. D0005 at 43 (Cl. Ex. 1, p. 2). Dr. Kim similarly confirmed that 
E-FILED                    CVCV068614 - 2025 AUG 31 11:42 AM             POLK

his recommended care was "medically necessary to treat the work injury." D0005 at 35 (Cl. Ex. 
5). Significantly, both treating physicians provided recent opinions after examining Padilla, 
unlike Dr. Iverson who had not seen Padilla in nearly a year. The agency reasonably relied on 
this distinction, noting that the treating physicians had "actually saw the Padilla recently, unlike 
Dr. Iverson." D0005 at 19. The Commissioner then went on to conclude that there had not been a 
significant change, stating: 
Judicial estoppel requires a change in the underlying facts to justify a subsequent denial 
of liability. Here, no such change has occurred. The defendant has presented no evidence 
showing that Ms. Padilla’s need for treatment is unrelated to her work injuries, nor has it 
shown any significant change in facts since its admissions of liability or the prior orders 
to provide care.  
 
D0005 at 18.   
 
In light of this determination, Mercy relies heavily on McGowan v. Brandt Constr. Co., 
786 N.W.2d 519 (Iowa App. 2010) to argue that in this case there was a significant change, and 
as a result, the Commission’s decision is not based upon substantial evidence. McGowen, 
however, is readily distinguishable from the case at bar. In McGowan, significant new evidence 
developed over more than a year between the employer's admission (September 2004) and the 
estoppel challenge (October 2005), including a new bone scan and opinion from a different 
physician (Dr. Carlstrom) stating the condition was unrelated to work. Id. at *1, *3-4. Here, no 
comparable new evidence exists. Dr. Iverson's December 2024 report merely restated his January 
2024 conclusion using different language.  
 
The absence of genuinely new evidence distinguishes this case from McGowan and 
aligns it more closely with Hormel Foods Corp. In Hormel Foods Corp. v. Tamayo-Perez, the 
Court of Appeals addressed whether an employer could deny liability for specific medical 
treatment after previously admitting liability for the underlying work injury in alternate medical

care proceedings. Hormel Foods Corp., 2 N.W.3d at 70. The court applied the doctrine of 
judicial estoppel, rejecting Hormel's argument that admitting to a compensable work injury while 
denying that specific treatment relates to that injury constitutes consistent positions. Id. at 71 
(Iowa Ct. App. 2023). The court found that Hormel used prior medical opinions, most of which 
predated its earlier admissions of liability, to deny treatment recommended by the authorized 
physician, thereby "essentially contesting liability for the stipulated 'back/spine' injury, which is 
inconsistent with its earlier judicially accepted admissions." Id. The court concluded that Hormel 
"has simply been carrying around a basis for denial in its back pocket which it chose not to use 
until this latest dispute," and therefore was judicially estopped from denying liability for the 
alternate care application. Id. In coming to this conclusion, the Court of Appeals seems to 
indicate that the burden on the employer is substantial and requires demonstrating material 
changes that genuinely justify a position reversal. 
CONCLUSION 
 
Here, Mercy has failed to demonstrate that the agency's decision was unsupported by 
substantial evidence, based on legal error, or represented an abuse of discretion. The agency 
properly applied the doctrine of judicial estoppel based on Mercy's clearly inconsistent positions, 
the acceptance of its prior admissions, and the unfair advantage gained through this conduct. 
Mercy's evidence of changed circumstances was insufficient under the standard established by 
the appellate courts. The agency's decision represents a reasonable application of established 
legal principles to facts supported by substantial evidence.

1. Petitioner Mercy Medical Center's Petition for Judicial Review is DENIED.
