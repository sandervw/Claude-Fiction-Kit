This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A.  The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Joshua Heishman. 
Claimant appeared telephonically and through his attorney, Andrew Portis. Defendants 
did not file an answer or appearance, and did not participate in the hearing. 
 
Claimant filed his petition on October 10, 2025. The petition alleges an injury 
arising out of and in the course of his employment on July 19, 2025, to his ribs, chest, 
and back.  
 
The alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on October 22, 2025. The 
proceedings were attempted to be digitally recorded. However, the digital recording 
application crashed toward the end of the hearing, and the undersigned was unable to 
recover the file. Pursuant to the Commissioner’s July 21, 2023 Order, the undersigned 
has been delegated authority to issue a final agency decision in this alternate medical 
care proceeding. Therefore, this ruling is designated final agency action and any appeal 
of the decision would be to the Iowa District Court pursuant to Iowa Code section 17A. 
 
The record consists of claimant’s exhibits 1 and 2, consisting of 2 pages.  
 
ISSUE 
 
The issue presented for resolution is whether the claimant is entitled to alternate 
medical care due to defendants’ failure to authorize follow-up care for claimant’s 
persistent severe pain.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
Claimant’s petition states that the insurance carrier has not responded to calls or 
emails for nearly two months. Claimant’s exhibit 2 is his attorney’s letter to the 
insurance carrier, dated September 25, 2025, advising of his representation of claimant. 
(Cl. Ex. 2) The letter also states that claimant is still in a lot of pain, and did not feel 
physically able to return to work in a full-duty capacity at that time. The letter includes a 
request for additional authorized care, and advises of claimant’s dissatisfaction with the 
care, or lack of care, provided. 
 
Claimant’s original notice and petition contains proof of service upon the 
employer. Claimant’s exhibit 1 contains a signed certified mail receipt, indicating 
delivery was made to the employer on October 16, 2025. (Cl. Ex. 1) It is found that the 
petition was properly served via certified mail upon the employer. Notice of hearing was 
given by this agency to the employer and insurance carrier via U.S. Mail on October 10, 
2025. (See Notice of Telephone Hearing) Nevertheless, the defendants have not 
entered an appearance or responded in any way to the petition for alternate medical 
care.  
 
Given defendants’ failure to appear for hearing or otherwise defend the petition 
for alternate medical care, all allegations of claimant’s petition are accepted as 
accurate. Under the record before the agency, it is more likely than not that the 
defendants have unreasonably abandoned care. As such, claimant’s request for 
alternate medical care is reasonable.  
REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Iowa Code section 85.27 requires an employer to furnish care related to an 
employee’s a work injury. Such care “must be offered promptly and be reasonably 
suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the employee.” Iowa Code 
§ 85.27(4). The legislature saw fit to pair this responsibility with the right to choose care. 
Id.; see also Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 433, 436 (Iowa 1997). 
“If the employer chooses the care, the employer shall hold the employee harmless for 
the cost of care until the employer notifies the employee that the employer is no longer 
authorizing all or any part of the care and the reason for the change in authorization.” 
Id.; Ramirez-Trujillo v. Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 N.W.2d 759, 771 (Iowa 2016). 
However, an employer’s right to choose care is not absolute under the statute. 
See id.; Ramirez-Trujillo, 878 N.W.2d at 769. An employee dissatisfied with employer-
furnished care (or lack thereof) has the right to seek an alternative. See id.; see also 
Iowa Admin. Code r. 876—4.48. A dissatisfied employee must first share the 
employee’s discontent with the employer. Id.; Bell Bros. Heating & Air Conditioning v. 
Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d 193, 204 (Iowa 2010). If the parties cannot reach an agreement on 
care, the employee may apply to the commissioner for alternate care. Id.; see also Iowa 
Admin. Code r. 876—4.48. And after an informal hearing, “the commissioner may, upon 
. . . reasonable proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and order other care.” Id.

The dispositive question in an alternate care case is whether the employer-
furnished care is reasonable. Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122, 124 (Iowa 
1995). “Reasonableness, of course, is a question of fact.” Reynolds, 562 at 436 (citing 
Long, 528 N.W.2d at 123). As the party seeking relief in the form of alternate care, the 
employee bears the burden of proving that the authorized care in question is 
unreasonable. Id. Because “the employer’s obligation under the statute turns on the 
question of reasonable necessity, not desirability,” an injured employee’s dissatisfaction 
with employer-provided care, standing alone, is not enough to satisfy the employee’s 
burden of proof. Long, 528 N.W.2d at 124. 
Defendants’ failure to offer prompt medical care is unreasonable, and constitutes 
an abandonment of defendants’ obligation to provide claimant medical care under Iowa 
Code section 85.27. The evidence before the agency reflects that it is more likely than 
not that defendants have abandoned care for the work injury, by failing to respond to 
telephone calls or emails or otherwise communicate with claimant about his request for 
care, and failing to participate in this contested case proceeding. The defendants’ 
abandonment of care constitutes a failure to promptly offer care that is reasonably 
suited to treat claimant’s injuries to his ribs, chest, and back. Defendants have therefore 
surrendered their right to direct care for claimant’s injuries to his ribs, chest, and back 
under Iowa Code section 85.27. 
 
Once an abandonment of care has occurred, the claimant is free to seek care on 
his own at defendant's cost. See West Side Transport v. Cordell, 601 N.W.2d 691 (Iowa 
1999) (the court upheld the holding that the defendant employer had “lost the right to 
choose the care” and that “allow and order other care” language is broad enough to 
include treatment by a doctor of the employee's choosing). 
 
As such, claimant is permitted to reasonably select and direct his own medical 
care for the work injuries moving forward. Defendants must hold claimant harmless for 
any and all reasonable care related to the work injuries to his ribs, chest, and back. 
 
ORDER 
 
THEREFORE IT IS ORDERED: 
 
The claimant's petition for alternate medical care is granted. 
 
Claimant is permitted to reasonably direct his own medical care related to the 
work injuries, given defendants’ abandonment of their responsibilities and right to direct 
care.  Defendants shall promptly take any and all action necessary for claimant to 
promptly receive care with his chosen providers, and hold claimant harmless for the 
costs of such reasonable care. 
 
Failure to comply with this decision may result in sanctions under Iowa 
Administrative Code 876—4.36.
