STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Terry Bloomquist, claimant, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from Valero Services, Inc., employer and Ace American 
Insurance Company, insurance carrier, and the Second Injury Fund of Iowa as 
defendants.  On the morning of the hearing the claimant reached a settlement with the 
employer and its insurance carrier; therefore, the employer and insurance carrier did not 
participate in the hearing. Hearing was held via Zoom videoconference on August 27, 
2025.     
The parties filed a hearing report wherein the parties entered into various 
stipulations.  All of those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into 
this arbitration decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.  
Terry Bloomquist was the only witness to testify live at trial.  The evidentiary 
record also includes joint exhibits 1, pp. 1-7; JE2, pp. 14 - JE10, p. 125; JE11, pp. 131- 
JE14, p. 1821. On August 25, 2025, the Second Injury Fund of Iowa (“The Fund”) filed a 
timely objection to joint exhibit 10, pages 126-130, a supplemental report from Dr. 
Kuhnlein, because it was not produced to defendants until August 5, 2025, after the 30-
day deadline.  I found that joint exhibit 10, pages 126-130, was not exchanged in 
compliance with the Commissioner’s October 31, 2024 Hearing Assignment Order and 
876 IAC 4.19(3)(d).  In its written motion and oral argument at hearing, The Fund 
demonstrated that receipt of the evidence would be unfairly prejudicial as required by 
876 IAC 4.19(3)(e).  See Hagen v. Serta/National Bedding Co., LLC, 1 N.W. 3d 1 (Iowa 
2024). Therefore, defendants’ objection was sustained and joint exhibit 10, pages 126-
130, was not admitted into evidence.  All other exhibits were received without objection.  
The evidentiary record remained open at the conclusion of the arbitration hearing for the 
sole purpose of allowing the parties to submit the approved settlement documents

ISSUES 
The parties submitted the following issues for resolution: 
1. Whether claimant proved he sustained a qualifying first injury. 
2. Whether claimant proved he sustained a qualifying second injury. 
3. If liability of the Fund is triggered, whether claimant proved he sustained any 
industrial loss.  If so, what amount. 
4. If claimant is entitled to benefits from the Fund, what is the Fund’s credit. 
5. If claimant is entitled to benefits from the Fund, the appropriate 
commencement date. 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned, having considered all the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
 
Claimant, Terry Bloomquist, is seeking workers’ compensation benefits from the 
Second Injury Fund of Iowa (“the Fund”).  He alleged he sustained an injury to his left 
ankle in February 2008 which constitutes a first qualifying injury.  He alleged that he 
sustained a work-related injury to his right shoulder and arm at Valero on November 2, 
2020. 
 
From 1979 to 2018 Terry was a self-employed farmer. (Transcript page 18) He 
did not have any physical difficulties performing his farm work.  (Tr. p. 60) While farming 
he also worked as a mechanic at Silgan Containers.  (Tr. p. 18) Silgan makes cans and 
lids for dog food and Campbell’s Soup and vegetables.  (Tr. pp. 18-19) He never had 
any physical restrictions placed on his activities at Silgan.  (Tr. p. 60) He did not have 
any physical difficulties performing that job.  (Tr. pp. 60-61)  
In 2011 Terry injured his left ankle while farming.  (Tr. p. 43) He sustained a left 
ankle fracture which required surgery by Dr. Dayton, a podiatrist.  (Tr. p. 44) He testified 
that he had a 5 or 6-inch plate and approximately 8 screws that hold his ankle together.  
(Tr. p. 44) Following surgery he was in a boot and off work at Silgan Containers for 
approximately 3 months.  (Tr. pp. 44, 63) After his treatment he returned to full-duty 
work.  (Tr. p.44-45, 63)  
In 2013 Terry left Silgan to return to farming.  (Tr. p. 19) After a few years Terry 
decided just farming was not enough so he went to work for the Iowa DOT as a highway 
maintenance technician.  (Tr. p. 19) He performed all aspects of road work, including 
plowing snow, fixing guardrails, and putting signs up. (Tr. p. 19) He worked at the DOT 
from 2015 to 2018.  He left the DOT due to drama that did not involve him.  (Tr. pp. 19-
21) He never had any physical restrictions placed on his activities at the DOT.  (Tr. p. 
61) He did not have any physical difficulties performing that job.  (Tr. p. 61)

On July 7, 2025, Dr. Aviles authored a missive which set forth his answers to a 
series of questions posed to him by the Fund.  (JE7, pp. 90-91) Dr. Aviles was asked if 
he agreed with Dr. Kuhnlein’s 5% right upper extremity impairment rating for motor 
deficits.  He replied, 
I do not agree with this assessment, as it very clearly states in the 
American Medical Association’s Guidelines for the Evaluation of 
Permanent Impairment, Fifth Edition, Section 16.8, Strength Evaluation: 
Strength Evaluation should not be recorded, given that “many subjective 
or non-measurable factors, including fatigue, handedness, time of day, 
age, nutritional state, pain, and individual cooperation, further influence 
strength measurements.”  Furthermore, there are clear prospective 
randomized trials that show that there is no strength deficit associated with 
biceps tenodesis.  This can be seen in the article from the Journal of 
Shoulder and Elbow Surgery International in 2020 entitled Lesions of the 
Biceps Pulley:  A prospective study and classification update.  This is 
further supported in the American Journal of Sports Medicine article in

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffers loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based. A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause. A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible. George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996).  
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability. 
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 

BLOOMQUIST V. SECOND INJURY FUND OF IOWA 
Page 11 
 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); 
Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995). Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted expert medical 
testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 
N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 1994). 
Claimant is seeking workers’ compensation benefits from the Fund.  Iowa Code 
section 85.64 governs Second Injury Fund liability.  Before liability of the Fund is 
triggered, three requirements must be met.  First, the employee must have lost or lost 
the use of a hand, arm, foot, leg, or eye.  Second, the employee must sustain a loss or 
loss of use of another specified member or organ through a compensable injury.  Third, 
permanent disability must exist as to both the initial injury and the second injury.

In the present case, claimant seeks benefits from the Second Injury Fund of 
Iowa.  The first dispute to be addressed is whether claimant sustained a first qualifying 
injury as required by the statute.   
Based on the above findings of fact, I conclude that as the result of the 2011 left 
ankle injury, claimant sustained 7 percent lower extremity permanent impairment.  
(JE10, p. 121) This is based on the unrebutted expert medical opinion of Dr. Kuhnlein 
who cited to the AMA Guides, Fifth Edition to assign his impairment rating.  Thus, I 
conclude claimant has demonstrated by a preponderance of the evidence that he lost or 
lost the use of a hand, arm, foot, leg, or eye and has sustained a first qualifying injury. 
We now turn to whether claimant’s November 2, 2020 work injury constitutes a 
second qualifying injury.  In 2017 the Iowa Legislature amended Iowa Code section 
85.34. Before the 2017 changes, shoulder injuries were considered proximal to the arm 
and compensated as a body as a whole injury, under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(u). 
Prior to the 2017 changes, a shoulder injury was compensated as an unscheduled 
injury, and based on industrial disability. See Alm v. Morris Barick Cattle Co., 240 Iowa 
1174, 38 N.W.2d 161 (1949). Through the 2017 legislative changes, the legislature 
added the shoulder to the list of scheduled members. Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(n) 
states: “[f]or the loss of a shoulder, weekly compensation during four hundred weeks.” 
Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(n). This amendment went into effect on July 1, 2018. The 
legislature did not define the term “shoulder.”  
The Iowa Supreme Court in Chavez v. MS Tech. LLC, 972 N.W.2d 662 (Iowa 
Apr. 1, 2022), and Deng v. Farmland Foods, Inc., 972 N.W.2d 727 (Iowa Apr. 1, 2022), 
affirmed that the shoulder was construed in a functional sense to include the 
glenohumeral joint as well as all of the muscles, tendons, and ligaments that were 
essential for the shoulder to function. The Court stated, “[d]efining ‘shoulder’ in the 
functional sense under section 85.34(2)(n) best achieves the statues’ purpose.” Chavez, 
at 669.  Chavez’s injury included full-thickness rotator cuff tear, severe AC arthrosis, 
tendonitis, and tearing of the biceps tendon.  Id. at 664.  Based on the above findings of 
fact, I conclude that as the result of the November 2, 2020 work injury the claimant in 
this case sustained an injury to the long head of the biceps tendon.  I further conclude 
that claimant’s injuries are included within the injuries considered by the Court in 
Chavez and determined to constitute a scheduled member shoulder injury.  Accordingly, 
I conclude the Fund’s argument is more persuasive and consistent with the Iowa 
Supreme Court’s holding in Chavez. Claimant failed to demonstrate by a 
preponderance of the evidence that he sustained any permanent partial disability to his 
right arm as the result of the November 2, 2020, injury. I conclude the evidence 
demonstrates that claimant’s injury is contained to the shoulder in the functional sense. 
See Chavez.  
  
Therefore, I conclude that claimant has proven a right shoulder injury and that his 
claim is limited to a schedule member injury and recovery pursuant to Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(n). 
I conclude claimant failed to demonstrate that he sustained permanent injury to 
his right arm through a compensable injury.  I conclude that claimant sustained an injury

to his right shoulder.  Before liability of the Fund is triggered one of the requirements is 
that the employee must sustain a loss of or loss of use of the use of a hand, arm, foot, 
leg, or eye.  Because a shoulder is not one of the listed members in Iowa Code section 
85.64, an injury to the shoulder does not trigger liability of the Fund.  As such, claimant 
has failed to prove by a preponderance of the evidence that he sustained a second 
qualifying loss.  Because claimant failed to carry his burden to demonstrate a second 
qualifying loss, I conclude that liability of the Fund has not been triggered.  As such, all 
remaining issues are rendered moot.
