STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Brian Abel, filed petitions in arbitration seeking workers’ compensation 
benefits from City of Marshalltown, employer, and Iowa Municipalities Workers’ 
Compensation Association, insurance carrier, both as defendants, as a result of an 
alleged injury on January 28, 2021 and a stipulated injury sustained on June 20, 2022. 
This matter came on for hearing before Deputy Workers’ Compensation Commissioner 
Erica J. Fitch, via videoconference, using Zoom.  The record in this case consists of 
joint exhibits 1 through 7, claimant’s exhibits 1 through 8, defendants’ exhibits A through 
P, and the testimony of the claimant and Christopher McGahuey.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned, having considered all of the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
Claimant was 51 years of age at the time of hearing. (Hearing Transcript, page 
11)  He resides near Marshalltown, Iowa. He is married and the father of two adult 
children. (Defendants’ Exhibit P, Depo. Tr. pp. 4-6) He is right-hand dominant. (DEP, 
Depo. Tr. p. 21)  
Claimant graduated high school in 1991 and joined the US Navy. While in the US 
Navy, claimant worked as an engineer, performing ship maintenance. He received an 
engineering certificate, with corresponding college credits. Claimant left the US Navy in 
1993 and began construction work. He subsequently worked maintaining cable lines, 
operating manufacturing equipment, delivering products, security work, and human 
resources tasks. Throughout his work history, claimant received training and 
certifications in various subjects, including forklift operation and CPR. (DEL, pp. 216-
218) 
In 2007, claimant began work at defendant-employer. He initially worked in the 
parks and recreation department, cutting trees, mowing, and performing concrete work. 
In 2009, he transitioned to the street department, where he would cut trees, maintain 
vehicles, pick up trash, and plow snow. (DEL, p. 219) In March 2009, claimant 
transitioned to the sewer department as a sewer maintenance worker I.  A sewer 
maintenance worker I was tasked with performance of manual labor to maintain, repair, 
and construct sewer systems, as well as operation of snow removal equipment. The job 
description for the sewer maintenance position required lifting up to 50 pounds from 
ground to waist level and lifting up to 160 pounds with assistance. (Claimant’s Exhibit 3, 
pp. 48, 50) He successfully underwent a pre-employment physical, which deemed 
claimant capable of performing the essential functions of the job of sewer maintenance 
worker. (CE3, pp. 46-47) 
Claimant’s relevant medical history includes intermittent back complaints. In 
October 2007, claimant presented to primary care physician, Joseph Pollpeter, M.D. 
with complaints of shooting discomfort of the right back and buttocks off and on for a 
year, as well as some shoulder discomfort, primarily right sided. (Joint Exhibit 1, pp. 3-4)

Claimant received a home exercise program for right piriformis syndrome, with the 
therapist’s opinion claimant’s pain was probably attributable to some type of nerve 
impingement caused by heavy lifting or heavy work. (JE1, p. 5) In October 2008, 
claimant sought medical care for bilateral low back pain. Claimant reported his work for 
defendant-employer required him to get in and out of trucks and work on streets. He 
expressed belief he likely overdid it during these activities, resulting in symptoms. (JE1, 
p. 2) In April 2013, claimant returned to Dr. Pollpeter and relayed he felt a pop in his 
back while bending over at work to put socks on. Dr. Pollpeter noted claimant had a 
prior history of intermittent back discomfort. He assessed a lumbar spine strain with 
spasming and ordered conservative care. (JE1, p. 6) In November 2017, claimant was 
treated by a chiropractor for complaints of neck, low back, and right hip pain. (JE7, pp. 
243-244) In September 2019, claimant suffered with a perianal abscess, for which he 
underwent a pelvic MRI. (JE1, pp. 8) On April 15, 2020, claimant participated in a virtual 
telephone visit with Dr. Pollpeter due to COVID pandemic protections. Claimant 
reported coughing and one day of low back pain. Dr. Pollpeter assessed allergic rhinitis 
and low back pain. He prescribed medication for allergic rhinitis and a prednisone burst 
to treat claimant’s back pain and nasal symptoms. (JE1, p. 11) 
Claimant testified his job duties in the months preceding the first alleged work 
injury included lifting manhole covers, setting rings for manholes, concrete work, 
shoveling, jackhammering, hauling rock, street and intake repairs, snow plowing, 
locates, inspections, and operation of equipment. (Hrg. Tr. pp. 15-16) Claimant testified 
he experienced some pain of his low back and/or right hip in the weeks leading up to his 
alleged work injury, but no constant or ongoing pain. (Hrg. Tr. p. 21) He did not miss 
significant time from work or require any work restrictions. (Hrg. Tr. pp. 24-25) 
Claimant testified that on January 28, 2021, he was plowing snow using a dump 
truck with a side wing and front blade.  He described the truck as stiff, bouncy, and 
jostling. (Hrg. Tr. pp. 12-13) Claimant testified that the bouncing caused increasing pain 
in his low back and right hip as the night progressed. He described the pain as 
excruciating, with radiation shooting down to his right ankle. Claimant testified the pain 
became so severe that he was forced to stop to splash snow on his face, as he was 
sweating, shaking, and very dizzy. (Hrg. Tr. p. 14) This incident occurred between 
approximately 4:00 a.m. and 5:00 a.m. (DEP, Depo. Tr. p. 25) Claimant testified he was 
unable to sleep after his shift and decided to seek medical care with Dr. Pollpeter. (Hrg. 
Tr. p. 26) 
On January 28, 2021, claimant texted his supervisor, Troy McGahuey. He 
indicated he would be taking a sick day the next day in order to have his sciatic[a] 
evaluated. Mr. McGahuey requested claimant bring a fit for duty certificate prior to 
returning to work. (CE4, p. 55; DEM, pp. 223-224) He indicated the form would “protect” 
himself, claimant, and defendant-employer, as they would “know it’s not an injury and 
that it’s ok to return to duties so it won’t get worse.” (DEM, p. 224) 
Christopher Troy (Troy) McGahuey testified at evidentiary hearing. Mr. 
McGahuey began work at defendant-employer in 1997 and currently serves as sewer 
superintendent, having held that position since 2018. Mr. McGahuey has worked with or

On January 29, 2021, claimant presented to Dr. Pollpeter. Dr. Pollpeter noted a 
5-week history of back pain. Claimant reported he was very uncomfortable and felt 
severe pain radiating from the right buttock down to the right foot. Dr. Pollpeter noted 
claimant had been putting in 13-hour days in the snowplow and had hit two cars over 
the winter due to pain. Dr. Pollpeter noted claimant had experienced similar pain before, 
but nothing persistent. Dr. Pollpeter expressed belief the majority of claimant’s pain was 
sourced from the hip, but indicated that would not explain the radiating pain to the foot. 
He prescribed a course of medication and home exercises and removed claimant from 
work. (JE1, pp. 12-13) Dr. Pollpeter ordered x-rays of claimant’s lumbar spine, read as 
indicating very minimal degenerative arthritis. (JE1, pp. 14-15)  
Dr. Pollpeter also ordered x-rays of the pelvis and right hip, to be compared with 
the September 2019 pelvic MRI. X-rays did not reveal acute findings. However, 
comparison to the September 2019 pelvic MRI, raised questions regarding the status of 
the right femoral head. As a result, a radiologist reviewed the pelvic MRI and opined it 
revealed an area of probable early avascular necrosis of the right femoral head which 
was not reproduced on other images. The radiologist recommended correlation with risk 
factors for avascular necrosis such as chronic alcohol use, chronic steroid use, 
diabetes, tobacco use, and other potential risk factors. The radiologist recommended 
consideration of further imaging if claimant presented with right hip pain. (JE1, pp. 9, 16-
17) 
At evidentiary hearing, claimant testified that he recalls discussing the alleged 
work injury with Mr. McGahuey. Claimant indicated he informed Mr. McGahuey that his 
back was problematic due to plowing snow. Claimant initially testified he informed Mr. 
McGahuey by text and phone call that evening or early the next morning, that his back 
was very painful due to plowing snow. He subsequently admitted he did not recall if the 
conversation was done via text, phone call, or when he took Dr. Pollpeter’s work excuse 
to Mr. McGahuey in his office. (Hrg. Tr. pp. 14-15, 77, 125-126, 133)  
On February 9, 2021, claimant underwent an MRI of the right hip. Results 
revealed avascular necrosis of the right femoral head without cortical collapse, as well 
as a degenerative tear of the labrum. (JE1, pp. 18-19)

with “lifting heavy things” after a storm in Marshalltown. (JE2, p. 88) Dr. Bremner

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
File No. 22007548.01 (Date of Injury January 28, 2021): 
The first issue for determination is whether claimant sustained an injury arising 
out of and in the course of his employment on January 28, 2021. 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e). 
A personal injury contemplated by the workers’ compensation law means an 
injury, the impairment of health or a disease resulting from an injury which comes about, 
not through the natural building up and tearing down of the human body, but because of 
trauma.  The injury must be something that acts extraneously to the natural processes 
of nature and thereby impairs the health, interrupts or otherwise destroys or damages a 
part or all of the body.  Although many injuries have a traumatic onset, there is no 
requirement for a special incident or an unusual occurrence.  Injuries which result from 
cumulative trauma are compensable.  Increased disability from a prior injury, even if 
brought about by further work, does not constitute a new injury, however.  St. Luke’s 
Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 650-51 (Iowa 2000); Ellingson v. Fleetguard, Inc., 599 
N.W.2d 440, 444 (Iowa 1999); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 
845, 850-51 (Iowa 1995); McKeever Custom Cabinets v. Smith, 379 N.W.2d 368, 373-
74 (Iowa 1985).  An occupational disease covered by chapter 85A is specifically 
excluded from the definition of personal injury.  Iowa Code section 85.61(4)(b); Iowa 
Code section 85A.8; Iowa Code section 85A.14. 
When the injury develops gradually over time, the cumulative injury rule applies.  
The date of injury for cumulative injury purposes is the date on which the disability 
manifests.  Manifestation is best characterized as that date on which both the fact of 
injury and the causal relationship of the injury to the claimant’s employment would be 
plainly apparent to a reasonable person.  The date of manifestation inherently is a fact-
based determination.  The fact finder is entitled to substantial latitude in making this 
determination and may consider a variety of factors, none of which is necessarily 
dispositive in establishing a manifestation date.  Among others, the factors may include 
missing work when the condition prevents performing the job, or receiving significant 
medical care for the condition.  For time limitation purposes, the discovery rule then 
becomes pertinent so the statute of limitations does not begin to run until the employee, 
as a reasonable person, knows or should know, that the cumulative injury condition is 
serious enough to have a permanent, adverse impact on his or her employment.  
Herrera v. IBP, Inc., 633 N.W.2d 284, 287-88 (Iowa 2001); Oscar Mayer Foods Corp. v. 
Tasler, 483 N.W.2d 824, 829-30 (Iowa 1992); McKeever Custom Cabinets v. Smith, 379 
N.W.2d 368, 374 (Iowa 1985). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury actually occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 
employment. Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 150 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. 
Dial Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309, 311 (Iowa 1996). The words “arising out of” refer to the 

ABEL V. CITY OF MARSHALLTOWN 
Page 20 
 
cause or source of the injury. The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 
circumstances of the injury. 2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124, 128 (Iowa 
1995).  An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists 
between the injury and the employment. Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. The injury must 
be a rational consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely 
incidental to the employment. Koehler Elec. v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1, 3 (Iowa 2000); 
Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it 
happens within a period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may 
be when performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties 
or doing an activity incidental to them. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d at 150. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
A possibility of causation is not sufficient; a probability is necessary. Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996) (citing Holmes v. Bruce 
Motor Freight, Inc., 215 N.W.2d 296, 297 (Iowa 1974)). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 
621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 
(Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
Claimant alleges he sustained an acute-on-cumulative injury to his right hip and 
lumbar spine. He relies upon the medical opinions of Dr. Bansal, as arguably buttressed 
by those of Dr. Pollpeter. Defendants argue claimant has not proven he suffered a 
compensable injury to either body part, citing preexisting issues, lack of mention of the 
event to medical providers, and the lack of causation opinions from treating physicians. 
It is undisputed that claimant received medical treatment for low back complaints 
prior to the alleged January 2021 work injury. However, the medical records do not 
establish that claimant required significant, ongoing treatment. Rather, it appears 
claimant suffered with bouts of pain which, by the evidence, appeared to resolve to at 

ABEL V. CITY OF MARSHALLTOWN 
Page 21 
 
least manageable levels prior to the alleged work injury. Claimant’s argument that he 
suffered an acute-on-cumulative injury is possible. 
However, possibility is insufficient to meet claimant’s burden of proof. Rather, 
claimant must establish it is probable that he suffered a work-related injury. Dr. Bansal 
specifically opined claimant sustained a work-related aggravation of lumbar facet 
arthropathy with respect to his low back, and aggravation of degenerative joint disease

The next issue for determination is whether claimant is entitled to reimbursement 
for an independent medical examination under Iowa Code section 85.39.  
Iowa Code section 85.39 permits an employee to be reimbursed for subsequent 
examination by a physician of the employee's choice where an employer-retained 
physician has previously evaluated “permanent disability” and the employee believes 
that the initial evaluation is too low.   Iowa Code § 85.39(2) (2023).  The section also 
permits reimbursement for reasonably necessary transportation expenses incurred and 
for any wage loss occasioned by the employee attending the subsequent examination.  
Iowa Code § 85.39 (1), (2).   
The Iowa Supreme Court has provided a literal interpretation of the plain 
language of Iowa Code section 85.39, stating that section only allows the employee to 
obtain an independent medical evaluation at the employer’s expense if an evaluation of 
permanent disability has already been made by an employer retained physician.  Des 
Moines Area Reg’l Transit Auth. v. Young, 867 N.W.2d 839, 847 (Iowa 2015). Under 
the Young decision, an employee can only obtain an IME at the employer's expense if 
an evaluation of permanent disability has been made by an employer-retained 
physician. Id. 
An employer is only liable to reimburse an employee for the cost of an 
examination conducted pursuant to this subsection if the injury for which the employee 
is being examined is determined to be compensable.  Iowa Code § 85.39(2) (2017).  
“An employer is not liable for the cost of such examination if the injury for which the 
employee is being examined is determined not to have a compensable injury. A 
determination of the reasonableness of a fee for an examination made pursuant to this 
subsection, shall be based on the typical fee charged by a medical provider to perform 
an impairment rating in the local area where the examination is conducted.”  Iowa Code 
§ 85.39(2); Tweeten v. Tweeten, 999 N.W.2d 270, 282 (Iowa 2023).

The parties stipulate claimant sustained a compensable injury to his left shoulder 
in the work-related motor vehicle accident on June 20, 2022. Defendants, however, 
dispute whether claimant also suffered disability to his upper, thoracic back.  
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
 
Dr. Miller served as claimant’s authorized, treating physician related to the 
stipulated June 20, 2022 injury. He began treating claimant shortly following the motor 
vehicle accident and guided his course of treatment over the following six months. On 
August 5, 2022, Dr. Miller opined claimant’s back pain was muscular/soft tissue in 
nature, the motor vehicle accident was minor, and the injury did not cause the 
worsening of a preexisting back condition. Claimant’s active treatment of his back 
complaints ceased by December 2022. Thereafter, in February 2023, Dr. Miller opined 
he was unable to state that any ongoing pain complaints were related to work and to the 
extent claimant sustained an injury to his upper back, it was temporary in nature and 
would have achieved MMI within six months.  
Dr. Bansal examined claimant on one occasion, 1-1/2 years post-injury. He 
opined the motor vehicle accident caused anatomic injury to claimant’s thoracic spine. 
However, claimant did not receive active treatment of his upper back complaints for 
approximately one year prior to Dr. Bansal’s evaluation. Had claimant’s MRIs 
demonstrated an acute injury, one must ask why the ordering physician, Dr. Pollpeter, 
did not order further care for claimant. Claimant was also not selected for further 
evaluation by the Mayo Clinic.  
On comparison, I award greater weight to the opinions of Dr. Miller who 
contemporaneously evaluated and treated claimant for a period of six months post-
injury. I find he was in a superior position to opine as to the nature and extent of 
claimant’s back complaints and therefore, find claimant did not sustain more than a 
temporary aggravation of a preexisting condition to his upper back. I further find 
claimant achieved MMI for the temporary aggravation as of late December 2022, 6 
months following the June 20, 2022 injury.

Iowa Code section 85.34(1) provides that healing period benefits are payable to 
an injured employee who has suffered permanent partial disability until (1) the employee 
has returned to work; (2) it is medically indicated that significant improvement from the 
injury is not anticipated; or (3) the employee is medically capable of returning to 
employment substantially similar to the employment in which the employee was 
engaged at the time of injury, whichever occurs first.  The healing period can be 
considered the period during which there is a reasonable expectation of improvement of 
the disabling condition.  See Armstrong Tire & Rubber Co. v. Kubli, 312 N.W.2d 60, 65 
(Iowa Ct. App. 1981).  Healing period benefits can be interrupted or intermittent.  Teel v. 
McCord, 394 N.W.2d 405, 406 (Iowa 1986). 
 
Claimant was removed from work following shoulder surgery, from April 27, 2023 
through August 27, 2023. He received temporary disability benefits for this period. The 
additional dates requested by claimant are not dates when claimant was removed from 
work or excused from work by a medical provider. Rather, they appear to be dates that 
claimant chose not to work due to subjective reports of back pain, shoulder pain, or a 
combination thereof. As previously stated, I do not find claimant was a credible witness 
and the evidentiary record is lacking in supportive documentation that would excuse 
claimant from work on these dates. Accordingly, I find claimant is not entitled to 
additional temporary disability benefits.  
 
The next issue for determination is the nature and extent of permanent disability. 
Having found claimant did not sustain a permanent injury to his back, claimant’s 
permanent disability is limited to the stipulated left shoulder. The next issue is the 
proper commencement date for permanent disability benefits. These issues will be 
considered together. 
 
Under the Iowa Workers' Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is 
compensated either for a loss or loss of use of a scheduled member under Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(a)-(u) or as an unscheduled injury pursuant to the provisions of section 
85.34(2)(v). The extent of scheduled member disability benefits to which an injured 
worker is entitled is determined by using the functional method. Functional disability is 
"limited to the loss of the physiological capacity of the body or body part.” Mortimer v. 
Fruehauf Corp., 502 N.W.2d 12, 15 (Iowa 1993); Sherman v. Pella Corp., 576 N.W.2d 
312, 320 (Iowa 1998).   
 
Determination of this issue ultimately rests in a battle of the experts. Dr. Aviles 
served as claimant’s treating physician and surgeon; he opined claimant sustained a 2 
percent upper extremity impairment due to deficits in range of motion. Dr. Bansal opined 
claimant sustained an 8 percent upper extremity impairment. Dr. Aviles provided 
contemporaneous, ongoing care of claimant’s injury. He had the benefit of observing 
claimant on multiple occasions, including intraoperatively, and guided claimant’s 
rehabilitation. Further, at the time of Dr. Bansal’s evaluation, claimant continued to

Aviles’ opinion as to the extent of claimant’s disability to be more persuasive.  
 
This finding entitles claimant to 8 weeks of permanent partial disability benefits (2

and claimant was married and entitled to 2 exemptions. The proper rate of

The next issue for determination is whether defendants are responsible for

The next issue for determination is whether claimant is entitled to reimbursement

The final issue for determination is a specific taxation of costs. Claimant seeks
