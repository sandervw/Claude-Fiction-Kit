STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Somphong Xaysana, claimant, filed a petition for arbitration against Johnsrud 
Transport, as the employer and Great West Casualty Company, as the insurance 
carrier.  This case came before the undersigned for an arbitration hearing on October 
21, 2020.  As a result of the ongoing pandemic in the state of Iowa and pursuant to an 
order of the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commissioner, this hearing was conducted 
via videoconferencing using the CourtCall platform.  Claimant appeared from his 
attorney’s office.  Defense counsel and the court reporter also appeared remotely from 
separate locations. 
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the hearing.  On the 
hearing report, the parties entered into numerous stipulations.  Those stipulations were 
accepted and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be made 
or discussed.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations. 
The evidentiary record includes Joint Exhibits 1 through 3 (including pages 22 
through 25 contained in a supplemental joint exhibit set filed by defense counsel), 
Claimant’s Exhibits 1 through 6, and Defendants’ Exhibits A through J.   
Claimant testified on his own behalf.  No other witnesses testified at trial.  The 
evidentiary record closed at the conclusion of the evidentiary hearing on October 21, 
2019.

ISSUES 
The parties submitted the following disputed issues for resolution: 
1. Whether the July 15, 2018 work injury caused permanent disability. 
2. Whether the work injury should be compensated as a scheduled member 
injury or with industrial disability. 
3. The extent of claimant’s entitlement to permanent disability benefits, if any. 
4. Whether claimant is entitled to reimbursement of his independent medical 
evaluation pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.39. 
5. Whether claimant is entitled to alternate medical care for treatment of post-
traumatic stress disorder (PTSD). 
6. Whether costs should be assessed against either party and, if so, in what 
amount. 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned, having considered all of the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
Somphong Xaysana is a 37-year-old gentleman, who sustained an admitted 
injury while working for Johnsrud Transport on July 15, 2018.  (Claimant’s testimony; 
Hearing Report)  Mr. Xaysana began his employment for Johnsrud less than a year 
before his injury.  He worked as a laborer assigned to clean larger food grade trailers.  
He was required to hook up a machine to clean the inside of the tankers and to wash 
and clean the outside of the tankers manually.  (Claimant’s testimony) 
As part of the duties, Mr. Xaysana would hook up a sprayer that dispersed hot 
water and chemicals inside the tankers.  The evidence discloses that the water was 
applied at 180 degrees Fahrenheit and mixed with a cleaning chemical.  On July 15, 
2018, a hose that delivered the hot water and chemical mixture burst and sprayed on 
claimant.  Mr. Xaysana immediately went to the chemical shower at the employer’s 
plant and was transported to Iowa Methodist Medical Center’s emergency room via 
ambulance.  (Claimant’s testimony; Joint Exhibits 1 and 2)  The medical providers at 
Iowa Methodist stabilized claimant, wrapped his burns, and transported him to the 
University of Iowa Hospitals and Clinics’ burn unit on the same day as the accident.  
(Joint Ex. 2)

Claimant spent five days in the hospital.  After some physical therapy and time 
for recovery, claimant’s treating physician at the University of Iowa Hospitals and Clinics 
released him to return to work and all other normal activities without restrictions on 
September 6, 2018.  (Joint Ex. 3, p. 25)  In that office note, claimant’s treating physician 
further noted that claimant was doing very well, experienced minimal pain that was 
controlled with Tylenol, and that claimant denied itchiness of his burn areas.  (Joint Ex. 
3, p. 21)  Mr. Xaysana concedes that he has not obtained any medical treatment for his 
burns since September 6, 2018.  He also admits that he did not express any concerns 
about his breathing or symptoms of post-traumatic stress disorder to his physicians 
before he was released from their care.  (Claimant’s testimony) 
Nevertheless, claimant asserts that he has ongoing symptoms.  Mr. Xaysana 
testified that he has difficulties with walking.  It causes his back to tighten and claimant 
testified that he has difficulties breathing in less than a minute while walking.  He 
testified that his chest will tighten up and he loses his breath easily.  Claimant testified 
that he cannot sit or stand too long without symptoms and that he has reduced his 
social activities and withdraws from social situations since this injury.  (Claimant’s 
testimony) 
Mr. Xaysana testified that he has low self-esteem since the accident due to the 
scarring on his body.  He testified that warm water still causes itching and burning on 
his skin where he was burned.  Mr. Xaysana also testified that he has nightmares four 
to five times per week since the accident, that he loses sleep since the accident, and 
that he experiences anxiety and depression thinking about what the chemicals he was 
exposed to may do to his body in the future and worrying about an accident occurring 
again.  Mr. Xaysana requests alternate medical care to include treatment for his mental 
health issues, whether they are anxiety, depression, or post-traumatic stress disorder.  
Nevertheless, Mr. Xaysana concedes that he has been working full-time and at full pay 
without any medical restrictions since September 2018.  He continues to work his pre-
injury job with the employer.  (Claimant’s testimony) 
Defendants ultimately sent claimant to an occupational medicine physician, 
Robert L. Broghammer, M.D. for evaluation and an opinion regarding permanent 
restrictions and permanent impairment.  Dr. Broghammer evaluated Mr. Xaysana on 
August 19, 2019.  Dr. Broghammer found no evidence of scarring and documented full 
range of motion in the upper and lower extremities.  Dr. Broghammer also noted no 
significant scarring on claimant’s back, but indicated that there was some mild 
discoloration in the gluteal region.  He opined that claimant has no significant skin 
dryness relative to the burns he sustained.  (Defendants’ Ex. A) 
Dr. Broghammer opined that claimant achieved maximum medical improvement 
(MMI) on September 6, 2018, the last date he sought treatment at the University of Iowa 
Hospitals and Clinics.  Dr. Broghammer opined that claimant does not require further 
treatment for his burn injuries, that he requires no permanent work restrictions, and that 
claimant does not qualify for a permanent impairment rating.  However, Dr. 
Broghammer did recommend a mental health evaluation to assess claimant’s potential 
post-traumatic stress disorder and symptoms.  (Defendants’ Ex. A)

Defendants subsequently scheduled and had claimant evaluated by a 
psychologist, Phillip L. Ascheman, Ph.D.  Following his October 28, 2019 psychological 
evaluation, Dr. Ascheman opined that claimant does not show evidence of a mental 
health disorder.  He specifically opined that Mr. Xaysana does not show symptoms 
consistent with post-traumatic stress disorder.  He recommended no further mental 
health treatment and opined that claimant does not have any limitations or restrictions 
related to a mental health diagnosis.  (Defendants’ Ex. B) 
Claimant countered with an independent medical evaluation of his own. Dr. 
Jacqueline M. Stoken, D.O., evaluated claimant on July 15, 2020.  Dr. Stoken 
documented a history that included nervousness and panic symptoms while at work.  
Dr. Stoken also documented claimant’s reports of nightmares, ongoing pain complaints, 
problems breathing, and noted a new symptom reported by claimant of scarring on the 
cornea of his right eye due to the burns sustained on July 15, 2018.  (Claimant’s Ex. 1) 
Ultimately, Dr. Stoken diagnosed claimant with chemical burns to his bilateral 
arms, abdomen, chest and back.  She diagnosed chronic itchiness and irritation of the 
skin following these burns.  Dr. Stoken also diagnosed post-traumatic stress disorder 
and respiratory distress after jogging for two minutes.  (Claimant’s Ex. 1, p. 5)  Dr. 
Stoken opined that claimant sustained a nine percent permanent impairment of the 
whole person due to his skin disorder.  She did not offer an impairment rating for the 
respiratory condition but recommended an evaluation with a pulmonologist.  Claimant 
has not sought and there is no evidence that he has requested a pulmonary evaluation.  
(Claimant’s Ex. 1, p. 6) 
Finally, Dr. Stoken opines that claimant sustained a seven percent permanent 
impairment of the whole person as a result of post-traumatic stress disorder.  
(Claimant’s Ex. 1, p. 6)  In spite of offering these diagnoses and permanent impairment 
ratings, Dr. Stoken does not impose any permanent work restrictions on claimant.  
Instead, she notes he has been “able to work full duty without restrictions.”  (Claimant’s 
Ex. 1, p. 6) 
Considering the competing medical opinions, I first note that no physician or 
mental health provider has imposed permanent work restrictions upon claimant as a 
result of the July 15, 2018 work injury.  I find that Mr. Xaysana requires no permanent 
work restrictions and is capable of working any job without restrictions or limitations.  I 
further find that he could work any of his prior positions for which he has experience and 
is qualified. 
With respect to the issue of the pulmonary injury and scarred cornea allegations, 
I find that claimant has not reported symptoms of pulmonary distress to his treating 
physicians or prior to his evaluation with Dr. Stoken.  Dr. Stoken did not impose 
restrictions or permanent impairment for the respiratory condition.  Claimant has not 
followed-up to obtain additional testing or treatment with a pulmonary specialist.  
Similarly, claimant has not reported symptoms or conditions related to his eye that 
would be attributed to the July 15, 2018 work injury until Dr. Stoken evaluated him in

July 2020.  Claimant failed to prove he sustained a permanent injury to his respiratory 
system or his right eye as a result of the July 15, 2018 work injury. 
Arguably, claimant established he sustained post-traumatic stress disorder 
through the opinions of Dr. Stoken.  However, Dr. Stoken Is not a mental health 
specialist.  Defendants had claimant evaluated by a mental health specialist, Dr. 
Ascheman.  Dr. Ascheman found no mental health diagnosis and opined that claimant 
requires no limitations or restrictions related to a mental health condition.  I find that Dr. 
Ascheman is a specialist and that his opinion should carry greater weight than that 
offered by Dr. Stoken.  I find that claimant failed to prove he sustained post-traumatic 
stress disorder.  I find that claimant failed to prove he sustained permanent impairment 
or that he requires any permanent restrictions or treatment as a result of the alleged 
mental injury. 
Finally, Dr. Broghammer and Dr. Stoken disagree about whether claimant 
qualifies for any permanent impairment related to his burns.  Claimant demonstrated the 
scarring on his body at the hearing.  Obviously, the hearing was conducted remotely via 
conference call.  However, the scarring claimant refers to is minimal and not significant 
or obvious to my untrained eye. 
I acknowledge claimant’s testimony about ongoing symptoms of itchiness.  
However, none of the physicians have imposed permanent restrictions or recommended 
additional treatment for the burn injuries.  Instead, I find that the burn injuries achieved 
maximum medical improvement when claimant was released from the University of 
Iowa Hospitals and Clinics’ care on September 6, 2018.  I find that claimant requires no 
permanent restrictions related to the burns.  Nor does he require further medical care 
for those injuries. 
Considering the permanent impairment ratings offered, Dr. Broghammer’s 
opinion seems more consistent with the other medical evidence.  Given that claimant 
requires no permanent restrictions, no further treatment for the burns, and has returned 
to full-time and full-duty work, it is difficult to accept Dr. Stoken’s impairment rating as 
accurate.  She provides no analysis of how claimant receives impairment without any 
need for treatment or any limitations.  Having rejected Dr. Stoken’s opinions on the 
mental health issues, I similarly reject them with respect to the permanent impairment 
rating.  I accept the opinion of Dr. Broghammer as most credible, convincing, and 
consistent with the other medical evidence in this record.  Therefore, I find that claimant 
failed to prove he sustained permanent impairment as a result of the burn injuries 
sustained on July 15, 2018. 
Ultimately, while claimant’s injuries seem gruesome and likely very painful, Mr. 
Xaysana has achieved a very good result after recovery.  He has no limitations and is 
fully capable of performing his current job as well as all jobs he has held in the past.  He 
has no actual ongoing loss of income form the injury.  As a result, I find that Mr. 
Xaysana failed to prove that he sustained permanent disability as a result of the July 15, 
2018 work injury.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony.  The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question.  The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances.  The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); 
Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995).  Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994).  Unrebutted expert medical 
testimony cannot be summarily rejected.  Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 
N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 1994). 
Having accepted the opinions of Dr. Broghammer, Dr. Ascheman, and the 
treating physician at the University of Iowa Hospitals and Clinics’ burn unit as most 
convincing, I found that Mr. Xaysana failed to prove he sustained permanent disability 
as a result of the July 15, 2018 work injury.  Therefore, I conclude claimant is not 
entitled to an award of permanent disability.   
Mr. Xaysana also seeks an award of alternate medical care.  The employer shall 
furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, chiropractic, podiatric, 
physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance, and hospital services and supplies for all 
conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The employer shall also 
allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred for those services.  
The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except where the employer 
has denied liability for the injury.  Section 85.27.  Holbert v. Townsend Engineering Co., 
Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening 
October 16, 1975). 
Of course, claimant still bears the burden to prove that the care sought in an 
alternate medical care claim is causally related, reasonable and necessary.  Iowa Code 
section 85.27.  In this instance, I found the opinions of Dr. Ascheman to be most 
convincing and credible on the mental health issues.  Dr. Ascheman opined that 
claimant did not have a mental health diagnosis and does not require future treatment 
for a mental health condition.  Having accepted Dr. Ascheman’s opinions as convincing 
and accurate, I conclude that Mr. Xaysana failed to prove he sustained a mental health

injury as a result of the July 15, 2018 work injury.  Accordingly, I conclude that claimant 
cannot establish entitlement to alternate medical care and specifically treatment for an 
alleged but unproven post-traumatic stress disorder. 
Claimant also seeks reimbursement for his independent medical evaluation with 
Dr. Stoken pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.39.  Section 85.39(2) was amended in 
2017 and now provides: 
If an evaluation of permanent disability has been made by a physician 
retained by the employer and the employee believes this evaluation to be 
too low, the employee shall . . .  be reimbursed by the employer the 
reasonable fee for a subsequent examination by a physician of the 
employee’s own choice. 
The amendment to the statute provides, “An employer is not liable for the cost of 
such an examination if the injury for which the employee is being examined is 
determined not to be a compensable injury.”  Iowa Code section 85.39(2). 
The burn injuries for which claimant was primarily evaluated by Dr. Stoken are an 
admitted injury and clearly constituted a “compensable injury.”  (Hearing Report)  
Therefore, if claimant met the remainder of the requirements of Iowa Code section 
85.39, he may obtain reimbursement.  In this case, Dr. Broghammer performed an 
evaluation and rendered an opinion about permanent impairment in August 2019.  Dr. 
Stoken evaluated claimant in July 2020.  Accordingly, Dr. Stoken’s evaluation was in 
response to the opinions of Dr. Broghammer and I conclude is an appropriate 
evaluation pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.39.  I conclude that defendants should be 
ordered to reimburse claimant’s independent medical evaluation fee in the amount of 
$2,600.00, less the amount ($800.00) they have already advanced.  I conclude 
defendants should be ordered to reimburse claimant an additional $1,800.00 for Dr. 
Stoken’s evaluation.  Iowa Code section 85.39(2). 
Finally, claimant seeks assessment of his costs associated with this contested 
case proceeding.  Specifically, claimant seeks assessment of his filing fee ($100.00).  
Costs are assessed at the discretion of the agency.  Iowa Code section 86.40.  In this 
instance, claimant has failed to establish entitlement to any permanent disability or 
future medical treatment.  Exercising the agency’s discretion, I decline to assess 
claimant’s costs.
