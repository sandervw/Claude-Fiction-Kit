PROCEDURAL HISTORY AND FACTUAL BACKGROUND 
On December 17, 2025, claimant filed an application for alternate medical care 
under Iowa Code section 85.27(4), invoking the provisions of rule 876 IAC 4.48.  In her 
application, the claimant alleges that she sustained an injury to her whole body as a 
result of an injury at work in 2022.  Claimant also filed six pages of exhibits which are 
part of the record. 
A hearing was scheduled to be held by telephone.  Just prior to hearing, on 
December 30, 2025, defendants filed an answer to the application wherein defendants 
stated that they are seeking a further causation opinion regarding the condition for 
which the claimant is seeking treatment.  The defendants indicate specifically that they 
can neither admit nor deny the condition for which claimant is seeking treatment until 
they receive this opinion.  Upon review of this answer, I contacted counsel for both 
parties via email and instructed them of the following:   
The defendants have refused to accept liability for the condition for which 
claimant is seeking treatment.  By our standards, the agency considers 
this a “denial of liability.”  My plan is to cancel the hearing and enter an 
order dismissing the alt care petition but I wanted to give both parties an 
opportunity to let me know if there are any other matters I should consider. 
Claimant’s counsel responded as follows:

Claimant’s counsel argued that the claimant’s condition is Long Covid.  She 
asserted that the claimant has Long Covid from a work injury which has resulted in a 
number of disabling symptoms, including cognitive dysfunction.  She argued the 
defendants accepted the claim and authorized treatment.  When claimant was sent for 
an impairment rating for this condition, a neuropsychological evaluation was arranged 
with Daniel Tranel, Ph.D, in October 2025.  As part of his opinion, Dr. Tranel 
recommended further treatment in the form of “cognitive rehabilitation therapy” to 
address her weaknesses in “sustained attention, working memory, processing speed 
and comprehension …”  (Claimant Exhibit, page 6) The claimant is seeking an order 
from this agency, instructing the defendants to authorize the treatment recommended 
by Dr. Tranel.  
Claimant’s counsel argued that the defendants have accepted this claim and the 
matter should proceed to hearing to determine whether her cognitive symptoms are 
causally connected to her Long Covid.  Moreover, she asserted that the failure or 
refusal of this agency to do so violates the claimant’s Due Process and other 
constitutional rights.  I find that she has preserved this issue. 
The defendants agree that they have accepted the claimant’s Long Covid as a 
work injury and argue that they have authorized reasonable medical care for this 
condition.  They contend, however, that her cognitive dysfunction is a condition which 
was only diagnosed after the neuropsychological evaluation with Dr. Tranel in October 
2025.  They further contend that within Dr. Tranel’s opinion, it is not entirely clear 
whether her cognitive dysfunction is truly causally connected to her work injury.  The 
defendants assert that, since Dr. Tranel issued his report in October 2025, they have 
attempted to secure a clarification regarding the causal connection of this condition with 
the work injury.  Defense counsel indicates this process has been challenging, but did 
say he expects to receive an answer within a few weeks or so, although this was clearly 
just an estimate.  It is the position of the defendants that until Dr. Tranel provides a 
clarification, they can neither admit nor deny the claimant’s cognitive condition as part of 
this claim, and in the meantime, this claim must be dismissed since liability for this 
condition has not been established at this time. 
CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Before any benefits can be ordered, including medical benefits, compensability of 
the claim must be established, either by admission of liability or by adjudication.  The 
summary provisions of Iowa Code section 85.27(4) as more particularly described in 
rule 876 IAC 4.48, are not designed to adjudicate disputed compensability of a claim.

Iowa Code Section 85.27(4) states: 
For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish reasonable 
services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has the right to 
choose the care. If the employer chooses the care, the employer shall hold 
the employee harmless for the cost of care until the employer notifies the 
employee that the employer is no longer authorizing all or any part of the 
care and the reason for the change in authorization. An employer is not 
liable for the cost of care that the employer arranges in response to a 
sudden emergency if the employee’s condition, for which care was 
arranged, is not related to the employment. The treatment must be offered 
promptly and be reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue 
inconvenience to the employee. If the employee has reason to be 
dissatisfied with the care offered, the employee should communicate the 
basis of such dissatisfaction to the employer, in writing if requested, 
following which the employer and the employee may agree to alternate care 
reasonably suited to treat the injury. If the employer and employee cannot 
agree on such alternate care, the commissioner may, upon application and 
reasonable proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and order other care. In 
an emergency, the employee may choose the employee’s care at the 
employer’s expense, provided the employer or the employer’s agent cannot 
be reached immediately. An application made under this subsection shall 
be considered an original proceeding for purposes of commencement and 
contested case proceedings under section 85.26. The hearing shall be 
conducted pursuant to chapter 17A. Before a hearing is scheduled, the 
parties may choose a telephone hearing or an in-person hearing. A request 
for an in-person hearing shall be approved unless the in-person hearing 
would be impractical because of the distance between the parties to the 
hearing. The workers’ compensation commissioner shall issue a decision 
within ten working days of receipt of an application for alternate care made 
pursuant to a telephone hearing or within fourteen working days of receipt 
of an application for alternate care made pursuant to an in-person hearing. 
The employer shall notify an injured employee of the employee’s ability to 
contest the employer’s choice of care pursuant to this subsection.  
876 Iowa Administrative Code Section 4.48(6) states: 
Employer liability. Application cannot be filed under this rule if the liability of 
the employer is an issue. If an application is filed where the liability of the 
employer is an issue, the application will be dismissed without prejudice. 
(Petitions for alternate care where liability of the employer is an issue should 
be filed pursuant to rule 876—4.1(85,85A,85B,86,87,17A).) 
 
The Iowa Supreme Court has held:   
We emphasize that the commissioner’s ability to decide the merits of a 
section 85.27(4) alternate medical care claim is limited to situations where

the compensability of an injury is conceded, but the reasonableness of a 
particular course of treatment for the compensable injury is disputed. . . .  
Thus, the commissioner cannot decide the reasonableness of the 
alternate care claim without also necessarily deciding the ultimate 
disputed issue in the case:  whether or not the medical condition Barnett 
was suffering at the time of the request was a work-related injury.  
 
. . . . 
Once an employer takes the position in response to a claim for alternate 
medical care that the care sought is for a noncompensatory injury, the 
employer cannot assert an authorization defense in response to a 
subsequent claim by the employee for the expenses of the alternate 
medical care.  
R. R. Donnelly & Sons v. Barnett, 670 N.W.2d 190, 197-198 (Iowa 2003) (fn 2). 
I have reviewed the arguments of both parties, as well as the relevant statutes, 
rules and case law.  I find the claimant’s petition seeking medical treatment must be 
dismissed because the defendants refuse to accept liability for the condition for which 
claimant seeks treatment (cognitive dysfunction).  I further find the defendants thereby 
lose their right to control the medical care claimant seeks in this proceeding, and the 
claimant is free to choose that care on her own.  Bell Bros. Heating and Air Conditioning 
v. Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d 193 (Iowa 2010). 
As a result of the denial of liability for the condition sought to be treated in this 
proceeding, claimant may obtain reasonable medical care from any provider for this 
treatment, but at claimant’s expense, and seek reimbursement for such care using 
regular claim proceedings before this agency. 
The claimant argues that the condition for which she is seeking treatment is Long 
Covid and that the defendants have accepted liability for this condition.  She argues that 
the agency should hold a hearing and determine whether her cognitive symptoms are in 
fact related to the condition of Long Covid.  Moreover, she argues it is a violation of the 
statute and her Constitutional rights to deny her a full evidentiary hearing.  She also 
asserts that agency rule 876 IAC Section 4.48(6) exceeds the statutory authority 
derived from Section 85.27(4).  She has gone to some lengths to appropriately preserve 
this issue for appeal.  The defendants argue that the claimant’s alleged cognitive 
challenges are, in fact, a condition which must be causally connected to her work injury 
before they are required to provide treatment.  Until such time, they argue, this agency 
cannot order them to provide treatment. 
I am sympathetic to the claimant’s position; however, I find that the law as written 
and previously interpreted is not in her favor.  I am sympathetic because I understand 
that alternate medical care is one area of our workers’ compensation law that works 
efficiently for injured workers.  It is an expedited process with strict time deadlines, 
unlike regular claim proceedings.  The parties to these proceedings receive fast, binding

determinations of their legal questions regarding medical treatment.  The problem is, it 
is not a particularly conducive process to allow a full hearing on a complex medical 
causation issue.  For example, the rules specifically prohibit a party from entering more 
than 10 pages of exhibits.  There is also essentially no discovery or other common due 
process requirements afforded to the parties.  Under this framework, it would be quite 
difficult to accurately and efficiently determine complex medical causation issues.  
Generally, these types of issues are better determined in regular claim proceedings, 
which are available to the claimant in this instance. 
For all these reasons, I find that this claim must be dismissed without prejudice at 
this time. 
IT IS THEREFORE ORDERED that this case should be and is hereby dismissed 
without prejudice. 
IT IS FURTHER ORDERED that if claimant seeks to recover the charges 
incurred in obtaining the care for which defendants deny liability, defendants are barred 
from asserting lack of authorization as a defense for those charges. 
The commissioner issued an order under Iowa Code section 10A.305 delegating 
authority to deputy workers’ compensation commissioners, such as the undersigned, to 
issue final agency decisions on applications for alternate care. Consequently, there is 
no appeal of this decision to the commissioner. If a party disagrees with the alternate 
care decision, the party may seek judicial review in district court under the Iowa 
Administrative Procedure Act, Iowa Code chapter 17A.
