STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Zachary Parson (“Parson”), filed a petition in arbitration seeking 
workers’ compensation benefits against defendants, Menard, Inc. and AIU Insurance 
Co.  The hearing occurred before the undersigned on October 9, 2023.  This case 
proceeded to a live video hearing via Zoom. 
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the hearing. In the 
hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations. All of those stipulations 
were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration decision, and no factual 
or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised or discussed in this 
decision. The parties are bound by their stipulations. 
The evidentiary record consists of Joint Exhibits 1 through 6; Claimant’s Exhibits 
1 through 7, and Defendants’ Exhibits A through M. Claimant testified on his own behalf. 
Mark Erickson testified on behalf of defendants.

When comparing the impairment ratings offered, I note that Dr. Gainer based his

Additionally, the permanent restrictions recommended by Dr. Bansal are 
significantly more restrictive that what Parson was able to demonstrate through work

For these reasons, I favor and accept the impairment rating and restrictions

Suspension of temporary benefits is allowed if suitable work is offered and 
intentionally declined. The offer of suitable temporary work must be in writing.  The 
written offer must also communicate to the claimant that if he refuses the offer of 
temporary work, the claimant shall communicate the refusal and the reason for the 
refusal to the employer in writing and that during the period of the refusal the claimant

Page 7 
 
will not be compensated with temporary partial, temporary total, or healing period 
benefits, unless the work refused is not suitable.   
It is undisputed Parson returned to work in a light duty capacity and the 
defendant employer accommodated Parson’s restrictions for a period of time before his 
termination.  Clearly, the defendant employer offered suitable work to Parson; however, 
the offer of work was not in writing and it did not notify Parson of the consequences of 
refusing to accept said work. 
Defendants assert Parson’s intentional acts on September 14, 2022, were 
tantamount to a refusal of suitable light duty work, and as such, Parson is not entitled to 
any additional temporary benefits.   
Parson testified that assistant manager Oelke gave him permission to run home 
on September 14, 2022. (Hr. Tr., p. 49)  No witnesses to this alleged interaction testified 
at the evidentiary hearing; however, Oelke produced a written statement that provides, 
“At no time did Zach state that he needed to leave for any kind of emergency.” (Ex. D, p. 
47)  Oelke was not deposed and did not testify live at hearing.  As such, I did not have 
the opportunity to observe Oelke or his demeanor.  Additionally, Oelke’s statement is 
not a sworn statement.  The written statement comes in the form of an email between 
Oelke and a representative for defendants’ third-party administrator.  The e-mail is not 
signed or notarized.  While Oelke’s statement appears to challenge Parson’s testimony, 
it sidesteps explicitly denying that Parson sought permission to leave work to tend to his 
wound.  In other words, the language used by Oelke leaves room for the interpretation 
that Parson did indeed seek approval to leave work on September 14, 2022.  For these 
reasons, I do not afford Oelke’s statement significant weight. 
Regardless of whether Parson sought approval to leave work for a period of time 
on September 14, 2022, he was ultimately terminated for “time theft.”  In other words, 
Parson was not terminated because he left work; he was terminated because his 
timecard did not reflect that he left work for a period of time.  There is no evidence 
Parson’s timecard was finalized or submitted for processing and payment.  Indeed, 
Menard’s Human Resources Coordinator, Mark Erickson, testified that employees have, 
in the past, amended their punch card “a number of days” after they missed work. (Hr. 
Tr., p. 71)  Parson failed to amend his punch card on the day he spent time away from 
work and was terminated at the start of his shift the next day.   
Time theft can have a serious adverse economic impact on the employer; 
however, given Mr. Erickson’s testimony, it is clear failing to amend a punch card within 
24 hours is not the type of conduct that would cause any employer, including Menard, to 
terminate any employee.  While an employee is not entitled to act with impunity toward 
the employer, not every act of misconduct justifies disqualifying an employee from 
workers' compensation benefits.  I do not find that Parson engaged in the type of 
conduct that is tantamount to a refusal of suitable work.  I find defendants failed to prove 
Parson should be disqualified from receiving temporary benefits.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The first issue to be addressed is whether the claimant is entitled to additional 
temporary disability benefits.  Claimant asserts he is entitled to temporary benefits from 
September 15, 2022, to October 25, 2022, and from November 22, 2022, through May 
1, 2023.  Claimant is also seeking a running award of healing period benefits beginning 
August 11, 2023.   
Iowa Code section 85.33 (2021) governs temporary disability benefits, and Iowa 
Code section 85.34 governs healing period and permanent disability benefits. Dunlap v. 
Action Warehouse, 824 N.W.2d 545, 556 (Iowa Ct. App. 2012). 
An employee has a temporary partial disability when because of the employee's 
medical condition, “it is medically indicated that the employee is not capable of returning 
to employment substantially similar to the employment in which the employee was 
engaged at the time of the injury, but is able to perform other work consistent with the 
employee's disability.” Iowa Code § 85.33(2). Temporary partial disability benefits are 
payable, in lieu of temporary total disability and healing period benefits, due to the 
reduction in earning ability as a result of the employee's temporary partial disability, and 
“shall not be considered benefits payable to an employee, upon termination of 
temporary partial or temporary total disability, the healing period, or permanent partial 
disability, because the employee is not able to secure work paying weekly earnings 
equal to the employee's weekly earnings at the time of the injury.” (Id.) 
Temporary total, temporary partial, and healing period benefits can be interrupted 
or intermittent. Teel v. McCord, 394 N.W.2d 405 (Iowa 1986); Stourac-Floyd v. MDF 
Endeavors, Inc., File No. 5053328 (App. Sept. 11, 2018); Stevens v. Eastern Star 
Masonic Home, File No. 5049776 (App. Mar. 14, 2018). 
Defendants assert Parson is not entitled to any additional temporary disability 
benefits as a result of his September 16, 2022, termination for time theft. (Ex. 2, p. 16)   
Refusal of suitable work has been an affirmative defense in workers' 
compensation cases in Iowa for many years. In 2017, the Iowa Legislature codified the 
affirmative defense and imposed additional requirements on the parties that were not 
required under the common law.  Iowa Code section 85.33(3) precludes an employee 
who refuses suitable work offered by the employer, consistent with the employee's 
disability, from receiving temporary or healing period benefits during the period of 
refusal. Neal v. Annett Holdings, Inc., 814 N.W.2d 512, 520 (Iowa 2012).  The employer 
bears the burden to establish the prerequisites of Iowa Code section 85.33(3). Koehler 
v. American Color Graphics, File No. 1248489 (App. Feb. 25, 2005).  
Dr. Gainer allowed claimant to return to work with a restriction of no use of his 
right arm on August 19, 2022. (JE2, p. 20)  By all accounts, the defendant employer 
provided a suitable work offer and claimant accepted the offer by presenting for light 
duty work on August 19, 2022.  Claimant worked in a light duty capacity until the 
defendant employer terminated his employment.

For misconduct to disqualify an employee from compensation, the misconduct 
must be tantamount to a refusal of suitable work. Reynolds v. Hy-Vee, Inc., 2017 WL 
5176028, File No. 5046203 (Iowa Workers' Comp. Comm'n Oct. 31, 2017). Termination 
by itself is not sufficient grounds to disqualify an employee from temporary benefits 
under Iowa Code section 85.33(3). Gully v. Liguria Foods, Inc., 2020 WL 599659, File 
No. 5063429 (Iowa Workers' Comp. Comm'n Jan. 30, 2020) (internal citations omitted). 
The Commissioner has held, 
The misconduct must be serious and the type of conduct that would cause 
any employer to terminate any employee. The misconduct must have a 
serious adverse impact on the employer. The misconduct must be more 
than the type of inconsequential misconduct that employers typically 
overlook or tolerate. An employee working with restrictions is not entitled to 
act 
with 
impunity 
toward 
the 
employer 
and 
the 
employer's 
interests. Nevertheless, not every act of misconduct justifies disqualifying 
an employee from workers' compensation benefits even though the 
employer may be justified in taking disciplinary action. 
Reynolds, at 2.   
I found defendants failed to establish that the claimant engaged in disqualifying 
misconduct.  The precipitous nature of the termination, coupled with Mr. Erickson’s 
testimony that employees have, in the past, been able to amend their punch card “a 
number of days” after they have missed work, served as aggravating factors in this 
case. 
Claimant was placed at maximum medical improvement and released to work 
without restrictions for his right wrist on May 1, 2023.  Therefore, I find claimant is 
entitled to temporary disability benefits from September 15, 2022, through October 25, 
2022, and from November 22, 2022, through May 1, 2023.   
Claimant was excused from work on August 12, 2023, and August 14, 2023, for 
his right shoulder complaints.  On August 14, 2023, claimant was restricted from lifting 
with his right arm.  Unfortunately, Fareway could not accommodate claimant’s 
restrictions.  Claimant has not worked for Fareway since August 11, 2023. (Hr. Tr., p. 
25)   As of the date of the evidentiary hearing, claimant is not capable of performing 
substantially similar employment.  I found claimant had not yet reached maximum 
medical improvement at the time of the evidentiary hearing.  Therefore, I conclude that 
claimant satisfied his burden to show he is entitled to a running award of temporary 
disability benefits. 
Claimant seeks an order for alternate medical care; however, now that the 
parties have stipulated to causation with respect to the right shoulder, it appears 
claimant is merely seeking an order for ongoing medical treatment.   
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance, and hospital services

and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law. The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services. The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury. Iowa Code section 85.27. Holbert 
v. Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial

Claimant is entitled to ongoing medical care relating to the right wrist and right 
shoulder. 
The next issue for determination is whether claimant is entitled to reimbursement 
for an independent medical evaluation under Iowa Code section 85.39. Section 85.39 
permits an employee to be reimbursed for subsequent examination by a physician of 
the employee's choice where an employer-retained physician has previously evaluated 
“permanent disability” and the employee believes that the initial evaluation is too low. 
The Iowa Supreme Court provided a literal interpretation of the plain-language of 
Iowa Code section 85.39, stating that section 85.39 only allows the employee to obtain 
an independent medical evaluation at the employer's expense if dissatisfied with the 
evaluation arranged by the employer. Des Moines Area Reg'l Transit Auth. v. Young, 
867 N.W.2d 839, 847 (Iowa 2015). Under the Young decision, an employee can only 
obtain an IME at the employer's expense if an evaluation of permanent disability has 
been made by an employer-retained physician. 
Dr. Gainer, an employer-retained physician, conducted an impairment 
assessment on May 1, 2023.  Dr. Bansal conducted an independent medical evaluation 
of claimant on August 8, 2023.  I conclude that the prerequisites of section 85.39 were 
met, and defendants shall reimburse claimant for the reasonable costs of Dr. Bansal’s 
IME in the amount of $3,279.00. 
The last issue to be addressed is costs. Assessment of costs is a discretionary 
function of the agency. Iowa Code § 10A.328. Because claimant was successful in his 
claim, I conclude it is appropriate to assess claimant's costs in some amount. 
Claimant seeks reimbursement for his filing fees. These costs are appropriate 
and assessed pursuant to 876 IAC 4.33(7).

must be filed via Workers’ Compensation Electronic System (WCES) unless the filing party has been granted 
permission by the Division of Workers’ Compensation to file documents in paper form. If such permission has been 
granted, the notice of appeal must be filed at the following address: Workers’ Compensation Commissioner, Iowa 
Division of Workers’ Compensation, 150 Des Moines Street, Des Moines, Iowa 50309-1836. The notice of appeal 
must be received by the Division of Workers’ Compensation within 20 days from the date of the decision. The appeal 
period will be extended to the next business day if the last day to appeal falls on a weekend or legal holiday.
