This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A.  The 
expedited procedures of rule 876 IAC 4.48, the “alternate medical care” rule, are 
invoked by claimant, Danial Kray.   
This alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on August 6, 2020.  The 
proceedings were recorded digitally and constitute the official record of the hearing.  By 
an order filed by the workers’ compensation commissioner, this decision is designated 
final agency action.  Any appeal would be a petition for judicial review under Iowa Code 
section 17A.19.   
The record in this case consists of Claimant’s Exhibits 1-6, Defendants’ Exhibit 
A-B, and the testimony of claimant.  
ISSUE 
The issue presented for resolution in this case is whether claimant is entitled to 
alternate medical care consisting of authorization of care to see Kyle Galles, M.D. for a 
second opinion. 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
Defendants accept liability for a work-related injury to claimant occurring on 
January 16, 2019.

On December 9, 2019, claimant was evaluated by Mathew Bollier, M.D. with the 
University of Iowa Hospitals and Clinics (UIHC) Orthopedic unit.  Claimant still had 
some weakness with overhead activity.  Dr. Bollier found claimant was at maximum 
medical improvement (MMI).  He returned claimant to work with no restrictions.  
Dr. Bollier found claimant had a two percent permanent impairment to the body as a 
whole as a result of his injury.  Dr. Bollier noted he believed claimant’s condition would 
continue to improve over the next few months.  He noted it was possible claimant would 
develop more pain in the shoulder or would fail to fully heal.  He also noted that while it 
was unlikely, claimant could also require additional medical treatment.  (Exhibit 1) 
Claimant testified he had two left shoulder surgeries.  He said the second surgery 
was performed by Dr. Bollier.  He said he received small improvement in his left 
shoulder condition following Dr. Bollier’s surgery.  Claimant testified that in December of 
2019 he did not feel he was able to return to work without restrictions. 
Claimant underwent an independent medical evaluation (IME) with Charles 
Wenzel, D.O.  In a May 27, 2020 report, Dr. Wenzel recommended claimant get a 
second opinion for his shoulder condition from Kyle Galles, M.D. regarding ongoing 
shoulder problems.  (Ex. 2) 
In a June 22, 2020 email, claimant’s counsel requested defendants authorize the 
second opinion visit with Dr. Galles.  (Ex. 3) 
In a July 10, 2020 email, defendants indicated they would authorize a return visit 
to Dr. Bollier for a follow-up exam.  (Ex. 5, page 2) 
In a July 15, 2020 email, Dr. Wenzel indicated a second opinion needed to be 
provided by another physician and not Dr. Bollier.  (Ex. 4) 
In a July 20, 2020 email, claimant’s counsel again requested defendants 
authorize a second opinion with Dr. Galles.  (Ex. 3) 
In a July 28, 2020 email, defendants indicated claimant was authorized to treat 
with Dr. Bollier.  (Ex. B) 
Claimant testified he still has loss of strength, pain and loss of range of motion in 
the left shoulder.  He testified he wants to see Dr. Galles for a second opinion. 
CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa Rule of Appellate 
Procedure 6.14(6).

Iowa Code section 85.27(4) provides, in relevant part:  
For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish reasonable 
services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has the right to choose the 
care. . . .  The treatment must be offered promptly and be reasonably suited to treat the 
injury without undue inconvenience to the employee.  If the employee has reason to be 
dissatisfied with the care offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such 
dissatisfaction to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the employer and 
the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited to treat the injury.  If the 
employer and employee cannot agree on such alternate care, the commissioner may, 
upon application and reasonable proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and order other 
care   
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking 
alternate care – claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized 
care is unreasonable.  See Iowa R. App. P 14(f)(5); Long v. Roberts Dairy 
Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  Determining what care is reasonable 
under the statute is a question of fact.  Id.  The employer’s obligation turns 
on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.  Id.; Harned v. 
Farmland Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983).  In Pirelli-Armstrong 
Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 433 (Iowa 1997).   
The employee requesting the care has the burden to prove the care being 
offered by the employer is unreasonable.  R.R. Donnelly & Sons v. Barnett, 670 N.W.2d 
190, 196–96 (Iowa 2003); Lynch Livestock v. Bursell, No. 14-1133, Filed May 20, 2015 
(Iowa Ct. App.).   
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  Long v. Roberts Dairy Company, 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).     
Claimant has had two shoulder surgeries.  The most recent surgery was 
performed by Dr. Bollier.  In December of 2019, Dr. Bollier found claimant at MMI.  He 
noted it was possible claimant would develop more pain and may fail to fully heal.  He 
also noted it was possible claimant would require additional medical care in the future.   
Claimant wants to see Dr. Galles for a second opinion regarding his left shoulder.  
This is because claimant still has pain, loss of strength and loss of range of motion in 
the shoulder.  Defendants have not authorized claimant to see Dr. Galles, but have 
authorized claimant to return to Dr. Bollier.

I appreciate and empathize with claimant’s situation.  He wants to regain the 
strength in his shoulder.  He wants to improve the range of motion in his shoulder.  And 
he wants to not have pain in the left shoulder.   
However, defendants have authorized claimant to return to Dr. Bollier.  Dr. Bollier 
has not refused to treat claimant.  Dr. Bollier has not indicated there are no other 
treatment options for claimant.  Dr. Bollier did acknowledge in December of 2019 
claimant might have further pain and might require further medical treatment.   
Given this record, it is found claimant has failed to carry his burden of proof the 
offered care, the authorization to return to Dr. Bollier, is unreasonable. 
ORDER 
For the reasons detailed above, claimant’s petition for alternate medical care is 
denied.
