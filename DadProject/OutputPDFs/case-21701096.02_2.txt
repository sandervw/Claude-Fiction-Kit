Defendant filed a motion for order nunc pro tunc. Claimant has not filed a 
response to the motion. It is clear that the undersigned made a scrivener’s error in the 
order section of the arbitration decision that can and should be corrected by nunc pro 
tunc order. 
 
The phrase, “nunc pro tunc” means “now for then.” See: Black’s Law Dictionary, 
page 1218 (Revised 4th Edition 1968). The definition in Black’s Law Dictionary further 
provides: “A phrase applied to acts allowed to be done after the time when they should 
be done, with a retroactive effect, i.e., with the same effect as if regularly done.” Black’s 
at 1218. A nunc pro tunc order “is not for the purpose of correcting judicial thinking, a 
judicial conclusion, or a mistake of law.” Headley v. Headley, 172 N.W.2d 104, 108 
(Iowa 1969). The nunc pro tunc order can be employed to correct obvious errors or to 
make an order conform to the judge’s original intent. Graber v. Dist. Court for 
Washington Cty., 410 N.W.2d 224, 229 (Iowa 1987). Brinson v. Spee Dee Delivery 
Service, No. 8-754/06-2074 (Iowa Ct. App. November 13, 2008) “[T]he intent of the trial 
judge is crucial to the determination of whether a nunc pro tunc order is appropriate to 
‘correct’ a record.” Freeman v. Ernst & Young, 541 N.W.2d 890, 893 (Iowa 1995), citing 
McVay v. Kenneth E. Montz Implement Co., 287 N.W.2d 149, 151 (Iowa 1980). 
 
The undersigned issued an arbitration decision in these consolidated cases on 
May 12, 2025. The decision stated, for file number 21701096.02: “Defendant is entitled 
to a credit under Iowa Code section 85.34(4)-(5) in the amount of one thousand twenty-
three and 11/100 dollars ($1,023.11), as outlined above.” For file number 22700204.02, 
the decision stated: “Defendant is entitled to a credit under Iowa Code section 85.34(4)-
(5) in the amount of three thousand seven hundred sixty-one and 99/100 dollars 
($3,761.99), as outlined above.” The credit amounts in the original decision were the 
combined total for both the overpayment of TTD and PPD benefits on each file. 
 
Defendant notes that while the “conclusions of law” section of the arbitration 
decision accurately noted that defendant paid 50.857 weeks of permanent partial 
disability (PPD) benefits for the October 20, 2020 date of injury, and 12 weeks of PPD
