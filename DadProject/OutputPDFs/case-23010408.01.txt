STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Adrian Johnson, claimant, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from John Deere Des Moines Works (hereinafter “Deere”), self-
insured employer, as defendant.  Hearing was held via Zoom videoconference on 
December 5, 2024.     
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision.  The parties agreed that the issue of permanent impairment was not ripe for 
determination and the issue of permanency was bifurcated.  (Hearing Transcript, page 
7)  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.  
Adrian Johnson was the only witness to testify live at trial.  Defendant attempted 
to call Lainey Latta as a witness.  (Tr. p. 31) Claimant’s counsel objected to the witness 
because her name had not been previously disclosed as a witness.  (Tr. p. 32)  Defense 
counsel then made the decision to not have Ms. Latta testify.  (Tr. p. 33) The evidentiary 
record also includes Joint Exhibits 1-3, Claimant’s Exhibits 1-4, and Defendant’s 
Exhibits A-I.  All exhibits were received without objection.  The evidentiary record closed 
at the conclusion of the arbitration hearing.       
The parties submitted post-hearing briefs on January 17, 2025, and the case was 
considered fully submitted to the undersigned on January 20, 2025.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 
621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 
(Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
The Iowa Workers Compensation Act imposes time limits on injured employees 
both as to when they must notify their employees of injuries and to when injury claims 
must be filed.  Iowa Code section 85.26(1) requires an employee to bring an original 
proceeding for benefits within two years from the date of the occurrence of the injury if 
the employer has paid the employee no weekly indemnity benefits for the claimed injury.  
If the employer has paid the employee weekly benefits on account of the claimed injury, 
however, the employee must bring an original proceeding within three years from the 
date of last payment of weekly compensation benefits.  
That the employee failed to bring a proceeding within the required time period is 
an affirmative defense which the employer must plead and prove by a preponderance of 
the evidence.  See Dart v. Sheller-Globe Corp., II Iowa Industrial Comm’r Rep. 99 (App. 
1982).

Claimant argues that his petition is not barred by operation of Iowa Code section 
85.26 because the three-year statute of limitations should apply.  Defendant contends 
claimant’s claim is also time barred by the three-year statute of limitations.  There is no 
dispute that defendant did not pay any weekly workers’ compensation benefits to this

The issue of wages in lieu of weekly workers’ compensation benefits was 
recently addressed by the Commissioner.  The Commissioner stated,  
This agency enacted an administrative rule, which considers and

Iowa Code sections 85.31, 85.34, 85.36, 85.37, and 85.61.”  876 IAC 8.4.  
Obviously, the administrative rule does not list the statute of limitations 
(Iowa Code section 85.26) as a statute it is intending to interpret or 
implement. 
     In Moffitt v. Super Valu Stores, Inc., File No. 1059425 (Appeal June 
1998), former Commissioner Post pondered when a continuation of wages 
constituted payment of wages in lieu of weekly benefits.  The Commissioner 
phrased the legal question as “whether regular salary paid to a salaried 
employee absent due to work injury can be construed as weekly benefits … 
under section 86.13.”  Commissioner Post concluded: 
It would be illogical to allow an employer to disguise the true 
nature of disability payments merely by designating them 
something else.  Such an approach would be unfair and 
misleading to claimants, in that although payments were 
made, the statute of limitations to bring an action would be two

years instead of the three contemplated by the statute when 
payments are voluntarily made.  If the true nature of the 
payments was workers’ compensation weekly benefits, that 
nature controls over the label of salary assigned by the 
employer.  
Moffitt v. Super Valu Stores, Inc., File No. 1059425 (Appeal June 
1998). 
Ultimately, Moffitt held that not every continuance of salary during periods 
of absences constitute payment of wages in lieu of benefits.  However, the 
case rejected a merely subjective test to determine whether wages are paid 
in lieu of weekly benefits.  Moffitt established a three-prong test to determine 
whether wages are paid in lieu of weekly benefits.  Under the Moffitt test, 
wages are considered to be paid in lieu of benefits if:  (1) the employer was 
aware of the work-related nature of the injury; (2) the employee was off work 
due to the injury, and (3) the employer paid regular salary or wages to the 
injured worker while off work “as a substitute for the workers’ compensation 
payments it would otherwise be obligated to pay.”  Moffitt v. Super Valu 
Stores, Inc., File No. 1059425 (Appeal June 1998).  
Hodges v. St. Mary’s Catholic Church and Diocese of Davenport, Agency File Nos. 
22700635.01, 1597200.01 (App. January 15, 2025). 
Deere paid claimant WI pay from May 15, 2023 through May 21, 2023.  (Cl. Ex. 
2, p. 6) Claimant does not cite any legal authority for his contention that payment of “WI 
pay” is the equivalent of payment of wages.  I conclude that claimant failed to 
demonstrate that payment of WI pay is the equivalent to the payment of wages.  Based 
on the above findings of fact, I conclude that claimant has failed to demonstrate by a 
preponderance of the evidence that he received payment of wages in lieu of workers’ 
compensation benefits in May 2023.  
Furthermore, based on the above findings of fact, I conclude claimant failed to 
demonstrate that he was off work in May 2023 due to his work injury.  (Tr. p. 20) There 
is no medical documentation that he was restricted by a medical provider from work due 
to the August 3, 2021 date of injury.  Additionally, claimant’s own answers to 
interrogatories admit that as of July 2024 he had not lost any time from work due to his 
August 3, 2021 work injury. Therefore, I conclude that claimant’s claim is barred by 
operation of Iowa Code section 85.26.
