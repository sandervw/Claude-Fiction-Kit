STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
Ronald Hodges, claimant, filed a petition for arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from employer St. Mary’s Catholic Church and Diocese of 
Davenport and their insurer GuideOne Insurance Company. This case came before the 
undersigned for an arbitration hearing on September 6, 2023. The case proceeded to a 
live video hearing via Zoom.   
 
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the hearing. On the 
hearing report, the parties entered into numerous stipulations. Those stipulations were 
accepted, and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be made 
or discussed. The parties are now bound by their stipulations. 
 
The evidentiary record includes Joint Exhibits 1 through 9, Claimant’s Exhibits 1 
through 7, and Defendants’ Exhibits A through J.  
 
Claimant testified on his own behalf. Tiara Hatfield testified on behalf of 
defendants. The evidentiary record closed at the conclusion of the evidentiary hearing.  
All parties served their post-hearing briefs on October 16, 2023, at which time this case 
was deemed fully submitted to the undersigned.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
Ronald Hodges, claimant, sustained a stipulated work injury on November 3, 
2013, while conducting the liturgical ceremony of Mass. More specifically, Father (Fr.) 
Hodges was moving from the altar to the ambo, a reading desk, to proclaim the gospel 
when he rolled his left foot. Fr. Hodges heard a “pop” and felt immediate pain. (Hearing 
Transcript, pp. 20-21) X-rays revealed a left fifth metatarsal fracture for which Fr. 
Hodges was provided crutches and a walking boot. (Joint Exhibit 1, pp. 1-2) On March 
4, 2014, due to the fracture separating further, Matthew McKnight, DPM, discussed the 
possibility of surgical correction. (JE 2, p. 4) Fr. Hodges ultimately decided to hold off on 
undergoing surgical intervention. (Hr. Tr., pp. 21-22) 
 
Fr. Hodges sustained a second injury to the left foot while putting books away in 
the church library on or about July 18, 2014. When Dr. McKnight saw Fr. Hodges on 
July 31, 2014, in addition to the prior fifth metatarsal fracture, he diagnosed him with a 
complete subluxation of the 2nd and 3rd metatarsocuneiform joint, elevation of the 
navicular and plantar flexion of the cuboid, acute Charcot, and concern for a Lisfranc 
disruption in his left lower extremity. (JE 2, pp. 6-7) Dr. McKnight recommended Fr. 
Hodges utilize a roundabout scooter as he had significant difficulties with crutches and a 
walker. (Id.) 
 
Dr. McKnight causally related the condition to the July 18, 2014, work injury and 
performed surgery on Fr. Hodges’ left lower extremity on September 19, 2014. (JE 2, 
pp. 12-15)

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
The initial dispute submitted by the parties is whether the statute of limitations 
bars claimant's claim for additional weekly benefits.   
 
The Iowa Workers' Compensation Act imposes time limits on injured employees 
both as to when they must notify their employers of injuries and as to when injury claims 
must be filed. Iowa Code section 85.26(1) requires an employee to bring an original 
proceeding for benefits within two years from the date of the occurrence of the injury if 
the employer has paid the employee no weekly indemnity benefits for the claimed injury. 
If the employer has paid the employee weekly benefits on account of the claimed injury, 
however, the employee must bring an original proceeding within three years from the 
date of last payment of weekly compensation benefits. 
 
That the employee failed to bring a proceeding within the required time period is 
an affirmative defense which the employer must plead and prove by a preponderance of 
the evidence. See Dart v. Sheller-Globe Corp., II Iowa Industrial Comm'r Rep. 99 (App. 
1982). 
 
The undisputed evidence demonstrates that defendants paid 15 weeks of 
permanent partial disability benefits more than three years prior to the filing of claimant’s 
original notice and petition. The last and only payment of weekly benefits occurred on 
March 5, 2015. Accordingly, the statute of limitations for weekly benefits in this claim 
expired in March 2018. 
 
However, claimant asserts that defendants paid him salary or wages in lieu of 
weekly worker's compensation benefits between February 1, 2019 and June 30, 2019, 
and that those payments extend the statute of limitations.1   
 
In Moffitt v. Super Valu Stores, File No. 1059425 (App. June, 1998) the 
commissioner dealt with the question of wages paid in lieu of benefits. In that case, the 
wages paid to claimant were not described as workers' compensation benefits. The 
commissioner concluded that it is the true nature of payments made to an employee 
who is off work that controls over the label attached to it by the employer. In Moffitt, the 
commissioner found three primary characteristics that led to the conclusion that wages 
were paid in lieu of weekly benefits. Those characteristics were: (1) that the employer 
was aware of the work-related nature of the injury; (2) the employee was off work due to 
the injury; and, (3) the employer paid regular salary payments to the injured worker 
while they were off work as a substitute for the workers' compensation payments that it 
otherwise would have been obligated to pay. 
 
In the present case, defendants placed claimant on a leave of absence for 
“health reasons.” Defendants were aware of the work-related nature of claimant’s left

foot injury; however, this was not the only medical condition claimant was dealing with 
at the time of his leave. Additionally, the director of human resources and risk 
management for the Diocese of Davenport believed that Fr. Hodges was placed on 
leave to help him heal from his health issues and to focus on losing weight. (Hr. Tr., p. 
106) Claimant confirmed that the Bishop offered him the opportunity to attend a weight 
loss camp or program. (Hr. Tr., p. 75) 
 
In the weeks and months leading up to the Bishop’s January 2, 2019 letter, 
claimant’s treatment largely focused on his left foot. On October 1, 2018, Dr. Pehde 
removed the external fixator from Fr. Hodges’ left lower extremity and applied an 
internal fixator. (JE 5, pp. 228-230) While he placed claimant on light duty restrictions, 
Dr. Pehde did not restrict claimant from working between February and July 2019. No 
physician restricted claimant from working during this time. 
   
In order for salary or wages to act as compensation, so as to extend the statute 
of limitations to three years from the last date of payment, the compensation benefit 
must be actually due and payable for the period of time for which wages or salary are 
paid. Boyd v. Ankeny Community School District, File No. 1225693, (App. December 
31, 2003). 
 
Fr. Hodges’ leave of absence occurred well after March 5, 2018, the date the 
statute of limitations ran for the July 18, 2014 work injury. In other words, claimant was 
not entitled to receive any indemnity benefits after March 5, 2018. No weekly 
compensation was due and payable to claimant as his claim for such benefits had 
already been extinguished. Additionally, no physician had restricted claimant from 
working between February 1, 2019, and June 30, 2019. The wages he received could 
not have been paid as a substitute for weekly benefits because none were owed.  This 
is not a case in which claimant was taken off work by a physician for a period of time 
and the employer paid regular wages to avoid turning the matter over to a workers' 
compensation insurance carrier. In this case, the evidence that claimant was owed any 
workers' compensation benefits on the dates in question, is lacking. 
 
Having reached these findings of fact, I conclude that the defendants provide that 
more than three years passed between their last payment of weekly benefits and the 
filing of claimant’s original notice and petition. I conclude that claimant failed to establish 
that the payment of wages he received between February 1, 2019, and June 30, 2019, 
constituted payment of wages in lieu of benefits. Ultimately, I conclude that any claim for 
additional weekly benefits is barred pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.26(2). 
 
The remaining issues are moot.
