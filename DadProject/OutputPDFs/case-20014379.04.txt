STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A.  The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Troy Lindholm.  
Claimant appeared through Attorney Ben Roth.  Defendants appeared through their 
attorney, Pat Waldron. 
 
The alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on November 1, 2024.  
The proceedings were digitally recorded through Google Meet.  That recording 
constitutes the official record of this proceeding.  Pursuant to the Commissioner’s Order, 
the undersigned has been delegated authority to issue a final agency decision in this 
alternate medical care proceeding.  Therefore, this ruling is designated final agency 
action and any appeal of the decision would be to the Iowa District Court pursuant to 
Iowa Code section 17A. 
 
The record consists Claimant’s Exhibit 1 and Defense Exhibits A through C, 
which were received without objection.  The defendants do not dispute liability for 
claimant’s November 2020, work injury. 
 
ISSUE 
 
The issue presented for resolution is whether the claimant is entitled to alternate 
medical care.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
The claimant sustained a serious head injury while working for his employer on 
November 23, 2020.  This injury has resulted in numerous compensable disabilities.  
The matter was settled in June 2024, on an open file, meaning that claimant has the 
right to ongoing medical care under Section 85.27. 
 
Prior to hearing, the parties narrowed the issue somewhat in comparison to the 
pleadings.  The issue is whether the claimant is entitled to a broad order authorizing 
chiropractic care as recommended by his chiropractor.  Claimant alleges that he 
requires regular ongoing chiropractic care to treat his symptoms.  He further alleges that 
since the settlement was reached, the defendants have had a pattern of delaying 
authorization of the chiropractic care. 
 
Defendants have authorized ongoing medical care through Joseph Chen, M.D.  
At hearing, defense counsel characterized Dr. Chen as a gatekeeper of sorts, in order 
to monitor the necessity of ongoing chiropractic care, and probably other care.  Defense 
counsel asserts that the specific care claimant is seeking at this time, chiropractic visits, 
has been authorized.  To prove this, they submitted a report from Dr. Chen.  
(Defendants’ Exhibit A, pages 2-4)  Defense counsel further stated that the defendants’ 
plan was to seek more visits with Dr. Chen as the gatekeeper to continue to ensure that 
the chiropractic visits are reasonable and necessary, but also ensure that the 
chiropractic care is authorized faster.  I interpreted defense counsel’s statement as an 
assurance that they intend to modify their process to ensure that the chiropractic care is 
authorized faster in the future. 
 
Claimant presented Claimant’s Exhibit 1, which is a series of emails between 
counsel going back to February 2024, wherein claimant is asking for defendants to 
authorize chiropractic visits.  The inference from these emails is that claimant has 
repeatedly had challenges getting his chiropractic care authorized since even before the 
settlement.  Defendants submitted Defendants’ Exhibits A and C which show defense 
counsel’s specific responses to claimant’s counsel's requests. 
 
REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Iowa Code Section 85.27 (2013). 
 
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See 
Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  Determining what care is 
reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.  Id.  The employer’s obligation turns

on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.  Id.; Harned v. Farmland 
Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983).   
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995). 
An employer’s statutory right is to select the providers of care and the employer 
may consider cost and other pertinent factors when exercising its choice. Long, at 124. 
An employer (typically) is not a licensed health care provider and does not possess 
medical expertise. Accordingly, an employer does not have the right to control the 
methods the providers choose to evaluate, diagnose and treat the injured employee. An 
employer is not entitled to control a licensed health care provider’s exercise of 
professional judgment. Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 866389 (Declaratory 
Ruling, May 18, 1988). An employer’s failure to follow recommendations of an 
authorized physician in matters of treatment is commonly a failure to provide reasonable 
treatment. Boggs v. Cargill, Inc., File No. 1050396 (Alt. Care, January 31, 1994). 
The specific care claimant is seeking has already been authorized at the time of 
hearing.  (Def. Ex. A)  Often, this leads to a finding that the care offered has been 
reasonable.  Claimant’s argument is that the repeated delays are unreasonable, and, 
more specifically, the repeated delays are leading to untenable gaps in his medical 
treatment.  I am sympathetic to this theory; however, in this record, I find that the 
claimant has failed to meet his burden of proof.  It is evident there have been some 
delays caused by the defendants’ process for authorizing the chiropractic treatment; 
however, in light of the fact that the specific care has been authorized (i.e., more 
chiropractic visits), I must determine whether the defendants’ pattern of delays is 
pervasive and severe enough to find that the entire process is unreasonable.  In this 
record, I find claimant has failed to meet this burden.  The specific evidence I would 
need to see to make such a finding is the number and length of the delays and the 
impact these delays specifically have upon the claimant. 
While I have found that the claimant has failed to meet his burden at this 
particular hearing, if the delays continue, he may obviously refile.  Moreover, it is the 
expectation of this agency that the defendants shall carry through with their 
authorization of the additional chiropractic visits, as well as their assurance that they will 
modify their process for approving such visits in the future to ensure that these types of 
delays are minimized or eliminated. 
 
ORDER 
 
THEREFORE IT IS ORDERED: 
 
The claimant's petition for alternate medical care is DENIED.
