STATEMENT OF THE CASE   
 
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A. The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Mark Greeley. 
Claimant appeared telephonically and through his attorney, Christopher Spaulding. 
Defendants participated through their attorney, Bryan Brooks. Defendants filed an 
answer to the petition on January 21, 2025, admitting liability for the injury at issue. 
 
The alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on January 24, 2025. The 
proceedings were digitally recorded. That recording constitutes the official record of this 
proceeding. Pursuant to the Commissioner’s July 21, 2023 Order, the undersigned has 
been delegated authority to issue a final agency decision in this alternate medical care 
proceeding. Therefore, this ruling is designated final agency action and any appeal of 
the decision would be to the Iowa District Court pursuant to Iowa Code section 17A. 
The record consists of claimant’s exhibits 1 and 2 (attached to the petition), 
consisting of four pages. No witnesses were called, but both attorneys made statements 
for the record.  
Claimant also filed a Motion for Sanctions, which will be addressed in this 
decision. Defendants filed a response to the motion on January 23, 2025.

ISSUE 
The issue presented for resolution is whether the claimant is entitled to an order 
directing defendants’ to comply with the consent order issued on December 30, 2024, in 
file number 24700691.04. Claimant has also requested an order that he be allowed to 
direct his own medical care, and for sanctions pursuant to 876 IAC 4.36. 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned, having considered all of the evidence in the record, finds: 
The parties stipulated that claimant sustained an injury to his right arm on August 
22, 2024, arising out of and in the course of his employment. The parties agree that the 
authorized treating medical provider, Dr. Gibbons, ordered physical therapy in 
November of 2024. Claimant filed a petition for alternate medical care on December 17, 
2024, seeking an order requiring defendants to authorize and schedule the physical 
therapy. A hearing was held on December 30, 2024, before Deputy Workers’ 
Compensation Commissioner Benjamin Humphrey. That hearing resulted in a Consent 
Order, which was filed on December 30, 2024. The order states: 
1) On or before January 9, 2025, the defendants shall schedule a physical 
therapy appointment for Greeley at Synergy Physical Therapy and Sports 
Medicine. 
 
2) Failure to comply with this order may result in sanctions under Iowa 
Administrative Code rule 876—4.36. 
Greeley v. XPO Logistics, File No.24700691.04 (Alt. Care, 12/30/2024). 
 
As of today’s date, no physical therapy appointment has been scheduled for 
claimant.  
REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
 
Iowa Code section 85.27(4) provides, in relevant part: 
For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish reasonable 
services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has the right to 
choose the care. . . .  The treatment must be offered promptly and be 
reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the 
employee. If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the care 
offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such 
dissatisfaction to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the 
employer and the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited 
to treat the injury. If the employer and employee cannot agree on such 
alternate care, the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable 
proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and order other care.

Iowa Code § 85.27(4). 
Defendants’ “obligation under the statute is confined to reasonable care for the 
diagnosis and treatment of work-related injuries.” Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 
N.W.2d 122, 124 (Iowa 1995) (emphasis in original). In other words, the “obligation 
under the statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.” Id. 
Similarly, an application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained 
because claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving. Mere 
dissatisfaction with the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for 
alternate medical care. Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered 
promptly, was not reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly 
inconvenient for the claimant. See Iowa Code § 85.27(4). Thus, by challenging the 
employer’s choice of treatment and seeking alternate care, claimant assumes the 
burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable. See Iowa R. App. P 14(f)(5); 
Long, 528 N.W.2d at 124.   
Ultimately, determining whether care is reasonable under the statute is a 
question of fact.  Long, 528 N.W.2d at 123. 
In this case, claimant has met his burden to prove that the care offered by 
defendants is unreasonable. Defendants have not secured any physical therapy 
appointments for claimant despite being ordered to do so by this agency. Defendants 
are not offering prompt care that is reasonably suited to treat the injury. The evidence 
demonstrates that defendants are not meeting their obligation to provide reasonable 
care for the work-related injury. As such, claimant is entitled to alternate care. The 
petition is granted. 
Claimant also seeks sanctions under 876 IAC 4.36. That rule states, in relevant 
part:  
. . . any party to a contested case or an attorney representing such party 
shall fail to comply with these rules or any order of a deputy workers’ 
compensation commissioner or the workers’ compensation commissioner, 
the deputy workers’ compensation commissioner or workers’ 
compensation commissioner may impose sanctions which may include 
dismissing the action without prejudice, excluding or limiting evidence, 
assessing costs or expenses, and closing the record in whole or in part 
to further activity by the party. (Emphasis added) 
 
Defendants argue that efforts have been made to secure physical therapy for 
claimant. On December 12, 2024, the claims adjuster sent an email to Dr. Gibbons’ 
office indicating that 12 visits of physical therapy were authorized. (Defendants’ Exhibit 
A, p. 2) After the December 30, 2024 consent order was filed, the same claims adjuster 
sent an email to a national scheduling service, Homelink, asking for 12 physical therapy 
appointments to be scheduled. (Def. Ex. A, p. 1) That email was sent on December 31,

2024. 1 Defense counsel also represented his understanding was that the adjuster 
emailed Synergy Physical Therapy directly on January 23, 2025. 
Despite those efforts, physical therapy has not yet been scheduled. Deputy 
Humphrey’s order was for defendants to schedule physical therapy at Synergy Physical 
Therapy and Sports Medicine on or before January 9, 2025. An email sent to a 
nationwide scheduling center on New Year’s Eve, with no follow up until the day before 
the second hearing, is not reasonable compliance with the December 30, 2024 order. 
Claimant should not have to file multiple petitions for alternate care in order to receive 
treatment that was ordered two months ago by the authorized treating physician. As 
such, claimant’s motion for sanctions is granted. Claimant’s Exhibit 2 is an affidavit of 
attorney fees through January 13, 2025. Claimant is entitled to recover those fees, plus 
any additional fees incurred from January 13 through the date of hearing. 
Claimant also asked at hearing that defendants’ right to direct medical care be 
terminated. Given that defendants did make some effort to schedule the physical 
therapy, at this time, that request is denied. However defendants are cautioned that 
further noncompliance with agency orders may result in the termination of the ability to 
direct care. 
ORDER 
THEREFORE IT IS ORDERED: 
1. The claimant's petition for alternate medical care is granted. 
 
2. Within seven (7) days of the date of this ruling, defendants shall schedule 
physical therapy for claimant at Synergy Physical Therapy and Sports 
Medicine, or another comparable physical therapy provider. 
 
3. Claimant’s Motion for Sanctions is granted. Defendants shall pay 
claimant’s attorney’s costs and fees involved with preparing for and 
presenting this case, as represented in Exhibit 2, and including additional 
fees incurred from January 13, 2025 to the date of hearing. 
 
4. Failure to comply with this order may result in additional sanctions under 
Iowa Administrative Code rule 876—4.36, including but not limited to the 
termination of defendants’ ability to direct care for the work injury.
