Introduction 
 
This is a judicial review action of a worker’s compensation appeal that was heard by the 
Court on August 2, 2024. Petitioners, Hope Haven Inc. (“Hope Haven”) and First Dakota 
Indemnity Company (“First Dakota”), appeared through counsel, Caroline Westerhold. 
Respondent, Saydi Borges-Tun (“Borges-Tun”), appeared through counsel, Mary Hamilton. 
After hearing oral arguments of counsel and reviewing briefs submitted by the parties, the Court 
enters this ruling.  
Procedural History 
 
This case arises out of two petitions filed by Borges seeking workers’ compensation 
benefits against Hope Haven, employer, First Dakota, insurer, and Second Injury Fund of Iowa. 
Attachment to D0001, Ex. 2 (3/11/24). The claims relate to Borges-Tun’s wrist injuries that 
occurred on January 26, 2020, and February 8, 2021. Id. at 1. Borges-Tun settled the case against 
Second Injury Fund of Iowa on March 30, 2023. Id. An arbitration hearing against Hope Haven 
and First Dakota was held before Deputy Workers’ Compensation Commissioner Jennifer S. 
Gerrish-Lampe on April 4, 2023. Id.

freely, fill work shifts, and train and supervise co-workers. Id.

Tun felt pain in her right wrist. Id. Borges-Tun reported this injury as a work injury on January 
30, 2020, after speaking with her supervisor. Id. Extensive documentation of Borges-Tun’s

Standard of Review 
 
In a judicial review action of an administrative proceeding, the Court reviews the case 
under Iowa Code section 17A.19. The party asserting invalidity has the burden of demonstrating

5 
 
the required prejudice and invalidity of the agency action. Iowa Code § 17A.19(8)(a). The party 
may satisfy its burden in several ways, including proving the agency’s action was based upon a 
determination of fact that is not supported by substantial evidence in the record when the record 
is viewed as a whole. Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(f). 
 
The district court, exercising its power of judicial review, acts in an appellate capacity to 
correct errors of law on the part of the agency. Grundmeyer v. Weyerhaeuser Co., 649 N.W.2d 
744, 748 (Iowa 2002) (citing Heartland Express, Inc. v. Terry, 631 N.W.2d 260, 265 (Iowa 
2001)). Determining which evidence is stronger or weaker “is not an assessment for the district 
court or the court of appeals to make when it conducts a substantial evidence review of an 
agency decision.” Arndt v. City of Le Claire, 728 N.W.2d 389, 394 (Iowa 2007). The 
Commissioner’s role as trier of fact is “to determine the credibility of the witnesses, weigh the 
evidence, and decide the facts in issue.” Id. at 394-95. “The reviewing court only determines 
whether substantial evidence supports a finding ‘according to those witnesses whom the 
[commissioner] believed.’” Id. at 395. (citing Tim O’Neill Chevrolet, Inc. v. Forristall, 551 
N.W.2d 611, 614 (Iowa 1996)). 
Merits 
 
The award of medical benefits related to an ECU tear and carpal tunnel syndrome, award 
of permanent impairment, and benefit rate calculation for the January 26, 2020, date of accident 
were questions of fact for Deputy Commissioner Gerrish-Lampe and Commissioner Cortese II to 
resolve. The “Commissioner’s findings have the effect of a jury verdict.” Coffey v. Mid Seven 
Transp. Co., 831 N.W.2d 81, 89 (Iowa 2013). Under judicial review, fact determinations may be 
overturned if the agency’s discretionary decision “is not supported by substantial evidence in the 
record before the court when that record is viewed as a whole.” Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(f).

“‘Substantial evidence’ means the quantity and quality of evidence that would be deemed 
sufficient by a neutral, detached, and reasonable person, to establish the fact at issue when the 
consequences resulting from the establishment of that fact are understood to be serious and of 
great importance.” Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(f)(1).  
Further, “evidence is substantial when a reasonable mind could accept it as adequate to 
reach the same findings.” Organic Techs. Corp. v. State ex el. Iowa Dept. of Nat. Res., 609 
N.W.2d 809, 815 (Iowa 2000) (citing IBP, Inc. v. Al-Gharib, 604 N.W.2d 621, 632 (Iowa 2000)). 
The Court “appl[ies] agency findings broadly and liberally to uphold, rather than to defeat, an 
agency’s decision.” Id. To determine whether the agency’s decision is supported by substantial 
evidence, this Court looks to the record as a whole. Coffey, 831 N.W.2d at 89. 
 
In her Arbitration Decision, Deputy Commissioner Gerrish-Lampe weighed all the 
evidence provided by both parties, including expert testimony and extensive documentation of 
Borges-Tun’s medical treatments. Deputy Commissioner Gerrish-Lampe summarizes the parties’ 
positions by stating, “defendants lean on the opinions of Dr. Manon-Matos and Dr. Bruggeman.” 
D0001, Ex. 2 at 12. On the other hand, she notes, “claimant relies upon the opinions of Dr. 
Bansal.” Id. After assessing the credibility of the witnesses and weighing all the evidence 
presented in the record, Deputy Commissioner Gerrish-Lampe made the following findings of 
fact: 
Dr. Manon-Matos’ records present a somewhat disconcerting picture. The greatest 
error was identifying the claimant’s injury as left-sided in the operative report 
rather than right-sides, but while that might have been a mere scrivener’s error, 
there were a few other circumstances where it appeared claimant’s pain 
complaints may have been overlooked in the rush to return claimant to full duty, 
particularly after the second injury. 
Dr. Manon-Matos’ findings that claimant was at MMI and needed no additional 
care was in direct opposition to his partners’ treatment of claimant. Because of 
this, as well as the other errors in the report, the fact that most of the visits were

On appeal, Commissioner Cortese II “performed a de novo review of the evidentiary 
record and the detailed arguments of the parties” and “reach[ed] the same analysis, findings, and 
conclusions as those reached by the deputy commissioner.” Attachment to D0001, Ex. 1 at 2 
(3/11/24).

responsibility for determining credibility of the witnesses, and we are bound by the 
commissioner’s findings if supported by substantial evidence.” Sherman v. Pella Corp., 576

N.W.2d 312, 320 (Iowa 1998). The Commissioner’s credibility determination is based “on the 
accuracy of the facts relied upon by the expert and other surrounding circumstances.” Id. at 321. 
Therefore, this analysis depends upon whether the evidence supports the findings made by the 
Commissioner, rather than whether the evidence supports a different finding. Id. at 320. 
Deputy Commissioner Gerrish-Lampe found Dr. Bansal’s testimony to be more credible 
than Dr. Manon-Matos’ and Dr. Bruggeman’s opinions. On appeal, Commissioner Cortese II 
affirmed the findings of fact and conclusions of law explained in the Deputy Commissioner’s 
arbitration decision. Additionally, both the Deputy Commissioner and Commissioner found 
substantial evidence warranting Hope Haven and First Dakota to pay Borges-Tun 5 weeks of 
permanent partial disability benefits at the weekly rate of $571.59 commencing on May 27, 
2021, for the February 8, 2021, injury. Both the Deputy Commissioner and Commissioner also 
found substantial evidence to award past and future medical expenses for both injuries on 
January 26, 2020, and February 8, 2021. There is substantial evidence in Dr. Bansal’s expert 
opinion to support these findings. 
Both the Deputy Commissioner and Commissioner found there was substantial evidence 
to award Borges-Tun with 15 weeks of permanent partial disability benefits at the weekly benefit 
rate of $575.50 commencing on November 19, 2020 for the January 26, 2020, injury. However, 
this Court disagrees with the weekly benefit rate calculation. Both parties agree that when 
Borges-Tun was paid overtime, the rate should have been calculated at the straight time rate 
rather than the higher overtime rate that was actually paid. See Iowa Code § 85.36(6). The Court 
agrees with the parties that the agency erred in this regard. The dispute between the parties 
concerns what weeks should be used for the benefit calculation. The Deputy Commissioner gave 
a well-reasoned explanation for why the week of [dates] should be excluded. See id. (“A week

Conclusion and Disposition 
IT IS THE ORDER OF THE COURT that the Petition for Judicial Review is 
DENIED. Petitioner ordered to pay Borges-Tun with 15 weeks of permanent partial disability 
benefits at the modified weekly benefit rate of $540.77 commencing on November 19, 2020, for 
the January 26, 2020 injury.
