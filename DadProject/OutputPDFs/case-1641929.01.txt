STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Harvey Mulder, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers' 
compensation benefits from Joel Schuiteman (Schuiteman), employer, and Grinnell 
Mutual Reinsurance Company, insurer, both as defendants.  This matter was heard on 
September 26, 2023, with a final submission date of December 1, 2023.   
The record in this case consists of Joint Exhibits 1-18, Claimant’s Exhibits 1-3, 
Defendants’ Exhibits A-F, and the testimony of claimant and Sandy Griffioen. 
STIPULATIONS 
 
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised 
or discussed in this decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The first issue to be determined is the extent of claimant’s entitlement to 
permanent partial disability benefits. 
 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3). 
 
Since claimant has an impairment to the body as a whole, an industrial disability 
has been sustained. Industrial disability was defined in Diederich v. Tri-City Ry. Co. of 
Iowa, 219 Iowa 587, 258 N.W. 899 (1935) as follows: "It is therefore plain that the 
Legislature intended the term 'disability' to mean 'industrial disability' or loss of earning 
capacity and not a mere 'functional disability' to be computed in terms of percentages of 
the total physical and mental ability of a normal man." 
 
Functional impairment is an element to be considered in determining industrial 
disability which is the reduction of earning capacity, but consideration must also be 
given to the injured employee's age, education, qualifications, experience, motivation, 
loss of earnings, severity and situs of the injury, work restrictions, inability to engage in 
employment for which the employee is fitted and the employer's offer of work or failure 
to so offer. McSpadden v. Big Ben Coal Co., 288 N.W.2d 181 (Iowa 1980); Olson v. 
Goodyear Service Stores, 255 Iowa 1112, 125 N.W.2d 251 (1963); Barton v. Nevada 
Poultry Co., 253 Iowa 285, 110 N.W.2d 660 (1961). 
 
Compensation for permanent partial disability shall begin at the termination of the 
healing period. Compensation shall be paid in relation to 500 weeks as the disability 
bears to the body as a whole. Section 85.34.

Claimant was injured at work on December 20, 2017, when he was hit by a 
charging steer.  Claimant’s head hit the concrete, and he was briefly unconscious.  
Claimant vomited and was confused when he was taken by the ambulance to the 
hospital.  
 
Claimant was hospitalized for approximately two days.  In the hospital he was 
assessed as having a multifocal hemorrhagic contusion of the bilateral frontal lobes and 
a subdural hematoma.  Claimant was transferred to inpatient rehabilitation for 
approximately two weeks.  Claimant credibly testified his first memory after the accident 
occurred five days after he was knocked unconscious.  While he was in rehabilitation, 
he underwent therapies for helping his balance, motor skills, speech, and cognitive 
difficulties.  (JE 7, pp. 165-168) 
 
After rehabilitation, claimant had approximately 80 therapy sessions to help him 
with speech, motor skills and cognitive difficulties.  Claimant credibly testified therapists 
recommended he continue therapy, but the insurance company terminated care.  
 
The unrebutted testimony of claimant and his sister is that approximately six 
years after the date of injury claimant still has difficulties with memory, cognitive function 
and balance.  Ms. Griffioen’s unrebutted testimony is that six years after the accident, 
she still needs to write information down for claimant regarding taking medication and 
payment of bills.   
 
Claimant credibly testified that six years after the accident, he still has severe 
headaches and needs to take medication for those headaches.  The record indicates 
that claimant uses a memory book and sticky pads to help him remember to complete 
activities.  (Ex. 1, pp. 2-3) 
 
The record indicates that before the accident claimant worked between 50-75 
hours per week.  Claimant’s treating physician, Dr. Raut, limited claimant to working as 
tolerated.  (JE 12, p. 440)  Claimant credibly testified that he believes he can only work 
4 hours a day.  (Tr., p. 37)  In brief, prior to the accident, claimant worked up to 75 hours 
a week.  After the accident, claimant can now work approximately 20 hours a week.   
 
At the time of hearing, claimant was on Social Security Disability due to his work 
injury of December 2017.   
 
Two experts have opined regarding the extent of claimant’s condition.  Dr. 
Ripperda evaluated claimant once for an IME in May 2021.  Dr. Ripperda found that 
claimant had a 14 percent permanent impairment for his closed head injury.  He based 
his opinion, in part, on Table 13-5 and 13-6 of the Guides, and the fact that claimant had 
documented instances of loss of memory and balance.  (Ex. 1, p.13)   
 
Dr. Martin evaluated claimant once for an IME in December 2018.  Dr. Martin 
found claimant had a 5 percent permanent impairment to the body as a whole.  Dr.

Martin did not believe claimant’s continued headaches were due to his injury, but were 
caused by other causes or factors.  Dr. Martin suggested claimant’s sleep apnea was a 
potential cause of headaches.  (Ex. D, p. 35; Ex. F, depo pp. 9, 11) 
 
Dr. Martin also believed claimant’s symptoms were due or aggravated, in part, to 
his failure to return to work.  He opined that claimant’s desire to not return to work was a 
causative factor to claimant’s symptoms.  (Ex. D, p. 36) 
 
Dr. Martin’s opinions regarding permanent impairment and the cause of 
claimant’s ongoing symptoms are problematic for several reasons.  As noted, Dr. Martin 
opined claimant’s headaches were potentially caused by claimant’s sleep apnea.  (Ex. 
D, p. 35; Ex. F, depo pp. 9, 11)  His opinion is based solely on speculation.  There is no 
evidence in the record to support Dr. Martin’s “theory” claimant’s headaches may be 
due to sleep apnea.  Treating physicians have routinely opined that claimant’s 
headaches are the result of his traumatic brain injury.  (JE 6, pp. 63, 67, 71, 76, 79, 84, 
93, 96, 100, 104, 107; JE 12, pp. 356, 362, 412, 423-424, 430, 433, 437, 440; JE 13, p. 
444)  Dr. Martin also suggested claimant’s symptoms were due or aggravated, in part, 
because claimant had “elected not to return to work.”  (Ex. D, p. 36)  The record 
indicates that prior to his accident, claimant worked between 50-75 hours a week.  
Claimant credibly testified he enjoyed his job.  (Tr., p. 20)  Immediately after his injury, 
claimant’s goal was to return to work.  (Tr., p. 25; JE 10, p. 185)  The record indicates 
that claimant was a hard-working person and enjoyed his work.  The record also 
indicates that claimant has tried to return to work, but has difficulty working full-time due 
to his limitations with headaches and memory issues.  (Tr., pp. 28, 40, 68; JE 12, p. 
438)  Dr. Martin’s suggestion that claimant has “elected” to not return to work is contrary 
to the record and without merit. 
 
Dr. Martin’s opinions that claimant’s headaches may be due to sleep apnea are 
speculative and not supported by the record.  Dr. Martin, when rating claimant, failed to 
take into account claimant’s issues with short-term memory loss.  Dr. Martin’s opinions 
that claimant has “elected” to not return to work are inaccurate and without merit.  Given 
this record, it is found that the opinions of Dr. Martin regarding claimant’s impairment, 
causation of symptoms, and restrictions are found not credible. 
 
Defendants suggest that claimant’s symptoms may be due, in part, to his 2017 
viral infection (Defendants’ Post-Hearing Brief, page 25)  There is no evidence in the 
record to support this position.  Even defendants’ own IME expert, Dr. Martin, indicated 
that claimant’s current symptoms are not due to the 2017 viral infection.  (Ex. D, p. 31) 
 
Claimant had a traumatic brain injury.  He was a patient in the hospital for 
approximately 3 weeks following his injury.  Claimant had approximately 80 therapy 
sessions to help him with balance, speech and cognitive issues following his injury. Dr. 
Ripperda found that claimant had a 14 percent permanent impairment due to his 
traumatic brain injury.  Claimant is on Social Security Disability because of his work-
related traumatic brain injury.  The record indicates claimant has continued headaches 
and cognitive problems, approximately 6 years after the date of injury, that limit his

ability to return to full-time work.  Claimant worked 50-75 hours a week prior to his 
injury.  Claimant now works approximately 4 hours a day subsequent to the injury.  
Given this record as detailed above, it is found that claimant has an 80 percent 
industrial disability.   
 
ORDER 
 
THEREFORE, IT IS ORDERED: 
 
That defendants shall pay claimant 400 weeks of permanent partial disability 
benefits at the rate of five hundred eighty and 35/100 dollars ($580.35) commencing on 
December 20, 2018. 
 
That defendants shall pay accrued weekly benefits in a lump sum together with 
interest at an annual rate equal to the one-year treasury constant maturity published by 
the federal reserve in the most recent H15 report settled as of the date of injury, plus 
two percent.  
 
That defendants shall be given credit for benefits previously paid. 
 
That defendants shall pay for claimant’s ongoing medical care and treatment 
related to his closed head injury. 
 
That defendants shall pay costs. 
 
That defendants shall file subsequent reports of injury as required by Rule 876 
IAC 3.1(2).
