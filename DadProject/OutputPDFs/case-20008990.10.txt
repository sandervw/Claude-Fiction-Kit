This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A. The 
expedited procedures of rule 876 IAC 4.48, the “alternate medical care” rule, are 
invoked by claimant, Joshua Hanawalt.  
This alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on October 29, 2024. The 
proceedings were recorded digitally and constitute the official record of the hearing. By 
an order filed by the Workers’ Compensation Commissioner, this decision is designated 
final agency action. Any appeal would be by petition for judicial review under Iowa Code 
section 17A.15(1).  
The record in this case consists of Claimant’s Exhibit 1. 
ISSUE 
The issue presented for resolution in this case is whether claimant is entitled to 
alternate medical care consisting of an order from this agency for defendants to approve 
and pay for treatment within seven days and sanctions. 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
Defendants accept liability for a work-related injury to claimant’s low back on 
June 25, 2020.

CONCLUSION OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3).  
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance and hospital services

and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Section 85.27.  Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening October 16, 1975).       
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  Long v. 
Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122, 123 (Iowa 1995).  Determining what care is 
reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.  Id.  The employer’s obligation turns 
on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.  Id. at 124; Harned v. Farmland 
Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98, 101 (Iowa 1983).      
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).     
In the June of 2022 arbitration decision, defendants were ordered to provide 
medical care for claimant.  Since that time, this agency has heard seven different 
petitions for alternate medical care because defendants have delayed care or ignored 
the recommendations of the authorized provider.  As a result of this record, in the April 
19, 2024, alternate medical care decision, claimant was allowed to direct his own 
medical care at the defendants’ expense. 
Despite this, the payment of outstanding medical bills, care that was previously 
ordered by this agency, continues to be delayed. 
I find defendants’ failure to pay for and authorize care recommended by the 
authorized provider and ordered by this agency is unreasonable.  As noted in the April 
of 2024 alternate medical care decision, it is also found unreasonable claimant should 
have to file seven different petitions for alternate medical care for one date of injury that 
should have been resolved by the June of 2022 arbitration decision. 
Defendants’ continued delay of authorization and payment of treatments ordered 
by this agency borders on bad faith.  It is also a waste of agency resources.  
Defendants are ordered to authorize, provide and pay for water therapy, cryotherapy, 
massage, a gym membership, acupuncture, and decompression treatment within seven 
business days of the date of this decision. 
Claimant has also requested sanctions in this case given the repeated failure to 
comply with recommendations of care by the authorized provider and the repeated 
failure to comply with agency orders.

Rule 876 IAC 4.36 states in relevant part:  
[A]ny party to a contested case or an attorney representing such party 
shall fail to comply with these rules or any order of a deputy workers’ 
compensation commissioner or the workers’ compensation commissioner, 
the deputy workers’ compensation commissioner or workers’ 
compensation commissioner may impose sanctions which may include 
dismissing the action without prejudice, excluding or limiting evidence, 
assessing costs or expenses, and closing the record in whole or in part 
to further activity by the party.  (Emphasis added) 
Given the repeated noncompliance with agency orders in these alternate medical 
care petitions, defendants shall pay claimant’s attorney’s costs and fees involved with 
preparing for and presenting this case.  Defendants may be required to pay claimant’s 
attorney’s fees in the future if future noncompliance with agency orders occurs. 
ORDER 
Claimant’s petition for alternate medical care is granted.  Defendants are ordered 
to authorize, provide and pay for water therapy, cryotherapy, massage, a gym 
membership, acupuncture, and decompression treatment within seven business days of 
the date of this decision. 
Defendants shall also pay claimant’s attorney’s costs and fees involved with 
preparing for and presenting this case
