Claimant, Martin Valtierra, appeals from an August 28, 2025 arbitration decision.  
Defendant, Second Injury Fund of Iowa, responds to the appeal and urges that the 
arbitration decision be fully affirmed.  The presiding deputy commissioner held an 
arbitration hearing in this case on May 15, 2025, and the case was fully submitted 
before the deputy on June 12, 2025. 
This case involves a claim for Second Injury Fund benefits.  The parties 
stipulated that claimant sustained a second qualifying injury as a result of work activities 
on August 1, 2023.  The parties further stipulate that the August 1, 2023 injury caused 
permanent disability.  The dispute in this case arises out of whether claimant proved a 
qualifying first injury and, if so, the extent of permanent disability resulting from that first 
qualifying injury. 
In the arbitration decision, the deputy commissioner found that claimant failed to 
prove he sustained a permanent, qualifying first injury to his left eye.  Therefore, the 
deputy commissioner denied the claim for Second Injury Fund benefits. 
Claimant contends that he introduced unrebutted evidence that he sustained a 
three percent permanent impairment of the whole person as a result of his left eye 
injury.  Claimant contends that he should be entitled to a significant industrial disability 
award against the Second Injury Fund.  The Second Injury Fund asserts that claimant 
did not prove permanent impairment or a permanent loss of use of the left eye.  
Therefore, the Fund urges the arbitration decision be affirmed in its entirety. 
Pursuant to Iowa Code section 10A.321 and section 17A.15(3), I performed a de 
novo review of the evidentiary record and the detailed arguments of the parties.  Those 
portions of the proposed arbitration decision pertaining to issues not raised on appeal 
are adopted as part of this appeal decision.  With respect to the issues properly raised 
on appeal, I affirm the arbitration decision with the following analysis:

Iowa Code section 85.64 governs Second Injury Fund liability.  Before liability of 
the Fund is triggered, three requirements must be met.  First, the employee must have 
lost or lost the use of a hand, arm, foot, leg, or eye.  Second, the employee must sustain 
a loss or loss of use of another specified member or organ through a compensable 
injury.  Third, permanent disability must exist as to both the initial injury and the second 
injury.   
The Second Injury Fund Act exists to encourage the hiring of handicapped 
persons by making a current employer responsible only for the amount of disability 
related to an injury occurring while that employer employed the handicapped individual 
as if the individual had had no preexisting disability.  See Anderson v. Second Injury 
Fund, 262 N.W.2d 789, 792 (Iowa 1978); 15 Iowa Practice, Workers’ Compensation, 
Lawyer, Section 17:1, p. 211 (2014-2015). 
The Fund is responsible for the industrial disability present after the second injury 
that exceeds the disability attributable to the first and second injuries.  Section 85.64.  
Second Injury Fund of Iowa v. Braden, 459 N.W.2d 467, 471 (Iowa 1990); Second Injury 
Fund v. Neelans, 436 N.W.2d 355, 358 (Iowa 1989); Second Injury Fund v. Mich Coal 
Co., 274 N.W.2d 300, 304 (Iowa 1979). 
To establish a qualifying injury for purposes of a Second Injury Fund claim, 
claimant must establish that he sustained a permanent loss of use of his eye.  It is not 
enough that the injury merely affected claimant’s eye without causing a permanent loss 
of use of the eye.  Second Injury Fund of Iowa v. Strable, 14 N.W.3d 742, 749 (Iowa 
2024); Strumpff v. Second Injury Fund of Iowa, 543 N.W.2d 904, 907 (Iowa 1996) (“We 
rejected the suggestion that a shoulder injury that affects the arm is sufficient to make 
the Fund liable.”).  In other words, an injury to an unenumerated member under the 
Second Injury Fund statute that merely affects the eye but does not cause a permanent 
loss of use of the eye does not establish a qualifying first injury for purposes of a 
Second Injury Fund claim.  Strable, 14 N.W.2d at 749; Blake v. Second Injury Fund of 
Iowa, 967 N.W.2d 221 (Iowa Ct. App. 2021) (table of unpublished decisions) (2021 WL 
4304274) (“disability to her body as a whole that also affects an eye does not constitute 
a first qualifying injury.  Similarly, our supreme court has expressly rejected the 
argument that an unscheduled injury that affects an enumerated member is enough to 
trigger the Fund’s liability.”) 
The initial question that must be determined in this appeal is whether claimant 
sustained a permanent loss of use of the left eye.  If claimant failed to prove a 
permanent loss of use of the left eye prior to his stipulated August 1, 2023 leg injury, he 
failed to prove a qualifying first injury and the arbitration decision should be affirmed.  
On the other hand, if claimant proved a permanent loss of use of the left eye prior to his 
August 1, 2023 work injury, he is entitled to an award in some amount from the Second 
Injury Fund of Iowa. 
The situs of claimant’s injury was a closed fracture of the orbital wall surrounding 
claimant’s left eye.  That fracture appears to have healed without incident.  The question

now presented is whether claimant sustained a loss of use of the left eye as a result of 
that left orbital fracture. 
Claimant’s ophthalmologist, James Wymore, M.D., expressed concerns that the 
orbital fracture could result in the ocular muscles getting entrapped.  However, on Dr. 
Wymore’s December 21, 2022 evaluation, he opined that the muscles appeared to be 
working properly.  Again, on January 9, 2023, Dr. Wymore documented full range of 
motion of claimant’s eyes and noted his vision was good.  Dr. Wymore opined that 
claimant could return to work without restrictions related to his left eye or left orbital 
fracture.  Claimant successfully returned to work following the orbital fracture and 
worked until his subsequent leg injury in August 2023.   
Connor S. Davenport, M.D., an ophthalmologist in Dr. Wymore’s office, evaluated 
claimant on April 8, 2024, due to complaints of decreased vision in both eyes.  Dr. 
Davenport noted claimant’s vision problems were worse in his right eye than his left.  
Dr. Davenport diagnosed claimant with bilateral cataracts and recommended surgical 
intervention.  Dr. Davenport was aware of claimant’s prior left orbital fracture.  As part of 
his evaluation, Dr. Davenport tested claimant’s extraocular motion (EOM) and 
determined claimant had full eye range of motion in both eyes as of April 8, 2024.  He 
documented normal adnexa (surrounding structures such as tear ducts, orbital tissues, 
eyelids, conjunctiva, and extraocular muscles) in claimant’s left eye.  (Joint Ex. 3, pp. 
49-54) 
Two other physicians have offered opinions pertaining to Mr. Valtierra’s alleged 
left eye injury.  Sunil Bansal, M.D., evaluated claimant on March 21, 2025.  He opined 
that claimant qualifies for a permanent impairment rating related to the left orbital 
fracture.  Dr. Bansal noted that claimant reported he “continues to have headaches and 
visual disturbances, particularly when looking to the left.”  (Claimant’s Ex. 1, p. 13)   
Dr. Bansal explained that claimant “demonstrates measurable functional deficits 
in extraocular movements, particularly limited movement on left lateral gaze with 
associated diplopia.”  (Claimant’s Ex. 1, p. 17)  He concludes this is consistent with an 
extraocular muscle entrapment of the inferior rectus.  Dr. Bansal opines that claimant 
has “permanent damage to the visual pathway secondary to the fracture.”  (Claimant’s 
Ex. 1, p. 17)   
Dr. Bansal also opines that claimant has photophobia as a permanent sensory 
disturbance as a result of the fracture and decreased sensation in the left infraorbital 
region as a result of the fall and trauma to his face.  Ultimately, Dr. Bansal assigns a 
three percent permanent functional impairment for claimant’s condition pursuant to the 
AMA Guides to the Evaluation of Permanent Impairment, Fifth Edition.  However, Dr. 
Bansal opines that claimant requires no permanent work restrictions as a result of the 
orbital fracture or resulting limitations.  (Claimant’s Exhibit 1) 
Dr. Bansal’s findings and opinions are contradicted somewhat by the evaluations 
performed by the specialist, Dr. Wymore.  Dr. Wymore found that claimant had full 
range of motion in his eyes.  He also indicated that claimant’s vision looked good at his

evaluation on January 9, 2023.  Dr. Wymore indicated that claimant also reported his 
visual acuity (VA) was stable at this appointment.  (Joint Ex. 3, p. 44) 
Dr. Davenport’s finding that claimant had full extraocular motion in his left eye in 
April 2024 also contradicts or at least challenges the findings offered by Dr. Bansal.  At 
the very least, claimant had full ocular motion in his left eye, pursuant to the findings of 
two specialists, by April 2024.  If claimant had loss of range of motion in his left eye by 
the time of Dr. Bansal’s evaluation in March 2025, there is no explanation why that 
developed between March 2024 and March 2025.  This lack of an explanation why 
claimant’s range of motion changed after the evaluations of the ophthalmologists 
damages the credibility of Dr. Bansal’s opinions on this issue.  
Joseph Chen, M.D., performed a records review at the request of the employer.  
He opined:  
Mr. Valtierra-Quintana’s ongoing symptoms of headaches and visual 
disturbances as noted by Dr. Bansal are no longer related to his work 
injury of December 20, 2022, as more than 2 years have elapsed for full 
recovery of any bony or soft-tissue injuries that occurred.  Headache and 
visual symptoms are common complaints in the general population.  
These records indicate that Mr. Valtierra-Quintana has had a history of 
cataracts due to his long-time use of prednisone to treat his psoriasis 
which can also cause visual disturbances and associated headaches.  
(Claimant’s Ex. 7, p. 39) 
Nevertheless, Dr. Chen concurred with the three percent permanent functional 
impairment offered by Dr. Bansal.  He also concurred that claimant does not require any 
permanent work restrictions as a result of the orbital fracture.  (Claimant’s Ex. 7)  Dr. 
Chen’s opinions can be critiqued given that he did not personally evaluate claimant and 
performed only a record review. 
Dr. Bansal reviewed the medical records documenting claimant’s cataracts after 
the work injury.  That medical record notes the cataracts were bilateral and were age-
related.  Dr. Bansal provides no analysis of the effects of the cataracts on claimant’s 
visual acuity, or the potential for headaches or visual disturbances related to cataracts 
or cataract surgery.  Dr. Bansal’s opinions can be critiqued given this lack of any 
analysis of the effects of claimant’s cataracts.  Similarly, Dr. Bansal makes no mention 
of claimant’s use of prednisone, as discussed by Dr. Chen, and its potential long-term 
effects on claimant’s vision and symptoms. 
Ultimately, I find the opinions of the treating ophthalmologists, Dr. Wymore and 
Dr. Davenport, are the most credible and convincing.  Drs. Wymore and Davenport 
were treating claimant as a patient and did not offer opinions specifically for this 
litigation.  Both found full range of motion in claimant’s left eye through March 2024 and 
Dr. Wymore found that claimant’s visual acuity was stable in January 2023.  I accept 
those findings.  I also find that, if claimant’s left eye symptoms and range of motion

changed between the evaluations of Dr. Davenport and Dr. Bansal, that the changes 
occurred after claimant’s second qualifying injury and cannot serve as the basis for a 
first qualifying injury. 
I find the opinions of Dr. Chen to be more convincing on the issue of claimant’s 
current headaches and visual disturbances.  Dr. Chen considered other potential 
causes of those issues and provided an explanation of why those may be occurring at 
the present time.  Dr. Chen’s analysis is more thorough and convincing than Dr. 
Bansal’s on these issues.  I reject Dr. Bansal’s opinions for these reasons and those 
outlined by the deputy commissioner in the arbitration decision.  Therefore, I find that 
claimant failed to prove by a preponderance of the evidence that his current left eye 
symptoms or conditions are related to the December 2022 fall at work that resulted in 
an orbital fracture.  Those conditions may be related to age-related factors, medications 
used by claimant, due to cataracts, or some combination of factors.  However, those 
conditions developed or caused visual issues after claimant’s August 2023 work injury. 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence. Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e).  
I conclude claimant failed to prove he sustained a permanent loss of use of his left eye 
prior to his August 1, 2023 left leg injury at work.  Therefore, I conclude claimant failed 
to prove a qualifying first injury for purposes of his Second Injury Fund claim.  I affirm 
the arbitration decision. 
ORDER 
IT IS THEREFORE ORDERED: 
The August 28, 2025 arbitration decision is affirmed with the above additional 
analysis.
