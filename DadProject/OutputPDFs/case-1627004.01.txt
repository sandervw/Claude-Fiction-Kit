STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Cheryl Duffey, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from Hy-Vee, Inc. (Hy-Vee), employer, EMC Insurance 
Companies, insurer, and the Second Injury Fund of Iowa (Fund), all as defendants.  
This matter was heard on December 3, 2024, with a final submission date of January 7, 
2025.  
The record in this matter consists of Joint Exhibits 1-8, Claimant’s Exhibits 1-4, 
Defendants Hy-Vee and insurer (Defendants) Exhibits A-E, Fund Exhibits AA-DD, and 
the testimony of claimant and Amanda Woodard. 
STIPULATIONS 
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised 
or discussed in this decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The first issue to be determined is the extent of claimant’s entitlement to 
permanent partial disability benefits. 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e).

Page 6 
 
Under the Iowa Workers' Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is 
compensated either for a loss or loss of use of a scheduled member under Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(a)-(u) or as an unscheduled injury pursuant to the provisions of section 
85.34(2)(v). The extent of scheduled member disability benefits to which an injured 
worker is entitled is determined by using the functional method. Functional disability is 
"limited to the loss of the physiological capacity of the body or body part.” Mortimer v. 
Fruehauf Corp., 502 N.W.2d 12, 15 (Iowa 1993); Sherman v. Pella Corp., 576 N.W.2d 
312, 320 (Iowa 1998).   
Iowa Code section 85.34(x) states:  
x. In all cases of permanent partial disability described in 
paragraphs “a” through “u”, or paragraph “v” when determining functional 
disability and not loss of earning capacity, the extent of loss or percentage 
of permanent impairment shall be determined solely by utilizing the guides 
to the evaluation of permanent impairment, published by the American 
medical association, as adopted by the workers' compensation 
commissioner by rule pursuant to chapter 17A. Lay testimony or agency 
expertise shall not be utilized in determining loss or percentage of 
permanent impairment pursuant to paragraphs “a” through “u”, or 
paragraph “v” when determining functional disability and not loss of 
earning capacity.  
Iowa Code section 85.34 (x).  
This agency has adopted the Guides to the Evaluation of Permanent Impairment, 
Fifth Edition, published by the American Medical Association for determining the extent 
of loss or percentage of impairment for permanent partial disabilities.  See 876 IAC 2.4.  
Claimant had a right carpal tunnel release in July of 2016.  (JE 4, p. 35)  She had 
a right cubital tunnel release, a right distal ulnar tunnel release, and a right revision 
carpal tunnel release in August of 2017.  (JE 6)  In November 2020, claimant had a third 
surgery to her right upper extremity consisting of an ulnar nerve decompression with 
transposition and a second revision of the carpal tunnel release.  (JE 8, pp. 128-130) 
Dr. Buckwalter was the last treating physician to give claimant a permanent 
impairment rating.  On June 22, 2021, Dr. Buckwalter found claimant at MMI.  He found 
claimant had a 13 percent permanent impairment to the right upper extremity.  (JE 8, p. 
152)

with claimant’s history and medical presentation.  Based on this, it is found that Dr. 
Buckwalter’s findings regarding permanent impairment to claimant’s right upper

The next issue to be determined is whether claimant is entitled to Fund benefits.  
Iowa Code section 85.64 governs Second Injury Fund liability.  Before liability of 
the Fund is triggered, three requirements must be met.  First, the employee must have 
lost or lost the use of a hand, arm, foot, leg, or eye.  Second, the employee must sustain 
a loss or loss of use of another specified member or organ through a compensable 
injury.  Third, permanent disability must exist as to both the initial injury and the second 
injury.   
The Second Injury Fund Act exists to encourage the hiring of handicapped 
persons by making a current employer responsible only for the amount of disability 
related to an injury occurring while that employer employed the handicapped individual 
as if the individual had had no preexisting disability.  See Anderson v. Second Injury 
Fund, 262 N.W.2d 789, 792 (Iowa 1978); 15 Iowa Practice, Workers’ Compensation, 
Lawyer, Section 17:1, p. 211 (2014-2015). 
The Fund is responsible for the industrial disability present after the second injury 
that exceeds the disability attributable to the first and second injuries.  Section 85.64.  
Second Injury Fund of Iowa v. Braden, 459 N.W.2d 467, 471 (Iowa 1990); Second Injury 
Fund v. Neelans, 436 N.W.2d 355, 358 (Iowa 1989); Second Injury Fund v. Mich Coal 
Co., 274 N.W.2d 300, 304 (Iowa 1979). 
The record indicates claimant had a left carpal tunnel release in April 2002.  
Claimant credibly testified that surgery was not beneficial.  She credibly testified she 
continues to have symptoms in her left upper extremity due to the carpal tunnel and 
subsequent surgery.  
As detailed above, claimant underwent three surgeries on the right upper 
extremity in 2016, 2017 and 2020.  There is no dispute claimant has a permanent 
impairment regarding her right upper extremity.  The Fund disputes claimant has any 
permanent impairment from her 2002 left carpal tunnel release.   
Dr. Bansal evaluated claimant once for an IME.  He found that claimant had a 5 
percent permanent impairment to the left upper extremity based upon Tables 16-10 and 
16-15 of the Guides.  (Ex. 2, p. 35)

Dr. Chen did a records review.  He did not evaluate claimant.  Dr. Chen opined 
that claimant did not have any impairment to the left upper extremity due to the 2002 
carpal tunnel injury and subsequent surgery.  (Ex. BB, p. 12)  Dr. Chen’s opinion 
regarding permanent impairment are problematic for several reasons.   
First, as indicated, Dr. Chen did not evaluate claimant, but only reviewed her 
medical records.   
Second, Dr. Chen’s notes indicate that he believed claimant did not have any 
permanent impairment on the left because “Ms. Duffey was seen multiple times by Dr. 
Donna Bahls for repeat nerve conduction studies of the right upper extremity.  These 
records do not indicate that Dr. Bahls found Ms. Duffey had any abnormalities with her 
left median nerve.”  (Ex. BB, p. 12) 
The testing records for Dr. Bahls are not in evidence in the record.  The EMGs 
and nerve conduction testing from Dr. Bahls are referenced in the record.  (Ex. 2, pp. 
14-15)  While it is true Dr. Bahls did find abnormalities in claimant’s right upper 
extremity, there is no evidence that Dr. Bahls tested claimant’s left upper extremity.  
(Ex. 2, pp. 14-15)   
Finally, Dr. Chen opines claimant’s neuropathic pain in the left upper extremity is 
actually not caused by her carpal tunnel syndrome, but by diabetes and vitamin 
deficiencies.  (Ex. BB, p. 12)  This opinion is purely speculation as there is no evidence 
in the record to support the position that claimant’s left upper extremity symptoms are 
caused by diabetes and vitamin deficiencies.  Based on this record, it is found the 
opinions of Dr. Chen regarding claimant’s permanent impairment to the left upper 
extremity are not convincing.   
Claimant’s credible testimony is that she is still symptomatic in her left upper 
extremity from the 2002 carpal tunnel surgery.  Dr. Bansal opined that claimant has a 
permanent impairment to the left upper extremity. The opinions of Dr. Chen regarding 
permanent impairment are found not convincing.  Given this record, claimant has 
carried her burden of proof that she has a qualifying first and second injury for the 
purposes of Fund benefits. 
The next issue to be determined is the extent of claimant’s entitlement to Fund 
benefits. 
Since claimant has an impairment to the body as a whole, an industrial disability 
has been sustained.  Industrial disability was defined in Diederich v. Tri-City Ry. Co. of 
Iowa, 219 Iowa 587, 591, 258 N.W. 899, 902 (1935) as follows:  "It is therefore plain that 
the Legislature intended the term 'disability' to mean 'industrial disability' or loss of 
earning capacity and not a mere 'functional disability' to be computed in terms of 
percentages of the total physical and mental ability of a normal man." 
Functional impairment is an element to be considered in determining industrial 
disability which is the reduction of earning capacity, but consideration must also be

given to the injured employee's age, education, qualifications, experience, motivation, 
loss of earnings, severity and situs of the injury, work restrictions, inability to engage in 
employment for which the employee is fitted and the employer's offer of work or failure 
to so offer.  McSpadden v. Big Ben Coal Co., 288 N.W.2d 181, 192 (Iowa 1980); Olson 
v. Goodyear Service Stores, 255 Iowa 1112, 1120, 125 N.W.2d 251, 256 (1963); Barton 
v. Nevada Poultry Co., 253 Iowa 285, 110 N.W.2d 660, 663 (1961). 
Compensation for permanent partial disability shall begin when it is medically 
indicated that maximum medical improvement from the injury has been reached and 
that the extent of loss or percentage of permanent impairment can be determined by the 
use of the guides to the evaluation of permanent impairment, published by the American 
Medical Association, as adopted by the Commissioner. Iowa Code section 85.34(2)   
Claimant was 66 years old at the time of hearing.  Claimant went up to the 10th 
grade.  She does not have a GED.  Claimant has worked as a housekeeper and in 
restaurants.  Claimant began at Hy-Vee as a cook in October 1999.   
At the time of hearing claimant was still working as a breakfast cook at Hy-Vee.  
Claimant worked from 35 to 40 hours before March of 2023.  In March of 2023, claimant 
reduced her hours to 22 hours per week due to arm pain.  No expert has opined that 
claimant could not continue to work 35-40 hours per week.  When all relevant factors 
are considered, it is found that claimant has a 30 percent loss of earning capacity or an 
industrial disability. 
Defendant Fund has a credit of 12.5 weeks for the first injury to the left upper 
extremity (250 weeks x 5 percent).  Defendant Fund also has a 32.5-week credit for the 
second injury (250 weeks x 13 percent).  Defendant Fund is due a credit of 45 weeks.  
Based on the above, Defendant Fund shall pay claimant 105 weeks worth of Fund 
benefits (150 weeks – 45 weeks). 
Fund benefits commence after the period of the employer’s payments.  Second 
Injury Fund of Iowa v. Shank, 516 N.W.2d 808 (Iowa 1994).  Permanent partial disability 
benefit payments for the employer and insurer commence on June 22, 2021, and run for 
32.5 weeks.  Second Injury Fund benefits shall commence 32.5 weeks after June 22, 
2021.
