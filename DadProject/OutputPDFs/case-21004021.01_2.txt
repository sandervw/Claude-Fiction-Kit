This matter comes before the Court on Petitioners' Petition for Judicial Review of the 
Workers' Compensation Commissioner's Appeal Decision. The Court has reviewed Petitioners' 
Brief in Support of Petition for Judicial Review, Respondent's Brief in Resistance, Petitioners' 
Reply Brief, and the certified agency record. The Court now enters the following order. 
I. PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND 
 
On June 9, 2022, Claimant Samantha Wells filed a petition with the Iowa Workers' 
Compensation Commissioner against Concept Rehab, Inc. (Employer) and Eastern Alliance 
Insurance Group (Insurance Carrier), collectively Petitioners. The matter proceeded to hearing 
before Deputy Commissioner Andrew Phillips on May 7, 2024. On October 15, 2024, Deputy 
Phillips issued an Arbitration Decision finding that Claimant's right thoracic outlet syndrome 
arose out of and in the course of her employment and awarding temporary partial disability 
benefits, healing period benefits, and permanent partial disability benefits based on 55% 
industrial disability.

II. STANDARD OF REVIEW 
 
Judicial review of an administrative agency action is governed by the Iowa 
Administrative Procedure Act, Iowa Code chapter 17A. An agency decision shall be reversed or 
modified if the agency decision is unsupported by substantial evidence, affected by error of law, 
or unreasonable, arbitrary, or capricious. Iowa Code § 17A.19(10). 
 
The Court is bound by agency findings of fact if they are supported by substantial 
evidence in the record as a whole. Meyer v. IBP, Inc., 710 N.W.2d 213, 218 (Iowa 2006). The 
workers' compensation commissioner is the trier of fact and is charged with weighing witness 
credibility, weighing the evidence, and deciding factual issues. Arndt v. City of Le Claire, 728 
N.W.2d 389, 394–95 (Iowa 2007). The Court will broadly and liberally apply agency findings to 
uphold, rather than overturn, an agency decision. Stephenson v. Furnas Elec. Co., 522 N.W.2d 
828, 831 (Iowa 1994). The reviewing court may only disregard the agency's conclusions if it 
decides, after reviewing the entire record, the direct and circumstantial evidence is so compelling 
that a reasonable mind would find the evidence inadequate to reach the same conclusions. 
Ringland Johnson, Inc. v. Hunecke, 585 N.W.2d 269, 272 (Iowa 1998). 
 
In the context of workers' compensation cases, the agency's findings are afforded 
significant deference and are treated as equivalent to a jury verdict. Caterpillar Tractor Co. v. 
Mejorado, 410 N.W.2d 675, 676–77 (Iowa 1987). The Court will reverse the application of law 
to facts if it is irrational, illogical, or wholly unjustifiable. Neal v. Annette Holdings, Inc., 814 
N.W.2d 512, 518 (Iowa 2012).

III. ANALYSIS 
A. Temporary Partial Disability Benefits 
 
Petitioners argue that the Commissioner erroneously concluded Claimant met her burden 
of proving entitlement to temporary partial disability benefits because no evidence in the record 
shows Claimant's underlying earnings during the alleged TPD periods. Petitioners have narrowed 
their challenge to this single contention: that regardless of what Exhibit 1 contains, the record 
lacks the underlying weekly earnings from Claimant's various employers necessary to calculate 
TPD benefits under Iowa Code § 85.33(4). In support, Petitioners rely exclusively on agency 
decisions holding that TPD benefits cannot be calculated without such evidence. Petitioners do 
not contest the Deputy Commissioner's credibility findings. 
 
Respondent contends that Petitioners waived any challenge to Claimant's Exhibit 1, 
which documented her earnings during the relevant periods. The record reflects that Petitioners 
received the underlying support documents during discovery, received Exhibit 1 and its 
supporting documents before the hearing, and failed to object to the admission of Exhibit 1 at 
hearing. Petitioners maintain that Exhibit 1 is irrelevant because, in their view, the evidentiary 
defect lies not in Exhibit 1's foundation but in the absence of any evidence of Claimant's 
underlying weekly earnings during the TPD periods. Petitioners therefore do not engage 
Respondent's waiver analysis directly. 
 
The Court concludes that Petitioners waived any foundational challenge to Exhibit 1. 
Where a party objects to the offer of evidence on the ground of no proper foundation, the party 
must point out what in particular of the foundation is deficient so the adversary may have an 
opportunity to remedy the alleged defect. Deaver v. Armstrong Rubber Co., 170 N.W.2d 455, 
465 (Iowa 1969); Iowa R. Evidence 5.103. The tactic of lying in wait and asserting objections for

the first time after the initial decision is prejudicial and contrary to Iowa law. Webb v. Iowa-
Nebraska Coal Co., 200 N.W. 225, 226–28 (1924). Had Petitioners timely objected, Claimant 
would have had the opportunity to remedy any alleged defect while the record was still open. 
McSpadden v. Big Ben Coal Co., 288 N.W.2d 181, 187 (Iowa 1980). Because Petitioners did not 
object, Exhibit 1 was properly admitted, and the agency was entitled to rely on it as part of the 
record. Additionally, Claimant testified that Exhibit 1 reflected her pay stub earnings and 
testified about her wages and hours worked. Both her average weekly wage and workers' 
compensation rate were stipulated in the Amended Hearing Report. The Commissioner’s 
conclusion that Claimant met her burden of proving entitlement to TPD benefits is supported by 
substantial evidence in the record and is affirmed. 
B. Healing Period Benefits for February and March 2022 
 
Petitioners argue that Claimant testified she was off work in February and March 2022 
because of diagnostic testing for non-work-related ailments, specifically mitochondrial disorders. 
 
A careful review of Claimant's testimony reveals that she was undergoing diagnostic 
testing to rule out mitochondrial disorders as a potential cause of the symptoms she was 
experiencing, which included progressive loss of strength and sensation in her hands and neck. 
Claimant testified that she was attempting to rule out other possible causes of her symptoms, 
stating she did not want to claim her condition was work-related if she was not certain. This 
testimony demonstrates that Claimant was prudently utilizing her time to attend necessary 
medical appointments while attempting to determine the cause of symptoms that ultimately were 
found to arise from her work-related right thoracic outlet syndrome. 
 
Nothing in Iowa Code § 85.33 regarding healing period benefits prohibits a claimant 
from utilizing time to attend necessary medical appointments while receiving such benefits. The

Deputy Commissioner found that Lisa Brekke, Dr. Hosn, and the FCE all clearly supported the 
finding that Claimant was temporarily disabled as a result of her work injury. Petitioners 
presented no contrary evidence at the hearing. The Commissioner's award of healing period 
benefits during February and March 2022 is supported by substantial evidence and is affirmed. 
C. Healing Period Benefits from July 21, 2023 Through September 11, 2023 
 
Petitioners argue that Claimant was off work during this period as a result of Dr. Hosn's 
July 21, 2023 surgery to repair Claimant's left thoracic outlet syndrome, which was found not to 
be work related. 
 
However, as pointed out by the Respondent, the fact that an injured worker has a non-
work-related disabling condition does not necessarily relieve an employer from liability for 
healing period benefits during the worker's recovery from a work injury. Iowa Code § 85.33. The 
Arbitration Decision found that Claimant reached maximum medical improvement for her right 
thoracic outlet syndrome on September 11, 2023. During the period from July 21, 2023 through 
September 11, 2023, Claimant was still recovering from her compensable right thoracic outlet 
syndrome. Claimant's medical records document her physical restrictions throughout the period 
of her convalescence. The Commissioner's finding that healing period benefits were warranted 
during this period is supported by substantial evidence and is affirmed. 
D. Permanent Partial Disability Benefits 
 
Petitioners argue that under Delaney v. Second Injury Fund of Iowa, 6 N.W.3d 714 (Iowa 
2024), and the Commissioner's Jimmerson Appeal Decision, the Commissioner should have 
limited the award to a scheduled member disability rather than industrial disability. Petitioners 
contend that Dr. Rondinelli's 20% right upper extremity rating should have resulted in an award 
of 50 weeks of scheduled member benefits, not 275 weeks based on 55% industrial disability.

A review of Dr. Rondinelli's report reveals that because there is no categorical rating for 
thoracic outlet syndrome in the AMA Guides, he utilized right upper extremity ratings by 
analogy, which were then converted to the body as a whole. The AMA Guides require upper 
extremity values to be used by analogy where no categorical thoracic outlet syndrome rating 
exists. Dr. Rondinelli indicated that Claimant's permanent impairment was 12% to her body as a 
whole. He did not conclude that Claimant's impairment was limited to the right upper extremity. 
 
Petitioners' reliance on Delaney actually supports the Commissioner's decision. In 
Delaney, the Iowa Supreme Court rejected a per se approach and held that "whether an injury 
results in the 'loss of use' to a scheduled member or extends to the body as a whole is a fact-
based inquiry that must be determined on a case-by-case basis." Delaney, 6 N.W.3d at 720. The 
Court explained that "in some cases, an injury to a bodily system may extend beyond a localized 
area and amount to an injury to the body as a whole," while "in other cases an injury to a bodily 
system may result only in localized loss of use to a scheduled member." Id. at 720-21. 
 
The Arbitration Decision quoted Delaney and applied its case-by-case requirement. 
Deputy Commissioner Phillips acknowledged that "the right upper extremity alone would result 
in a scheduled member injury," but found that "it is clear from the record that her injury extends 
to her body as a whole." The FCE revealed that Claimant experienced limitations with lifting and 
carrying, elevated work, forward bending, walking, and climbing stairs. Claimant's medical 
records consistently reflected symptomatology of neck and chest pain associated with her right 
thoracic outlet syndrome, not just upper extremity impairment. Petitioners contend that the 
impairment should be treated as limited to the right upper extremity. However, under Delaney, 
the analysis does not turn solely on the anatomical situs of the rated impairment, but on the 
totality of functional loss.

Additionally, Petitioners entered into a stipulation providing that if either the alleged 
bilateral thoracic outlet syndrome or the alleged vision injuries were found to have arisen out of 
the employment and to have caused permanent disability, the disability would be compensated 
industrially. Petitioners argue the stipulation is not triggered because neither bilateral TOS nor 
vision injuries were found compensable. Although the stipulation's wording refers to bilateral 
TOS or vision injuries, the Arbitration Decision interpreted it to apply to any TOS injury meeting 
the causation and permanency requirements. This interpretation is reasonable and supported by 
the Deputy Commissioner's explicit finding that right TOS satisfied both conditions. 
 
The unrebutted FCE revealed that Claimant has very restricted physical abilities. At the 
time of hearing, she continued to experience numbness among other significant complaints. Even 
three years after the date of injury, she was unable to work per the instruction of treater Lisa 
Brekke. 
 
The Commissioner's award of PPD benefits based on 55% industrial disability is 
supported by substantial evidence, is consistent with the parties' stipulation, and properly applies 
the Delaney framework. The Deputy Commissioner did not classify TOS as an unscheduled 
injury per se, but instead evaluated the evidence and concluded that its functional effects 
extended beyond the right upper extremity. This determination is not irrational, illogical, or 
wholly unjustifiable. The Commissioner's decision is affirmed. 
IV. CONCLUSION 
 
The Court finds that the Commissioner's Appeal Decision is supported by substantial 
evidence in the record when viewed as a whole and is not affected by error of law. The 
Commissioner's determinations regarding credibility, weight of evidence, and application of law
