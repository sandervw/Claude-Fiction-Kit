STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A. The 
expedited procedures of rule 876 IAC 4.48, the “alternate medical care” rule, are 
invoked by claimant, Carrie Devenney.  
This alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on August 5, 2025. The 
proceedings were recorded digitally and constitute the official record of the hearing.  By 
an order filed by the Workers’ Compensation Commissioner, this decision is designated 
final agency action. Any appeal would be by petition for judicial review under Iowa Code 
section 17A.15(1).  
The record in this case consists of Claimant’s Exhibit 1, Defendants’ exhibits A-C 
and the testimony of claimant and Amy Dunbar.  
ISSUE  
The issue presented for resolution in this case is whether claimant is entitled to 
alternate medical care consisting of authorization to transfer claimant’s care to a Unity 
Point Pain Management Clinic in Dubuque and for a spinal cord stimulator (SCS) 
consultation.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
Defendants accept liability for an injury occurring on February 27, 2022. 
On July 17, 2024, claimant was evaluated by Joysree Subramanian, M.D., a pain 
management specialist with the University of Iowa Hospitals and Clinics (UIHC).  
Claimant had a L-1 burst fracture in 2022 resulting in bilateral low back pain radiating 
into the sacral area.  Claimant had numbness and tingling in her feet.  Claimant was 
recommended to have an eight-week course of physical therapy before any further 
treatments.  (Exhibits B and C) 
A July 22, 2024, letter from the UIHC indicated the most recent MRI showed a 
chronic retropulsed burst fracture at the L-1 level.  The fracture was unchanged.  Based 
on claimant’s current symptoms there was no need for surgical intervention.  The letter 
indicates claimant may benefit from further pain management treatment.  (Ex. 1, p. 5) 
On March 13, 2025, claimant was evaluated by Michael Berry, M.D., an 
orthopedic spine surgeon.  He found claimant was at maximum medical improvement 
(MMI). Dr. Berry recommended claimant have a functional capacity evaluation and be 
given permanent restrictions.  (Ex. A, p.1) 
In a March 27, 2025, note Dr. Berry indicated claimant had a lumber MRI on 
March 25, 2025.  He opined the MRI showed a healed fracture and a stable spine.  He 
indicated he felt no further surgical intervention was needed.  (Ex. A, p. 1) 
The record suggests claimant was evaluated on June 10, 2025, by Timothy 
Miller, M.D., a pain specialist.  Claimant had lower extremity radiculopathy with ongoing 
bladder leakage.  Claimant’s medications were refilled.  Claimant was scheduled with 
Dr. Miller for a consult for a SCS.  (Ex. 1, pp. 6-7) 
In an email, claimant’s counsel requested defendants authorize claimant for a 
“possible” SCS.  He also requested authorization for pain management through the Pain 
Clinic and that claimant have counseling/mental health treatment with Dr. Wood (No 
First Name Given).  (Ex. 1, p. 9) 
In a July 11, 2025 email, defense counsel indicated claimant was authorized to 
return to Dr. Subramanian for pain management.  (Ex. 1, p. 10) 
Claimant testified the trip to the UIHC in Iowa City takes approximately one and 
one-half hours.  She said that because of the distance and her symptoms, she has to 
have someone drive her to the UIHC and she has to occasionally stop due to pain.  
Claimant said she wants her care transferred to the Findley Pain Clinic as it is closer to 
her home, more convenient, is easier for her to get to due to her symptoms, and does

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3).  
 
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers&#39; compensation law. 
The employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses 
incurred for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, 
except where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Section 85.27.  Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening October 16, 1975).          
 
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See Iowa 
R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e); Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995). 
Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact. Id.  The 
employer’s obligation turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.  Id.; 
Harned v. Farmland Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983).

Claimant requests she be sent for a consult for a SCS at the Pain Clinic at Finley 
Hospital.  (Ex. 1, p. 9) 
Claimant was evaluated in 2022 and 2024 by a pain management specialist at 
the UIHC.  She was evaluated four times in 2022 and 2024 by a neurosurgeon at the 
UIHC.  She was evaluated by Dr. Berry, a spine surgeon.  None of these providers have 
recommended claimant have a consult for a SCS.  Defendants have offered claimant to 
return to the UIHC pain specialist.  Defendants indicate that if a provider at the UIHC 
recommends a trial SCS, they will authorize the treatment.   
Claimant contends the travel to Iowa City for the authorized provider is difficult 
and inconvenient. I appreciate claimant’s issues with travel. However, there is no 
evidence in the record that from 2022, until this hearing, travel has been a problem for 
claimant.  Claimant indicates her next appointment with the UIHC is not until October of 
2025.  The record also indicates claimant was contacted in May of 2025 by the UIHC for 
an appointment which claimant turned down. 
Claimant has seen three different specialists.  None of these specialists have 
indicated claimant requires a SCS trial.  Defendants have authorized claimant to return 
to a pain specialist at the UIHC.  Defendants are on record that if a UIHC provider 
recommends a trial SCS, they will authorize the treatment.  Based on this record, 
claimant has failed to carry her burden of proof the care provided by defendants is 
unreasonable.   
ORDER 
THEREFORE, IT IS ORDERED: The claimant’s petition for alternate medical 
care is denied.
