Tommy Powell, claimant, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from ABM Industries, Inc., employer, and Indemnity Insurance 
Company of North America, insurance carrier, as defendants.  The hearing was held on 
June 26, 2024, at Iowa Workforce Development in Des Moines, Iowa, with all 
participants and the court reporter appearing in-person.  
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  Those 
stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration decision and 
no factual issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised or discussed in this 
decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.  
Tommy Powell testified live at the trial.  Robert Gilbert also testified on behalf of 
defendants. The evidentiary record also includes joint exhibits 1-3, claimant’s exhibits 1-
4, and defendants’ exhibits A-F.  Defendants offered exhibit F as impeachment 
evidence at hearing.  The claimant objected to it as duplicative of his hearing testimony.  
That objection was overruled, and exhibit F was received into the record, along with the 
rest of the exhibits listed above.  
Defendants submitted their post-hearing brief on July 31, 2024.  Claimant 
submitted his post-hearing brief on August 1, 2024, at which time the case was fully 
submitted to the undersigned.

ISSUES 
 
The parties identified the following disputed issues on the hearing report: 
1. Whether the alleged injury on April 13, 2023, resulted in any permanent 
disability; and if so,  
2. The nature and extent of claimant’s entitlement to permanent disability benefits.  
3. Whether claimant is entitled to payment of the medical expenses in claimant’s 
exhibit 2. 
4. Whether claimant is entitled to recover the cost of an independent medical 
examination pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.39.  
5. Assessment of costs.  
FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned, having considered all the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds as follows: 
At the time of the hearing, the claimant, Tommy Powell (hereinafter “Powell”) was 
79 years old. (Hearing Transcript, p. 14).  Powell attended school through the 11th grade 
and later obtained his GED while serving in the army. (Id. at 15-16).  Powell’s past work 
experience includes working in a shipyard, as a correctional officer, a tax preparer, and 
for juvenile service administration and the City of Tacoma.  (Id. at 16-17).   
On October 16, 2017, ABM Industries hired Powell to work as a parking lot 
attendant. (Tr., pp. 18-19).  As a parking lot attendant, Powell is responsible for serving 
customers, monitoring the ticket machines, changing light bulbs, cleaning stairwells and 
debris, light maintenance, and occasionally fixing flat tires.  (Defendants’ Exhibit D, p. 
45; Tr., p. 19).  At the time of the alleged injury in this case, Powell worked full-time

employed by ABM as a parking lot attendant, though he had voluntarily transferred to a

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 
employment.  Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 150 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. 
Dial Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309, 311 (Iowa 1996).  The words “arising out of” refer to the 

POWELL V. ABM INDUSTRIES, INC. 
Page 7 
 
cause or source of the injury.  The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 
circumstances of the injury.  2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124, 128 (Iowa 
1995).  An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists 
between the injury and the employment.  Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311.  The injury must 
be a rational consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely 
incidental to the employment.  Koehler Electric v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1, 3 (Iowa 2000); 
Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311.  An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it 
happens within a period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may 
be when performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties 
or doing an activity incidental to them.  Ciha, 552 N.W.2d at 150. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony.  The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question.  The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances.  The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410, 420 
(Iowa 2001); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995).  Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994).  Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected.  Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
When an expert's opinion is based upon an incomplete or incorrect history, it is 
not necessarily binding on the commissioner or the court. It is then to be weighed, 
together with other facts and circumstances, the ultimate conclusion being for the finder 
of the fact.  Musselman v. Central Telephone Company, 154 N.W.2d 128, 133 (Iowa 
1967); Bodish v. Fischer, Inc., 257 Iowa 516, 522, 133 N.W.2d 867, 870 (1965).  The 
commissioner as trier of fact has the duty to determine the credibility of the witnesses 
and to weigh the evidence, together with the other disclosed facts and circumstances, 
and then to accept or reject the opinion.  Dunlavey, 526 N.W.2d at 853.

The parties agree that Powell sustained a work-related injury on April 13, 2023, 
but disagree on whether that incident caused any permanent impairment.3  Based on 
the above findings of fact, I conclude Powell failed to meet his burden of proof to show 
he sustained permanent impairment from the work incident on April 13, 2023.  Powell 
relied upon the opinion of Dr. Bansal to causally relate his shoulder, neck and head 
complaints to the work incident.  However, Dr. Bansal’s opinion is entitled to little weight 
as it does not have supporting medical evidence.  Powell has not proven entitlement to 
permanent disability benefits in this action.  
Powell seeks payment of the medical expenses listed in Claimant’s Exhibit 2. For 
all compensable injuries under Iowa Code chapter 85 or 85A, the employer must 
“furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, chiropractic, podiatric, 
physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance, and hospital services and supplies therefor 
and shall allow reasonably necessary transportation expenses incurred for such 
services.” Iowa Code § 85.27(1). Above, I determined Powell did not suffer permanent 
impairment as a result of the work incident on April 13, 2023.  He, however, did miss 
work after the incident.  According to the evidence, Powell was off work and paid 
temporary total benefits by ABM from April 14, 2023, through April 30, 2023. (Ex. E, p. 
49).4   
Powell seeks payment of a bill for $475.22 for an office visit with Melinda 
Hubbard, PA-C, on April 25, 2023.  (Ex. 2, pp. 5-6).  On that date, Powell presented to 
Hubbard complaining of tenderness on the right side of his face and blurry vision after 
“being struck by the mirror of a truck when he was at work.”  (JE 2, p. 20).  Ms. Hubbard 
diagnosed him with a contusion of the right orbit with post-concussion symptoms and 
indicated he should remain off work until May 1, 2023. (Id. at 22). Clearly, the need for 
this visit was causally related to the work incident on April 13, 2023.  In their post-
hearing brief, defendants argue Powell is not entitled to payment/reimbursement of this 
medical expense because he “failed to prove his current symptoms are related to the 
work injury.”  (Defendants’ Post-Hearing Brief, p. 12).  They, however, have never 
argued that the incident itself did not occur.  Nor do they argue that the incident itself did 
not produce temporary symptoms.  Given the record before me, I find that defendants 
have stipulated that a work incident occurred on April 13, 2023, and that incident 
produced temporary symptoms for which Powell sought treatment on April 25, 2023, 
with Melinda Hubbard, PA-C.  This treatment was necessitated by the work incident.  If 
Powell has paid the medical expense listed in Claimant’s Exhibit 2, he is entitled to 
reimbursement of that expense.  If Powell has not paid the expense listed in Claimant’s 
Exhibit 2, defendants are ordered to pay the provider directly.  See, Krohn v. State, 420 
N.W.2d 463, 464-465 (Iowa 1988).

Powell also seeks reimbursement of the IME performed by Dr. Bansal.  His 
entitlement to IME reimbursement is governed by Iowa Code section 85.39(2).  That 
Code section provides:  
If an evaluation of permanent disability has been made by a physician 
retained by the employer and the employee believes this evaluation to be 
too low, the employee shall, upon application to the commissioner and upon 
delivery of a copy of the application to the employer and its insurance 
carrier, be reimbursed by the employer the reasonable fee for a subsequent 
examination by a physician of the employee’s own choice. . . . An employer 
is only liable to reimburse an employee for the cost of an examination 
conducted pursuant to this subsection if the injury for which the employee 
is being examined is determined to be compensable under this chapter or 
chapter 85A or 85B. 
Iowa Code § 85.39(2).   
Under the plain language of the Code, certain pre-requisites must be established 
before a claimant is entitled to IME reimbursement.  Among those pre-requisites are a 
prior evaluation of permanent disability by a physician retained by defendants, and a 
determination that the injury is compensable.  See Des Moines Area Reg’l Transit Auth. 
v. Young, 867 N.W.2d 839, 847 (Iowa 2015).  In this case, defendants sought a 
permanency opinion from Dr. Chen on May 17, 2024. (Ex. A, p. 1).  Dr. Chen released 
his report on May 23, 2024. (Id. at 4).  That report found Powell’s current symptoms are 
not causally related to the work incident on April 13, 2023; it opined he did not have any 
permanent impairment as a result of that work incident. (Id. at 12). Dr. Bansal’s IME 
report was issued on May 27, 2024. (Cl Ex. 3, p. 16).  An “opinion on lack of causation 
[is] tantamount to a zero impairment rating,” which is reimbursable under Iowa Code 
section 85.39.  Kern v. Fenchel, Doster & Buck, P.L.C., 966 N.W.2d 326 (Table) (Iowa 
Ct. App. 2021). Given this, Powell meets the first pre-requisite listed in Code section 
85.39(2).     
The closer question is whether Powell meets the second pre-requisite; i.e., does 
he have a compensable injury.  Defendants argue Powell is not entitled to IME 
reimbursement under Iowa Code section 85.39, because he has not proven that his 
“current medical conditions” are causally related to the work incident. (Defendants’ Post-
Hearing Brief, pp. 11-12).  They are correct that Powell has not proven entitlement to 
permanent benefits in this proceeding.  However, a finding of permanency is not 
necessary to trigger IME reimbursement.  Iowa Code section 85.39 states an IME is 
reimbursable “if the injury for which the employee is being examined is determined to be 
compensable under this chapter or chapter 85A or 85B.” Iowa Code § 85.39(2) 
(emphasis added).  Iowa Code Chapter 85 provides authority for both medical benefits 
and indemnity payments.  Powell received temporary benefits for his injury. He was also 
awarded medical expenses in this action.  Under this record, his injury is compensable.  
He is entitled to reimbursement for the reasonable fee of a subsequent examination by 
a physician of his choice.

Dr. Bansal charged $2,947.00 for Powell’s IME. (Cl Ex. 1, p. 4).  Defendants are 
responsible only for reasonable fees associated with claimant's independent medical 
examination. Claimant has the burden of proving the reasonableness of the expenses 
incurred for the examination. See Schintgen v. Economy Fire & Casualty Co., File No. 
855298 (App. April 26, 1991).  Based upon prior agency decisions, I find Dr. Bansal’s 
fee is reasonable and typical for an IME performed in the Des Moines metro area.  (See 
also, Cl Ex. 1, p. 4).   
Finally, Powell seeks an award of costs as outlined in Claimant’s Exhibit 1.  Costs 
are to be assessed at the discretion of the deputy commissioner hearing the case. See 
876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.33; Iowa Code § 10A.328. Iowa Administrative Rule 
4.33 provides as follows:  
[c]osts taxed by the workers’ compensation commissioner or a deputy 
commissioner shall be (1) attendance of a certified shorthand reporter or 
presence of mechanical means at hearings and evidential depositions, (2) 
transcription costs when appropriate, (3) costs of service of the original 
notice and subpoenas, (4) witness fees and expenses as provided by Iowa 
Code sections 622.69 and 622.72, (5) the costs of doctors’ and 
practitioners’ deposition testimony, provided that said costs do not exceed 
the amounts provided by Iowa Code sections 622.69 and 622.72, (6) the 
reasonable costs of obtaining no more than two doctors’ or practitioners’ 
reports, (7) filing fees when appropriate, including convenience fees 
incurred by using the WCES payment gateway, and (8) costs of persons 
reviewing health service disputes. 
876 IAC 4.33. Powell incurred costs for the filing fee for his petition, as well as service of 
that petition. (Cl Ex. 1, p. 1).  Powell failed to prove that he is entitled to permanent 
benefits in this proceeding; therefore, I conclude his costs should not be assessed 
against defendants.  Each party shall bear its own costs.
