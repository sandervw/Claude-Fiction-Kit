I.

of the right shoulder performed by Dr. David Hart on April 19, 2021. Id. at 8. Dr. Hart opined that

II. 
SCOPE AND STANDARD OF REVIEW. 
 
The Iowa Administrative Procedure Act, Iowa Code Chapter 17A, governs the scope of the 
Court’s review in workers' compensation cases. Iowa Code § 86.26; Meyer v. IBP, Inc., 710 
N.W.2d 213, 218 (Iowa 2006). A party challenging agency action bears the burden of 
demonstrating the action's invalidity and resulting prejudice. Iowa Code § 17A.19(8)(a). The 
Petitioner is claiming error with the agency’s interpretation of the law; therefore, the question on 
review is whether the agency’s interpretation was erroneous. See Meyer, 710 N.W.2d at 219. If the 
error was erroneous, the court may substitute its interpretation for the agency’s. Id. (citing Clark 
v. Vicorp Rests., Inc., 696 N.W.2d 596 (Iowa 2005)). The Court is not bound by an agency’s legal 
conclusions and may correct misapplications of the law. Ginsburg v. Iowa Dept of Transp., 508 
N.W.2d 663, 664 (Iowa 1993); Sallis v. Employment Appeal Bd., 437 N.W.2d 895, 896 (Iowa 
1989). Ultimately, the workers' compensation law should be liberally construed to accomplish the 
object and purpose of the legislation: to benefit the worker and his dependents. Dillinger v. City of 
Sioux City, 368 N.W.2d 176, 180 (Iowa 1985).

III. 
MERITS. 
 
The issue before this Court is whether Horne’s injury to his right upper extremity is a 
second qualifying injury under Iowa Code Section 85.64(1).2 
 
Iowa Code Section 85.64(1) states: 
If an employee who has previously lost, or lost the use of, one hand, one arm, one foot, one 
leg, or one eye, becomes permanently disabled by a compensable injury which has resulted 
in the loss of or loss of use of another such member or organ, the employer shall be liable 
only for the degree of disability which would have resulted from the latter injury if there 
had been no pre-existing disability. In addition to such compensation, and after the 
expiration of the full period provided by law for the payments thereof by the employer, the 
employee shall be paid out of the “Second Injury Fund ” created by this division the 
remainder of such compensation as would be payable for the degree of permanent disability 
involved after first deducting from such remainder the compensable value of the previously 
lost member or organ. 
 
Second Inj. Fund of Iowa v. George, 737 N.W.2d 141, 146 (Iowa 2007) (alteration in original).  
 
To successfully claim Second Injury Fund benefits, a claimant needs to prove, “(1) the 
employee has either lost, or lost the use of[,] a hand, arm, foot, leg, or eye; (2) the employee 
sustained the loss, or loss of use of[,] another such member…through a work related…injury; and 
(3) there must be some permanent disability from the injuries.” Second Inj. Fund of Iowa v. Shank, 
516 N.W.2d 808, 812 (Iowa 1994); Anderson v. Second Inj. Fund of Iowa, 262 N.W.2d 789, 790 
(Iowa 1978). Both the first and second injuries must be scheduled injuries to invoke Fund liability. 
Shank, 516 N.W.2d at 814. Scheduled member injuries are those parts of the body specifically 
listed in Iowa Code Section 85.34(2)(a)-(t). Saylor v. Manpower Inc., No. 01-0560, 2002 WL 
31425208, *4 (Iowa Ct. App. Oct. 30, 2002). Whereas injuries to the body as a whole are 
unscheduled injuries and are not subject to Fund liability. Second Inj. Fund of Iowa v. Nelson, 544 
N.W.2d 258, 268 (Iowa 1995).

Here, the Commissioner relied on the district court case, Anderson v. Bridgestone 
Americas, Inc. in determining that a second injury to an arm and a shoulder caused by a single 
accident was not a scheduled member injury. Arb. Dec. 2-3. In his finding, the Commissioner 
stated, “[t]he District Court in Anderson found that a claimant with an arm and a shoulder injury, 
caused by a single incident, should receive workers’ compensation benefits under Iowa Code 
Section 85.34(2)(v). That should be the outcome in this case.” Id.  
However, on March 29, 2024, the Iowa Supreme Court overturned the Commissioner’s 
decision and the district court’s decision in the Anderson case. The Anderson Court ruled that a 
shoulder and an arm injury should be compensated as scheduled injuries under Iowa Code Section 
85.34(2)(m) and (n), respectively, and not as unscheduled injuries under Iowa Code Section 
85.34(2)(v). Bridgestone Americas, Inc., et al. v. Anderson, No. 22-1328, 2024 WL 1334165, at 
*4 (Iowa Sup. Ct. March 29, 2024). The Court held: 
An injury can be unscheduled only if it fits in paragraph (v). And by its plain terms, 
paragraph (v) can only apply to injuries other than those … described or referred to in 
paragraphs ‘a’ through ‘u.’ But Anderson’s injuries are not other than those … described 
or referred to in paragraphs ‘a’ through ‘u.’ Anderson’s injuries are to his shoulder and 
arm. Both are “described or referred to” in the schedule composed of paragraphs ‘a’ 
through ‘u.’   
 
Id. (citing Iowa Code Section 85.34(2)(v) (alteration in original) (citations and internal quotation 
marks omitted)). Because the Commissioner based his decision in part on Anderson, when finding 
that Horne’s injuries were unscheduled and should fall under Iowa Code Section 85.34(2)(v), this 
Court must reverse and remand this case to the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commission in light 
of the Iowa Supreme Court’s decision in Bridgestone Americas Inc. v. Anderson. 
IV. 
CONCLUSION AND DISPOSTION. 
 
For all the reasons set forth above, the Court concludes the Commissioner erred in his 
interpretation of the law to the facts, given the Iowa Supreme Court’s reversal decision in the
