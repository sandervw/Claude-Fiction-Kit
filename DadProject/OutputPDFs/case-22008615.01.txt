STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
The claimant, Brian McKenzie, filed a petition for arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from self-insured employer City of Des Moines (“Des Moines”).  
Eric Loney appeared on behalf of the claimant.  John Haraldson appeared on behalf of 
the defendant.   
 
The matter came on for hearing on January 16, 2024, before Deputy Workers’ 
Compensation Commissioner Andrew M. Phillips.  Pursuant to an order of the Iowa 
Workers’ Compensation Commissioner, the hearing occurred electronically via Zoom.  
The hearing proceeded without significant difficulty.  
 
The record in this case consists of Joint Exhibits 1-5 and Claimant’s Exhibits 1-9.  
The claimant objected to Defendant’s Proposed Exhibit A based upon the defendant’s 
failure to timely serve their witness and exhibit list, and failure to timely serve all of their 
intended exhibits on the claimant.  The claimant further argued that the defendant failed 
to serve their proposed exhibit A until January 5, 2024.  It was determined that including 
the defendant’s exhibit would unfairly prejudice the claimant.      
The claimant testified on his own behalf.  Theresa Kenkel was appointed the 
official reporter and custodian of the notes of the proceeding.  The evidentiary record 
closed at the conclusion of the hearing, and the matter was considered fully submitted 
following briefing by the parties on March 15, 2024.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa Rule of Appellate 
Procedure 6.904(3).   
Permanent Partial Disability 
In this case, the parties stipulated that the claimant’s work injury was a cause of 
permanent disability.  The parties further stipulated that the claimant’s permanent 
disability was to be analyzed as an industrial disability.  Despite the admission by the 
claimant that he earned the same or more at the time of the hearing that he earned at 
the time of the injury which would seem to implicate certain provisions of Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(v).  However, the parties are bound by their stipulations.   
Under the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is 
compensated either for a loss of use of a scheduled member under Iowa Code 
85.34(2)(a)-(u) or for loss of earning capacity under Iowa Code 85.34(2)(v).  The extent 
of scheduled member disability benefits to which an injured worker is entitled is 
determined by using the functional method.  Functional disability is “limited to the loss of 
the physiological capacity of the body or body part.”  Mortimer v. Fruehauf Corp., 502 
N.W.2d 12, 15 (Iowa 1993); Sherman v. Pella Corp., 576 N.W.2d 312 (Iowa 1998).   
 
An injury to a scheduled member may, because of after effects or compensatory 
change, result in permanent impairment of the body as a whole.  Such impairment may 
in turn be the basis for a rating of industrial disability.  It is the anatomical situs of the 
permanent injury or impairment which determines whether the schedules in Iowa Code 
85.34(a) – (u) are applied.  Lauhoff Grain v. McIntosh, 395 N.W.2d 834 (Iowa 1986); 
Blacksmith v. All-American, Inc., 290 N.W.2d 348 (Iowa 1980); Dailey v. Pooley Lumber 
Co., 233 Iowa 758, 10 N.W.2d 569 (1943); Soukup v. Shores Co., 222 Iowa 272, 268 
N.W. 598 (1936).   
 
Since the claimant has an impairment to the body as a whole, an industrial 
disability has been sustained.  Industrial disability was defined Diederich v. Tri-City R. 
Co. of Iowa, 219 Iowa 587, 258 N.W. 899 (1935) as follows: “[i]t is therefore plain that 
the Legislature intended the term ‘disability’ to mean ‘industrial disability’ or loss of 
earning capacity and not a mere ‘functional disability’ to be computed in terms of 
percentages of the total physical and mental ability of a normal man.”   
 
Functional impairment is an element to be considered in determining industrial 
disability which is the reduction of earning capacity, but consideration must also be 
given to the injured employee’s age, education, qualifications, experience, motivation, 
loss of earnings, severity and situs of the injury, work restrictions, inability to engage in 
employment for which the employee is fitted, and the employer’s offer of work or failure 
to so offer.  McSpadden v. Big Ben Coal Co., 288 N.W.2d 181 (Iowa 1980); Olson v. 
Goodyear Service Stores, 255 Iowa 1112, 125 N.W.2d 251 (1963); Barton v. Nevada 
Poultry Co., 253 Iowa 285, 110 N.W.2d 660 (1961).

A loss of earning capacity due to voluntary choice or lack of motivation to return 
to work is not compensable.  Malget v. John Deere Waterloo Works, File No. 5048441 
(Remand Dec. May 23, 2018); Rus v. Bradley Puhrmann, File No. 5037928 (App. 
December 16, 2014); Gaffney v. Nordstrom, File No. 5026533 (App. September 1, 
2011); Snow v. Chevron Phillips Chemical Co., File No. 5016619 (App. October 25, 
2007); Copeland v. Boone’s Book and Bible Store, File No. 1059319 (App. November 6, 
1997); See also Brown v. Nissen Corp., 89-90 IAWC 56, 62 (App. 1989) (no prima facie 
showing that claimant is unemployable when claimant did not make an attempt for 
vocational rehabilitation).   
The claimant’s compensation will be analyzed as an industrial disability.  There 
are portions of Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v) which could potentially apply here and 
entitle the claimant to compensation solely based upon his functional disability; 
however, the defendant stipulated that the claimant sustained an industrial disability.  
They made no mention in the hearing report, during the hearing, or in their posthearing 
briefing, as to the potential application of the foregoing provisions of Iowa Code section 
85.34(2)(v).  Therefore, they have waived that argument.   
Mr. McKenzie was 42 years old at the time of the hearing.  He is right-hand 
dominant.  He possesses a high school education.  He was a below average student in 
high school.  His work history indicates that he began working for Des Moines in 2001 
as a refuse collector.  Since that time, he has held no other full-time position.  As a 
refuse collector, he pulled wheeled dumpsters from corrals and hooked them up to a 
front-loading garbage truck.  He opined that the job required him to push, pull and lift.  
There are likely not many other careers for which the claimant is qualified considering 
his history.     
In April of 2022, Mr. McKenzie operated an automated garbage truck.  As he 
attempted to turn a corner, his power steering malfunctioned causing him to injure his 
left shoulder, bicep, and neck.  He eventually was diagnosed with a superior labral tear, 
tendinopathy in his shoulder, and tendinopathy of the long head of the biceps.  He 
visited with Dr. Aviles on several occasions before Dr. Aviles performed an arthroscopic 
surgery on his left shoulder.  Within the arthroscopic procedure, Dr. Aviles completed a 
subacromial decompression, a distal clavicle excision, and a biceps tenodesis.   
Following his surgery, Mr. McKenzie completed a round of physical therapy.  He 
also returned to work in a light duty capacity during this time.  By September of 2022, 
Mr. McKenzie told Dr. Aviles that he was doing quite well in his recovery.  After 23 
sessions of physical therapy, Mr. McKenzie showed improvement in his lifting, pushing, 
and pulling.  He noted an incident where he threw a soda can away which irritated his 
left shoulder.  In November of 2022, functional testing was completed.  Mr. McKenzie 
completed this functional testing with no issues, and was cleared for discharge.  
Following this appointment, Dr. Aviles released the claimant to return to work as

examination was rather cursory in nature, and testified that he still experienced pain at 
the time of the appointment.  This contradicts a number of medical records, which 
indicated that Mr. McKenzie exhibited no pain in his left shoulder.

Dr. Aviles released Mr. McKenzie to work with no restrictions.  Mr. McKenzie

Dr. Aviles used Figures 16-40 and 16-46 of the Guides to provide an impairment 
rating based upon the range of motion exhibited by the claimant.  Considering these 
portions of the Guides, Dr. Aviles issued the claimant a 2 percent left upper extremity 
impairment rating.   
At the arrangement of claimant’s counsel, Dr. Bansal issued an IME report 
outlining certain findings and opinions.  Dr. Bansal based some of his opinions on the 
claimant’s subjective belief that he could not lift more than 20 pounds from the floor to 
the table with his left arm.  Dr. Bansal provided an impairment rating based upon deficits 
in range of motion of 5 percent of the left upper extremity.  Dr. Bansal added 10 percent 
to this based upon the clavicle excision.  He combined these to arrive at a 15 percent 
left upper extremity rating, or a 9 percent whole person impairment.  He added on a 2 
percent upper extremity impairment based upon the alleged 10 percent elbow flexion 
strength loss.  This equated to a 1 percent whole person impairment.  Dr. Bansal never 
combined this rating with any of the other whole person impairments.   
Dr. Bansal also recommended that the claimant not lift more than 15 pounds with 
his left arm.  He also recommended that Mr. McKenzie avoid all overhead lifting with his 
left arm.  It is hard to find credibility in Dr. Bansal’s restrictions, as they appear to have 
had some basis in Mr. McKenzie’s subjective statements surrounding his ability to lift 
certain amounts.  These subjective statements are contradicted by the objective 
measurements taken during his physical therapy visits.   
The key element in this case when considering the claimant’s industrial disability 
is the reduction in the claimant’s earning capacity.  The claimant admits in their 
posthearing brief that they are earning around the same amount as they were prior to 
the surgery.  Additionally, the claimant continues to work.  He provided no evidence of a 
reduction in earnings or a reduction in his hours.  Certainly, there could be more 
significant industrial disability if Des Moines released Mr. McKenzie from employment; 
however, he continues to work.  He certainly experiences pain while working, but there 
is no evidence in the record that he is unable to complete his job.  He testified that he 
intended to complete his career to retirement with Des Moines.   
Based upon my consideration of the foregoing factors and the applicable factors 
of an industrial disability analysis, I find that the claimant sustained a 15 percent 
industrial disability.  Industrial disability benefits are based upon 500 weeks.  See Iowa 
Code section 85.34(2)(v).  This results in an award of 75 weeks of benefits at the 
stipulated rate (500 weeks x 0.15 = 75 weeks).

Costs 
The claimant seeks an assessment of costs as listed in Claimant’s Exhibit 9.  
Costs are to be assessed at the discretion of the deputy commissioner hearing the 
case.  See 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.33; Iowa Code section 10A.328.  876 Iowa 
Administrative Code 4.33(86) provides:  
[c]osts taxed by the workers’ compensation commissioner or a deputy 
commissioner shall be (1) attendance of a certified shorthand reporter or 
presence of mechanical means at hearings and evidential depositions, (2) 
transcription costs when appropriate, (3) costs of service of the original 
notice and subpoenas, (4) witness fees and expenses as provided by Iowa 
Code sections 622.69 and 622.72, (5) the costs of doctors’ and 
practitioners’ deposition testimony, provided that said costs do not exceed 
the amounts provided by Iowa Code sections 622.69 and 622.72, (6) the 
reasonable costs of obtaining no more than two doctors’ or practitioners’ 
reports, (7) filing fees when appropriate, including convenience fees 
incurred by using the WCES payment gateway, and (8) costs of persons 
reviewing health service disputes.   
Pursuant to the holding in Des Moines Area Regional Transit Authority v. Young, 
867 N.W.2d 839 (Iowa 2015), only the report of an IME physician, and not the 
examination itself, can be taxed as a cost according to 876 IAC 4.33(6).  The Iowa 
Supreme Court reasoned, “a physician’s report becomes a cost incurred in a hearing 
because it is used as evidence in lieu of the doctor’s testimony,” while “[t]he underlying 
medical expenses associated with the examination do not become costs of a report 
needed for a hearing, just as they do not become costs of the testimony or deposition.”  
Id.  (noting additionally that “[i]n the context of the assessment of costs, the expenses of 
the underlying medical treatment and examination are not part of the costs of the report 
or deposition”).  The commissioner has found this rationale applicable to expenses 
incurred by vocational experts.  See  Kirkendall v. Cargill Meat Solutions Corp., File No. 
5055494 (App. December 17, 2018); Voshell v. Compass Group, USA, Inc., File No. 
5056857 (App. September 27, 2019).   
The claimant seeks to recover the costs of the filing fee and Dr. Bansal’s IME 
report.  Dr. Bansal’s invoice is not sufficiently detailed to prove which portion of his costs 
pertain to the drafting of his report.  Therefore, I decline to award the costs of Dr. 
Bansal’s IME.  However, I award the claimant one hundred three and 00/100 dollars 
($103.00) for the filing fee.
