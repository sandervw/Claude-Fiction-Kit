STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Jory Dunlap, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits against the Second Injury Fund of Iowa.  
This case was heard on December 17, 2024, and considered fully submitted on 
February 4, 2025, upon the simultaneous filing of briefs.  
The record consists of Joint Exhibits 1–13, Claimant’s Exhibits 1–3, Fund 
Exhibits AA-BB, and the testimony of Claimant. 
ISSUES 
1. Whether Claimant sustained a prior qualifying loss to the left hand, and if so 
the extent of the loss;  
 
2. Whether Claimant sustained a compensable loss to the left arm on March 7, 
2022, and if so the extent of the loss;  
 
3. Whether Claimant is entitled to benefits from the Fund, and if so, the amount;  
 
4. The commencement date of SIF benefits, if any are awarded;  
 
5. Whether the Fund is entitled to a credit, and if so the amount. 
STIPULATIONS 
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised 
or discussed in this decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.

Fontanelle, Iowa. His educational background consists of graduation from high school in

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury actually occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 
employment. Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 150 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. 
Dial Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309, 311 (Iowa 1996). The words “arising out of” refer to the 
cause or source of the injury. The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 
circumstances of the injury. 2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124, 128 (Iowa 
1995).  An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists 
between the injury and the employment. Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. The injury must 
be a rational consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely 
incidental to the employment. Koehler Elec. v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1, 3 (Iowa 2000); 
Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it 
happens within a period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may 
be when performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties 
or doing an activity incidental to them. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d at 150. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an

expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 
621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 
(Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
Iowa Code Section 85.64 governs Second Injury Fund Liability. Before Fund 
benefits are triggered, the employee need only establish that (1) he has previously lost, 
or lost the use of, one hand, one arm, one foot, one leg, or one eye (i.e., fist qualifying 
loss); (2) he sustained the loss, or loss of use of another such member or organ through 
a compensable work-related injury (i.e., a second qualifying loss); and (3) he has 
sustained some permanent disability from the two injuries. Iowa Code § 85.64. 
Both Dr. Chen and Dr. Hawk agree that Claimant sustained an injury to his left 
ring finger in 1992. Dr. Chen wrote that Claimant has a greatly limited range of motion of 
the 4th finger metacarpal joint with a 30-degree extension lag which is likely due to his 
tendon laceration that occurred in 1992. The Fund argues that Claimant never worked 
under any restrictions due to his left hand or finger injury, and Claimant testified that 
prior to March 2022, the left ring finger injury did not prevent him from work or leisure 
activities.   
Despite Claimant not having work restrictions due to his left ring finger injury, 
there is no dispute that he had a loss of use of that finger. The loss of use may have 
been minimal, but there is uncontradicted evidence that there was a loss of use.  
In Stumpff v. Second Injury Fund of Iowa, the Supreme Court of Iowa ruled that a 
finger injury was not considered a qualifying injury as the statute specifies hand but not 
finger. Stumpff v. Second Injury Fund of Iowa, 543 N.W.2d 904, 906 (Iowa 1996).  
Stumpff's initial injury involved a severe fracture to his right index finger, resulting in a 
73 percent permanent partial disability to that finger. (Id.) Stumpff did not have an 
impairment rating assigned for the hand. (Id.) The court concluded that this injury did 
not qualify as a "permanent loss of use" of the hand, as required by the statute, but was 
limited to the finger itself. Consequently, Stumpff did not meet the criteria for Second 
Injury Fund benefits. (Id.) 
Claimant argues that he had impairments to his hand as evidenced by the 
impairment ratings assigned by the two experts as well as the work restrictions 
imposed.  Dr. Chen’s impairment rating was given specifically for diminished use of his 
left 4th finger with decreased lateral pinch grip strength.” (SIF AA:9) The 12 percent 
impairment of the finger was converted to a 1 percent impairment of the hand.  
Similarly, Dr. Hawk’s rating for the hand was a conversion related to an 
impairment for decreased range of motion of the 4th left digit. (CE 1:33) He assigned a 
14 percent impairment to the ring finger which converted to a hand impairment of 1

percent. (CE 1:33) “1% upper extremity impairment of the upper extremity equals 1% 
impairment of the whole person as it relates to the range of motion deficit of the left 4th 
digit.” (CE 1:33)  
While Claimant was given restrictions to avoid gripping with his left hand and 
avoid repetitive pinching, and grasping, these were given due to the left 4th digit 
impairment. (CE 1; SIF AA:10) 
Claimant maintains that the impairment rating to the finger then was converted to 
the hand, which differentiates this case from Stumpff. However, the loss of use is to the 
finger, not the hand. Stumpff is controlling authority in this case. It is found that Claimant 
did not sustain a first qualifying loss to an enumerated body part pursuant to the statute.
