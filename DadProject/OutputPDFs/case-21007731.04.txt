STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Ivan Short, claimant, filed a petition in arbitration seeking worker’s compensation 
benefits against Rosenmans, Inc. and Pioneer Specialty Insurance Company.  
In accordance with agency scheduling procedures, a hearing occurred on July 
17, 2024, via Zoom.  The case was considered fully submitted on August 30, 2024, 
upon the filing of briefs.

hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties' stipulations will be raised 
or discussed in this decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.

1 through 8, and Defendants’ Exhibits A through G.  A redacted version of Exhibit F was 
admitted into the evidentiary record subsequent to the evidentiary hearing.  Claimant

The outcome in this case appears straightforward.  The medical evidence and 
established legal principles strongly support a finding that Mr. Short’s left hip condition is 
a proximate result of the original left ankle injury.  While the defendants argue that Mr. 
Short’s left hip pain stems from his personal decisions—specifically, his failure to 
strengthen his ankle through exercise and his choice to walk with his foot turned 
outward—I find that these factors do not sever the causal relationship between the 
original left ankle injury and the subsequent hip condition. Regardless of the personal 
nature of Mr. Short's gait or the continued weakness in his left leg, the evidence 
supports that his altered walking pattern and compensatory mechanics are directly 
attributable to the initial ankle injury and the resulting period of immobility. Mr. Short’s 
response to the injury, including his method of walking, is a foreseeable consequence of 
the significant left ankle injury and subsequent surgery.  
It is a longstanding principle of the workers’ compensation system that 
defendants are liable for all consequences that naturally and proximately flow from the 
original work injury.  The left hip condition is a proximate result of the original left ankle 
injury.  After sustaining a significant left ankle injury and undergoing surgery, Mr. Short 
experienced prolonged immobility and weakness in his left leg.  It is well documented 
that following surgery, Mr. Short consistently demonstrated weakness, pain, and 
atrophy in his left leg.  To mitigate his pain, Mr. Short elected to – rightly or wrongly – 
walk with an outward foot position.  This compensatory walking pattern directly resulted 
from the initial left ankle injury and its aftermath.  Mr. Short’s actions, while less than 
ideal from a rehabilitative standpoint, were responses to the pain and functional 
limitations caused by the left ankle injury. (See Ex. 1, p. 8)  It is fairly clear, at least to 
the undersigned, that the left ankle injury was a substantial contributing factor in the 
development of the left hip condition.  Consistent with Dr. Bansal’s causation opinion, I 
find the left hip pain is properly considered a sequela of the original left ankle injury. 
Having concluded Mr. Short carried his burden of proving a left hip sequela 
injury, I must now determine the nature and extent of his permanent disability.   
When comparing the impairment assessments of Dr. Bansal and Dr. Mooney, I 
find Dr. Bansal’s impairment assessment to be the most thorough and convincing.  He 
is the only expert physician to assign permanent impairment to the left hip condition.  
His assessment most accurately reflects Mr. Short’s permanent disability.  Therefore, I 
accept Dr. Bansal’s impairment ratings and find Mr. Short sustained 12 percent whole 
person impairment as a combined result of the left ankle and left hip injuries.  
The undersigned is cognizant of Dr. Mooney’s opinion that claimant’s diagnoses 
do not constitute a ratable condition.  Such an opinion directly conflicts with Dr. Bansal’s 
impairment assessment.  Dr. Mooney’s opinion also appears to conflict with the AMA 
Guides.  Both Dr. Mooney and Dr. Bansal assessed ischial and greater trochanteric 
bursitis of the left hip. (Ex. 1, p. 10; Ex. C, p. 27)  While the undersigned is not a medical 
doctor, Table 17-33 appears to indicate “Trochanteric bursitis (chronic) with abnormal 
gait” is indeed a ratable condition.  Dr. Mooney provides no analysis to substantiate his 
opinion.  For these reasons, I do not find Dr. Mooney’s opinion convincing.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony.  The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question.  The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances.  The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke's Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410, 420 
(Iowa 2001); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995).  Miller v. Lauridsen Foods. Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994).  Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected.  Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling. Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
A personal injury contemplated by the workers' compensation law means an 
injury, the impairment of health or a disease resulting from an injury which comes about, 
not through the natural building up and tearing down of the human body, but because of 
trauma.  The injury must be something that acts extraneously to the natural processes 
of nature and thereby impairs the health, interrupts or otherwise destroys or damages a 
part or all of the body.  Although many injuries have a traumatic onset, there is no 
requirement for a special incident or an unusual occurrence.  Injuries which result from 
cumulative trauma are compensable.  Increased disability from a prior injury, even if 
brought about by further work, does not constitute a new injury, however.  St. Luke's 
Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 650-51 (Iowa 2000); Ellingson v. Fleetguard, Inc., 599 
N.W.2d 440, 444 (Iowa 1999); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 
845, 850-51 (Iowa 1995); McKeever Custom Cabinets v. Smith, 379 N.W.2d 368, 373-
74 (Iowa 1985).  An occupational disease covered by chapter 85A is specifically

Page 11 
 
excluded from the definition of personal injury. Iowa Code section 85.61(4) (b); Iowa 
Code section 85A.8; Iowa Code section 85A.14. 
While a claimant is not entitled to compensation for the results of a preexisting 
injury or disease, its mere existence at the time of a subsequent injury is not a defense.  
Rose v. John Deere Ottumwa Works, 247 Iowa 900, 76 N.W.2d 756, 760-61 (1956).  If 
the claimant had a preexisting condition or disability that is materially aggravated, 
accelerated, worsened or lighted up so that it results in disability, claimant is entitled to 
recover.  Nicks v. Davenport Produce Co., 254 Iowa 130, 135, 115 N.W.2d 812, 815 
(1962); Yeager v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co., 253 Iowa 369, 374-75, 112 N.W.2d 299, 
302 (1961). 
An employer may be liable for a sequela of an original work injury if the employee 
sustained a compensable injury and later sustained further disability that is a proximate 
result of the original injury.  Mallory v. Mercy Medical Center, File No. 5029834 (Appeal 
February 15, 2012). 
The Iowa Supreme Court held long ago that “where an accident occurs to an 
employee in the usual course of his employment, the employer is liable for all 
consequences that naturally and proximately flow from the accident.”  Oldham v. 
Scofield & Welch, 222 Iowa 764, 266 N.W. 480, 482 (1936). 
A sequela can be an after effect or secondary effect of an injury.  Lewis v. Dee 
Zee Manufacturing, File No. 797154, (Arb. September 11, 1989).  One form of sequela 
of a work injury is an adverse effect from medical treatment for the original injury.  
Where treatment rendered with respect to a compensable injury itself causes further 
injury, the subsequent injury is also compensable.  Young v. United Fire & Casualty Co., 
256 Iowa 813, 129 N.W.2d 75 (1964).  For example, the death of a claimant who died 
on the operating table during surgery for a work injury may be compensable, since the 
injury caused the need for surgery.  Breeden v. Firestone Tire, File No. 966020, (Arb. 
February 27, 1992).  As another example, a claimant who fell as a result of dizziness 
from medication he was taking to treat a work injury is to be compensated for both the 
original injury and the resulting fall as a sequela of the first injury.  Hamilton v. 
Combined Ins. of America, File Nos. 854465, 877068, (Arb. February 21, 1991). 
A sequela can also take the form of a secondary effect on the claimant's body 
stemming from the original injury.  For example, where a leg injury causing shortening 
of the leg in turn alters the claimant's gait, causing mechanical back pain, the back 
condition can be found to be a sequela of the leg injury.  Fridlington v. 3M, File No. 
788758, (Arb. November 15, 1991). 
A sequela can also take the form of a later injury that is caused by the original 
injury.  For example, where a leg injury leads to the claimant's knee giving out in a 
grocery store, the resulting fall is compensable as a sequela of the leg injury.  Taylor v. 
Oscar Mayer & Co., Ill Iowa Ind. Comm. Rep. 257, 258 (1982).

The parties stipulated that the December 23, 2020, work injury is a cause of 
permanent disability.  As such, the nature and extent of claimant’s entitlement to 
permanent partial disability benefits must be determined.  Claimant’s injuries involve the 
left lower extremity and the body as a whole.  As such, Claimant’s injury should be 
compensated pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v). 
Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v) provides: 
If an employee who is eligible for compensation under this paragraph 
returns to work or is offered work for which the employee receives or 
would receive the same or greater salary, wages, or earnings than the 
employee received at the time of the injury, the employee shall be 
compensated based only upon the employee's functional impairment 
resulting from the injury, and not in relation to the employee's earning 
capacity. 
Mr. Short asserts that his claim should be assessed industrially because he does 
not make the same or greater earnings when compared to his pre-injury status.  In this 
respect, I found Claimant’s argument convincing.  Claimant was offered and accepted a 
return to work.  Initially, he earned the same or greater wages as he received at the time 
of injury; however, his earnings were eventually reduced when the defendant employer 
limited Claimant’s hours.  
An injured worker’s hourly wage must be considered in tandem with the actual 
hours worked or offered by the employer  when comparing pre- and post-injury wages 
and earnings under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v). McCoy v. Menard, Inc., File No. 
1651840.01 (App. April 9, 2021) 
Leading up to and on the date of the claimant’s injury, he was consistently 
offered 40 or more hours of work per week. However, following the work injury, the 
defendant employer reduced the number of hours offered to the claimant to 28 hours 
per week. (Hr. Tr., pp. 52-53.) The defendants argue that this reduction in hours was 
unrelated to the work injury, as there were no formal work restrictions imposed 
attributable to the claimant’s left ankle and/or left hip conditions. Based on the evidence 
presented, I find this argument unpersuasive.

The reduction in the claimant’s hours directly corresponds with the time he 
missed due to pain complaints and medical appointments necessitated by the work 
injury. Rather than accommodating the claimant with light-duty work or a modified 
schedule, the employer effectively reduced his available work hours to reflect the time 
he was actually able to perform his job while managing his symptoms. In doing so, the 
employer essentially imposed a functional work restriction, even in the absence of a 
formal medical limitation. The practical effect of this reduction was a loss of earning 
capacity directly attributable to the work injury. Accordingly, I find that the claimant’s 
reduction in hours was causally related to the work injury, and he is entitled to industrial 
disability benefits.  In other words, his recovery is not limited to his functional 
impairment.   
Since Claimant has an impairment to the body as a whole, an industrial disability 
has been sustained. Industrial disability was defined in Diederich v. Tri-City R. Co. of 
Iowa, 219 Iowa 587, 258 N.W. 899 (1935) as follows: “It is therefore plain that the 
Legislature intended the term ‘disability’ to mean ‘industrial disability’ or loss of earning 
capacity and not a mere “functional disability' to be computed in the terms of 
percentages of the total physical and mental ability of a normal man.” 
Functional impairment is an element to be considered in determining industrial 
disability which is the reduction of earning capacity, but consideration must also be 
given to the injured employee's age, education, qualifications, experience, motivation, 
loss of earnings, severity and situs of the injury, work restrictions, inability to engage in 
employment for which the employee is fitted and the employer's offer of work or failure 
to so offer. McSpadden v. Big Ben Coal Co., 288 N.W.2d 181, 192 (Iowa 1980); Olson 
v. Goodyear Service Stores, 255 Iowa 1112, 1120, 125 N.W.2d 251, 256 (1963); Barton 
v. Nevada Poultry Co., 253 Iowa 285, 110 N.W.2d 660, 663 (1961). 
Having considered all of the relevant factors of industrial disability, I found that 
Mr. Short proved a 25 percent loss of future earning capacity as a result of his 
December 23, 2020, injury. Accordingly, I conclude that Mr. Short is entitled to a 25 
percent industrial disability award, or 125 weeks of permanent partial disability benefits. 
Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v).

Iowa Code section 10A.315(4)(a) provides: 
If a denial, a delay in payment, or a termination of benefits occurs without

delayed, or terminated without reasonable or probable cause or excuse.

An issue of law is fairly debatable if viable arguments exist in favor of each party.
