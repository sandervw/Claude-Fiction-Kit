STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A.  The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Brian Aulwes.  
Claimant appeared personally and through his attorney, Mark Sullivan.  Defendant 
appeared through their attorney, Coreen Sweeney. 
The alternate medical care claim was filed on January 14, 2025, and came on for 
hearing on January 23, 2025.  The proceedings were digitally recorded on Google Meet.  
That recording constitutes the official record of this proceeding.  Pursuant to the 
Commissioner’s Order, the undersigned has been delegated authority to issue a final 
agency decision in this alternate medical care proceeding.  Therefore, this ruling is 
designated final agency action and any appeal of the decision would be to the Iowa 
District Court pursuant to Iowa Code section 17A. 
The record consists of claimant’s exhibits 1 through 2 and defense exhibits A 
through F.  Claimant objected to Defendant Exhibit A, which was overruled.  Mr. Aulwes 
testified live and under oath at hearing, as did Stephanie Holland, Deere’s third-party 
claims manager.  The defendant filed an answer on January 22, 2025, and does not 
dispute liability for claimant’s March 2015, work injury, or the condition for which he is 
seeking treatment. 
ISSUE 
The issue presented for resolution is whether the claimant is entitled to additional 
“Aqua Therapy exercise” and if, so, the nature and extent of this.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
The claimant sustained a work injury on March 26, 2015.  This injury turned out 
to be catastrophic and resulted in his permanent and total disability.  Mr. Aulwes has 
significant ongoing disabilities and has had a constant stream of medical care.  He 
testified live and under oath at hearing, and I find his testimony highly credible. 
Currently, his authorized treating provider is the University of Iowa Hospitals and 
Clinics (UIHC) Pain Clinic in Iowa City.  He has been seen at this facility on an annual 
basis.  He testified that his annual appointment at UIHC Pain Clinic is scheduled for 
approximately April 2025. 
Mr. Aulwes testified that he was authorized to see his primary care provider, 
Shelly Klein, DNP, ARNP, FNP-BC for prescribing certain medications (his lidocaine 
patches in particular).  A representative from Deere’s third-party administrator (TPA) 
testified live and under oath and generally denied this assertion.  In any event, I 
certainly believe Mr. Aulwes that it is his understanding that Ms. Klein was authorized at 
least for the limited purpose of prescribing his pain patches. 
Mr. Aulwes testified he has had discussions with his authorized physicians at 
UIHC about the benefits of aqua therapy in the past, which I understand to mean 
physical therapy in a swimming pool.  In response, Mr. Aulwes has invested in a 
swimming pool at his home that he can use in the summer months to get regular 
exercise.  He testified he has benefited from this informal aqua therapy.  He has also 
pressed Deere to provide more formal aqua therapy directed by a physical therapist 
who specializes in this.  In approximately August 2024, Deere authorized aqua therapy 
at a facility in Dubuque, Iowa, named Stonehill Wellness Center.  The parties described 
this as a state-of-the art aqua therapy center which is very much in demand.  (See also 
Defendant’s Exhibit C)  The treatment records for these visits are not in evidence.  Mr. 
Aulwes testified credibly that this therapy was directed by a physical therapist and it was 
highly beneficial for his condition.  Mr. Aulwes further testified that his therapist 
recommended more aqua therapy.  Following this course of treatment, Mr. Aulwes had 
surgery for a non-work related condition.  Deere promised to review his request for 
further therapy at a later date.  It is also noted that there was some delay in the payment 
of Stonehill’s bill.   
On January 7, 2025, Shelly Klein authored a letter recommending “continue with 
Aqua Therapy exercise daily.”  (Cl. Ex. 1)  She provided a detailed explanation for her 
opinion.  Mr. Aulwes interpreted this recommendation to allow him to return to Stonehill 
for appointments with his authorized physical therapist.  Mr. Aulwes, through his 
attorney, forwarded this request to Deere’s attorney. 
In response, Deere’s attorney obtained a report from Tim Miller, M.D., a 
physician Mr. Aulwes last saw possibly in 2022.  Dr. Miller wrote the following opinion: 
I am a very large proponent of Aqua therapy and have offered it to 
thousands of patients that have limitations in joints or spine where

REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Iowa Code Section 85.27 (2013). 
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See 
Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  Determining what care is 
reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.  Id.  The employer’s obligation turns 
on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.  Id.; Harned v. Farmland 
Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983).   
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995). 
An employer’s statutory right is to select the providers of care and the employer 
may consider cost and other pertinent factors when exercising its choice. Long, at 124. 
An employer (typically) is not a licensed health care provider and does not possess 
medical expertise. Accordingly, an employer does not have the right to control the

methods the providers choose to evaluate, diagnose and treat the injured employee. An 
employer is not entitled to control a licensed health care provider’s exercise of 
professional judgment. Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 866389 (Declaratory 
Ruling, May 19, 1988). An employer’s failure to follow recommendations of an 
authorized physician in matters of treatment is commonly a failure to provide reasonable 
treatment. Boggs v. Cargill, Inc., File No. 1050396 (Alt. Care Dec. January 31, 1994). 
Based upon the record before me, I find that the opinion of Dr. Miller is 
reasonable.  Dr. Miller has recommended that Mr. Aulwes have four to six therapy 
sessions with a physical therapist to develop a self-directed exercise program.  I 
disagree with Deere’s interpretation, however, that this has already happened.  The 
therapy records from Stonehill are not in evidence.  It is unknown in this record if this is 
what the therapist’s plan with Mr. Aulwes was at all.  In fact, based upon the testimony 
of Mr. Aulwes, this is not what the plan was.  In any event, I find that it is unreasonable 
for Deere to assume that simply because Mr. Aulwes has already had some visits with a 
physical therapist at Stonehill that he should be able to direct his own aqua therapy at 
this point in time, especially since he has not been treated at Stonehill since August 
2024. 
The claimant argued that Shelly Klein is an authorized treating provider and that 
she ordered him to “continue aqua therapy exercises daily.”  (Cl. Ex. 1)  This opinion is 
reasonable as well.  The problem is, this recommendation is vague.  I certainly find that 
it is likely that her opinion is that Mr. Aulwes should have some further visits with a 
physical therapist at Stonehill.  I doubt that she intended to suggest that he should have 
daily visits at Stonehill with a physical therapist 365 days per year. 
The claimant also made a number of detailed arguments regarding the 
insufficiency of self-directed treatment and exercise at Stonehill, including scheduling 
issues, sharing the facility with other members, etc.  While I am sympathetic to these 
concerns, these fears bear little weight on this ruling.  Since Mr. Aulwes has already had 
some success with self-directed exercise at his home pool, I strongly suspect his final 
treatment plan will have to include some self-directed therapy and exercise, although it 
will ultimately be the treatment providers who make the final decision on this. 
Thus, my ruling is that Mr. Aulwes should return to a physical therapist at 
Stonehill and then, if necessary, work with his authorized physicians, if necessary, to 
develop a concrete plan for his future aqua therapy based upon his actual medical 
needs. 
ORDER 
THEREFORE IT IS ORDERED: 
The claimant's petition for alternate medical care is GRANTED.  Defendant shall 
immediately authorize a return evaluation including four to six visits with the physical 
therapist specializing in aqua therapy at Stonehill to develop a concrete treatment plan 
for his present condition.
