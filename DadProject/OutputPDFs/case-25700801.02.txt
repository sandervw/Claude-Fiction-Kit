STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
On November 17, 2025, the claimant filed a petition for alternate medical care 
pursuant to Iowa Code 85.27(4) and 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.48.  The 
defendants filed an answer admitting liability for the injuries to the claimant’s left 
shoulder and left hip. 
The undersigned presided over the hearing held electronically and recorded 
digitally on December 1, 2025.  That recording constitutes the official record of the 
proceeding pursuant to 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.48(12).  The claimant 
participated personally and through his attorney, Andrew Giller.  The defendants 
participated through their attorney, Caitlin Kilburg.   
Prior to the hearing, the claimant submitted six pages of exhibits, marked as 
Exhibits 1 through 3.  The defendants submitted five pages of exhibits, marked as 
Exhibits A and B.  The exhibits were received into the record without objection. 
 
On July 21, 2023, the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commissioner issued an 
order delegating authority to deputy workers’ compensation commissioners, such as the 
undersigned, to issue final agency decisions on applications for alternate care.  
Consequently, this decision constitutes final agency action, and there is no appeal to 
the commissioner.  Judicial review in a district court pursuant to Iowa Code Chapter 17A 
is the avenue for an appeal.

ISSUE 
The issue under consideration is whether the defendants should be ordered to 
authorize and provide the claimant with treatment for his left shoulder and a left hip 
replacement. 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
 James Raney, the claimant, testified on his own behalf during the hearing.  Mr. 
Raney testified that he had an appointment with a Dr. Nassif on November 11, 2025.  
He indicated that Dr. Nassif refused to treat his left shoulder, as it was not authorized.  
He also testified that Dr. Nassif recommended that he undergo a left hip replacement.   
 
Included in the evidence is a record from Physicians’ Clinic of Iowa, dated 
November 11, 2025.  (Claimant’s Exhibit 3:1-2).  This record represents the results of a 
visit by Mr. Raney with Jeffrey Nassif, M.D.  (CE 3:1-2).  Mr. Raney outlined falling at a 
hotel on December 7, 2024, which resulted in “significant bruising” to his left hip.  (CE 
3:1).  He also described worsening pain in his hip following the injury.  (CE 3:1).  Mr. 
Raney told Dr. Nassif that he never had hip issues in the past, and actually over the 
summer and fall before he fell he was doing very well.  (CE 3:1).  Dr. Nassif found Mr. 
Raney to have painful, reduced, range of motion in his left hip.  (CE 3:2).  He diagnosed 
Mr. Raney with left hip pain and left hip osteoarthritis.  (CE 3:2).  The record indicated 
that Dr. Nassif discussed a left hip replacement with Mr. Raney, but there is no 
indication that Dr. Nassif made a recommendation that Mr. Raney have a hip 
replacement.  (CE 3:2).  The record simply indicates that Dr. Nassif discussed treatment 
options with him.  (CE 3:2).   
 
On November 12, 2025, counsel for the claimant emailed defendants’ counsel 
opining that Dr. Nassif was recommending hip replacement surgery and stating, 
“[p]lease make sure the surgery is promptly authorized.”  (CE 1:1-2).   
 
Claimant’s counsel emailed defendants’ counsel again on November 14, 2025, 
stating, “[f]ollowing up on the below.  Please let me know.”  (CE 1:1).   
 
On November 25, 2025, defendants’ counsel emailed claimant’s counsel asking 
if they had any medical notes from Dr. Nassif.  (Defendants’ Exhibit A).  Claimant’s 
counsel responded, indicating that they had not received any records, but that they 
requested them on November 12, 2025.  (DE A).  Defendants’ counsel replied indicating 
that their client could not make a decision on the requested hip surgery without being 
able to review the records.  (DE A).  Claimant’s counsel responded by arguing that it 
was not his responsibility to obtain medical records, and that defendants’ counsel 
should have obtained the records.  (DE A).   
 
On the same day, defendants sent a letter to Dr. Nassif requesting his opinions 
on the causation of the claimant’s ongoing left hip treatment.  (DE B).

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Iowa Code 85.27(4) provides, in relevant part: 
For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish reasonable 
services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has the right to 
choose the care….  The treatment must be offered promptly and be 
reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the 
employee.  If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the care 
offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such dissatisfaction 
to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the employer and 
the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited to treat the 
injury.  If the employer and employee cannot agree on such alternate care, 
the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable proofs of the 
necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
Iowa Code 85.27(4). See Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 433 (Iowa 
1997).   
“Iowa Code section 85.27(4) affords an employer who does not contest the 
compensability of a workplace injury a qualified statutory right to control the medical 
care provided to an injured employee.”  Ramirez-Trujillo v. Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 
N.W.2d 759, 769 (Iowa 2016) (citing R.R. Donnelly & Sons v. Barnett, 670 N.W.2d 190, 
195, 197 (Iowa 2003)).  “In enacting the right-to-choose provision in section 85.27(4), 
our legislature sought to balance the interests of injured employees against the 
competing interests of their employers.”  Ramirez-Trujillo, 878 N.W.2d at 770-71 (citing 
Bell Bros., 779 N.W.2d at 202, 207; IBP, Inc. v. Harker, 633 N.W.2d 322, 326-27 (Iowa 
2001)).   
The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except where the 
employer has denied liability for the injury.  Iowa Code section 85.27; Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening, October 16, 1975).  An employer’s right to select 
the provider of medical treatment to an injured worker does not include the right to 
determine how an injured worker should be diagnosed, evaluated, treated, or other 
matters of professional medical judgment.  Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 
866389 (Declaratory Ruling, May 18, 1988).  Reasonable care includes care necessary 
to diagnose the condition, and defendants are not entitled to interfere with the medical 
judgment of its own treating physician.  Pote v. Mickow Corp., File No. 694639 (Review-
Reopening Decision, June 17, 1986).   
The employer must furnish “reasonable medical services and supplies and 
reasonable and necessary appliances to treat an injured employee.”  Stone Container 
Corp. v. Castle, 657 N.W.2d 485, 490 (Iowa 2003)(emphasis in original).  Such 
employer-provided care “must be offered promptly and be reasonably suited to treat the 
injury without undue inconvenience to the employee.”  Iowa Code section 85.27(4).  
More specifically in Long, the court noted that “the employer’s obligation under the

statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.”  Long v. Roberts 
Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122, 128 (Iowa 1995).   
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment - and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See e.g. 
Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3); Bell Bros. Heating and Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d 
193, 209 (Iowa 2010); Long, 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  More specifically in Long, 
the court noted that “the employer’s obligation under the statute turns on the question of 
reasonable necessity, not desirability.”  Long, 528 N.W.2d at 128.   
“Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.”  
Long, 528 N.W.2d at 123; Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 436.  As the party 
seeking relief in the form of alternate care, the employee bears the burden of proving 
that the authorized care is unreasonable.  Id. at 124; Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d at 209; Pirelli-
Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 436.  Because “the employer’s obligation under the 
statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability,” an injured 
employee’s dissatisfaction with employer-provided care, standing alone, is not enough 
to find such care unreasonable.  Id.  However, if substantial evidence exists that the 
care authorized by the employer was ineffective, it may be found that the care was not 
reasonable.  Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 437.   
Iowa Code section 85.27(4) requires that an injured worker “should communicate 
the basis of such dissatisfaction to the employer, in writing if requested…”  876 Iowa 
Administrative Code 4.48(3) requires an injured worker to communicate the basis of 
dissatisfaction of the care to the employer prior to filing an application for alternate care.  
These requirements are in place in order to allow the parties an opportunity to reach an 
understanding prior to the need for agency intervention.  See Iowa Code section 
85.27(4).   
In this case, the defendants argue that the claimant did not convey their 
dissatisfaction with the offered care prior to applying to the agency for alternate medical 
care in the form of a left hip replacement.  In response, the claimant points to their 
November 12, 2025, email in which claimant’s counsel stated, “[Dr. Nassif] 
recommended hip replacement surgery.  Please make sure the surgery is promptly 
authorized.”  This email included no records from the visit.  The claimant’s argument is 
that their dissatisfaction is implied by their request for a “prompt authorization” of the hip 
replacement surgery.   
The next email correspondence from claimant’s counsel is on November 14, 
2025, at 4:43 p.m., in which claimant’s counsel simply indicates that they are “[f]ollowing 
up on the below.  Please let me know.”   
The claimant then filed an original notice and petition concerning application for 
alternate care on November 17, 2025, at about 4:30 p.m.   
Iowa Code section 85.27(4) and 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.48(3) are clear 
insofar as they require that an injured worker communicate their dissatisfaction prior to

filing an application for alternate care.  This communication cannot be implied.  
Claimant’s counsel emailing defendants’ counsel requesting “prompt authorization” of a 
procedure and then a subsequent email “following up…” is not an expression of 
dissatisfaction.  The claimant failed to abide by the requirements imposed by Iowa Code 
section 85.27(4) and 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.48(3).  Therefore, the claimant’s 
application for alternate care related to his left hip is denied. 
With regard to the claimant’s allegations surrounding his left shoulder, it should 
be noted that the parties entered into a consent order on October 14, 2025.  The 
defendants agreed to authorize care for the claimant’s left shoulder.  While that care 
has not been provided, the claimant did not move for sanctions based upon the 
defendants’ failure to abide by the consent order.  Additionally, the defendants agreed 
to return Mr. Raney to Dr. Nassif in order for the doctor to provide care to the claimant’s 
left shoulder.  Therefore, the claimant’s alternate care petition as it relates to that issue 
is granted. 
IT IS THEREFORE ORDERED: 
1. The claimant’s petition for alternate care is granted in part, and denied in part. 
  
2. The claimant’s application for alternate care regarding his left hip is denied. 
 
3. The defendants shall arrange for an appointment for care for the claimant’s 
left shoulder.
