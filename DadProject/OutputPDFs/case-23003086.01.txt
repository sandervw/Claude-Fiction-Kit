STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Nate Snell, claimant, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ compensation 
benefits from Gordon Farms, Inc., employer and Farm Bureau Property and Casualty, 
insurance carrier, as defendants.  Hearing was held via Zoom videoconference on 
October 7, 2025.     
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.  
Nate Snell, Melanie Snell, and Sarah Ottersland testified live at trial.  The 
evidentiary record also includes joint exhibits 1-8, claimant’s exhibits 1-7, and 
defendant’s exhibits A-G.  All exhibits were received without objection.  The evidentiary 
record closed at the conclusion of the arbitration hearing.       
The parties submitted post-hearing briefs on November 12, 2025, at which time 
the case was fully submitted to the undersigned.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned, having considered all the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
 
 Claimant, Nate Snell, has alleged he injured his back while working at Gordon 
Farms.  The parties stipulated that he sustained work-related injuries on March 7, 2023 
and August 7, 2023 at Gordon Farms, Inc. (Hearing Reports, numbered paragraph 2)   
Prior to working for Gordon Farms, Nate had a lower back condition.  (Claimant’s 
Exhibit 1, p. 1) He had sciatica with symptoms radiating into his right leg.  (CE1, p. 1) 
Dr. Palit performed an L4-L5 decompression for right radiculopathy in 2003.  (CE1, p. 1) 
His back condition improved after the surgery.  He had activity related pain from time to 
time but it was not severe enough to seek medical care.  (CE1, p. 2) His symptoms 
were not significant enough to limit his activities.  He worked without restrictions before 
the injuries at Gordon Farms.  (CE1, p. 2; Tr. p. 24) 
At the time of the hearing Nate was 41 years old.  (Tr. p. 15) In 2019 he was 
hired by Gordon Farms which is located near Webster City, Iowa.  (Defendant’s Ex. C, 
p. 12) Gordon Farms has crops and pigs.  (Tr. p. 23) Nate was hired to perform 
maintenance on tractors and semis, maintenance in pig buildings, changing steel roofs, 
welding, grinding, and other duties.  (Tr. p. 23; Def. Ex. C, p. 12) Nate described his job 
as very physical, including lifting 90 to 100 pounds.  (Tr. p. 23) He was able to perform 
all of his job duties prior to the first date of injury, March 7, 2023.   
 
 The first work injury at Gordon Farms occurred on March 7, 2023.  (Tr. p. 24) 
Nate and three other individuals were moving pigs in pens.  (Tr. pp. 24-25) He was 
closing a gate, a pig came at him and he used his leg to swoosh the pig back.  (Tr. p. 
25) Due to the motion and turning there was a pop and sharp pain that caused him to 
go down on to the gate.  (Tr. p. 25) The other people, including his boss, had to help 
him back up.  (Tr. p. 25) Nate sought treatment the next day. (Tr. pp. 25-26) 
 
On March 8, 2023, Nate was seen at the hospital at Trinity Regional Hospital in 
Fort Dodge.  (JE2, pp.6-22) He reported the work injury and his history of low back

As set forth above, both Dr. Palit and Dr. Kuhnlein cited the AMA Guides, Fifth 
edition and offered their opinions regarding permanent functional impairment.  Dr. Palit 
assigned 28 percent whole person impairment.  (Def. Ex. B, p. 10) Dr. Kuhnlein 
assigned 34 percent whole person impairment.  (CE1, p. 8) In his report Dr. Kuhnlein 
indicated that the initial injury was on March 7, 2023 with ongoing cumulative 
exposures. (CE 1, p. 7) I find Dr. Kuhnlein’s opinion that Nate sustained an acute injury 
on March 7, 2023 with ongoing cumulative exposures to be persuasive.  (CE1, p. 7) 
This is consistent with the evidentiary record that shows Nate sustained an acute injury 
on March 7, 2023 and experienced lower back tightness and pain until and beyond 
August 7, 2023.  (Tr. p. 27; CE1, p. 2) I find Dr. Kuhnlein’s detailed rating carries greater 
weight than Dr. Palit’s. Thus, I find Nate has sustained 34 percent whole person 
impairment as the result of the March 7, 2023 work injury.   
 
Claimant is seeking payment of past medical expenses and medical mileage as

Next, claimant is seeking penalty benefits for late payment of his permanent 
partial disability (PPD) benefits. In a letter dated May 14, 2024, Dr. Palit issued a PPD 
rating.  (DE B, pp. 9-10) The final line of his May 14, 2024 letter states, “[a]dditionally, 
Mr. Snell has reached maximum medical improvement as of March 19, 2024.”  (DE B, 
p. 10)  
 
Sarah Ottersland testified at the hearing.  Sarah is a workers’ compensation 
claim specialist at Farm Bureau Financial Services.  (Tr. pp. 96-97) She is the adjuster 
on Nate’s workers’ compensation claims.  (Tr. p. 97) She testified that when she first 
received the correspondence from Dr. Palit regarding the impairment rating the last line 
of the letter regarding MMI was not part of the letter.  (Tr. pp. 98-99) It is her testimony 
that defendants’ exhibit B, p. 10 is not a true and accurate copy of the letter she initially 
received from Dr. Palit’s office.  (Tr. p. 98) On April 25, 2025, approximately 11 months 
after receipt of Dr. Palit’s letter, defendants sent a letter to Dr. Palit seeking his opinion 
on MMI.  (DE B, p. 7) Sarah testified that the delay in issuance of PPD benefits was due 
to confusion as to whether Nate had reached MMI and whether the impairment rating 
was final.  (Tr. p. 99) I find the only version of the May 14, 2024 letter in evidence from 
Dr. Palit clearly stated Nate reached MMI as of March 19, 2024.  (DE B, p. 10) 
Furthermore, even if the reason for the delay in benefits was due to confusion over the 
MMI date, I find the reason for the delay was not contemporaneously conveyed to Nate.   
The parties have stipulated that the appropriate commencement date for any 
PPD benefits was March 19, 2024.  I find that the defendants paid $11,060.55 in PPD 
benefits on August 24, 2024.  The adjuster testified that said payment brought the PPD 
payments current to the date of the check.  (Tr. p. 100) I find there was a delay in the

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony.  The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question.  The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances.  The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); 
Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995).  Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994).  Unrebutted expert medical 
testimony cannot be summarily rejected.  Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 
N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 1994). 
Claimant has sustained a back injury, which is an impairment to the body as a 
whole, therefore his claim will be adjudicated under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v).

during the number of weeks in relation to five hundred weeks as the 
reduction in the employee's earning capacity caused by the disability 
bears in relation to the earning capacity that the employee possessed 
when the injury occurred. A determination of the reduction in the 
employee's earning capacity caused by the disability shall take into

In 2021, the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commissioner addressed this Code 
section.  An injured worker's hourly wage must be considered in tandem with the actual 
hours worked or offered by the employer when comparing pre-and post-injury wages 
and earnings under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v). McCoy v. Menard, Inc., File No. 
1651840.01 (App. April 9, 2021) 
 
Recently, the Iowa Supreme Court addressed Iowa Code Section 85.34(2)(v).  
The Court stated,  
[t]he present case requires us to declare the meaning of Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(v) (2019), and most particularly its third sentence.  That 
sentence provides,  
If an employee who is eligible for compensation under this 
paragraph returns to work or is offered work for which the 
employee receives or would receive the same or greater 
salary, wages, or earnings than the employee received at 
the time of the injury, the employee shall be compensated 
based only upon the employee’s functional impairment 
resulting from the injury, and not in relation to the 
employee’s earning capacity. 
We interpret those words according to their plain meaning.  Thus, we hold 
that an employee eligible for compensation under that paragraph who 
returns to work at the same or greater salary, wages, or earnings should 
be compensated based upon their functional impairment resulting from the 
injury.  We are not persuaded by the contrary, nontextual arguments

advanced in this litigation.  In some other cases, references to a 
“bifurcated ligation process,” to a rule of construction that workers’ 
compensation laws should be interpreted in claimants’ favor to effectuate 
their humanitarian purpose, and to other policy arguments might carry the 
day.  But they cannot overcome plain text here. 
Den Hartog Industries v. Dungan, 26 N.W.3d 377, 379 (Iowa 2025). 
 
In the present case claimant returned to work for the defendants after both dates 
of injury.  He was earning more per hour at the time of the hearing than he was on the 
dates of the injuries.  However, as noted above, the evidence clearly demonstrates that 
his overall earnings from the defendant-employer have decreased since the injuries.  
Although he does not have any medical restrictions on the number of hours he may 
work, he credibly testified that he is not physically capable of working as many hours as 
he did prior to the injury.  Additionally, he credibly testified that he does not feel that it is 
safe for him to operate certain machinery.  However, the evidence in the record 
indicates that there is always work to be done on the farm, but the reason claimant’s 
hours have varied since the injuries is because when he is sore, he goes home.  I 
conclude that the defendants have offered him unlimited hours of work.  I conclude that 
defendants offered him work at greater hourly wages after the injury. I further conclude 
that defendants have offered him work with the same or greater hours as he did prior to 
the injury.  However, because claimant is sore or does not feel comfortable performing 
certain duties because of his injuries, he does not perform the offered work and 
defendants have graciously accommodated the injured worker.  The plain language of 
the statute states that if an employee returns to work or is offered work for which the 
employee would receive the same or greater salary, wages, or earnings than he 
received at the time of the injury, the employee shall be compensated based only upon 
the employee’s functional impairment resulting from the injury.  Accordingly, applying 
the plain text of Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v), I conclude claimant’s permanent partial 
disability benefits should be based on functional impairment, rather than earning 
capacity.

Next, claimant is seeking penalty benefits for defendants’ unreasonable delay in 
payment of the PPD benefits.   
Iowa Code section 10A.315(4) provides: 
a. 
If a denial, a delay in payment, or a termination of benefits occurs without 
reasonable or probable cause or excuse known to the employer or 
insurance carrier at the time of the denial, delay in payment, or termination 
of benefits, the workers’ compensation commissioner shall award benefits 
in addition to those benefits payable under this chapter, or chapter 85, 
85A, or 85B, up to fifty percent of the amount of benefits that were denied, 
delayed, or terminated without reasonable or probable cause or excuse. 
 
b. 
The workers’ compensation commissioner shall award benefits under this 
subsection if the commissioner finds both of the following facts: 
(1) 
The employee has demonstrated a denial, delay in 
payment, or termination in benefits. 
(2) 
The employer has failed to prove a reasonable or 
probable cause or excuse for the denial, delay in payment, or 
termination of benefits. 
 
In Christensen v. Snap-on Tools Corp., 554 N.W.2d 254 (Iowa 1996), and Robbennolt 
v. Snap-on Tools Corp., 555 N.W.2d 229 (Iowa 1996), the supreme court said: 
Based on the plain language of section [10A.315(4)]1, we hold an 
employee is entitled to penalty benefits if there has been a delay in 
payment unless the employer proves a reasonable cause or excuse.  A 
reasonable cause or excuse exists if either (1) the delay was necessary 
for the insurer to investigate the claim or (2) the employer had a 
reasonable basis to contest the employee’s entitlement to benefits.  A 
“reasonable basis” for denial of the claim exists if the claim is “fairly 
debatable.” 
Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 260. 
The supreme court has stated: 
 
1 Iowa Code section 10A.315(4) was previously codified as Iowa code section 86.13(4).  No 
substantive changes were made to the statute when transferred from section 86.13 to section 10A.315.  
References to the current location of this statute are substituted to avoid confusion.

(1) If the employer has a reason for the delay and conveys that reason 
to the employee contemporaneously with the beginning of the delay, no 
penalty will be imposed if the reason is of such character that a 
reasonable fact-finder could conclude that it is a "reasonable or probable 
cause or excuse" under Iowa Code section [10A.315].  In that case, we 
will defer to the decision of the commissioner.  See Christensen, 554 
N.W.2d at 260 (substantial evidence found to support commissioner’s 
finding of legitimate reason for delay pending receipt of medical report); 
Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 236. 
 
(2) If no reason is given for the delay or if the “reason” is not one that 
a reasonable fact-finder could accept, we will hold that no such cause or 
excuse exists and remand to the commissioner for the sole purpose of 
assessing penalties under section [10A.315(4)].  See Christensen, 554 
N.W.2d at 261. 
 
(3) Reasonable causes or excuses include (a) a delay for the 
employer to investigate the claim, Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 260; 
Kiesecker v. Webster City Custom Meats, Inc., 528 N.W.2d at 109, 111 
(Iowa 1995); or (b) the employer had a reasonable basis to contest the 
claim⎯the “fairly debatable” basis for delay.  See Christensen, 554 
N.W.2d at 260 (holding two-month delay to obtain employer’s own medical 
report reasonable under the circumstances).  
 
(4) For the purpose of applying section [10A.315], the benefits that are 
underpaid as well as late-paid benefits are subject to penalties, unless the 
employer establishes reasonable and probable cause or excuse.  
Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 237 (underpayment resulting from application 
of wrong wage base; in absence of excuse, commissioner required to 
apply penalty). 
   If we were to construe [section 10A.315] to permit the 
avoidance of penalty if any amount of compensation benefits 
are paid, the purpose of the penalty statute would be 
frustrated.  For these reasons, we conclude section 
[10A.315] is applicable when payment of compensation is 
not timely . . . or when the full amount of compensation is not 
paid. 
Id. 
 
(5) For purposes of determining whether there has been a delay, 
payments are “made” when (a) the check addressed to a claimant is 
mailed (Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 236; Kiesecker, 528 N.W.2d at 112), 
or (b) the check is delivered personally to the claimant by the employer or 
its workers’ compensation insurer.  Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 235.

(6) In determining the amount of penalty, the commissioner is to 
consider factors such as the length of the delay, the number of delays, the 
information available to the employer regarding the employee’s injury and 
wages, and the employer’s past record of penalties.  Robbennolt, 555 
N.W.2d at 238. 
(7) An employer’s bare assertion that a claim is “fairly debatable” does not 
make it so.  A fair reading of Christensen and Robbennolt, makes it clear 
that the employer must assert facts upon which the commissioner could 
reasonably find that the claim was “fairly debatable.”  See Christensen, 
554 N.W.2d at 260. 
Meyers v. Holiday Express Corp., 557 N.W.2d 502 (Iowa 1996).   
Weekly compensation payments are due at the end of the compensation week.  
Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d 229, 235. 
Penalty is not imposed for delayed interest payments.  Davidson v. Bruce, 594

When an employee’s claim for benefits is fairly debatable based on a good faith 
dispute over the employee’s factual or legal entitlement to benefits, an award of penalty 
benefits is not appropriate under the statute.  Whether the issue was fairly debatable 
turns on whether there was a disputed factual dispute that, if resolved in favor of the 
employer, would have supported the employer's denial of compensability.  Gilbert v. 
USF Holland, Inc., 637 N.W.2d 194 (Iowa 2001). 
The parties stipulated that the appropriate commencement date for PPD benefits 
in this case is March 19, 2024.  (Hearing Report, numbered paragraph 5(d))  However, 
defendants did not pay any PPD benefits until August 24, 2024. (CE6, p. 44) I find that 
the defendants paid $11,060.55 in PPD benefits on August 24, 2024.  Thus, there was a 
delay in the payment of PPD benefits of approximately 22 weeks.  I find that this delay 
is unreasonable.  I further find that a reason for the delay was not contemporaneously 
conveyed to Nate.  As such, under Iowa Code section 10A.315, penalty benefits shall 
be awarded.   
If the employer fails to show reasonable cause or excuse for the delay or denial, 
the commissioner shall impose a penalty in an amount up to 50 percent of the amount 
unreasonably delayed or denied.  Christensen v. Snap-on Tools Corp., 554 N.W.2d 254 
(Iowa 1996).  The factors to be considered in determining the amount of the penalty 
include the length of the delay, the number of delays, the information available to the 
employer and the employer’s past record of penalties.  Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 238.  
Considering these factors, I find that a penalty in the amount of three-thousand dollars 
($3,000.00) is appropriate in this case.
