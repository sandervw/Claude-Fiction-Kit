STATEMENT OF THE CASE

compensation benefits against Muscatine County, employer, and Iowa Municipalities

fully submitted as of December 19, 2025. The record consists of Joint Exhibits 1-10, 
Claimant’s Exhibits 1-6, Defendant employer and insurer’s Exhibits A-I, and the 
testimony of the claimant and Lieutenant Nick Doy.

Defendants’ primary argument is that claimant is not a credible witness and 
therefore her claims of CRPS must fail. “Most, if not all, of the diagnoses made by the 
providers in this case are based on Claimant’s subjective complaints rather than any 
objective medical evidence.” (Defendants’ Brief, page 12) In addition to the surveillance 
footage which defendants argue undermines claimant’s credibility, defendants point to 
other instances of inconsistency:  
1) Failure to report the significant activities captured on surveillance at her May 
29, 2024, emergency room visit;  
2) Reporting to the staff on the same date that she sustained a fracture of her 
right foot in February 2023, and underwent extensive treatment and repair; 
3) Testifying at hearing that the second nerve block administered by Dr. Miller 
improved her pain but in her physical therapy appointment of January 4, 
2024, she stated that she didn’t think the nerve block helped; 
4) Did not report her significant activities captured on surveillance to Dr. Motto, 
her personal care physician.  
In regards to the surveillance footage, the undersigned finds that the report either 
intentionally or unintentionally misrepresents the actual activity of claimant during the 
three hours captured on video. During those periods of time, claimant was seated for 

MCKITTRICK V. MUSCATINE COUNTY, IOWA 
Page 11 
 
much of the video, engaged in bending, standing and a small amount of walking. She 
also engaged in some lifting. These activities were all within the recommended activity 
range of Dr. Kari and Dr. Taylor. The failure to report these activities to healthcare 
providers such as the emergency room department on May 29, 2024, and Dr. Motto, are 
an oversight, but not sufficient to undermine claimant’s credibility. To claimant and to 
Drs. Taylor and Kari, these activities were not significant enough to change their 
opinions. Claimant has never maintained she is in a state of helplessness and her given 
work restrictions do not place her in a sedentary work position either. Claimant did not 
appear to lift more than 40 pounds. She sat and stood as needed for her comfort. She 
walked only short distances on uneven ground and engaged in no use of ladders or 
stair climbing.  
Claimant’s medical records indicate that a fracture was once contemplated but 
later ruled out, and her statement to her therapist that she did not believe the second 
nerve block was helpful was inconsistent with her statement at hearing. These 
statements, viewed holistically against her other consistent reports of pain and 
discomfort, are not sufficient to render her noncredible. Her MMPI results did not show 
signs of inauthenticity, and her functional complaints were not different than the 
activities she portrayed during surveillance when she was not aware she was being 
photographed and videotaped. It is found that claimant is a credible witness.  
CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury actually occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 
employment. Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 150 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. 
Dial Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309, 311 (Iowa 1996). The words “arising out of” refer to the 
cause or source of the injury. The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 
circumstances of the injury. 2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124, 128 (Iowa 
1995).  An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists 
between the injury and the employment. Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. The injury must 
be a rational consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely 
incidental to the employment. Koehler Elec. v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1, 3 (Iowa 2000); 
Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it 
happens within a period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may 
be when performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties 
or doing an activity incidental to them. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d at 150. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153

(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
A possibility of causation is not sufficient; a probability is necessary. Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996) (citing Holmes v. Bruce 
Motor Freight, Inc., 215 N.W.2d 296, 297 (Iowa 1974)). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability. 
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 
621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 
(Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
In arriving at the conclusions in this decision, Dr. Taylor’s opinions buttressed by 
Dr. Kari’s opinions are given greater weight and are consistent with the findings of Dr. 
Miller and Dr. Sundar before Dr. Sundar’s exposure to the surveillance documentation. 
Dr. Sundar did not view the video, but looked at a few photographs and read the 
narrative, arriving at the conclusion that claimant was not honest in her physical 
presentation to him. However, as previously discussed, the surveillance documentation 
did not accurately reflect the video.  
Dr. Koerner did watch the video surveillance and found that claimant did not walk 
with an antalgic gait, did not need an assistive device nor help from other people. She 
showed no grimacing of pain and was able to carry grocery bags without the use of a 
cart. She was able to walk, stoop and pick up and carry all objects normally.  Dr. 
Koerner blamed claimant’s pain complaints on her morbid obesity. He did not find that 
claimant was untruthful about her pain complaints, but rather CRPS was not the cause. 
Dr. Koerner’s post-surveillance opinions are given lower weight as they contradict the 
other doctors’ opinions and are primarily based on Dr. Koerner’s interpretation of the 
video surveillance which differs slightly from the undersigned’s interpretation. Claimant 
did walk with an antalgic gait, her face was not visible at all times, she sat for most of 
the three hours of video, and she did have help in carrying heavy objects while 
conducting the garage sale.  
Thus, the greater weight of the evidence is that claimant sustained an ankle 
injury on February 19, 2023, and then re-injured that same ankle, and together those 
two injuries led to a development of CRPS of the lower right extremity. There was 
objective evidence to support claimant’s subjective complaints of neuropathic pain and 
loss of function. She had a 4 degree temperature change in her right lower extremity

compared to the left according to Dr. Sundar, she had color change differences

Claimant argues that all CRPS injuries fall under the unscheduled whole body 
rubric. In Delaney, the Supreme Court of Iowa stated that an injury to a bodily system is 
not a per se whole body injury. Delaney v. Second Injury Fund of Iowa, 6 N.W.3d 714, 
720–21 (Iowa 2024) This is because a finding that “ any damage to the nervous system, 
vascular system, or skeletal system is unscheduled ... would largely do away with the 
statutory schedule altogether.” Nelson v. Artistic Waste Servs., Inc., Iowa Workers’ 
Comp. Comm'n No. 1304096, 2002 WL 32125502, at *5 (Sept. 4, 2002); see also 
Laguerre v. JBS USA Holdings, Inc., 2025 WL 3023022, at *4 (Iowa Ct. App., 2025) 
(holding that a skin injury was limited to the extremities rather than a whole body injury.) 
If a nervous system injury is limited to a worker’s extremity and there is nothing in 
the record to support the argument that the worker suffered from any systemic condition 
extending beyond the extremity, the injury is confined to a scheduled member. Delaney, 
6 N.W.3d at  721.  
In the present case, claimant presented evidence only that she had sustained a 
right lower extremity injury. The CRPS was confined to her right lower extremity as 
evidenced by the lack of CRPS symptomatology in the thigh. Her temperature change, 
lower extremity size differential, pain and sensitivity were confined to the lower right 
extremity. While CRPS is a nervous system injury, in this case it is limited to claimant’s 
right lower extremity.  
Because of this finding, claimant has sustained only a scheduled member loss. 
The Iowa Code dictates that the AMA Guides, Fifth Edition, loss of function finding shall 
be adopted. Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(x).   
Dr. Taylor assigned a 7 percent whole body loss based on Table 13-15. This 
chapter only allows for a whole body impairment rating and not extremity by extremity 
rating. In Laguerre, the court noted that the purpose of the Guides is to determine a 
whole body impairment. Laguerre v. JBS USA Holdings, Inc., 2025 WL 3023022, at *8. 
The Laguerre worker suffered a loss to an arm and a leg and pursuant to Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(t), simultaneous loss is compensated for 500 weeks but does not 
indicate whether it should be a whole body loss or individual extremity impairment. The 
deputy in Laguerre awarded a whole body loss based on a 500-week schedule. 
Correspondingly, because Dr. Taylor assigned a 7 percent whole body loss and that is 
the only loss contemplated by Table 13-15, a 7 percent loss is awarded utilizing the 
220-week leg schedule.

Claimant is also entitled to temporary total benefits from November 20, 2024,

Iowa Code section 85.34(1) provides that healing period benefits are payable to 
an injured employee who has suffered permanent partial disability until (1) the employee 
has returned to work; (2) it is medically indicated that significant improvement from the 
injury is not anticipated; or (3) the employee is medically capable of returning to 
employment substantially similar to the employment in which the employee was 
engaged at the time of injury, whichever occurs first.  The healing period can be 
considered the period during which there is a reasonable expectation of improvement of 
the disabling condition.  See Armstrong Tire & Rubber Co. v. Kubli, 312 N.W.2d 60, 65 
(Iowa Ct. App. 1981).  Healing period benefits can be interrupted or intermittent.  Teel v. 
McCord, 394 N.W.2d 405, 406 (Iowa 1986). 
The proper commencement date for permanent partial disability benefits is 
January 28, 2025, when claimant was returned to work following the placement of the 
spinal cord stimulator. 
Because Dr. Taylor determined that both injuries caused claimant’s CRPS, the 
later date is used as the starting point for calculating the appropriate rate based on the 
premise that an employer is responsible for the entire disability resulting from the 
combination of prior disability and present injury in the case of successive injuries. 
Venegas v. IBP, 638 N.W.2d 699 (Iowa 2002). There is no apportionment because Dr. 
Taylor was unable to determine which ankle injury resulted in CRPS, and defendants 
presented no apportionment claim at hearing. The burden of showing that disability is 
attributable to a preexisting condition is placed upon the defendant.  Where evidence to 
establish a proper apportionment is absent, the defendant is responsible for the entire 
disability that exists.  Bearce, 465 N.W.2d at 536-537; Sumner, 353 N.W.2d at 410-411. 
The parties stipulated the August 29, 2023 benefit rate was $621.44.  
Iowa Code section 85.39 allows a claimant to obtain an independent medical 
evaluation at the employer’s expense if an evaluation of permanent disability has been 
made by an employer-retained physician and that evaluation is deemed too low in the 
opinion of the claimant. Iowa Code section 85.39. Defendants retained expert Dr. 
Koerner to issue an evaluation report of the claimant. On June 27, 2024, Dr. Koerner 
opined claimant did not have any work-related impairment. Claimant obtained the report 
of Dr. Taylor on September 11, 2025. Therefore, claimant has met the criteria for 
reimbursement of Dr. Taylor’s IME. The fee presented in the amount of $4,990.00 is 
found to be reasonable and typical for fees charged by medical providers in the local 
area and is awarded in full.
