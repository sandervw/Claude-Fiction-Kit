This matter came before the Court on a Petition for Judicial Review from a 
final decision of the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commission.  A hearing was held 
on February 9, 2024. Petitioner, Jason Lee Tyer (“Tyer”), appeared through counsel 
Richard Schmidt.  Respondent, B-20 Auto, Inc. (“B-20 Auto”), appeared through 
counsel Brian Sciezinski. Upon review of the court file and applicable law, the Court 
enters the following order: 
I. 
BACKGROUND FACTS AND PROCEDURAL POSTURE 
Jason Tyer was fifty years of age at the time of the arbitration hearing, held 
May 27, 2022. He obtained his welding certification in 1987 and achieved his GED in 
2013. B-20 is a salvage yard for car parts located outside of Clear Lake, Iowa. Gene 
Baker (“Baker”) is the president of B-20, and there are no other corporate officers. 
Baker classifies every B-20 worker as an independent contract and gives them 1099 
forms for tax purposes.   
Tyer had acquired car parts from B-20 as a customer, but never considered 
working there until he saw a job posting. After completing an online application, Tyer 
had a meeting with Baker and began working for B-20 afterwards. Baker offered Tyer 
work as an independent contractor “spinning tires.” Which entailed removing rubber 
wheels from aluminum rims so the aluminum rims could be sold. Baker hired Tyer 
solely for this job, and did not hire Tyer to pull parts from vehicles for customers. 
Baker did not set Tyer’s schedule, agreed to pay Tyer one dollar per tire, and paid in 
cash.

Tyer sustained an injury on July 10, 2018, while removing a part from a vehicle 
in the scrap yard. Baker testified Tyer was not directed to remove parts, and if he did

at the time, but he nonetheless used the loader to lift the vehicle up. Something went 
wrong, and Tyer crashed the loader, knocking himself unconscious. 
The claim went to an arbitration hearing on May 27, 2022, and the case was 
considered fully submitted on June 24, 2022. The Deputy Workers’ Compensation

the deputy commissioner found Tyer was not a credible witness, and that Baker was 
credible in his testimony. Tyer appealed the Deputies’ Decision, and the Workers’ 
Compensation Commissioner affirmed the Decision in its entirety on February 9, 
2023. Tyer filed a Petition for Judicial Review on March 8, 2023. 
II. 
STANDARD OF REVIEW 
Final decisions rendered by the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commission are 
reviewed under Iowa Code Chapter 17A, the Iowa Administrative Procedures Act.1 
“Under the Act, [a court] may only interfere with the commissioner’s decision if it is 
erroneous under one of the grounds enumerated in the statute, and a party’s 
substantial rights have been prejudiced.”2 The standard of review depends on the 
type of error alleged by the Petitioner.3 
If the alleged error is one of fact, the standard of review is whether the findings 
are supported by substantial evidence.4 “[A] reviewing court can only disturb those 
factual findings if they are ‘not supported by substantial evidence in the record before 
the court when that record is reviewed as a whole.’”5 Additionally, in workers’ 
compensation cases, factual questions are “delegated by the legislature to the

[C]ommissioner.”6 Consequently, the Court does not apply a “scrutinizing analysis” 
to factual findings of the Commissioner, but only reverses the Commissioner’s 
findings if they are not supported by substantial evidence.7 
“Evidence is substantial if a reasonable person would find the evidence 
adequate to reach the same conclusion.”8 The Court is “not to determine whether the 
evidence supports a different finding; rather, our task is to determine whether 
substantial evidence, viewing the record as a whole, supports the findings actually 
made.”9 
If the claimed error is in the ultimate conclusion reached, “then the challenge 
is to the agency’s application of the law to the facts.”10 In workers’ compensation 
cases, “[t]he application of the law to the facts is also an enterprise vested in the 
[C]ommissioner.” As such, this Court “reverse[s] only if the [C]ommissioner's 
application was irrational, illogical, or wholly unjustifiable.”11

B. INJURY AROSE OUT OF COURSE OF EMPLOYMENT 
Pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.3(1), “[e]mployers are required to 
compensate employees for ‘personal injuries sustained by an employee arising out of 
and in the course of the employment.’”16  “[A]n injured employee has the burden of 
proving by a preponderance of the evidence that his injuries arose out of and in the 
course of his employment.”17  “An injury ‘arises out of’ the employment if there is a 
causal connection between the employment and the injury, and the injury occurs ‘in 
the course of’ the employment when the injury and the employment coincide with 
regard to time, place, and circumstances.”18  More specifically, “[a]n injury occurs in

fact is presented by the operative events that give rise to the injury.

of the law to the facts were not irrational, illogical, or wholly unjustifiable.
