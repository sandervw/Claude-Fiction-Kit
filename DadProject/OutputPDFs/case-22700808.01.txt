STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Spencer Klinge, claimant, filed a petition for arbitration against Anamosa State 
Penitentiary, as the employer, and the State of Iowa.  This case came before the 
undersigned for an arbitration hearing on November 2, 2023, via Zoom. 
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the hearing. On the 
hearing report, the parties entered into numerous stipulations.  Those stipulations were 
accepted and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be made 
or discussed.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations. 
The evidentiary record includes Claimant’s Exhibit 1 and Defendants’ Exhibits A 
through G.  All exhibits were received without objection. 
Claimant testified on his own behalf.  No other witnesses were called to testify.  
The evidentiary record closed at the conclusion of the arbitration hearing. 
All parties served their post-hearing briefs on or before November 27, 2023, at 
which time this case was deemed fully submitted to the undersigned. 
ISSUES 
 
The parties submitted the following disputed issues for resolution: 
1. The extent of claimant's entitlement to permanent partial disability benefits for a 
scheduled member injury to the left leg.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned, having considered all of the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
Spencer Klinge is a 31-year-old individual who sustained a stipulated injury to his 
left foot on September 26, 2020.  On the date of injury, Klinge was working as a 
corrections officer for Anamosa State Penitentiary. (Hearing Transcript, page 10)  Klinge 
was called to the cell house to assist in detaining a combative inmate. (Hr. Tr. pp. 10-
11)  In the course of taking the inmate down to the ground, Klinge sustained his left foot 
injury. (Hr. Tr., p. 11)   
Later that day, Klinge presented to a local Emergency Department and obtained 
x-rays of his left foot. (See Exhibit B, p. 8; Exhibit 1, p. 2)  Unfortunately, the evidentiary 
record is underwhelming with respect to the medical records, so the majority of the 
undersigned’s review comes from the medical record summaries provided in the expert 
reports.  In any event, the initial x-rays revealed no acute fractures or dislocation, and 
Klinge was diagnosed with a left foot sprain. (See Ex. 1, p. 2)   
An MRI dated November 4, 2020, revealed an acute non-displaced fracture of 
the lateral margin of the navicular. (See Ex. 1, p. 4)  Following review of the MRI report, 
Klinge was provided with a walking boot and referred to a podiatrist.  
David Hemmes, D.P.M. conducted an initial evaluation of Klinge on November 
10, 2020. (See Ex. 1, p. 4)  Dr. Hemmes recommended Klinge use crutches with his 
boot to minimize weight bearing on the left foot. (See Ex. 1, p. 4)   
Between November 10, 2020, and December 15, 2020, Klinge was limited to 
working in a seated/sedentary position. (See Ex. 1, pp. 4-5)  On December 15, 2020, 
Dr. Hemmes recommended a one-week trial of lessened restrictions, followed by a 
return to normal work activities. (See Ex. 1, p. 5)  On February 9, 2021, Klinge reported 
no symptoms in his left foot and Dr. Hemmes released him from care. (See Ex. 1, p. 5)  
Klinge did not present for any medical treatment related to his left foot between 
February 10, 2021, and March 9, 2022. 
It was later discovered that Klinge had a fragment of bone that did not heal 
properly located on the dorsal aspect of the foot.  The fragment caused pain with any 
movement or twisting of the foot. (Exhibit G, p. 33)  Dr. Hemmes recommended surgical 
intervention.   
On August 30, 2022, Dr. Hemmes performed an excision of the bone fragment 
from the displaced navicular fracture. (Ex. G, pp. 33-35)  He released Klinge to return to 
work without restrictions on September 26, 2022. (Exhibit A, p. 2)   
 
Klinge asserts that Dr. Hemmes discovered another fracture during the August 
30, 2022, surgery. (Hr. Tr., p. 17)  This additional fracture is not documented in the

operative report or any other medical record in evidence.  According to Klinge, Dr. 
Hemmes said the fracture could be addressed with surgical intervention; however, he 
was unwilling to perform the same as the fracture was too close to an artery. (Hr. Tr., 
pp. 17-18)  I have a difficult time accepting Klinge’s testimony in this regard.  I find it 
highly unlikely a new fracture or condition discovered during surgery would be excluded 
from the operative report and all subsequent medical records.  As such, I do not find 
Klinge’s testimony regarding a second fracture credible.   
Klinge presented for his final appointment with Dr. Hemmes on December 6, 
2022.  He was reportedly doing well and Dr. Hemmes instructed him to return on an as-
needed basis for treatment. (See Ex. 1, p. 6)  In closing, Dr. Hemmes recommended 
that Klinge wear orthotics and a supportive boot.  He also expressed that Klinge would 
likely always have some discomfort due to the fracture. (See Ex. 1, p. 6) 
Outside of independent medical examinations, Klinge has not presented for any 
treatment for his left leg injury since December 2022. (Hr. Tr., p. 21) 
Defendants produced a series of questions to Dr. Hemmes on July 13, 2023. (Ex. 
A, p. 5)  Dr. Hemmes responded on or about July 24, 2023. (Id.)  Dr. Hemmes 
confirmed that he released Klinge to return to work without restrictions; however, he 
could not provide whether Klinge had reached maximum medical improvement or 
sustained any permanent impairment as a result of his injury. (Id.) 
Dr. Hemmes produced a follow-up correspondence addressing defendants’ 
request for an impairment rating on August 24, 2023. (Ex. A, p. 7)  The correspondence 
provides, “I am not familiar giving official disability percentage qualification and would 
recommend referral for this assessment.” (Id.)  Despite his inability to provide an 
impairment assessment, Dr. Hemmes noted that Klinge will continue to experience 
stiffness to the rear foot, as well as pain with extended standing, walking, and use of the 
left foot.  He provided, “This will likely be permanent and affect his activity[.]” (Id.) 
Defendants ultimately sought a records review and impairment assessment from 
Joseph Chen, M.D. (Exhibit B)  Dr. Chen opined that Klinge should have been placed at 
maximum medical improvement on September 26, 2022, as he did not receive any 
substantial additional medical treatment thereafter. (Ex. B, p. 10)  With respect to 
permanent impairment, Dr. Chen opined that Klinge sustained at least a mild cavus 
midfoot deformity from having 1 cm of his navicular bone resected following the non-
united navicular fracture. (Ex. B, p. 11)  He assigned 2 percent impairment of the left 
lower extremity according to Table 17-33 on page 547 of the AMA Guides to the 
Evaluation of Permanent Impairment, Fifth Edition. (Id.; Ex. F, p. 32) 
In response, Klinge sought an independent medical evaluation with Sunil Bansal, 
M.D. (Ex. 1, p. 1)  Dr. Bansal carried out the physical examination of Klinge on July 7, 
2023. (Id.)  Unlike Dr. Chen, Dr. Bansal utilized Tables 17-7 and 17-8 of the AMA 
Guides, Fifth Edition and assigned 12 percent left lower extremity impairment for loss of 
dorsiflexion and 4/5 weakness. (Ex. 1, p. 10)

At this juncture, it is worth noting that Klinge was involved in a motor vehicle 
accident at some point in 2023. (Hr. Tr., p. 22)  The incident is detailed in Dr. Bansal’s 
report.  The report provides: 
He had another x-ray of his left foot recently due to an unrelated incident.  
While driving he was struck by a motorcyclist, causing him to total his 
vehicle.  He was concerned about his foot, and wanted to be sure that 
nothing had changed.  After comparing the recent images with the previous 
ones, the doctor confirmed that there had been no changes. 
(Ex. 1, p. 7)  At hearing, it was discovered that Dr. Bansal’s rendition of events was not 
accurate.  According to Klinge, he was struck by a car while riding his motorcycle. (Hr. 
Tr., p. 26)  His motorcycle was totaled as a result of the motor vehicle accident. (Hr. Tr., 
p. 22)   
Klinge has not presented for any additional treatment related to his left foot since 
he was released by Dr. Hemmes on December 6, 2022. (Hr. Tr., p. 21)  He continues to 
work for Anamosa State Penitentiary, albeit in a new position performing maintenance 
work.  He does not operate under any restrictions. (Hr. Tr., p. 25)  It does not appear as 
though the work injury has significantly impacted the way in which Klinge performs his 
job. 
When considering the impairment ratings in the context of the AMA Guides to the 
Evaluation of Permanent Impairment, Fifth Edition, as well as claimant's described 
ongoing symptoms, I find Dr. Bansal's impairment rating to be more reasonable and 
consistent with the described symptoms.  I acknowledge but do not share defendants’ 
concern with respect to the range of motion measurements collected by Dr. Bansal.  Dr. 
Bansal is the only expert physician to conduct a physical examination of Klinge.  In this 
respect, Dr. Bansal was in the best position – outside of Dr. Hemmes – to assign 
impairment.  I accept Dr. Bansal’s impairment rating as an accurate estimate of the 
anatomical and functional loss related to the left leg injury.  Therefore, I find that 
claimant sustained twelve percent lower extremity impairment as a result of the 
September 26, 2020, work injury. 
CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The sole issue to be addressed in this decision is the extent of claimant's 
entitlement to permanent partial disability benefits for a scheduled member injury to the 
left foot/ankle. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa

Page 5 
 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony.  The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question.  The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances.  The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke's Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc, v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); 
Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995); Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods. Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994).   
Under the Iowa Workers' Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is 
compensated either for a loss or loss of use of a scheduled member under Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(a)-(u) or for loss of earning capacity under section 85.34(2)(v).  The 
extent of scheduled member disability benefits to which an injured worker is entitled is 
determined by using the functional method.  Functional disability is “limited to the loss of 
the physiological capacity of the body or body part.”  Mortimer v. Fruehauf Corp., 502 
N.W.2d 12, 15 (Iowa 1993); Sherman v. Pella Corp., 576 N.W.2d 312 (Iowa 1998).   
In all cases of permanent partial disability described in paragraphs “a” through 
“u”, or paragraph “v” when determining functional disability and not loss of earning 
capacity, the extent of loss or percentage of permanent impairment shall be determined 
solely by utilizing the guides to the evaluation of permanent impairment, published by 
the American medical association, as adopted by the workers' compensation 
commissioner by rule pursuant to chapter 17A.  Lay testimony or agency expertise shall 
not be utilized in determining loss or percentage of permanent impairment pursuant to 
paragraphs “a” through “u”, or paragraph “v” when determining functional disability and 
not loss of earning capacity.  Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(x). 
Having found the twelve percent lower extremity impairment rating offered by Dr. 
Bansal to be most accurate and convincing, I conclude that claimant is entitled to an 
award of permanent partial disability benefits equivalent to twelve percent of the left 
lower extremity.  The Iowa legislature has established a 220-week schedule for leg 
injuries.  Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(p).  Claimant is entitled to an award of permanent 
partial disability benefits equivalent to the proportional loss of his leg.  Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(w). 
Twelve percent of 220 weeks is equal to 26.4 weeks.  Claimant is, therefore, 
entitled to an award of twenty-six point four weeks of permanent partial disability 
benefits against defendants.  Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(p), (w).

ORDER 
THEREFORE, IT IS ORDERED:
