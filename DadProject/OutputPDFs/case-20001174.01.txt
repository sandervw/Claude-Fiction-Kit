Ann Marie French, claimant, filed a petition seeking workers’ compensation 
benefits from Rockwell Collins, Inc., employer, and National Union Fire Insurance Co. of 
Pittsburg, PA, insurance carrier, defendants.  The hearing was held on January 23, 
2023.  Pursuant to an order from the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commissioner, this 
case was heard via videoconference using Zoom with all parties and the court reporter 
appearing remotely.   
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered various stipulations.  Those 
stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration decision and 
no factual issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised or discussed in this 
decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.  
Ann Marie French was the only witness to testify live at the trial. The evidentiary 
record also includes joint exhibits 1-11, claimant’s amended exhibits 1-12, and 
defendants’ amended exhibits A-J.  Prior to the hearing date, defendants filed a motion 
to continue the hearing, claiming they were unable to secure an independent medical 
examination to determine the compensability of claimant’s alleged mental health 
condition prior to the hearing date.  Claimant resisted.  On January 12, 2023, the 
undersigned issued an order denying defendants’ request for a continuance.  On that 
same date, defendants filed a motion to reconsider or in the alternative keep the record 
open to receive defendants’ IME report after the hearing date.  Defendants’ motion to 
reconsider was denied.  At the start of the hearing, defendants renewed their request to 
continue the hearing and/or to hold the record open for their IME report.  The motion to

FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned, having considered all the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds as follows: 
At the time of the hearing the claimant, Ann Marie French (hereinafter “French”), 
was 44 years old. (Hearing Transcript, p. 32).  French graduated from high school in 
1996. (Claimant Exhibit 7, depo p. 7).  Following high school, she took some courses in 
swine production management at Kirkwood Community College. (Id.).  
Rockwell Collins (hereinafter “Rockwell”) hired French in 1998.  (Tr., p. 32).  At 
the time of the hearing in this matter, she was still employed by Rockwell as a lead 
mentor in the bargaining unit. (Id.; Cl Ex. 7, depo pp. 8-9).  She started this position in 
approximately June of 2022. (Cl Ex. 7, depo p. 9).  As a lead mentor, French trains and 
supervises new hires. (Tr., pp. 33-34).  This requires her to perform several different 
jobs, including assembly operator, 195D, repair operator, sell operator, and inspector. 
(Id. at 32-33).  French voluntarily bid into this position.  It is a job only open to 
experienced employees with certifications for the inspector repair operator position and 
other related assembly positions. (Id. at 77-78).  French received a higher labor grade 
and pay raise with the position. (Cl Ex. 7, depo p. 9).  French performs this job without 
work restrictions or accommodations. (Id. at 10).  She also works part-time as the 
treasurer for the plant union, IBEW Local 1362. (Id. at 12-13).  
 
French sustained stipulated work injuries to her bilateral upper extremities on 
December 9, 2019. (See Hearing Report).  On the hearing report, French also asserted 
a mental health sequela from the December 9, 2019 date of injury. (Id.).  As the time of 
the hearing, defendants were unable to confirm or deny a causal connection between 
claimant’s mental health conditions and the work injury. (Tr., pp. 13-21).  However, after

Out of the permanent impairment opinions provided, I find Dr. Taylor’s to be the 
most persuasive. While the opinions are very similar, Dr. Taylor assigned additional 
impairment for sensory deficits to French’s right ulnar nerve—assigning her a grade 2 
sensory deficit rather than grade 3 deficit, and he used a midpoint modifier of 70 percent 
rather than the lower modifier of 61 percent to calculate her bilateral median nerve 
sensory deficits. (Cl Ex. 1, pp. 18-19).  At their core, these determinations ask an expert 
to assess the claimant’s degree of sensory loss based upon his or her ongoing

symptoms and daily loss of function.  While these factors are subjective, I find the 
hearing evidence supports Dr. Taylor’s conclusions.  His sensory evaluation took place 
in October 2022, while Dr. Pardubsky relied upon an occupational therapy evaluation 
performed by someone else in May 2020 for his rating.  Additionally, the occupational 
therapy evaluation is not in the hearing record, so it is not possible to compare it with 
the ongoing symptoms French testified to at hearing or her more recent sensory exams. 
Finally, French has consistently testified that ongoing pain and decreased sensation in 
her hands prevents her from performing a number of daily activities.  This testimony 
lines up with the description of sensory deficit and/or pain contained in Grade 2 of Table 
16-10 of the AMA Guides.  Dr. Taylor’s impairment ratings are adopted.

The parties have a rate disagreement.  They agree French was single and 
entitled to three exemptions at the time of the injury but disagree about her correct 
average weekly earnings. (Hearing Report).  On the hearing report, French claimed her 
average weekly wage should be $1,231.71. (Hearing Report; Cl Ex. 10, p. 118). 
However, in her post-hearing brief, she argues her average weekly wage is even 
higher—$1,250.00. (Claimant’s Post-Hearing Brief, p. 10).  Defendants claim French’s 
average weekly wage is $1,224.10. (Hearing Report; Ex. G, p. 45).  The difference in 
these amounts comes down to two weeks of earnings, which defendants claim are non-
representative and should be excluded. (Defendants’ Post-Hearing Brief, pp. 15-16).  
Those weeks are September 20, 2019, and October 25, 2019. (Id.). In her brief, French 
concedes that the week of October 25, 2019, should be excluded from the agency’s 
calculation.  (Claimant’s Post-Hearing Brief, p. 8).  That leaves the week of September 
20, 2019, through September 26, 2019.  (Ex. G, p. 45).  Defendants claim French’s 
wages were unusually high that week and do not accurately reflect her customary 
earnings, and therefore should be excluded by the agency. (Defendants’ Post-Hearing 
Brief, p. 16).  Defendants’ rate calculation indicates her total wages for that week were 
$1,451.08, which included $174.53 for overtime and $110.23 for double time hours paid 
at a straight rate. (Ex. G, p. 45).  French claims the overtime and double time hours are 
paid pursuant to a union contract. (Claimant’s Post-Hearing Brief, p. 10).  This assertion 
is supported by the evidence.  French’s exhibits included pages from Rockwell’s 
collective bargaining agreement with IBEW Local 1362. (Cl Ex. 10, pp. 120-122).  These 
pages indicate that Rockwell agreed to pay double time for work performed on Sundays 
and/or holidays, and for consecutive work in excess of 12 hours. (Id. at 120-121).  They

agreed to pay overtime for work performed in excess of eight hours a day and forty 
hours a week, and for work on Saturdays, and/or performed outside the employee’s 
regularly scheduled hours.  (Id.).   
Defendants do not argue that overtime and/or double time pay should be 
excluded from French’s calculation altogether as a premium; they just state that the 
special pay French received the week of September 20, 2019, was not representative of 
her customary earnings.  However, defendants’ calculation includes many weeks with

and earned $284.76 in combined overtime and double time pay at the straight rate. (Id.).  
Neither party provided any testimony about French’s customary overtime and/or double 
time pay at Rockwell, but defendants did submit a payment report showing how much 
overtime and double time pay French received from March 30, 2019, through December

overtime pay and occasionally received double time pay; it shows 36 paychecks that 
included overtime pay and 5 with double time.  (Id.).  Given this, I find it was customary 
for French to receive overtime and double time pay.  While the amount of overtime pay 
French received that pay period was a little higher than normal, the increase was not of 
such magnitude as to make her total earnings for that week unrepresentative of her 
customary total earnings, as French sometimes worked more than 38 hours per week

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 
employment.  Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. Dial 
Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309 (Iowa 1996).  The words “arising out of” refer to the cause or 
source of the injury.  The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 
circumstances of the injury.  2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124 (Iowa 1995).  
An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists between the 
injury and the employment.  Miedema, 551 N.W.2d 309.  The injury must be a rational 
consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely incidental to 
the employment.  Koehler Electric v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1 (Iowa 2000); Miedema, 551 
N.W.2d 309.  An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it happens within a 
period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may be when 
performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties or doing 
an activity incidental to them.  Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 

FRENCH V. ROCKWELL COLLINS, INC. 
Page 11 
 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony.  The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question.  The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances.  The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); 
Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995).  Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994).  Unrebutted expert medical 
testimony cannot be summarily rejected.  Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 
N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 1994). 
When an expert's opinion is based upon an incomplete or incorrect history, it is 
not necessarily binding on the commissioner or the court. It is then to be weighed, 
together with other facts and circumstances, the ultimate conclusion being for the finder 
of the fact.  Musselman v. Central Telephone Company, 154 N.W.2d 128, 133 (Iowa 
1967); Bodish v. Fischer, Inc., 257 Iowa 516, 522, 133 N.W.2d 867, 870 (1965).  The 
commissioner as trier of fact has the duty to determine the credibility of the witnesses 
and to weigh the evidence, together with the other disclosed facts and circumstances, 
and then to accept or reject the opinion.  Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Casualty Co., 
526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995). 
Under the Iowa Workers' Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is 
compensated either for a loss or loss of use of a scheduled member under Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(a)-(u) or for loss of earning capacity under section 85.34(2)(v). The 
extent of scheduled member disability benefits to which an injured worker is entitled is 
determined by using the functional method. Functional disability is “limited to the loss of 
the physiological capacity of the body or body part.” Mortimer v. Fruehauf Corp., 502 
N.W.2d 12, 15 (Iowa 1993); Sherman v. Pella Corp., 576 N.W.2d 312 (Iowa 1998).  On 
the hearing report, the parties stipulated that French suffered compensable injuries to 
her bilateral upper extremities. (See Hearing Report).  Thus, her permanent impairment 
for those injuries must be determined under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(m).  See 
Bridgestone Americas, Inc. v. Anderson, 4 N.W.3d 676, 684 (Iowa 2024) (stating 
cumulative injuries caused by “decades of work-related wear and tear” are not injuries 
“caused by a single accident,” even if they are adjudicated under the same injury date).  
Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(m) limits compensation for an arm to two hundred and fifty 
weeks.  Iowa Code § 85.34(2)(m) (2019).   
In all cases of permanent partial disability described in paragraphs “a” through “t”, 
or paragraph “u” when determining functional disability and not loss of earning capacity,

the extent of loss or percentage of permanent impairment shall be determined solely by

Ratings for upper extremity peripheral nerve injuries such as French’s are 
covered by Chapter 16 of the AMA Guides.  See AMA Guides, p. 480. According to 
Section 16.5a of the Guides, evaluation of impairment caused by peripheral nerve 
disorders or injuries is based on the anatomic distribution and severity of loss of function 
resulting from (1) sensory deficits or pain and (2) motor deficits and loss of power. Id.  
The Guides instructs the evaluator to calculate impairment by multiplying the grade of 
severity of the sensory deficit and/or of the motor deficit by the respective maximum 
upper extremity impairment value resulting from sensory and/or motor deficits in each 
nerve structure involved. Id. at 481.  Table 16-15 provides maximum impairment values 
for sensory and motor deficits to the major peripheral nerves. Id. Table 16-10 contains 
lists of the various impairment grade classifications possible for sensory deficits or pain 
from peripheral nerve disorders and injuries. Id.  Both Dr. Taylor and Dr. Pardubsky 
cited to Tables 16-10 and 16-15 in their impairment reports.  Additionally, both agree on 
the maximum impairment values provided by Table 16-15. Their difference of opinion 
concerns Table 16-10.   
Table 16-10 of the AMA Guides, asks the evaluator to determine the severity of a 
claimant’s sensory deficits and/or pain from a peripheral nerve injury. See AMA Guides, 
p. 482.  The grade classifications range from 0 to 5 with 0 being the most impaired and 
5 the least. Id.  Each grade is then assigned a range of acceptable modifiers, with a 
grade 5 being assigned 0 percent impairment and grade 0 assigned 100 percent 
impairment. Id. Dr. Taylor gave French’s right ulnar nerve injury a grade 2 classification; 
Dr. Pardubsky gave it a grade 3.  To receive a grade 2 classification, a claimant must 
demonstrate decreased protective sensibility with abnormal sensations or moderate 
pain which may prevent some daily activities. Id.  As noted above, the hearing evidence 
shows that French’s ability to consciously appreciate “pain, temperature, or pressure 
before tissue damage results” to her palm and fingers is impaired.  Id. at 483. She 
testified that she is no longer able to feel water temperatures, which has caused her to 
burn herself.  She also testified that she does not have enough sensation in her fingers 
to feel sharp objects and has cut herself while doing dishes and cooking.  This is 
unrebutted evidence of decreased protective sensibility.  She also testified to ongoing 
pain and gave a list of activities that she can no longer perform due to her nerve 
damage including opening twist doorknobs, putting on undergarments, and driving long 
distances.  Based on the evidence in the record, Dr. Taylor correctly assigned French a 
grade 2 classification for her ulnar nerve on Table 16-10.  
The AMA Guides states an “examiner must use clinical judgement to estimate the 
appropriate percentage of sensory deficits or pain within the range of values shown for

French also asserts a claim for penalty benefits.  Specifically, French claims she 
is entitled to penalty benefits because Dr. Pardubsky issued his permanent impairment 
rating on May 13, 2022, but defendants did not issue any permanent indemnity benefits 
until August 18, 2022—over thirteen weeks after Dr. Pardubsky’s rating was issued.  
(Claimant’s Post-Hearing Brief, pp. 10-11).  French requests penalty benefits for that 
thirteen-week delay.  (Id.).  In their brief, defendants argue no penalty benefits are 
warranted.  (Defendants’ Post-Hearing Brief, pp. 13-14).  They contend French’s 
counsel did not make them aware of the missing benefits until August 9, 2022, and they 
promptly issued the requested benefits after that. (Id.).   
Penalty benefits are created by Iowa Code section 86.13 (now Code section 
10A.315),2 which provides two clear prerequisites before the benefits can be imposed: 
(1) “a delay in payment or termination of benefits” that occurs (2) “without reasonable or 
probable cause or excuse.” Iowa Code § 10A.315. When the prerequisites have been 
met, the Iowa Code instructs the commissioner “shall award” penalty benefits “up to fifty 
percent of the amount of benefits that were unreasonably delayed or denied.” Id. “To 
receive a penalty benefit award under section 10A.315, the claimant must first establish 
a delay in the payment of benefits.” Schadendorf v. Snap–On Tools Corp., 757 N.W.2d 
330, 334 (Iowa 2008).  “The burden then shifts to the employer to prove a reasonable 
cause or excuse for the delay.” Id. at 334–35.  Weekly compensation payments are due 
at the end of the compensation week. Robbennolt v. Snap-on Tools Corp., 555 N.W.2d 
229 (Iowa 1996).  French has met her burden to prove a delay in payment under Iowa 
Code section 10A.315; the burden now shifts to defendants to prove there was a 
reasonable cause or excuse for the delay.  
In Christensen v. Snap-on Tools Corp., 554 N.W.2d 254 (Iowa 1996), the Iowa 
Supreme Court declared, 
A reasonable cause or excuse exists if either (1) the delay was necessary 
for the insurer to investigate the claim or (2) the employer had a reasonable 
basis to contest the employee's entitlement to benefits. A “reasonable basis” 
for denial of the claim exists if the claim is “fairly debatable.”  
Id. at 260.   The reasonableness of the employer's actions “does not turn on whether the 
employer was right. The issue is whether there was a reasonable basis for the 
employer's position that no benefits were owing.” Keystone Nursing Care Ctr. v. 
Craddock, 705 N.W.2d 299, 307-308 (Iowa 2005). 
                                                                 
2 Iowa Code section 10A.315 was previously codified as Iowa Code section 86.13.  No substantive changes 
were made to the statute when transferred from section 86.13 to section 10A.315.  References to the current location 
of this statute are substituted to avoid confusion.

In their post-hearing brief, defendants seem to argue penalty benefits should not 
be awarded because their delay was unintentional and of short duration.  They, 
however, make no claims or arguments that French was not owed permanent benefits 
in May 2022; however, that is what is required under the statutory language and 
accompanying case law.  Given this, I find defendants have not offered sufficient 
evidence of a reasonable cause or excuse for their delay in paying French’s 
permanency benefits. The purpose of penalty benefits is both punishment for 
unreasonable conduct and deterrence of similar conduct in future cases. Robbennolt, 
555 N.W.2d at 237.  The agency has discretion to determine the amount of the penalty 
imposed with a maximum penalty of 50 percent of the amount of the delayed or denied 
benefits. Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 261.  In determining the amount of the penalty, the 
agency shall consider such factors as the length of the delay, the number of the delays, 
the information available to the employer regarding the employee's injuries and wages, 
and the prior penalties imposed against the employer under section 10A.315.  Meyers v. 
Holiday Express Corp., 557 N.W.2d 502, 505 (Iowa 1996).  Taking into consideration the 
above factors, I find a penalty of $500.00 is appropriate under these circumstances.
