Alejandro Wickersham, claimant, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from Mbrico, LLC, employer, and Brickstreet Mutual Insurance 
Company, insurance carrier, as defendants.  A hearing was held on November 1, 2024.  
Pursuant to an order from the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commissioner, this case 
was heard via videoconference using Zoom with all parties and the court reporter 
appearing remotely.   
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  Those 
stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration decision and 
no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised or discussed 
in this decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.  
Alejandro Wickersham was the only witness to testify live at the trial.  The 
evidentiary record also includes joint exhibits 1-5; claimant’s exhibits 1-10; and 
defendants’ exhibits A-H. All exhibits were received into the record without objection.  
The parties submitted post-hearing briefs on December 3, 2024.  Attached to 
defendants’ post-hearing brief were two additional exhibits, labeled AA and BB. Both 
exhibits were portions of claimant’s deposition transcript.   
On December 4, 2024, claimant filed an objection/motion to exclude the exhibits 
attached to defendants’ post-hearing brief.  The motion alleged the exhibits were untimely 
and prejudicial. On December 6, 2024, defendants filed a response to claimant’s

ISSUES 
The parties identified the following disputed issues on the hearing report: 
1. Whether claimant sustained an injury that arose out of and in the course of 
his employment with Mbrico, LLC, on February 24, 2023.  
 
2. Whether the alleged injury resulted in any permanent disability; and if so, 
 
3. The nature and extent of claimant’s entitlement to permanent disability 
benefits. 
 
4. Whether the claimant is entitled to additional temporary disability or healing 
period benefits because of the alleged injury. 
 
5. Whether claimant is entitled to payment of the medical expenses in claimant’s 
exhibits 8 and 9. 
 
6. Whether claimant is entitled to alternate medical care. 
 
7. Whether claimant is entitled to reimbursement for an independent medical 
examination pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.39. 
 
8. Assessment of costs.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 
employment.  Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 150 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. 
Dial Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309, 311 (Iowa 1996).  The words “arising out of” refer to the 
cause or source of the injury.  The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 
circumstances of the injury.  2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124, 128 (Iowa 

WICKERSHAM V. MBRICO, LLC 
Page 9 
 
 
1995).  An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists 
between the injury and the employment.  Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311.  The injury must 
be a rational consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely 
incidental to the employment.  Koehler Electric v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1, 3 (Iowa 2000); 
Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311.  An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it 
happens within a period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may 
be when performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties 
or doing an activity incidental to them.  Ciha, 552 N.W.2d at 150. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony.  The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question.  The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances.  The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410, 420 
(Iowa 2001); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995).  Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994).  Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected.  Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
When an expert's opinion is based upon an incomplete or incorrect history, it is 
not necessarily binding on the commissioner or the court. It is then to be weighed, 
together with other facts and circumstances, the ultimate conclusion being for the finder 
of the fact.  Musselman v. Central Telephone Company, 154 N.W.2d 128, 133 (Iowa 
1967); Bodish v. Fischer, Inc., 257 Iowa 516, 522, 133 N.W.2d 867, 870 (1965).  The 
commissioner as trier of fact has the duty to determine the credibility of the witnesses 
and to weigh the evidence, together with the other disclosed facts and circumstances, 
and then to accept or reject the opinion.  Dunlavey, 526 N.W.2d at 853. 
While a claimant is not entitled to compensation for the results of a preexisting 
injury or disease, its mere existence at the time of a subsequent injury is not a defense. 
Rose v. John Deere Ottumwa Works, 247 Iowa 900, 908, 76 N.W.2d 756, 760-61 
(1956). If the claimant had a preexisting condition or disability that is materially 
aggravated, accelerated, worsened or lighted up so that it results in disability, claimant 
is entitled to recover. Nicks v. Davenport Produce Co., 254 Iowa 130, 135, 115 N.W.2d

812, 815 (1962); Yeager v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co., 253 Iowa 369, 374-75, 112

There are three separate causation opinions in the hearing record—Dr. Crosmer, 
Dr. Wojciehoski, and Dr. Bansal. Dr. Crosmer and Dr. Bansal opined that Wickersham’s 
bilateral upper-extremity conditions were causally related to his work for Mbrico. Dr. 
Wojciehoski found his upper extremity conditions unrelated to his employment at 
Mbrico.  Of these opinions, I find Dr. Crosmer’s to be the most convincing.  She treated 
Wickersham, so she had a complete picture of his treatment.  She also personally 
reviewed his objective medical testing, his MRI and EMG testing results, prior to issuing 
her opinion in this matter.  According to the IME reports in this case, neither Dr. Bansal, 
nor Dr. Wojciehoski had copies of Wickersham’s objective testing.  Additionally, Dr. 
Wojciehoski only reviewed the treatment notes from Wickersham’s first two visits at 
Orthopaedic Specialists before issuing his opinion.  By the time Wickersham saw Dr. 
Wojciehoski he had attended four appointments at Orthopaedic Specialists and had two 
objective medical tests completed. Dr. Wojciehoski was not provided with all of the 
medical evidence in this claim.  Finally, while the undersigned does not find 
Wickersham to be a particularly credible witness, his explanation of his job duties at 
Mbrico has not varied.  He described his job duties to his medical providers, as well as 
during his deposition and at hearing. Those descriptions are consistent. (See Tr, pp. 16-
19; Cl Ex. 5, pp. 10-13; JE 2, p. 6; JE 3, p. 10; JE 4, p. 28).   
Even more important, defendants have not introduced any evidence contradicting 
Wickersham’s descriptions. When defense counsel was asked for a formal job 
description for Wickersham’s position, he indicated there was no formal job description 
for the position, as well as his belief that there was not “significant disagreement with 
your client’s recitation of his day-to-day activities as set forth in his deposition.” (Cl Ex. 
5, p. 8).  Additionally, both parties’ exhibits contain a written description that is reported 
to be from Mbrico, though the exact author is unknown. (See Cl Ex. 5, p. 9; Ex. B, p. 8).  
That description indicates that Wickersham would sometimes work in other areas like 
crate assembly, shrink wrapping, and operating a forklift, but it does not give any 
indication of how often that would happen. (See Cl Ex. 5, p. 9; Ex. B, p. 8). More 
importantly, it does not materially disagree with Wickersham’s description of his job 
duties in the glue cell division. (Id.).  Given this, I find that Dr. Crosmer’s causation 
opinion is supported by the hearing evidence; it is adopted. Wickersham has met his 
burden to prove that he sustained injuries to his bilateral upper extremities that arose 
out of and in the course of his employment with Mbrico on February 24, 2023.   
Under the Iowa Workers' Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is 
compensated either for a loss or loss of use of a scheduled member under Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(a)-(u) or for loss of earning capacity under section 85.34(2)(v). The 
extent of scheduled member disability benefits to which an injured worker is entitled is 
determined by using the functional method. Functional disability is “limited to the loss of 
the physiological capacity of the body or body part.” Mortimer v. Fruehauf Corp., 502 
N.W.2d 12, 15 (Iowa 1993); Sherman v. Pella Corp., 576 N.W.2d 312, 320 (Iowa 1998). 
Wickersham suffered work-related injuries to his bilateral upper extremities.  Thus, his 
permanent impairment is determined under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(t) (2022).  Iowa

Page 11 
 
 
Code section 85.34(2)(t) states that impairment to “both arms . . . caused by a single 
accident, shall equal five hundred weeks.”  
In all cases of permanent partial disability described in paragraphs “a” through 
“u”, or paragraph “v” when determining functional disability and not loss of earning 
capacity, the extent of loss or percentage of permanent impairment shall be determined 
solely by utilizing the guides to the evaluation of permanent impairment, published by 
the American Medical Association, as adopted by the workers' compensation 
commissioner by rule pursuant to chapter 17A. Iowa Code § 85.34(2)(x). 
Dr. Bansal was the only doctor to provide permanent impairment ratings for 
Wickersham’s bilateral upper extremities.  He opined Wickersham suffered 5 percent 
permanent impairment of the right upper extremity for sensory deficits in all five digits 
and 7 percent permanent impairment of the left upper extremity for identical sensory 
deficits. (Cl Ex. 7, p. 24).  These ratings are adopted. Those ratings convert to 3 percent 
whole body for the right upper extremity and 4 percent whole body for the left upper 
extremity. See Table 16-3 of the AMA Guides, p. 439. Using the combined values chart 
at page 604, Dr. Bansal’s opinion results in a total impairment rating of 7 percent whole 
body.  Wickersham is entitled to 35 weeks of permanent partial disability benefits, 
commencing on February 20, 2024, at the stipulated rate of $467.79.   
The next issue that must be determined is whether Wickersham is entitled to 
temporary total or healing period benefits.  Wickersham has proven entitlement to 
permanent benefits in this action, thus, the benefits he seeks are healing period 
benefits.  See Dunlap v. Action Warehouse, 824 N.W.2d 545, 556 (Iowa Ct. App. 2012).  
According to the hearing report, Wickersham seeks healing period benefits from March 
4, 2023 through February 20, 2024, or for 50.571 weeks. (Hearing Report).  
  
Iowa Code section 85.34(1) governs healing period benefits. It states as follows: 
 
[T]he employer shall pay to the employee compensation for a healing 
period, as provided in section 85.37, beginning on the first day of disability 
after the injury, and until the employee has returned to work or it is medically 
indicated that significant improvement from the injury is not anticipated or 
until the employee is medically capable of returning to employment 
substantially similar to the employment in which the employee was engaged 
at the time of the injury, whichever occurs first.  
 
Iowa Code § 85.34(1).  Though the evidence is murky on whether Wickersham had work 
restrictions on March 4, 2023, which could not be accommodated by Mbrico, on the 
hearing report defendants stipulated that if they were found liable for Wickersham’s 
alleged upper extremity injuries, he was also entitled to healing period benefits from that 
date through February 20, 2024. (See Hearing Report, p. 1).  Defendants are bound by 
that stipulation.  Wickersham is awarded healing period benefits from March 4, 2023, 
through February 20, 2024, his MMI date, at the stipulated rate of $467.79.

Iowa Code section 85.27(4) provides, in relevant part: 
 
For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish reasonable 
services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has the right to 
choose the care .... The treatment must be offered promptly and be 
reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the 
employee. If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the care 
offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such dissatisfaction 
to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the employer and 
the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited to treat the 
injury. If the employer and employee cannot agree on such alternate care, 
the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable proofs of the 
necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
Id.; see also, Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 433, 436-37 (Iowa 
1997). 
“Iowa Code section 85.27(4) affords an employer who does not contest the 
compensability of a workplace injury a qualified statutory right to control the medical 
care provided to an injured employee.” Ramirez-Trujillo v. Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 
N.W.2d 759, 769 (Iowa 2016) (citing R.R. Donnelly & Sons v. Barnett, 670 N.W.2d 190, 
195-97 (Iowa 2003)). “In enacting the right-to-choose provision in section 85.27(4), our 
legislature sought to balance the interests of injured employees against the competing 
interests of their employers.” Id. at 770-71 (citing Bell Bros. Heating and Air 
Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d 193, 202, 207 (Iowa 2010); IBP v. Harker, 633 
N.W.2d 322, 326-27 (Iowa 2001)).  
The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except where the 
employer has denied liability for the injury. Iowa Code § 85.27; Holbert v. Townsend 
Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial Commissioner 78 
(Review-Reopening, October 16, 1975). An employer’s right to select the provider of 
medical treatment to an injured worker does not include the right to determine how an 
injured worker should be diagnosed, evaluated, treated, or other matters of professional 
medical judgment. Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 866389 (Declaratory Ruling, 
May 18, 1988). Reasonable care includes care necessary to diagnose the condition, 
and defendants are not entitled to interfere with the medical judgment of its own treating 
physician. Pote v. Mickow Corp., File No. 694639 (Review-Reopening Decision, June 
17, 1986).

The employer must furnish “reasonable medical services and supplies and 
reasonable and necessary appliances to treat an injured employee.” Stone Container 
Corp. v. Castle, 657 N.W.2d 485, 490 (Iowa 2003) (emphasis in original). Such 
employer-provided care “must be offered promptly and be reasonably suited to treat the 
injury without undue inconvenience to the employee.” Iowa Code § 85.27(4). 
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment - and seeking alternate care - 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable. See e.g., 
Iowa R. App. P. 14(f)(5); Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d at 209; Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 
N.W.2d 122, 124 (Iowa 1995). An injured employee dissatisfied with the employer-
furnished care (or lack thereof) may share the employee’s discontent with the employer 
and if the parties cannot reach an agreement on alternate care, “the commissioner may, 
upon application and reasonable proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and order the 
care.” Iowa Code § 85.27(4). “Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is 
a question of fact.” Long, 528 N.W.2d at 123; see also Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d at 436. As 
the party seeking relief in the form of alternate care, the employee bears the burden of 
proving that the authorized care is unreasonable. Long, 528 N.W.2d at 124; Gwinn, 779 
N.W.2d at 209; Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d at 436. Because “the employer’s obligation under 
the statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability,” an injured 
employee’s dissatisfaction with employer-provided care, standing alone, is not enough 
to find such care unreasonable. Long, 528 N.W.2d at 124. 
 
Wickersham’s post-hearing brief does not actually request alternate medical 
care, and a review of the hearing record does not reveal any treatment 
recommendations from his providers that still need to be completed.  Wickersham’s 
surgeries have already been performed and he has completed follow-up treatment and 
physical therapy.  Applications for alternate medical care are prospective only, it is not a 
vehicle for reimbursement of past treatment. See 876 IAC 4.48.  Dr. Crosmer’s last 
treatment note is dated February 20, 2024. (JE 3, p. 26).  On that date she released 
Wickersham and told him to follow up as needed. (Id.).  He has not returned to her 
office since that date and during the hearing he did not state that he needed additional 
treatment.  (See Tr., pp. 15-55).  Given this, a determination for future medical care is 
not ripe.  Wickersham’s request for alternate medical care is denied at this time.4
