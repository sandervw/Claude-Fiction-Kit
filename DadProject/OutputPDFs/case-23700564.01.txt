CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The first issue to be determined is whether claimant sustained a permanent 
disability regarding his November 1, 2022 injury (File No. 23003632.01).

The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
A possibility of causation is not sufficient; a probability is necessary. Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996) (citing Holmes v. Bruce 
Motor Freight, Inc., 215 N.W.2d 296, 297 (Iowa 1974)). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 
621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 
(Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
Regarding the November 1, 2022, knee injury, claimant was first evaluated by Dr. 
Studt on November 10, 2022.  At that time claimant was assessed as having a right 
knee strain.  He was instructed to use over-the-counter pain medication and to use ice 
and heat on his knee.  (JE 1, p. 5) 
He returned to Dr. Studt on November 17, 2022.  At that point his knee had 
improved and claimant was discharged from care.  (JE 1, pp. 5-6)  There is no evidence 
claimant sustained any permanent disability from the November 1, 2022 injury.  Given 
this record, claimant has failed to carry his burden of proof his November 1, 2022, injury 
resulted in a permanent disability. 
As claimant has failed to carry his burden of proof his November 1, 2022, date of 
injury resulted in a permanent disability, all other issues regarding File No. 23003632.01 
are moot.

Under the Iowa Workers' Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is 
compensated either for a loss or loss of use of a scheduled member under Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(a)-(u) or as an unscheduled injury pursuant to the provisions of section 
85.34(2)(v). The extent of scheduled member disability benefits to which an injured 
worker is entitled is determined by using the functional method. Functional disability is 
"limited to the loss of the physiological capacity of the body or body part.” Mortimer v. 
Fruehauf Corp., 502 N.W.2d 12, 15 (Iowa 1993); Sherman v. Pella Corp., 576 N.W.2d 
312, 320 (Iowa 1998).   
Iowa Code section 85.34(x) states:  
x. In all cases of permanent partial disability described in 
paragraphs “a” through “u”, or paragraph “v” when determining functional 
disability and not loss of earning capacity, the extent of loss or percentage 
of permanent impairment shall be determined solely by utilizing the guides 
to the evaluation of permanent impairment, published by the American 
medical association, as adopted by the workers' compensation 
commissioner by rule pursuant to chapter 17A. Lay testimony or agency 
expertise shall not be utilized in determining loss or percentage of 
permanent impairment pursuant to paragraphs “a” through “u”, or 
paragraph “v” when determining functional disability and not loss of 
earning capacity.  
Iowa Code section 85.34 (x).  
This agency has adopted the Guides to the Evaluation of Permanent Impairment, 
Fifth Edition, published by the American Medical Association for determining the extent 
of loss or percentage of impairment for permanent partial disabilities.  See 876 IAC 2.4.  
If claimant has an impairment to the body as a whole, an industrial disability has 
been sustained.  Industrial disability was defined in Diederich v. Tri-City Ry. Co. of Iowa, 
219 Iowa 587, 591, 258 N.W. 899, 902 (1935) as follows:  "It is therefore plain that the 
Legislature intended the term 'disability' to mean 'industrial disability' or loss of earning 
capacity and not a mere 'functional disability' to be computed in terms of percentages of 
the total physical and mental ability of a normal man." 
Functional impairment is an element to be considered in determining industrial 
disability which is the reduction of earning capacity, but consideration must also be 
given to the injured employee's age, education, qualifications, experience, motivation, 
loss of earnings, severity and situs of the injury, work restrictions, inability to engage in 
employment for which the employee is fitted and the employer's offer of work or failure 
to so offer.  McSpadden v. Big Ben Coal Co., 288 N.W.2d 181, 192 (Iowa 1980); Olson 
v. Goodyear Service Stores, 255 Iowa 1112, 1120, 125 N.W.2d 251, 256 (1963); Barton 
v. Nevada Poultry Co., 253 Iowa 285, 110 N.W.2d 660, 663 (1961). 
Compensation for permanent partial disability shall begin when it is medically 
indicated that maximum medical improvement from the injury has been reached and

Both Drs. Bansal and Vincent found that claimant sustained a 2 percent 
permanent impairment to the right knee in regard to his torn meniscus and subsequent 
surgery.  (Ex. 1, p. 12; JE 3, p. 29)  The parties differ regarding whether claimant’s DVT 
condition resulted in a permanent disability.   
The record indicates claimant took anticoagulant medication for 3 months 
beginning in approximately April of 2023.  Claimant has not taken any medication for his 
DVT condition since that time.  Claimant has not sought or received any treatment for 
his DVT condition beyond the 3 months of anticoagulant medication.  (Tr., p. 31)  
Claimant has no restrictions regarding his DVT condition.  (Tr., p. 33)  In his deposition 
claimant testified he has not had any issues with swelling in his right leg.  (Ex. C, depo 
p. 20)  At hearing claimant testified he does get swelling in his leg if he is sitting or 
standing too long.  (Tr., p. 32) 
Two experts have opined regarding permanent impairment to claimant’s lower 
extremity regarding his DVT.  Dr. Vincent treated claimant for an extended period of 
time and performed surgery on claimant.  Dr. Vincent noted on exams on May 12, 2023, 
and June 23, 2023, that claimant had no evidence of any residual venous stasis, 
venous congestion, swelling or vascular dysfunction at those exams.  At those exams 
claimant had no evidence of peripheral edema.  At those exams claimant denied any 
complaints of swelling or pain that could be attributed to venous insufficiency.  (JE 3, pp. 
24-28, 30)  Dr. Vincent found that claimant’s DVT had resolved, and as a result, he had 
no permanent impairment regarding his DVT pursuant to the Guides.  (JE 3, pp. 30-31) 
Dr. Bansal evaluated claimant once for an IME.  He found that claimant had a 3 
percent permanent impairment to the lower extremity for his DVT based on Table 17-38, 
Class I of the Guides.   
Class I of Table 17-38 indicates that a permanent impairment may be assessed 
for a DVT if claimant has transient edema, and if, on exam, claimant has loss of pulses; 
minimal loss of subcutaneous tissue; calcification of arteries as detected by x-ray 
examination; asymptomatic dilation of the arteries or of veins; and not resulting in 
curtailment of activity.   
There is no evidence Dr. Bansal did an exam finding that claimant had loss of 
pulse, minimal loss of acute subcutaneous tissue, calcification of arteries as detected by 
an x-ray exam, or asymptomatic dilation of the arteries or of the veins. 
Claimant testified in deposition he had no swelling in his right lower extremity.  
After initially testifying at hearing that he had no swelling in his lower extremity, claimant 
qualified then by indicating, “actually, I did.”  Claimant was unable to recall much detail 
at hearing regarding the swelling and said it occurred if he was on his feet or sat too

long.  (Tr., p. 32)  There is no evidence in the record if this swelling is due to claimant’s 
knee or any other condition.  
Claimant has not treated for his DVT after taking the 3 months of anticoagulation 
medication.  Claimant has not received any medication for his DVT since taking the 3 
months of anticoagulation medication.  Records from May of 2023 and June of 2023 
indicate claimant had no swelling in his leg after approximately one month of taking 
medication.  Claimant testified in deposition he had no swelling in his lower extremity.  
There is no evidence to suggest that claimant has any problems with activity for his

DVT condition resulted in a permanent impairment. 
Claimant has failed to carry his burden of proof his DVT condition resulted in a 
permanent impairment.  As a result, claimant is due permanent partial disability benefits 
based on the 2 percent rating to his right lower extremity or 4.4 weeks of permanent 
partial disability benefits (2 percent x 220 weeks). 
The next issue to be determined is if the defendants are liable for a penalty. 
In Christensen v. Snap-on Tools Corp., 554 N.W.2d 254 (Iowa 1996) and 
Robbennolt v. Snap-on Tools Corp, 555 N.W.2d 229 (Iowa 1996) the supreme court 
said: 
Based on the plain language of section 86.13,1 we hold an employee 
is entitled to penalty benefits if there has been a delay in payment unless 
the employer proves a reasonable cause or excuse.  [Iowa Code § 86.13 
(2015).]  A reasonable cause or excuse exists if either (1) the delay was 
necessary for the insurer to investigate the claim or (2) the employer had a 
reasonable basis to contest the employee’s entitlement to benefits.  A 
“reasonable basis” for denial of the claim exists if the claim is “fairly 
debatable.”  Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 260. 
The supreme court has stated: 
(1) If the employer has a reason for the delay and conveys that 
reason to the employee contemporaneously with the beginning of the delay, 
no penalty will be imposed if the reason is of such character that a 
reasonable fact finder could conclude that it is a "reasonable or probable 
cause or excuse" under Iowa Code section 86.13 [2015].  In that case, we 
will defer to the decision of the commissioner.  See Christensen, 554 
N.W.2d at 260 (substantial evidence found to support commissioner’s

finding of legitimate reason for delay pending receipt of medical report); 
Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 236. 
(2) If no reason is given for the delay or if the “reason” is not one that 
a reasonable fact finder could accept, we will hold that no such cause or 
excuse exists and remand to the commissioner for the sole purpose of 
assessing penalties under section 86.13.  See Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 
261. 
(3) Reasonable causes or excuses include (a) a delay for the 
employer to investigate the claim, Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 260; 
Kiesecker v. Webster City Custom Meats, Inc., 528 N.W.2d at 109, 111 
(Iowa 1995); or (b) the employer had a reasonable basis to contest the 
claimthe “fairly debatable” basis for delay.  See Christensen, 554 N.W.2d 
at 260 (holding two-month delay to obtain employer’s own medical report 
reasonable under the circumstances).  
(4) For the purpose of applying section 86.13, the benefits that are 
underpaid as well as late-paid benefits are subject to penalties, unless the 
employer establishes reasonable and probable cause or excuse. [Iowa 
Code § 86.13 (2015);] Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 237 (underpayment 
resulting from application of wrong wage base; in absence of excuse, 
commissioner required to apply penalty). 
If we were to construe [Iowa Code § 86.13 (2015)] to 
permit the avoidance of penalty if any amount of 
compensation benefits are paid, the purpose of the penalty 
statute would be frustrated.  For these reasons, we conclude 
section 86.13 is applicable when payment of compensation is 
not timely . . . or when the full amount of compensation is not 
paid.  Id. 
(5) For purposes of determining whether there has been a delay, 
payments are “made” when (a) the check addressed to a claimant is mailed 
(Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 236; Kiesecker, 528 N.W.2d at 112), or (b) the 
check is delivered personally to the claimant by the employer or its workers’ 
compensation insurer.  Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 235.   
(6) In determining the amount of the penalty, the commissioner is to 
consider factors such as the length of the delay, the number of delays, the 
information available to the employer regarding the employee’s injury and 
wages, and the employer’s past record of penalties.  Robbennolt, 555 
N.W.2d at 238. 
(7) An employer’s bare assertion that a claim is “fairly debatable” 
does not make it so.  A fair reading of Christensen and Robbennolt, makes 
it clear that the employer must assert facts upon which the commissioner

When an employee’s claim for benefits is fairly debatable based on a good faith 
dispute over the employee’s factual or legal entitlement to benefits, an award of penalty 
benefits is not appropriate under the statute.  Gilbert v. USF Holland, Inc., 637 N.W.2d

Claimant was found to be at maximum medical improvement on June 23, 2023.  
(JE 3, pp. 27-28)  On July 28, 2023, Dr. Vincent found claimant had a 2 percent

the delayed payment of the rating is appropriate.
