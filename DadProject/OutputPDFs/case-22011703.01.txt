STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
Joseph Looker, claimant, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from Allegis Group (Aerotek, Inc.), employer and Indemnity 
Insurance Company of North America, insurance carrier, as defendants. Hearing was 
held via Zoom videoconference on January 25, 2024.     
 
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing. On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations. All of those 
stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration decision. 
The parties are now bound by their stipulations.  
 
Joseph Looker was the only witness to testify live at trial. The evidentiary record 
also includes Joint Exhibits 1-3, Claimant’s Exhibits 1-8, and Defendants’ Exhibits A-G. 
All exhibits were received without objection. The evidentiary record closed at the 
conclusion of the arbitration hearing.  
 
There were two preliminary matters which were addressed at the outset of the 
hearing. The first issue involved whether the claimant preserved a claim of industrial 
disability against the defendant-employer. Claimant, Joseph Looker, filed a petition 
against Allegis Group (Aerotek, Inc.), employer and Indemnity Insurance Company of 
North America, insurance carrier, and The Second Injury Fund of Iowa (“the Fund”).  
(Claimant’s Exhibit 7, pp. 43-44) In the petition claimant alleged the parts of his body 
affected were his right fingers, hand, and arm. (Id., numbered paragraph 5) In his

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 
 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based. A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause. A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible. George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996). 
 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability. 
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); 
Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995). Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted expert medical

testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 
N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 1994). 
 
There is no dispute that the claimant sustained permanent disability as the result 
of the work injury. (Hearing Report, numbered paragraph 3) The hearing report lists the 
dispute regarding permanency as whether claimant’s permanency benefits should be 
compensated as a scheduled member disability to the right upper extremity or as an 
industrial disability injury. (Hearing Report, numbered paragraphs 5)      
 
Under the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is 
compensated either for a loss of use of a scheduled member under Iowa Code 
85.34(2)(a)-(u) or for loss of earning capacity under Iowa Code 85.34(2)(v). The extent 
of scheduled member disability benefits to which an injured worker is entitled is 
determined by using the functional method. Functional disability is “limited to the loss of 
the physiological capacity of the body or body part.” Mortimer v. Fruehauf Corp., 502 
N.W.2d 12, 15 (Iowa 1993); Sherman v. Pella Corp., 576 N.W.2d 312, 322 (Iowa 1998). 
An injury to a scheduled member may, because of aftereffects or compensatory 
change, result in permanent impairment of the body as a whole. Such impairment may 
in turn be the basis for a rating of industrial disability. It is the anatomical situs of the 
permanent injury or impairment which determines which schedules in Iowa Code 
85.34(a) - (u) apply. Lauhoff Grain v. McIntosh, 395 N.W.2d 834 (Iowa 1986); 
Blacksmith v. All-American, Inc., 290 N.W.2d 348 (Iowa 1980); Dailey v. Pooley Lumber 
Co., 233 Iowa 758, 10 N.W.2d 569 (1943); Soukup v. Shores Co., 222 Iowa 272, 268 
N.W. 598 (1936).

In his post-hearing brief, claimant argued that the laceration to his long finger 
ultimately resulted in injury and impairment to three separate scheduled members. He 
argues that his long finger involved his MP joint and therefore should be compensated 
as a hand. The infection spread to include his carpal tunnel and therefore he urges it 
should be compensated as an arm. Claimant argues that the injury also involved his 
right ring finger which he contends should be compensated as a third finger. Claimant 
contends he sustained an injury to three separate members, and therefore should be 
compensated as an industrial disability claim under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v). 
(Claimant’s Post-Hearing Brief, p. 9)     
 
First, we address the issue of how an injury should be compensated when the 
injury is near or involves multiple body parts.   
 
The Iowa Supreme Court has considered how an injury should be 
compensated when the injury is arguably between or in either of two body 
parts. The Court has consistently held that it is the anatomical situs of the 
permanent injury or impairment which determines how an injury should be 
compensated. Lauhoff Grain Co. v. McIntosh, 395 N.W.2d 834 (Iowa 
1986); Blacksmith v. All-American, Inc., 290 N.W.2d 348 (Iowa 1980); 
Dailey v. Pooley Lumber Co., 233 Iowa 758, 10 N.W.2d 569 (1943); 
Soukup v. Shores Co., 222 Iowa 272, 268 N.W. 598 (1936). An injury 
occurring proximal to a joint is to be compensated to the more proximal 
body part. But see Smidt v. JKB Restaurants, L.C., File No. 5067766 
(Appeal Dec. 2020) (“I also rejected the deputy commissioner's strict 
application of the bright line rule that whatever is proximal to the joint 
should be treated as an unscheduled injury under section 85.34(2)(v).”); 

LOOKER V. ALLEGIS GROUP (AEROTEK, INC.) 
Page 11 
 
Chavez v. MS Technologies, L.L.C., File No. 5066270 (Appeal Sept. 
2020); Deng v. Farmland Foods, Inc., File No. 5061883 (Appeal Sept. 
2020). 
 
For instance, a hip injury is generally an injury to the body as a whole and 
not an injury to the leg. The leg extends to the acetabulum or socket side 
of the hip joint. Lauhoff Grain Co. v. McIntosh, 395 N.W.2d 834 (Iowa 
1986); Dailey v. Pooley Lumber Co., 233 Iowa 758, 10 N.W.2d 569 
(1943). Prior to a statutory change in 2017, an injury to the shoulder was 
considered a body as a whole situation because a shoulder injury 
extended proximal to the arm. Alm v. Morris Barick Cattle Co., 240 Iowa 
1174, 38 N.W.2d 161 (1949). 
 
Perhaps the most instructive case involves a dispute about whether an 
injury involves the hand or the arm. In Holstein Elec. v. Breyfogle, 756 
N.W.2d 812 (Iowa 2008), the Iowa Supreme Court confronted an injury 
that involved a carpal bone in the wrist. The Court concluded that bones 
within the wrist are considered part of the arm for compensation purposes 
because the wrist is proximal to the hand. In making this decision, the 
Court held that the hand begins at the distal point of the bones of the wrist. 
Id. Carpal tunnel syndrome involves structures within the wrist. Wrist 
injuries are compensated as arm injuries under Iowa Code section 
85.34(2)(m). Holstein Elec. v. Breyfogle, 756 N.W.2d 812 (Iowa 2008).  
 
Therefore, carpal tunnel syndrome is compensated as an arm injury. 
 
Tressel v. Nordstrom Distribution Center, File No. 1662439.03 (Arb. March 24, 2022). 
 
In the present case, claimant sustained an injury to his right long finger which 
spread to the MP joint, into the hand, ring finger, and into his carpal tunnel. Claimant 
contends he sustained injury to three separate members, his right hand, right ring 
finger, and his right upper extremity; and therefore, he should be compensated under 
Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v). In support of his position, the claimant cites to an appeal 
decision from this agency. See Martinez v. Pavlich, Inc., File No. 5063900, 2020 WL 
5412838 at *3 (App. Dec. July 30, 2020). In Martinez, the Commissioner stated,  
 
I recently held, consistent with past agency precedent, that the permanent 
disability of three separate scheduled members occurring in the same 
incident entitles a claimant to industrial disability benefits under section 
85.34(2)(u) now section 85.34(2)(v)). Sparks v. P & J Equipment Corp., 
File No. 5058524 (App. Dec., May 18, 2020); see Bruce v. Hydecker 
Wheatlake Co., File No. 5036473 (Arb. Dec., Jan. 10, 2013); Wallingord v. 
Atlantic Carriers, File No. 5008405 (Arb. Dec., July 23, 2004). Because 
claimant’s injury in this case involved the permanent loss of use of three 
scheduled member, i.e., his right upper extremity and his bilateral lower

extremities, I therefore conclude application of Iowa Code section 
85.34(2)(v)(2019) is appropriate. 
 
Id. at 3. 
 
In Martinez, the claimant sustained an injury that involved permanent loss of use 
of three completely separate scheduled members: his right upper extremity, his left 
lower extremity, and his right lower extremity. The Commissioner held that an 
impairment to three separate scheduled members is compensated under subsection v, 
the catch-all provision of section 85.34(2). Id. at 3. The cases cited by the 
Commissioner in Martinez all involved claimants who also sustained permanent loss of 
use to three separate scheduled members. Defendants contend these cases are 
distinguishable from the case at bar.      
   
In support of their position, defendants point to a line of decisions from this 
agency which indicate that multiple impairment ratings to the same extremity are 
compensated as a single scheduled member injury. Specifically, in cases involving 
injuries to fingers, hand, wrist, and arm, claimants have been compensated as an injury 
to the upper extremity under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(m). In Smart v. Rathje 
Construction Co., the claimant was assigned impairment ratings for the right forearm, 
wrist, and hand/digits. The agency treats these injuries, which are part of a larger 
extremity, as one scheduled member injury. The deputy awarded permanent benefits 
equivalent to 44 percent of the claimant’s right upper extremity. Smart v. Rathje 
Construction Co., File No. 1641582.01, 2021 WL 5932882 (Arb. Dec. 7, 2021).  In 
Lynch v. Outdoor Group, LLC., the agency adopted the IME rating where the doctor 
assigned the claimant a 4 percent upper extremity impairment for his right elbow and 5 
percent upper extremity impairment for claimant’s right wrist and hand, for a total of 9 
percent upper extremity impairment. A portion of the impairment rating for the wrist and 
hand was assigned for sensory deficit to the first and second digits. Lynch v. Outdoor 
Group, LLC, File No. 1662284.01, 2021 WL 5871912 (Arb. Dec. 3, 2021). The agency 
compensated this injury as an injury to the upper extremity. In Maxwell v. Precision 
Pulley & Idler, the agency concluded that the claimant’s loss of contracture of the right 
digits and wrist should be compensated as 40 percent loss to the right arm under Iowa 
Code section 85.34(20(m). Maxwell v. Precision Pulley & Idler, File No. 5017423, 2008 
WL 4831363 (Arb. Dec. Oct. 27, 2008)1.  
 
I find defendants’ argument to be persuasive. Based on the above findings of 
fact, I conclude claimant sustained a laceration to his long finger, but the injury was not 
contained to his long finger; rather, his injury extended to his ring finger, hand, and 
wrist. The fingers, hand, and wrist are encompassed by the upper extremity. This 
agency has historically compensated these types of injuries as an injury to the upper 
extremity. I conclude claimant sustained a scheduled member injury to his right upper 
extremity and should be compensated under subsection (m).

In his supplemental brief, claimant contends that the recent decision from the 
Iowa Supreme Court does not apply to his case. See Bridgestone Americas Inc. v. 
Anderson, 4 N.W.3d 676, 683 (Iowa 2024). In, Anderson, the claimant argued that 
because he had injuries to his shoulder and arm (carpal tunnel and ulnar nerve) he was 
entitled to industrial disability benefits under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v). The 
Supreme Court held Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v) only applies to injuries that are not 
“described or referred to” in the schedule. Id. In making this determination, the Court 
highlighted the beginning of Code section 85.34(2)(v) which reads, “In all cases of 
permanent partial disability other than those described or referred to in paragraphs ‘a’ 
through ‘u’, the compensation shall be paid during the number of weeks in relation to 
five hundred weeks . . . .” Id. (emphasis added). The Court indicated that injuries to 
fingers, arms, hands, and shoulders are all described in subsections (a) through (u), so 
they would not fall under the catch all provision of Code section 85.34(2)(v). Anderson, 
4 N.W.3d at 685 (stating “And so, paragraph (v) generally doesn’t apply to injuries to 
fingers, arms, hands, shoulders . . . .”). Based on the above findings of fact, I conclude 
claimant’s injury does not fall into the catch-all provision of subsection (v).   
 
Claimant states that the Anderson case is distinguishable from his case, and 
therefore does not apply. Specifically, claimant states, “the Anderson decision is fact 
specific, addressing only whether a cumulative injury to a shoulder and an arm should 
be considered an industrial disability injury or scheduled member disability injury.”  
(Claimant’s Supplemental Brief, p. 1) Claimant contends that the Court made it clear 
that they were only deciding the issue of a cumulative injury to the shoulder and the arm 
and not a single accident involving multiple scheduled members. (Id.) The undersigned 
does not read Anderson as broadly. In Anderson, the Court stated, “paragraph (t) isn’t 
relevant to Anderson. By its plain terms, paragraph (t) applies to some situations in 
which multiple injuries are ‘caused by a single accident.’ Id. That is not what happened 
to Anderson. His injuries are the result of decades of work-related wear and tear.”  
(Anderson at 684.) Nonetheless, because claimant sustained an injury to a scheduled 
member, his upper extremity, under the Anderson holding, claimant’s argument that he 
be compensated pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v) fails.         
  
In the alternative, claimant contends that if Anderson does apply then he should 
be compensated under Iowa Code sections 85.34(2)(d) and 85.34(2)(t). Specifically, 
claimant states his ring finger should be compensated as a loss of his third finger under 
subsection (d), which is compensated on the basis of 25 weeks. He then contends that 
his injury to his long finger should be compensated as a hand, and his wrist should be 
compensated as an upper extremity. Therefore, because he sustained a single injury 
which resulted in permanent impairment to his right hand and his right arm, that portion 
of his injury should be compensated under subsection (t). Subsection (t) provides for the 
“Loss of both arms, or both hands, or both feet, or both legs, or both eyes, or any two 
thereof, caused by a single accident, shall equal five hundred weeks and shall be 
compensated as such; however, if said employee is permanently and totally disabled 
the employee may be entitled to benefits under subsection 3.” Subsection 3 includes 
permanent total disability benefits.

In their supplemental brief, defendants contend that claimant has conceded that 
all three of his injuries are all scheduled member injuries. Defendants argue that under 
Anderson all three scheduled members should be compensated under their respective 
sections in 85.34(2) and not under paragraph “v”. Defendants do not address 
subsection “t”. (Defendants’ Supplemental Brief, pp. 2-3)  
 
However, as previously noted, this agency has treated such injuries to the same 
member as an injury to the upper extremity. In 2017, the Iowa legislature made several 
changes to the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Act. See 2017 Iowa Acts, ch. 23. Prior to 
the 2017 changes to Iowa Code section 85.34, a shoulder injury was compensated as 
an unscheduled injury, and based on industrial disability. See Alm v. Morris Barick 
Cattle Co., 240 Iowa 1174, 38 N.W.2d 161(1949). The 2017 changes added a shoulder 
to the list of scheduled members. Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(n). The legislature did not 
make any other substantive changes to the list of scheduled members. See 2017 Iowa 
Acts, ch. 23. It is also noteworthy that the only change that was made to the current 
subsection “t” was to rename it from “s” to “t” due to the inclusion of the shoulder at 
subsection “n”. No substantive changes were made to subsection “t”. Id. 
 
In the present case, claimant sustained an injury to his right long finger, which 
was not contained to that digit. Rather, his injury spread to affect his third digit, hand, 
and wrist, resulting in an injury to his right upper extremity. Following the examples set 
forth by this agency, I conclude that as the result of a single injury, claimant sustained a 
scheduled member injury to his right upper extremity, and therefore should be 
compensated under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(m).   
 
Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(m) provides that a permanent disability to the arm 
shall be compensated based upon 250 weeks. Based on the above findings of fact, I 
conclude he sustained 25 percent impairment of his right upper extremity. As such he 
has demonstrated entitlement to 62.5 weeks (250 x 25 percent) of permanent partial 
disability benefits (PPD).   
 
We now turn to the appropriate commencement date for payment of PPD 
benefits. Claimant contends benefits should commence on March 30, 2023. Defendants 
contend benefits should commence on February 16, 2023. 
 
Under Iowa law, “[c]ompensation for permanent partial disability shall begin when 
it is medically indicated that maximum medical improvement from the injury has been 
reached and that the extent of loss or percentage of permanent impairment can be 
determined by use of the guides . . .”  Iowa Code section 85.34(2). Based on the above 
findings of fact, I conclude the appropriate commencement date in this case is March 
30, 2023. (JE 3, p. 126-128)  
 
The next issue is that of rate. Claimant asserts he is entitled to four exemptions. 
The dispute rests on whether both of claimant’s minor children can be counted. There is 
no specific code statute to determine the meaning of dependents; however, the Iowa 
Worker’s Compensation Commission has long held that the manner in which a claimant

files his taxes creates a rebuttable presumption of his tax status. Echols v. Elite Staffing 
et al., No. 5047498, 2016 WL 5348927, at *17 (Iowa Workers’ Comp. Comm’n Sept. 21, 
2016). This Commission has also reasoned that “where support is provided it is inferred 
that the person is entitled to the dependency exemption unless contrary evidence rebuts 
this inference.” Walker v. IBP, Inc., No. 1240597, 2002 WL 32125376, at *5 (Iowa 
Workers’ Comp. Comm’n Jan. 4, 2002). Further, “[t]he Iowa Supreme Court has 
instructed that the definition and application of the law to determine who is a dependent 
should not be restricted severely.” Cain v. Homes of Oakridge et al., No. 503836, 2013 
WL 5300572, at *19 (Iowa Workers’ Comp. Comm’n Sept. 18, 2013) (citing Murphy v. 
Franklin County, 145 N.W.2d 465, 468 (Iowa 1966).) “If a contribution is made to the 
ordinary comforts and conveniences which are reasonably appropriate to parties in their 
station in life, it should be considered as support and the recipient regarded as a 
dependent.” Murphy, 145 N.W.2d at 468. 
 
The Iowa Court of Appeals has relied on Iowa Code § 85.42 where “In the case 
of a deceased employee, a stepchild is conclusively presumed to be a dependent where 
the deceased employee ‘actually provided the principal support for such child or 
children.’” Moffett v Collision Center, Inc., File No. 6-156/05-0699 (Iowa Ct. App, May 
10, 2006) (quoting Iowa Code § 85.42). Based on the above findings of facts, it is 
determined that, at the time of the injury, claimant provided the principal support for both 
minor children and is therefore entitled to four exemptions. The claimant’s weekly 
benefit rate is $572.71.
