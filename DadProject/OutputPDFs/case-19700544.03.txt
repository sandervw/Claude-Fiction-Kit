STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A.  The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Jerry Locke.  Claimant 
appeared through attorney, Gary Nelson.  Defendant appeared through its attorney, 
Bruce Gettman. 
The alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on October 14, 2020.  The 
proceedings were digitally recorded.  That recording constitutes the official record of this 
proceeding.  Pursuant to the Commissioner’s Order, the undersigned has been 
delegated authority to issue a final agency decision in this alternate medical care 
proceeding.  Therefore, this ruling is designated final agency action and any appeal of 
the decision would be to the Iowa District Court pursuant to Iowa Code section 17A. 
The record consists of claimant’s exhibits 1 through 3 and defense exhibits A 
through I, which were received without objection.  The defendants do not dispute liability 
for claimant’s October 31, 2018, injury. 
ISSUE 
The issue presented for resolution is whether the claimant, who established 
medical care with a physician during a period in which the claim was denied, is entitled 
to an order for alternate medical care to continue treatment with the physician he chose. 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
The claimant did not testify in this case.  Claimant had low back surgery on 
October 12, 2020, at the Mayo Clinic in Minnesota.

REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Iowa Code Section 85.27 (2013). 
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See 
Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  Determining what care is 
reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.  Id.  The employer’s obligation turns 
on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.  Id.; Harned v. Farmland 
Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983).   
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).

An employer’s statutory right is to select the providers of care and the employer 
may consider cost and other pertinent factors when exercising its choice. Long, at 124. 
An employer (typically) is not a licensed health care provider and does not possess 
medical expertise. Accordingly, an employer does not have the right to control the 
methods the providers choose to evaluate, diagnose and treat the injured employee. An 
employer is not entitled to control a licensed health care provider’s exercise of 
professional judgment. Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 866389 (Declaratory 
Ruling, May 19, 1988). An employer’s failure to follow recommendations of an 
authorized physician in matters of treatment is commonly a failure to provide reasonable 
treatment. Boggs v. Cargill, Inc., File No. 1050396 (Alt. Care January 31, 1994). 
The summary procedures set forth in the alternate medical care statute and the 
rules provide injured workers with prospective relief, not for the payment of past bills, 
which is reserved for arbitration proceedings.  While neither the statute nor the rules 
actually come right out and state it in such a manner, this is how the statute has always 
been applied.  When the statute is read as a whole, this makes logical sense insofar as 
it would be difficult to manage a system where past medical bills were claimed in such 
an expedited, summary proceeding. 
Consequently, the agency has repeatedly articulated the following statement of 
law.  A decision in an alternate medical care proceeding operates prospectively only, 
not retroactively.  The claimant’s application for alternate care should be dismissed 
without prejudice when the claimant seeks payment for medical care that had been 
provided prior to the time the alternate medical care petition was filed.  Moline v. 
Nordstrom, File No. 1273226 (December 21, 2000); Donisi v. Norrell Services, File No. 
1276161 (August 8, 2000); Mobayed v. AMS Services, Inc., File No. 1168048 (May 20, 
1997); and Massie v. Madison Avenue Dairy Queen, File No. 1055168 (November 3, 
1995).  An injured worker may utilize regular proceedings (arbitration or medical only) to 
seek payment of past medical expenses.  In such cases, a full record may be made, 
rather than the summary record which is allowed in alternate medical care proceedings. 
Based upon the record before me, the claimant filed his alternate care petition on 
October 2, 2020, seeking authorization for surgery at Mayo Clinic on October 12, 2020.  
The City originally denied liability on this claim in January 2020, based upon the medical 
opinion of a physician with Allen Occupational Health.  The City continued to deny the 
claim up through September 2020, although once it learned that Mr. Locke was 
scheduled for surgery at the Mayo Clinic, it began further investigation of the claim.  The 
City reaffirmed its’ denial of the claim on October 5, 2020, after receiving Dr. 
Abernathey’s initial medical report. 
After the City received Dr. Abernathey’s supplemental report to claimant’s 
counsel on October 7, 2020, the City again reevaluated its’ position.  The City formally 
admitted liability for the low back condition on October 13, 2020, the day after surgery.  
Informally, the City had communicated with claimant’s counsel on October 9, 2020, 
indicating that it would accept liability and authorize care at the University of Iowa

Hospitals and Clinics, but not the Mayo Clinic.  At this time, Mr. Locke was already in 
Rochester, preparing for surgery. 
The question is whether it was reasonable for the City to refuse to authorize 
claimant’s scheduled surgery at Mayo Clinic. 
Two competing legal principles are involved here.  The first is that when liability 
on a claim is denied, the employer loses its’ authorization defense during the period of 
denial.  Bell Bros., Heating and Air Conditioning, v. Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d 193 (Iowa 
2010).  At the point a claim is denied, an injured worker is legally entitled to establish 
medical care with a physician of his or her choosing and the employer is barred from 
asserting an authorization defense. 
The second is that an employer as a legal right, and, in fact, a legal obligation to 
continue investigation of the claim.  Upon further investigation, an employer is allowed 
to change its position and accept liability for a denied claim.  See Brewer-Strong v. HNI 
Corp., 913 N.W.2d 235 (Iowa 2018).  In general, the Iowa Workers’ Compensation law 
encourages employers to continue to investigate claims.  In some circumstances, this 
would undoubtedly allow the employer to re-establish the right to direct the medical 
treatment. 
This case tests the limits of both principles. 
In these circumstances, I find that it is unreasonable to refuse to authorize Mr. 
Locke’s medical treatment with the Mayo Clinic since he had firmly established such 
medical care at a time when he was legally allowed to do so.  He not only established 
excellent care with an outstanding medical provider, he was literally at the facility 
preparing for the procedure which his medical provider recommended when the 
employer switched positions.  Allowing the City to cancel this scheduled treatment is 
both an unreasonable interference with claimant’s treatment, as well as an 
unreasonable delay or inconvenience to his care. 
Therefore, while I certainly agree with the principle that an employer has a right, 
upon further investigation, to admit a formerly denied claim and re-establish the right to 
direct medical treatment, I conclude that right to re-establish control is still subject to the 
reasonableness standard set forth in the alternate care statute. 
In Brewer-Strong, the Iowa Supreme Court upheld the denial of healing period 
benefits to an injured worker who sought unauthorized medical care during a period of 
recovery.  Id. at 237.  On its face, Brewer-Strong stands for the proposition that an 
employer, after initially denying a claim, may amend its’ answer and re-establish the 
right to direct medical treatment. 
It is important to distinguish Brewer-Strong from this case.  In Brewer-Strong, the 
employer initially denied liability for claimant’s injuries, first in the Answer to Petition for 
Arbitration, then in an Answer to an Alternate Care Petition.  Id. at 239.  The employer

continued to investigate the claim and eventually had claimant evaluated by a specialist 
who opined the condition was, in fact, work-related.  The employer formally amended 
its’ Answer at a time when the claimant was not receiving any treatment from any 
physician.  “Between November 8, 2012 [the date of the amended Answer admitting 
liability] and January 15, 2013, Brewer-Strong sought no medical care for her bilateral 
upper extremity complaints.”  Id. at 239.  This is the critical distinction.  The employer 
then communicated to the claimant that it had authorized care with a well-qualified 
physician at a time when the claimant was receiving no medical treatment at all.  Id. at 
240.  Subsequently, the employer in Brewer-Strong learned that the claimant planned to 
seek surgical medical care for her condition with another physician with whom care had 
not been previously established.  Interestingly, the claimant then filed for alternate 
medical care and the employer answered and accepted liability.  The claimant, 
however, voluntarily dismissed her alternate medical care petition before the claim was 
heard on the merits.  Id. at 240.  She then proceeded to establish care with a physician 
other than the authorized treating physician.  Id. at 240-41.  In other words, the claimant 
in Brewer-Strong did not establish care with any physician during the actual period of 
denial and then once the employer authorized reasonable care, and then explicitly 
refused to utilize the alternate medical care proceedings to challenge the care. 
The facts of this case stand in stark contrast to the facts of Brewer-Strong.  In 
this case, the City did not formally admit the claim until after the claimant had 
undergone surgery.  Informally, by letter, the employer sought to reestablish direction of 
the medical care on October 9, 2020, when Mr. Locke was already in Rochester for pre-
surgery examination.  Mr. Locke had firmly established medical treatment with the Mayo 
Clinic at a time in which he was legally entitled to do so.  While the City is certainly 
allowed to change its’ position in light of new evidence during the course of a 
reasonable investigation, it is not allowed to unreasonably interfere with or 
unreasonably delay the medical treatment of an injured worker who established such 
care at a point in time he was legally authorized to do so. 
The City did present a medical opinion from Dr. Abernathey that the surgery was 
not “urgent” and could be safely rescheduled.  I considered this medical opinion.  I have 
no doubt that Dr. Abernathey is correct from a purely medical standpoint that it would be 
medically safe to reschedule the surgery.  The question, however, is not whether it 
would be safe to delay.  The question is whether it would cause an unreasonable delay 
or substantial inconvenience, or otherwise unreasonably interfere with his treatment.  I 
find the employer’s attempt to change the care on the eve of surgery would 
unreasonably delay claimant’s treatment.  I find the employer’s attempt to change the 
care on the eve of surgery would cause substantial inconvenience to claimant’s 
treatment.  I find that the employer’s attempt to change the care on the eve of his 
surgery would unreasonably interfere with claimant’s efforts to receive quality care 
which is reasonably suited to treat his injury.
