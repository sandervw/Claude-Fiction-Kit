STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
Claimant, Astor Williams, filed a petition in arbitration for workers’ compensation 
benefits against John Deere Waterloo Works and asserted a separate claim against the 
Second Injury Fund of Iowa.  Claimant reached a settlement agreement with the 
employer prior to the scheduled hearing in this case.  The Iowa Workers' Compensation 
Commissioner has approved the agreement for settlement between claimant and the 
employer. 
The case against the Second Injury Fund of Iowa came before the undersigned 
for an arbitration hearing on May 31, 2023, via Zoom.   
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the evidentiary 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into numerous stipulations.  Those 
stipulations were accepted and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ 
stipulations will be made or discussed.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.   
The evidentiary record includes Joint Exhibits 1 through 7, Claimant’s Exhibits 1 
through 6, and Defendant’s Exhibits AA through FF.  Claimant testified on his own 
behalf.  The evidentiary record closed at the conclusion of the arbitration hearing.  All 
parties filed their post-hearing briefs on July 28, 2023, at which time the case was 
deemed fully submitted to the undersigned.

The parties submitted the following disputed issues for resolution:

FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned, having considered all of the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
The claimant at the center of this case is Astor Williams.  The parties stipulate 
that Williams sustained a work injury while working as an assembler for John Deere 
Waterloo Works (hereinafter referred to as “John Deere”) on December 4, 2018. 
(Hearing Report, page 1)  On the date of injury, Williams was attaching tractor roofs to 
carts. (Hearing Transcript., pp. 15-16)  In order to complete this task, Williams had to 
use a hydraulic hoist to lift each tractor roof before he maneuvered them into place. (Id.)  
In order to use the hoist, Williams had to squeeze a hand-held device.  While 
performing this job duty, Williams developed right elbow pain. (Id.)  Williams initially 
received conservative care consisting of physical therapy, a steroid injection, and 
platelet-rich plasma injections.  However, when conservative care failed to improve his 
condition, Williams required a common extensor tenotomy in July 2020. (Joint Exhibit 7, 
pp. 50-52)   
Williams last worked for John Deere in March 2020. (Hr. Tr., p. 18)  Mederic Hall, 
M.D. released claimant to return to work without restrictions on October 16, 2020. (JE7, 
pp. 56, 58)  Nevertheless, Williams decided to resign from John Deere because he did 
not believe he was capable of performing the type of work required by John Deere. (Hr. 
Tr., pp. 23-24)   
At the time of his resignation, Williams had already secured alternate 
employment as a student advisor with the University of Northern Iowa. (Hr. Tr., p. 24)  In 
this role, Williams assists middle school and high school students with navigating the 
post-secondary education process. (Id.)  It is not a physically demanding job, and he 
earns significantly less than he did while working at John Deere. (Hr. Tr., pp. 24-25)   
As mentioned, Williams resolved the December 4, 2018, injury claim with John 
Deere.  The Iowa Workers' Compensation Commissioner approved the full commutation 
on May 22, 2023.  
The instant matter involves Williams’ claim for benefits against the Second Injury 
Fund of Iowa.  On the Hearing Report, Williams alleges an initial qualifying injury to his 
right eye on August 28, 2004.   
Williams has required corrective lenses for his vision since he was young. (Hr. 
Tr., p. 10)  He has difficulty seeing at both near and far distances if he is not wearing 
glasses or contact lenses. (Hr. Tr., p. 11)  As a result, Williams is restricted from driving 
without corrective lenses. (Id.)  Even with his corrective lenses, Williams described 
difficulty seeing in low light conditions and from his peripherals. (Hr. Tr., p. 12)  His 
vision is worse in the right eye when compared to the left. (Hr. Tr., pp. 11-12)

Williams presented to the evidentiary hearing wearing glasses.  He testified that 
his vision is entirely corrected when wearing glasses or contact lenses. (Hr. Tr., p. 39) 
As part of the hiring process at John Deere, Williams underwent a pre-
employment physical. (Joint Exhibit 1, pp. 1-3)  His vision was tested during the 
physical.  The tests demonstrated uncorrected visual acuity scores for the right eye of 
20/70 from near and 20/50 from far. (JE1, pp. 1-2)  His visual acuity using his left eye 
was essentially normal. (JE1, p. 1)   
Williams’ visual issues necessitated accommodations while working for John 
Deere.  According to Williams, he was required to obtain and use prescription goggles 
while working on the assembly line. (Hr. Tr., p. 13)  It appears that Williams had the 
option of wearing his glasses or contacts under normal goggles; however, doing so 
would often result in his glasses fogging up, making it difficult for him to work. (Hr. Tr., 
pp. 12-13)  While certainly inconvenient, I am not convinced this example demonstrates 
that claimant’s vision has impacted his employability or earning capacity. 
Sunil Bansal, M.D. examined Williams’ vision as part of an independent medical 
evaluation on May 6, 2022. (See Claimant’s Exhibit 1, p. 15)  Dr. Bansal diagnosed 
bilateral myopia, bilateral regular astigmatism, and presbyopia. (Ex. 1, pp. 16-17)  
Based on Williams’ uncorrected visual acuity, Dr. Bansal assigned 24 percent right eye 
impairment. (Ex. 1, p. 18)  In terms of permanent restrictions, Dr. Bansal provided 
claimant must wear corrective lenses when driving. (Id.) 
The Second Injury Fund challenged Dr. Bansal’s impairment rating of the right 
eye.  It requested that Mark Wilkinson, O.D., perform an independent medical 
evaluation of Williams and comment upon Dr. Bansal’s impairment assessment.  Dr. 
Wilkinson is a clinical professor of ophthalmology and director of Vision Rehabilitation 
Service at the University of Iowa Carver College of Medicine. (Ex. CC, p. 26)  On 
examination, Dr. Wilkinson found Williams’ best corrected vision to be 20/20 in his right 
eye and 20/16 in his left eye. (Defendant’s Exhibit BB, p. 23)  He opined claimant’s 
binocular acuity was 20/16. (Id.)  Pursuant to the AMA Guides, Fifth Edition, Dr. 
Wilkinson assigned zero percent right eye impairment based on Williams’ corrected 
visual acuity. (Id.)  He assigned no vision related permanent restrictions to Williams 
based on the fact Williams has normal visual acuity when wearing glasses or contact 
lenses. (Id.) 
In his report, Dr. Wilkinson disagreed with Dr. Bansal’s method for assessing 
Williams’ visual impairment.  He explained that Dr. Bansal incorrectly based Williams’ 
impairment on his uncorrected visual acuity. (Id.)  He explained, “As noted on page 282 
of the AMA Guidelines, in Section 12.2b.3, ‘The impairment rating should be based on 
the best-corrected visual acuity.’” (Id.) 
Dr. Wilkinson is a doctor of optometry, with extensive experience in the field.  His 
IME report explains in detail the deficiencies in Dr. Bansal's rating with respect to 
claimant's vision.  I find his opinions highly credible and convincing.  I accept Dr.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Williams seeks an award of benefits from the Second Injury Fund of Iowa, and 
alleges significant industrial disability from the combination of his qualifying losses. 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence. Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3). 
Iowa Code section 85.64 governs Second Injury Fund liability. Before liability of 
the Fund is triggered, three requirements must be met. First, the employee must have 
lost or lost the use of a hand, arm, foot, leg, or eye. Second, the employee must sustain 
a loss or loss of use of another specified member or organ through a compensable 
injury. Third, permanent disability must exist as to both the initial injury and the second 
injury. Iowa Code section 85.64. 
The Second Injury Fund Act exists to encourage the hiring of handicapped 
persons by making a current employer responsible only for the amount of disability 
related to an injury occurring while that employer employed the handicapped individual 
as if the individual had had no preexisting disability. See Anderson v. Second Injury 
Fund, 262 N.W.2d 789 (Iowa 1978); 15 Iowa Practice, Workers' Compensation, Lawyer, 
section 17:1, p. 211 (2014-2015). 
The Second Injury Fund is responsible for the industrial disability present after 
the second injury that exceeds the disability attributable to the first and second injuries. 
Section 85.64; Second Injury Fund of Iowa v. Braden, 459 N.W.2d 467 (Iowa 1990); 
Second Injury Fund v. Neelans, 436 N.W.2d 355 (Iowa 1989); Second Injury Fund v. 
Mich Coal Co., 274 N.W.2d 300 (Iowa 1979). 
In this case, Williams alleges he sustained a first qualifying injury related to 
congenital right eye visual impairment.  Williams relies upon the medical opinion of Dr. 
Bansal to support his contentions and to establish permanent functional impairment of 
the right eye prior to the December 4, 2018, work injury with John Deere.  Dr. Bansal 
assigned 24 percent right eye impairment based on his uncorrected visual acuity.  
Additionally, Williams testified to his ongoing issues with his vision.  Based on this 
evidence, Williams asserts he has proven a qualifying first injury to the right eye.   
The Second Injury Fund of Iowa asserts Williams failed to prove whether 
permanent disability exists with respect to the alleged first qualifying injury.  The Second 
Injury Fund of Iowa produced the expert opinion of Dr. Wilkinson, who questioned the 
methodology for Dr. Bansal’s impairment assessment.  Dr. Wilkinson noted that Dr. 
Bansal’s impairment rating is based upon Williams’ uncorrected visual acuity and, 
according to the AMA Guides, the impairment rating should be based on Williams’ best-
corrected visual acuity.

As an initial matter, the objective medical records in evidence establish that 
Williams has a loss of use with respect to his right eye.  Williams has required corrective 
lenses since he was in the fourth or fifth grade. (Hr. Tr., p. 10)  His pre-employment 
physical documented uncorrected visual acuity scores for the right eye of 20/70 near 
and 20/50 far. (JE1, pp. 1-2)  He has difficulty seeing in low light conditions and he is 
restricted from driving without corrective lenses. (Hr. Tr., p. 12)  The fact claimant’s prior 
loss was congenital does not mean it does not qualify as a prior loss.  Second Injury 
Fund of Iowa v. Shank, 516 N.W.2d 808 (Iowa 1994); Bearden v. Al’s Corner Oil Co., & 
Second Injury Fund of Iowa, File No. 5028208 (App. Feb. 7, 2011). The statute does not 
limit the source, origin or circumstances under which the previous loss occurred as a 
condition for benefits from the Second Injury Fund.  It is therefore concluded that the 
source or origin of the previous loss is immaterial.  I find claimant has satisfied the first 
requirement to prove Fund liability.  
The parties stipulated that Williams sustained a compensable injury to his right 
arm on December 3, 2018, which resulted in six percent right upper extremity 
impairment.  As such, I conclude claimant has also satisfied the second requirement to 
prove Fund liability.   
The primary dispute in this matter centers on whether permanent disability exists 
with respect to the congenital right eye condition.  The third requirement that must be 
met to qualify for Second Injury Fund benefits is that permanent disability must exist as 
to both the initial injury and the second injury. Iowa Code section 85.64.  Permanent 
disability refers to a lasting injury or condition that significantly affects a worker’s ability 
to perform tasks or activities, often resulting in an inability to work or carry out activities 
of daily living.   
The evidentiary record contains two opinions regarding permanent impairment.  
Based on Williams’ uncorrected visual acuity, Dr. Bansal assigned 24 percent right eye 
impairment. (Ex. 1, p. 18)  In comparison, Dr. Wilkinson assigned zero percent right eye 
impairment due to claimant’s best corrected vision being 20/20.  Dr. Wilkinson explained 
that Dr. Bansal’s impairment rating, based on claimant’s uncorrected visual acuity, is in 
conflict with the AMA Guides.   
While Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(x) is not directly applicable to a first qualifying 
injury in a Second Injury Fund claim, it does demonstrate the legislature's preference for 
the use of the AMA Guides to determine permanent functional disability.  Additionally, 
the Iowa Workers' Compensation Commissioner has adopted the AMA Guides, Fifth 
Edition, as the governing standard “for determining the extent of loss or percentage of 
impairment for permanent partial disabilities.” 876 IAC 2.4. 
Both parties discuss the commissioner’s decision in Samaniego v. JTV Mfg., Inc., 
File No. 5049712 (App. July 16, 2018).   
In Samaniego, the commissioner found it appropriate to use claimant’s 
uncorrected vision when determining the threshold inquiry as to whether a permanent 
injury arising out of and in the course of employment had been sustained. (Id.)

However, the impairment rating ultimately accepted by the deputy commissioner – and 
affirmed on appeal – was based on Samaniego’s corrected visual acuity.  In other 
words, a claimant’s uncorrected vision is one factor that is considered when determining 
whether a permanent injury has been sustained; however, the claimant’s corrected 
vision is utilized when establishing the amount of permanent impairment sustained as 
the result of a work injury.  
For these reasons, I conclude claimant’s “best-corrected” vision should be 
utilized when determining impairment of a claimant’s visual acuity.  Dr. Wilkinson clearly 
utilized claimant’s best-corrected vision when calculating claimant’s overall visual 
impairment rating. (Ex. BB, p. 24)  Based on the above rationale, and his superior 
credentials, I accepted Dr. Wilkinson’s zero percent impairment rating as the most 
accurate and convincing impairment rating in the evidentiary record.  I therefore 
concluded that claimant failed to prove permanent disability exists with respect to the 
right eye.   
Claimant has failed to prove entitlement to workers’ compensation benefits from 
the Fund.  As claimant has failed to prove a qualifying first injury for the purposes of 
Fund benefits, all other issues are moot. 
ORDER 
Claimant shall take nothing in the way of Fund benefits from this proceeding. 
The parties shall pay their own costs.
