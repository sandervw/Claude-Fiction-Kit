LaSherryl Watkins, claimant, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from The Views Operator B, LLC, d/b/a The Views Senior Living 
of Marion, employer, and Iowa Long Term Care Risk Management Association, 
insurance carrier, as defendants.  A hearing was held on December 6, 2024. Pursuant 
to an order from the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commissioner, this case was heard 
via videoconference using Zoom with all parties and the court reporter appearing 
remotely.

hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  Those 
stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration decision and 
no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised or discussed 
in this decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.

record also includes joint exhibits 1-9, claimant’s exhibits 1-3, and defendants’ exhibits

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 
employment.  Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 150 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. 
Dial Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309, 311 (Iowa 1996).  The words “arising out of” refer to the 
cause or source of the injury.  The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 
circumstances of the injury.  2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124, 128 (Iowa 
1995).  An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists 
between the injury and the employment.  Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311.  The injury must 
be a rational consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely 
incidental to the employment.  Koehler Electric v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1, 3 (Iowa 2000); 
Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311.  An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it 
happens within a period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may

Page 8 
 
 
be when performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties 
or doing an activity incidental to them.  Ciha, 552 N.W.2d at 150. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony.  The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question.  The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances.  The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410, 420 
(Iowa 2001); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995).  Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994).  Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected.  Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
When an expert's opinion is based upon an incomplete or incorrect history, it is 
not necessarily binding on the commissioner or the court. It is then to be weighed, 
together with other facts and circumstances, the ultimate conclusion being for the finder 
of the fact.  Musselman v. Central Telephone Company, 154 N.W.2d 128, 133 (Iowa 
1967); Bodish v. Fischer, Inc., 257 Iowa 516, 522, 133 N.W.2d 867, 870 (1965).  The 
commissioner as trier of fact has the duty to determine the credibility of the witnesses 
and to weigh the evidence, together with the other disclosed facts and circumstances, 
and then to accept or reject the opinion.  Dunlavey, 526 N.W.2d at 853. 
While a claimant is not entitled to compensation for the results of a preexisting 
injury or disease, its mere existence at the time of a subsequent injury is not a defense. 
Rose v. John Deere Ottumwa Works, 247 Iowa 900, 908, 76 N.W.2d 756, 760-61 
(1956). If the claimant had a preexisting condition or disability that is materially 
aggravated, accelerated, worsened or lighted up so that it results in disability, claimant 
is entitled to recover. Nicks v. Davenport Produce Co., 254 Iowa 130, 135, 115 N.W.2d 
812, 815 (1962); Yeager v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co., 253 Iowa 369, 374-75, 112 
N.W.2d 299, 302 (1961). 
There are three separate causation opinions in the hearing record—Dr. Hart’s, 
Dr. Crites’, and Dr. Broghammer’s. In his January 17, 2024 report, Dr. Hart stated that 
the April 12, 2022 work injury and resulting surgery caused Watkins to overcompensate 
with her right knee causing it to be painful, swell, catch and lock. (JE 8, p. 42).  He 

WATKINS v. THE VIEWS OPERATOR B, LLC 
Page 9 
 
 
repeated this opinion in his May 1, 2024 report to defendants. (Cl Ex. 3, p. 2).  He stated 
“I believe with a reasonable degree of medical certainty that Ms. Watkins’ right knee 
condition was aggravated by her left knee injury on 4/12/2022.” (Id.).    In his IME report, 
Dr. Crites diagnosed Watkins with a medial meniscal tear in the right knee and a 
material aggravation of right knee pre-existing osteoarthritis. (Cl Ex. 2, p. 6).  Like Dr. 
Hart, he causally related these diagnoses to overcompensation after the April 12, 2022 
work injury. (Id.).  In his IME report Dr. Broghammer diagnosed Watkins with grade ¾ 
osteoarthritic changes of the right knee and a degenerative medial meniscal tear of the 
right knee.  (Ex. D, p. 32).  He opined that neither of these conditions were caused 
and/or materially aggravated by the left knee injury on April 12, 2022. (Id. at 33). 
However, this determination is based upon his belief that overcompensating cannot 
actually cause an injury and is not a plausible mechanism of injury in this case.  (Id.). 
He, however, admits that Watkins “may have developed symptoms in her right knee due 
to the pre-existing osteoarthritis because of the way she walked,” but states that is not a 
compensable injury. (Id. at 34).   
Dr. Hart and Dr. Crites agree that the April 12, 2022 injury to Watkins’ left knee 
caused a material aggravation of her pre-existing right knee osteoarthritis. Dr. 
Broghammer admits that Watkins developed symptoms in her right knee because of the 
way she walked after the left knee surgery but claims that overcompensation cannot be 
a source of injury, and therefore her symptoms are not compensable.  I do not find Dr. 
Broghammer’s opinion convincing.  While it is true that employers are not liable for 
personal conditions that merely manifest themselves or become symptomatic at work, 
employers remain liable for any permanent worsening or change to a pre-existing 
condition caused by the work injury. See, e.g., DeJaurez v. IBP, Inc., File Nos. 
1254856, 1254857 (App. January 31, 2003).  So, under the law, an aggravation of a 
pre-existing condition does constitute an injury, and employers are liable for 
compensating the extent of that aggravation. See Tussing v. George A. Hormel & Co., 
461 N.W.2d 450, 453 (Iowa 1990) (holding when a work injury advances disability for a 
latent prior condition, the entire disability is compensable).   
Dr. Broghammer opined that the work incident did not materially aggravate the 
osteoarthritis in Watkins’ right knee, but there is no evidence to support his assertion.  
Watkins never sought medical care for her right knee prior to the April 12, 2022 injury, 
so there are no prior x-rays or MRIs that show the extent of her arthritis before the 
injury. There is no medical evidence to support Dr. Broghammer’s assertion that the 
work incident did not worsen or increase her osteoarthritis level. In fact, the available 
evidence suggests the opposite is true.  Watkins did not need medical treatment for her 
right knee until after the left knee surgery and continued to need it at the time of the 
hearing—thus, there is no evidence her right knee condition ever returned to its 
baseline condition. I find the medical evidence supports the opinions of Dr. Hart and Dr. 
Crites.  Watkins met her burden to prove that the April 12, 2022 work injury caused a 
material aggravation of her pre-existing right knee osteoarthritis.  
Under the Iowa Workers' Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is 
compensated either for a loss or loss of use of a scheduled member under Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(a)-(u) or for loss of earning capacity under section 85.34(2)(v). The

Page 10 
 
 
extent of scheduled member disability benefits to which an injured worker is entitled is 
determined by using the functional method. Functional disability is “limited to the loss of 
the physiological capacity of the body or body part.” Mortimer v. Fruehauf Corp., 502 
N.W.2d 12, 15 (Iowa 1993); Sherman v. Pella Corp., 576 N.W.2d 312, 320 (Iowa 1998).  
Watkins suffered work-related injuries to her bilateral knees.  Thus, her permanent 
impairment is determined under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(t) (2022).  Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(t) states that impairment to “both legs . . . caused by a single accident, 
shall equal five hundred weeks.”  
In all cases of permanent partial disability described in paragraphs “a” through 
“u”, or paragraph “v” when determining functional disability and not loss of earning 
capacity, the extent of loss or percentage of permanent impairment shall be determined 
solely by utilizing the guides to the evaluation of permanent impairment, published by 
the American Medical Association, as adopted by the workers' compensation 
commissioner by rule pursuant to chapter 17A. Iowa Code § 85.34(2)(x). 
Dr. Crites was the only doctor to provide a permanent impairment for Watkins’ 
right knee. He opined Watkins suffered 10 percent permanent impairment to her right 
lower extremity for flexion of less than 110 degrees. (Cl Ex. 2, p. 7).  This impairment 
rating is adopted.  There are two different ratings proposed for Watkins’ left knee.  Dr. 
Hart assigned 2 percent permanent impairment, citing to Table 17-33 of the AMA 
Guides. (JE 7, p. 39).  Dr. Crites assigned 10 percent permanent impairment, also citing 
to Table 17-33 of the AMA Guides.  According to Dr. Crites, the difference in the ratings 
is that Dr. Hart only assigned impairment for repairing one meniscal tear, and Watkins 
had suffered tears to both her medial and lateral menisci, both requiring surgery. (Cl Ex. 
2, pp. 6-7).   
Table 17-33 provides impairment estimates for diagnosis based lower extremity 
impairments. See AMA Guides, pp. 545-547.  According to that Table, a partial medial 
or lateral meniscectomy surgery is assigned 1 percent of the whole person or 2 percent 
of the lower extremity. (Id. at 546).  In contrast, a partial medial and lateral 
meniscectomy surgery is assigned 4 percent of the whole person or 10 percent of the 
lower extremity. (Id.).  The MRI of Watkins’ left knee indicated her lateral meniscus was 
intact. (JE 2, p. 5).  Additionally, in his medical records, Dr. Hart labeled the surgery he 
performed on November 15, 2022, a left knee arthroscopy partial medial meniscectomy 
and chondroplasty. (JE 7, p. 37).  However, in the narrative portion of his operative 
report, Dr. Hart indicates he performed both a partial medial meniscectomy and a partial 
lateral meniscectomy.  (JE 3, p. 8).  Page 2 of the operative report reads,  
With the knee in a figure 4 position, a partial lateral meniscectomy 
was performed with baskets and a shaver, debriding the meniscal remnant 
back to a balanced and stable rim.  
(Id.).  Given the language of the operative report, it appears Dr. Hart did perform both a 
partial medial meniscectomy and partial lateral meniscectomy on Watkins’ left knee. 
The medical evidence supports Dr. Crites’ left knee rating; it is adopted.

Watkins also asserts a claim for alternate medical care.  Specifically, she seeks 
for Dr. Hart to be designated her authorized treater and continue to provide care for her 
right and left knees moving forward.  (Claimant’s Post-Hearing Brief, p.  8).   
Iowa Code section 85.27(4) provides, in relevant part: 
For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish reasonable 
services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has the right to 
choose the care .... The treatment must be offered promptly and be 
reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the 
employee. If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the care 
offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such dissatisfaction 
to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the employer and 
the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited to treat the 
injury. If the employer and employee cannot agree on such alternate care, 
the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable proofs of the 
necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
 
Id. 
In this case, Watkins’ left and right knee complaints were caused and/or 
materially aggravated by the work incident on April 12, 2022.   Dr. Hart has provided 
and recommended intermittent cortisone injections for both knees. (See JE 7, pp. 35, 
38). The injections and administration of the same are related to the work injury.   
 
Watkins has established by a preponderance of the evidence that there is 
reasonable and necessary treatment that can and should be offered to him. Watkins 
has proven she is entitled to alternate medical care, including future intermittent 
injections.  Watkins has requested that this treatment be provided by Dr. Hart, the 
authorized treating physician for her left knee.  In their post-hearing brief, defendants 
state they anticipate using a different provider.  They argue this is warranted because 
Dr. Hart gave a vague opinion regarding the cause of Watkins’ right knee symptoms 
and then refused to speak with defendants’ counsel a second time about the matter. 
(Defendants’ Post-Hearing Brief, p. 5).  Additionally, defendants argue it would be 
“premature for the Commission to preemptively determine who Claimant’s provider 
should be for future unknown treatment,” because defendants have not yet identified a 
replacement treater, therefore the Commission cannot state the authorized provider is 
unreasonable. (Id.).  Defendants’ argument is circular and unpersuasive.

Defendants’ assertion that claimant bears the burden of proof in an alternate care 
action is accurate. Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122, 124 (Iowa 1995).  They 
are also correct that defendants’ “obligation under the statute is confined to reasonable 
care for the diagnosis and treatment of work-related injuries.” Id. (emphasis in original).  
This means that the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant. See Iowa Code § 85.27(4). By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment 
and seeking alternate care, claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care 
is unreasonable. See Iowa R. App. P 14(f)(5); Long, 528 N.W.2d at 124.  Ultimately, 
determining whether care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact. Long, 
528 N.W.2d at 123. 
 
Defendants argue the care they are offering cannot be found unreasonable 
because they haven’t yet chosen a replacement provider for the Commission to assess.  
That, however, is the problem—as of the time of hearing, defendants argued Watkins’ 
right knee condition was not causally related to the work injury on April 12, 2022, and 
were not offering any treatment.   That is not reasonable.  An employer's right to select 
the provider of medical treatment for the injured worker does not include the right to 
determine how an injured worker should be diagnosed, evaluated, treated, or other 
matters of professional medical judgment. Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 
866389 (Declaratory Ruling, May 18, 1988).  An employer is not entitled to interfere with 
the medical judgment of its own treating physician. Pote v. Mickow Corp., File No. 
694639 (Review-Reopening Decision June 17, 1986).   
 
Defendants chose Dr. Hart as the authorized treating physician for the April 12, 
2022 date of injury.  Dr. Hart causally related Watkins’ right knee complaints to the April 
12, 2022 date of injury, and recommended intermittent steroid injections. It was not 
reasonable for defendants to interfere with that judgment. Watkins has established by a 
preponderance of the evidence that there is reasonable and necessary treatment that 
can and should be offered to her.  Watkins has proven that she is entitled to alternate 
care, specifically future intermittent injections.  Defendants shall designate Dr. Hart as 
the authorized treating physician for Watkins’ work-related bilateral knee conditions.  
 
Watkins requests reimbursement of the IME performed by Dr. Crites.  At the start 
of the hearing, defendants’ counsel stipulated that they would reimburse claimant’s IME. 
(Tr., p. 10). However, in their post-hearing brief defendants argued that Watkins is only 
entitled to reimbursement for a third of her IME cost because only one of the three 
issues addressed in Dr. Crites’ IME report is reimbursable. (Defendants’ Post-Hearing 
Brief, pp. 5-6).  According to defendants’ post-hearing brief, Dr. Crites’ IME opined on 
the amount of permanent impairment to Watkins’ left knee, the cause of her right knee 
complaints, and permanent impairment and suggested restrictions for the right knee. 
(Id.).  At hearing, defendants argued they are only liable for Watkins’ left knee condition; 
therefore, they should only be liable for the cost of addressing that issue. (Id.).  See 
Iowa Code § 85.39(2)(stating “An employer is only liable to reimburse an employee for 
the cost of an examination conducted pursuant to this subsection if the injury for which

the employee is being examined is determined to be compensable . . .”).  Watkins, 
however, met her burden to prove a causal relationship between her right knee 
condition and the work injury, as well as established permanent impairment to her right 
knee.  Given this, defendants’ argument is not relevant to this case.  
Iowa Code section 85.39 permits an employee to be reimbursed for a 
subsequent examination by a physician of the employee's choice where an employer-
retained physician has previously evaluated “permanent disability” and the employee 
believes that the initial evaluation is too low. The Iowa Supreme Court has provided a 
literal interpretation of the plain language of Iowa Code section 85.39, stating that 
section only allows the employee to obtain an independent medical evaluation at the 
employer’s expense if an evaluation of permanent disability has already been made by 
an employer retained physician.  Des Moines Area Reg’l Transit Auth. v. Young, 867 
N.W.2d 839, 847 (Iowa 2015).  Dr. Hart, the authorized treating surgeon, provided a 
rating for Watkins’ left knee on November 13, 2023.  Dr. Crites’ IME report was issued 
on October 24, 2024. Given this, Watkins is entitled to reimbursement for at least some 
portion of Dr. Crites’ report.  
Defendants are responsible only for reasonable fees associated with claimant's 
independent medical examination. Claimant has the burden of proving the 
reasonableness of the expenses incurred for the examination. See Schintgen v. 
Economy Fire & Casualty Co., File No. 855298 (App. April 26, 1991).  It is the agency’s 
duty to parse out whether an IME expense is reimbursable under the revised language 
of 85.39(2). Fuller v. Bimbo Bakeries, U.S.A., File No.  20012896.01 (App. Sept. 12, 
2023) (citing to P.M. Lattner Manufacturing Co. v. Rife, 2023 WL 3862594 (Iowa Ct. 
App. June 7, 2023) (unpublished decision).  Dr. Crites charged $4,270.00 for his 
services. (Cl Ex. 1, p. 4).  According to the preface of his IME, this amount includes 
charges for reviewing Watkins’ medical records, interviewing her, performing a physical 
examination, and preparing a report addressing diagnosis, causation, impairment, MMI, 
restrictions, and opinion on future medical care. (Cl Ex. 2, p. 1). According to the Iowa 
Supreme Court, all of these are reimbursable costs under the revised language of Iowa 
Code section 85.39. See Mid-American Construction, LLC v. Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d 838, 
851-852 (Iowa 2024).  Additionally, in his preface, Dr. Crites attests to the 
reasonableness of his IME fees for the local area. (Id.).  Defendants did not submit any 
hearing evidence addressing the reasonableness of Dr. Crites’ fees, they only argued 
the issues he addressed were not compensable.  On this record, I find Watkins is 
entitled to reimbursement of $4,270.00 from defendants pursuant to Iowa Code section 
85.39(2).
