This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A. The 
expedited procedures of rule 876 IAC 4.48, the “alternate medical care” rule, are 
invoked by claimant, James Carney.  
 
This alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on August 29, 2024. The 
proceedings were recorded digitally and constitute the official record of the hearing. By 
an order filed by the Workers’ Compensation Commissioner, this decision is designated 
final agency action. Any appeal would be by petition for judicial review under Iowa Code 
section 17A.15(1).  
 
The record in this case consists of Claimant’s Exhibits 1, Defendants’ Exhibit A, 
and the testimony of claimant. 
ISSUE 
 
The issue presented for resolution in this case is whether claimant is entitled to 
alternate medical care consisting of authorization and payment for claimant’s Complex 
Regional Pain Syndrome (CRPS) and to Mark Kline, M.D.  
FINDING OF FACTS 
 
Defendants admit liability for an injury to claimant’s right lower extremity 
occurring on June 7, 2021.  Claimant fractured his tibia and fibula on his right leg in that 
accident.  Defendants deny liability for an alleged complex regional pain syndrome 
(CRPS) and an injury to the right hand. 
 
On June 10, 2024, claimant filed a petition for alternate medical care in this 
matter.  That petition requested authorization for treatment of claimant’s complex

CONCLUSION OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3).   
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Section 85.27.  Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening, October 1975).   
 By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See Iowa 
R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e); Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  
Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.  Id.  The 
employer’s obligation turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.  Id.; 
Harned v. Farmland Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983).  In Pirelli-Armstrong Tire 
Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 433 (Iowa 1997), the court approvingly quoted Bowles v. 
Los Lunas Schools, 109 N.M. 100, 781 P.2d 1178 (App. 1989):   
[T]he words “reasonable” and “adequate” appear to describe the same 
standard.   
[The New Mexico rule] requires the employer to provide a certain 
standard of care and excuses the employer from any obligation to provide 
other services only if that standard is met.  We construe the terms 
"reasonable” and “adequate” as describing care that is both appropriate to 
the injury and sufficient to bring the worker to maximum recovery.   
 
In a Consent Order dated July 22, 2024, defendants agreed to authorize and pay 
for treatment for claimant’s CRPS with a pain specialist recommended by Dr. Hogue. 
 
Five weeks after that consent was filed, claimant still has not received treatment.  
Claimant contends the delay is unreasonable and requests to be treated by Dr. Kline.

I agree with claimant that a five-week delay in treatment is unreasonable.  
However, the record indicates the delay has largely been caused by the provider, the 
UIHC.  The record indicates defendants have attempted to get claimant treatment with 
the UIHC, but that due, in large part, to the bureaucracy at the UIHC, treatment has 
been delayed. 
 
Because the record suggests the delay in this case is not caused by the 
defendants, I cannot find in claimant’s favor, at this time.  However, claimant’s treatment 
should not continue to be delayed due to inefficiencies at the UIHC.  Defendants shall, 
within two weeks from the date of this decision, have a date for claimant’s treatment for 
CRPS at the UIHC.  If defendants are unable to have a date for treatment at the UIHC 
at that time, defendants shall, within two weeks of the date of this decision, have a date 
for treatment for claimant’s CRPS with another provider. 
ORDER 
 
Claimant’s petition for alternate medical care is denied at this time.  Defendants 
shall, within two weeks from the date of this decision, have a date for claimant’s 
treatment for CRPS at the UIHC.  If defendants are unable to have a date for treatment 
at the UIHC at that time, defendants shall, within two weeks of the date of this decision 
have a date for treatment for claimant’s CRPS with another provider.
