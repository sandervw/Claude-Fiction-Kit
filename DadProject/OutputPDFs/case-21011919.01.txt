STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
 
Claimant Suvad Sehic filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits against Titan Tire Corporation, employer, and Liberty Mutual 
Insurance Company, insurer, for an accepted work injury date of September 22, 2021. 
The case came before the undersigned for an arbitration hearing on May 29, 2024. 
Pursuant to an order of the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commissioner, this case 
proceeded to a live video hearing via Zoom, with all parties and the court reporter 
appearing remotely. The hearing proceeded without significant difficulties. 
 
The parties filed a hearing report prior to the commencement of the hearing. On 
the hearing report, the parties entered into numerous stipulations. Those stipulations 
were accepted and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be 
made or discussed. The parties are now bound by their stipulations. 
 
The evidentiary record includes Joint Exhibits 1 through 10, Claimant’s Exhibits 1 
through 4, and Defendants’ Exhibits A through C.  
 
Claimant testified on his own behalf through interpreter Ana Temim. The 
evidentiary record closed at the conclusion of the evidentiary hearing on May 29, 2024. 
The parties submitted post-hearing briefs on July 22, 2024, and the case was 
considered fully submitted on that date.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
The undersigned, having considered all of the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
 
Claimant’s testimony was consistent as compared to the evidentiary record, and 
his demeanor at the time of hearing gave the undersigned no reason to doubt his 
veracity. Claimant is found credible. 
 
At the time of hearing, claimant was 52 years old. (Hearing Transcript, p. 9) He 
was born in Bosnia, where he attended school through the eighth grade. (Tr., pp. 8-9) 
He came to the United States in 1998, and has lived in Iowa since that time. (Tr., p. 9) 
He speaks some English, but was more comfortable using an interpreter for the hearing 
since it is a legal proceeding. (Tr., p. 8) Before coming to the U.S., claimant worked as a 
farmer in Bosnia, with crops including potatoes, corn, and tomatoes. (Tr., p. 10) He 
never had any injuries while working in Bosnia, and was physically able to perform all 
the tasks required of farming. (Tr., p. 11) 
 
After moving to Iowa, claimant’s first job was working for Hanings Construction. 
He testified that the company made “chicken buildings,” and his job was to install 
plywood and clean. He described it as a physical job, and he was able to perform all of 
the work duties. (Tr., pp. 11-12) He did not sustain any injuries while working for 
Hanings. (Tr., p. 12) Claimant left Hanings during the winter season to work at Titan 
Tire, with the intention of returning to Hanings the following spring. He testified that he 
preferred to work outside at that time. However, by the next spring, he had decided to 
stay at Titan. 
 
Claimant had to take a physical when he started at Titan, which he passed with 
no problems. (Tr., pp. 12-13) He did not have any physical limitations or restrictions on 
his activities when he was hired. (Tr., p. 13) His first position involved building tires, and 
was hard manual work. He testified he was making large tires for combines and 
tractors. He said the heaviest materials they worked with weighed around 100 to 150 
pounds. (Tr., pp. 13-14)

Claimant testified that the machine is one that is used for grinding tires. He does

With respect to impairment, using the fifth edition of the AMA Guides, Dr. 
Kuhnlein used the same table as Dr. Smith, Table 15-3, but assigned 12 percent of the 
whole person, as opposed to the 10 percent Dr. Smith assigned. (Cl. Ex. 1, p. 8) Dr. 
Kuhnlein explained he chose the higher number because claimant has a combination of 
radiculopathy and multilevel posterior element fractures, which he opined would justify a 
greater than minimum rating within the category. 
 
With respect to the abdominal injury, Dr. Kuhnlein assigned 1 percent whole 
person impairment for the keloid abdominal scar, based on Table 8-2. He further noted 
that claimant’s surgical team had to repair and ligate the superior mesenteric artery and 
vein, which represents a “derangement of the bowel/vascular anatomy with surgery 
needed to repair the traumatic injury and restore it to its previous function.” As part of 
that treatment, claimant had a partial omentectomy, representing a loss of part of the 
organic structure within the abdomen. Dr. Kuhnlein explained that the “organic 
treatment methodologies” outlined in the AMA Guides do not specifically address loss of 
the omentum or repair of vascular injuries. However, those injuries represent “a loss, 
loss of use, or derangement of these systems within the abdomen,” meaning that 
assignment of impairment would be appropriate under the definition of impairment in the 
Guides. Therefore, using the principles outlined in Section 2.5g, he assigned an 
additional 2 percent of the whole person for the omentectomy and vascular repair. 
Using the combined values chart, Dr. Kuhnlein provided a total impairment rating of 15 
percent of the whole person. (Cl. Ex. 1, p. 9) 
 
I find Dr. Kuhnlein’s rating to be a more accurate representation of claimant’s 
total functional impairment. Dr. Smith’s rating did not include any consideration of 
claimant’s radicular complaints and multilevel posterior element fractures, which Dr. 
Kuhnlein opined justified a slightly higher rating. Additionally, no other physician 
provided an impairment rating for claimant’s severe abdominal injuries. Dr. Kuhnlein’s 
rating is detailed, well supported by the medical records, and justified by the AMA 
Guides. As such, I accept Dr. Kuhnlein’s impairment rating.

be able to do that kind of work.  (Tr., p. 33) In other words, a job search would be futile.  
 
Considering claimant’s age, level of education, work history, and permanent 
restrictions, I find he is permanently and totally disabled.  
 
CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence. Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the 
evidence that the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is 
based. A cause is proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it 
need not be the only cause. A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal 
connection is probable rather than merely possible. George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 
569 N.W.2d 148, 153 (Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 
156 (Iowa Ct. App. 1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa 
Ct. App. 1996).

The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410, 420 
(Iowa 2001); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994).  
The parties stipulated that if the injury is found to be a cause of permanent 
disability, the disability is an industrial disability. (See Hearing Report, p. 2) Industrial 
disability was defined in Diederich v. Tri-City Ry. Co. of Iowa, 219 Iowa 587, 591, 258 
N.W. 899, 902 (1935) as follows: “It is therefore plain that the Legislature intended the 
term ‘disability’ to mean ‘industrial disability’ or loss of earning capacity and not a mere 
‘functional disability’ to be computed in terms of percentages of the total physical and 
mental ability of a normal [person].” 
Functional impairment is an element to be considered in determining industrial 
disability which is the reduction of earning capacity, but consideration must also be 
given to the injured employee’s age, education, qualifications, experience, motivation, 
loss of earnings, severity and situs of the injury, work restrictions, inability to engage in 
employment for which the employee is fitted and the employer’s offer of work or failure 
to so offer. McSpadden v. Big Ben Coal Co., 288 N.W.2d 181, 192 (Iowa 1980); Olson 
v. Goodyear Service Stores, 255 Iowa 1112, 1120, 125 N.W.2d 251, 256 (1963); Barton 
v. Nevada Poultry Co., 253 Iowa 285, 110 N.W.2d 660, 663 (1961). The commissioner 
may also consider claimant’s medical condition prior to the injury, immediately after the 
injury, and presently in rendering an evaluation of industrial disability. IBP, Inc. v. Al-
Gharib, 604 N.W.2d 621, 632-633 (Iowa 2000) (citing McSpadden, 288 N.W.2d at 192). 
Total disability does not mean a state of absolute helplessness. Permanent total 
disability occurs where the injury wholly disables the employee from performing work 
that the employee’s experience, training, education, intelligence, and physical capacities 
would otherwise permit the employee to perform. See McSpadden v. Big Ben Coal Co., 
288 N.W.2d 181, 192 (Iowa 1980); Diederich, 219 Iowa 587, 258 N.W. 899 (1935). 
The focus for evaluating total disability is on the person’s ability to earn a living.  
Diederich, 258 N.W. at 902. The question is whether the person is capable of 
performing a sufficient quantity and quality of work that an employer in a well-
established branch of the labor market would employ the person on a continuing basis 
and pay the person sufficient wages to permit the person to be self-supporting.  Tobin-
Nichols v. Stacyville Community Nursing Home, File No. 1222209 (App. December 
2003). A finding that claimant could perform some work despite claimant's physical and 
educational limitations does not foreclose a finding of permanent total disability, 
however. See Chamberlin v. Ralston Purina, File No. 661698 (App. October 1987);

Eastman v. Westway Trading Corp., II Iowa Industrial Commissioner Report 134 (App. 
May 1982). Industrial disability is determined by the effect the injury has on the 
employee’s earning capacity. Bearce v. FMC Corp., 465 N.W.2d 531, 535 (Iowa 1991); 
Trade Professionals, Inc. v. Shriver, 661 N.W.2d 119, 123 (Iowa Ct. App. 2003). 
Another important factor in the consideration of permanent and total disability 
cases is the employer’s ability to retain the injured worker with an offer of suitable work.  
The refusal or inability of the employer to return a claimant to work in any capacity is, by 
itself, significant evidence of a lack of employability. Clinton v. All-American Homes, File 
No. 5032603 (App. April 17, 2013); Western v. Putco, Inc., File Nos. 5005190, 5005191 
(App. July 29, 2005); Pierson v. O’Bryan Brothers, File No. 951206 (App. January 20, 
1995); Meeks v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co., File No. 876894 (App. January 22, 1993); 
see also Larson, Workers’ Compensation Law, Section 57.61, pp. 10-164.90-95; 
Sunbeam Corp. v. Bates, 271 Ark 385, 609 S.W.2d 102 (1980); Army & Air Force 
Exchange Service v. Neuman, 278 F.Supp. 865 (W.D. La 1967); Leonardo v. Uncas 
Manufacturing Co., 77 R.I. 245, 75 A.2d 188 (1950). An employer knows the demands 
that are placed on its workforce. Its determination that the worker is too disabled for it to 
employ is entitled to considerable weight. If the employer in whose employ the disability 
occurred is unwilling or unable to accommodate the disability, there is no reason to 
expect some other employer to have more incentive to do so. 
Defendants argue that claimant has not proven that he is permanently and totally 
disabled, and argue his lack of motivation, lack of significant physical restrictions, and 
prior experience training and supervising employees preclude an award of permanent 
total disability. Defendants also argue that claimant “demonstrated the ability to start his 
own construction company.” (Def. Brief, p. 8) However, defendants misrepresent 
claimant’s testimony. Claimant specifically testified that he started an LLC in 2022 in 
order to help secure a bank loan so he could build a one-story house, since his current 
house has stairs. (Tr., p. 30) His plan was to hire others to do the actual work on 
building the house, and he would essentially act as a “general contractor” in order to 
secure the loan. However, the company “never took off,” and no house was built. (Tr., 
pp. 29-30) In other words, claimant did not “start his own construction company” in any 
meaningful way. 
With respect to claimant’s motivation, he has testified several times that it would 
be pointless to look for work consistent with his past experience, as he knows what 
would be required of him in those jobs, and he knows he is not physically capable of 
performing the work. His restrictions are not insignificant when considered against his 
work history. He has a permanent 20-pound lifting restriction, and restrictions against 
repetitive lifting, bending, climbing, and twisting. He has to change positions frequently, 
and has constant numbness in his right leg and pain in his back and hip. He was visibly 
uncomfortable during the hearing. His only past experience involves physically 
demanding work. Unlike the claimant in Kurdic v. Tyson Fresh Meats, File No. 5055279 
(App. July 10, 2020), his employer of 23 years was not able to offer him a position within 
his restrictions following the injury. It is unrealistic to expect some other employer to 
have more incentive to do so.

I have considered claimant’s age, education, employment history, inability to 
return to the prior jobs he has held, his ability to retrain, his motivation, the situs and 
severity of his injuries, his impairment and restrictions, and all other industrial disability 
factors identified by the Iowa Supreme Court. I find claimant’s restrictions, combined 
with his prior work experience and education, preclude him from performing his past 
jobs.  
Having considered all of the evidence in the record, the greater weight of 
evidence supports the finding that claimant is permanently and totally disabled as a 
result of his September 22, 2021 injury. Realistically, there are no jobs within the 
community that claimant can perform and for which he can realistically compete in his 
current condition. Second Injury Fund of Iowa v. Shank, 516 N.W.2d 808, 815 (Iowa 
1994). Accordingly, I conclude that claimant proved he is permanently and totally 
disabled.  
Permanent total disability compensation is payable during the period of the 
employee’s disability. As such, permanent total disability benefits generally commence 
on the date of injury. See Sandhu v. Nordstrom, Inc., File No. 5046628 (App. January 
24, 2019). As such, permanent total disability benefits commence on September 22, 
2021. Defendants are entitled to a credit for all weekly benefits previously paid, whether 
classified as temporary or permanent disability benefits. 
 
The next issue to determine is the correct weekly rate of compensation. Iowa 
Code section 85.36 states the basis of compensation is the weekly earnings of the 
employee at the time of the injury. The section defines weekly earnings as the gross 
salary, wages, or earnings to which an employee would have been entitled had the 
employee worked the customary hours for the full pay period in which the employee 
was injured as the employer regularly required for the work or employment. The various 
subsections of section 85.36 set forth methods of computing weekly earnings 
depending upon the type of earnings and employment.  
Both parties used section 85.36(6) to calculate claimant’s rate. That section 
states that if the employee is paid on a daily or hourly basis or by output, weekly 
earnings are computed by dividing by 13 the earnings, including shift differential pay but 
not including overtime or premium pay, over the 13-week period immediately preceding 
the injury. However, any week that does not fairly reflect the employee’s customary 
earnings is excluded, and replaced with the closest previous week with earnings that 
fairly represent the employee’s customary earnings. 
The parties agree that claimant was single and entitled to 4 exemptions on the 
date of injury. Claimant has calculated the average weekly wage to be $1,635.23, with a 
weekly benefit rate of $997.99. (Cl. Ex. 3, p. 16) Defendants have calculated the 
average weekly wage to be $1,321.81, with a rate of $836.37. The difference comes 
from the weeks each party included in the calculation. Claimant excluded any week that 
was under 48 hours, while defendants included all weeks with more than 40 hours.

Claimant argues that he regularly worked full time plus overtime, which is why 
only weeks with at least 48-hours should be included in the rate calculation. Claimant 
testified that at the time of the injury, he was the only mechanic in his area so he was 
working every Saturday, as well as Monday through Friday for 12-hour shifts. (Tr., p. 15) 
He stated that he rarely only worked 40 hours in a typical week. (Tr., p. 16) In reviewing 
the wage records, however, that does not appear to be accurate for the weeks 
immediately prior to the injury date. (Cl. Ex. 3, pp. 17-18) It appears he was working 
more hours earlier in the year, but by June of 2021 was more consistently working 
between 40 and 48 hours per week, with some additional overtime on occasion. 
 
Defendants argue that pursuant to the Commissioner’s decision in Ratliff v. 
Quaker Oats, File No. 5046704 (App., Jan. 5, 2017), all weeks that contain 40-hours or 
more should be included in the rate calculation. In Ratliff, the Commissioner held that 40 
hours per week is considered full time employment. As such, he held that any week of 
40 hours or more is a representative week under Iowa Code section 85.36(6), 
regardless of how little or how much overtime is worked during that week, and 
regardless of how many other weeks are worked with overtime. Ratliff, p. 6. 
 
Given the Commissioner’s holding in Ratliff, I find that all weeks prior to the date 
of injury in which claimant worked at least 40 hours are representative, and should be 
included in the rate calculation. I have calculated the rate as follows:
