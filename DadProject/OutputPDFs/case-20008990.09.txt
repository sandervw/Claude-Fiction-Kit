This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A. The 
expedited procedures of rule 876 IAC 4.48, the “alternate medical care” rule, are 
invoked by claimant, Joshua Hanawalt.  
This alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on July 24, 2024. The 
proceedings were recorded digitally and constitute the official record of the hearing. By 
an order filed by the Workers’ Compensation Commissioner, this decision is designated 
final agency action. Any appeal would be by petition for judicial review under Iowa Code 
section 17A.15(1).  
The record in this case consists of Claimant’s Exhibits 1-4, Defendants’ Exhibit 1, 
and the testimony of claimant 
ISSUE 
The issue presented for resolution in this case is whether claimant is entitled to 
alternate medical care consisting of an order from this agency for defendants to approve 
and pay for treatment within seven days and sanctions. 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
Defendants accept liability for a work-related injury to claimant’s low back on 
June 25, 2020.

CONCLUSION OF LAW  
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3).

The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Section 85.27.  Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening October 16, 1975).          
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See Iowa 
R. App. P. 6.904(3); Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995). 
Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.  Id.  The 
employer’s obligation turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.  Id.; 
Harned v. Farmland Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983).            
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).     
In the June of 2022 arbitration decision, defendants were ordered to provide 
medical care for claimant.  Since that time, this agency has heard six different petitions 
for alternate medical care because defendants have delayed care or ignored the 
recommendations of the authorized provider.  As a result of this record, in the April 19, 
2024, alternate medical care decision, claimant was allowed to direct his own medical 
care at the defendants’ expense. 
Despite this, the payment of outstanding medical bills, care that was previously 
ordered by this agency, and the authorization of treatments recommended by Dr. 
Spooner, continues to be delayed. 
I appreciate the efforts of defense counsel to pay medical bills and authorize care 
recommended by Dr. Spooner.  However, the bills at issue date back to September of 
2023.  Defendants have an affirmative duty to authorize, provide and pay for the care 
ordered by this agency and recommended by Dr. Spooner.  The record indicates 
defendants have only recently made efforts to pay bills and provide care as a response 
to the most recent filing of a petition for alternate medical care. 
I find defendants failure to pay for and authorize care recommended by the 
authorized provider and ordered by this agency unreasonable.  As noted in the April of 
2024 alternate medical care decision, it is also found unreasonable claimant should 
have to file six different petitions for alternate medical care for one date of injury that 
should have been resolved by the June of 2022 arbitration decision.

Defendants are ordered to pay the medical bills detailed in Exhibit 1 and 2, that 
were previously ordered by this agency.  Defendants are also ordered to authorize, 
provide and pay for the laser therapy, acupuncture, biofeedback and cryotherapy. 
Claimant has also requested sanctions in this case given the repeated failure to 
comply with recommendations of care by the authorized provider and the repeated 
failure to comply with agency orders. 
Rule 876 IAC 4.36 states in relevant part that: 
… any party to a contested case or an attorney representing such party 
shall fail to comply with these rules or any order of a deputy workers’ 
compensation commissioner or the workers’ compensation commissioner, 
the deputy workers’ compensation commissioner or workers’ 
compensation commissioner may impose sanctions which may include 
dismissing the action without prejudice, excluding or limiting evidence, 
assessing costs or expenses, and closing the record in whole or in part 
to further activity by the party. (Emphasis added) 
Given the repeated noncompliance with agency orders in these alternate medical 
care decisions, defendants shall pay claimant’s attorney’s costs and fees involved with 
preparing for and presenting this case.  Defendants may be required to pay claimant’s 
attorney’s fees in the future if future noncompliance with agency orders occurs. 
ORDER 
Claimant’s petition for alternate medical care is granted.  Defendants are ordered 
to pay bills detailed in Exhibit 1 and 2, as previously ordered by this agency, within 
seven (7) business days.  
Defendants shall authorize, provide and pay for laser therapy, acupuncture, 
biofeedback and cryotherapy for claimant, within seven (7) business days.  
Defendants shall also pay claimant’s attorney’s costs and fees involved with 
preparing for and presenting this case.
