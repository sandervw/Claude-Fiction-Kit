On March 15, 2024, the above-captioned matter came before this Court for hearing. 
Petitioner Leisa Brown (“Brown”) was represented by Erik Luthens. Respondents Fort Dodge 
Community School District (“FDCSD”) and Employers Mutual Casualty Company appeared 
through Brian Scieszinski, and the Second Injury Fund of Iowa was represented by Meredith 
Cooney. After hearing the arguments of Counsel and reviewing the court file, including the briefs 
filed by the parties and the Certified Administrative Record (“Cert. Rec.”), the Court enters this 
Order.  
I. 
BACKGROUND FACTS.

appeal stems from an incident on December 11, 2020, where a chair that Brown was sitting on 
broke. Arbitration Decision (“Arb. Dec.”) 2. After this accident, Brown reported severe pain in her 
right knee. Id. She was later diagnosed with a medial meniscal tear, though her treating doctor, 
Benjamin Beecher, M.D., was unsure how much of her pain was related to that tear and how much 
was related to her underlying arthritis. Id. at 3. On May 6, 2021, after a few months of physical 
therapy and injections, Dr. Beecher determined Brown had reached maximum medical

II. 
STANDARD OF REVIEW. 
 
The Iowa Administrative Procedure Act, Iowa Code chapter 17A, governs the scope of the 
Court’s review in workers’ compensation cases. Iowa Code § 10A.322(1); Meyer v. IBP, Inc., 710 
N.W.2d 213, 218 (Iowa 2006). A party challenging agency action bears the burden of 
demonstrating the action's invalidity and resulting prejudice. Iowa Code § 17A.19(8)(a). This can 
be shown in a number of ways, including proof the action was ultra vires; legally erroneous; 
unsupported by substantial evidence in the record when that record is viewed as a whole; or 
otherwise, unreasonable, arbitrary, capricious, or an abuse of discretion. See id. § 17A.19(10).  
The district court acts in an appellate capacity to correct errors of law on the part of the 
agency. Grundmeyer v. Weyerhaeuser Co., 649 N.W.2d 744, 748 (Iowa 2002). Where the issue is 
one of fact, the Court must accept the agency’s factual findings unless they are “not supported by

III. 
ANALYSIS. 
Brown argues that the Deputy Commissioner and the Commissioner committed reversible 
error by not awarding permanent partial disability benefits, by ruling that Brown was not qualified 
to receive Second Injury Fund benefits, and by not awarding Brown costs. 
When evaluating an agency finding regarding permanent disability, a reviewing court may 
reverse if the finding is not supported by substantial evidence. See Hill v. Fleetguard, Inc., 705 
N.W.2d 665, 673 (Iowa 2005). When determining if there is substantial evidence, “[t]he ultimate 
question is not whether the evidence supports a different finding but whether the evidence supports 
the findings actually made.” Gaskey v. Iowa Dep’t of Transp., Motor Vehicle Div., 537 N.W.2d 
695, 698 (Iowa 1995). In this particular case, the Deputy Commissioner found that the only expert 
that supported a finding of permanent disability, Dr. Stoken, was not reliable. As the finder of fact, 
the agency determines the weight to assign an expert opinion, assessing the accuracy of the facts 
provided to the expert as well as other surrounding circumstances. Sherman v. Pella Corp., 576 
N.W.2d 312, 321 (Iowa 1998). The agency may reject or accept the expert evidence entirely or in 
part. Cedar Rapids Cmty. Sch. Dist. v. Pease, 807 N.W.2d 839, 850 (Iowa 2011). In our appellate 
posture, we “are not at liberty to accept contradictory opinions of other experts in order to reject 
the finding of the commissioner.” Id. (internal quotations omitted).

Deputy Commissioner Pals articulated specific reasons that Dr. Stoken’s findings were not 
credible, and these reasons are each supported by substantial evidence in the record. She found 
that Dr. Stoken did not take into account Brown’s previous knee injury. There are several pieces 
of evidence that support this finding. In her report, Dr. Stoken wrote that Brown “states that about 
one year prior to this injury she heard a pop in her knee. She did not seek care for it.” Cert. Rec. 
353. As the Deputy Commissioner noted, this is plainly contradicted by the records provided from 
Active Health Clinic, where Brown sought treatment for her knee prior to the chair collapse 
incident on December 11, 2020. It shows that she received treatment there thirteen times in 2020, 
and at every single one of them she received low-voltage electronic muscle stimulation on her right 
knee. See generally id. at 201-226 Despite this, Dr. Stoken’s report summarizes her review of the 
records from Active Health Clinic thusly: 
On 01/15/2020 she was seen and evaluated by Joshua Mason, DC with complaints 
of right knee pain after sitting too long. She was given chiropractic care to the 
lumbar pelvic and thoracic region. She was last seen on 12/16/2020. 
 
Id. at 354 (emphasis added). Whether purposeful or accidental, the complete omission of treatment 
provided for Brown’s knee prior to the incident at issue could certainly give a fact-finder pause. 
 
The Deputy Commissioner also concluded that Dr. Stoken’s evaluation did not comply 
with the AMA Guides. The Workers’ Compensation Commission has adopted the standards of the 
AMA Guides “for determining the extent of loss or percentage of impairment for permanent partial 
disabilities . . . not involving a determination of reduction in an employee's earning capacity.” Iowa 
Admin. Code r. 876-2.4. The agency relied on the following language from the AMA Guides: 
Lower extremity impairment can be evaluated by assessing the range of motion of 
its joints, recognizing that pain and motivation may affect the measurements. If it 
is clear to the evaluator that a restricted range of motion has an organic basis, three 
measurements should be obtained and the greatest range measured should be used. 
If multiple evaluations exist, and there is inconsistency of a rating class between 
the findings of two observers, or in the findings on separate occasions by the same

observer, the results are considered invalid. 
 
Arb. Dec. 6 (citing AM. MED. ASS’N, GUIDES TO THE EVALUATION OF PERMANENT IMPAIRMENT § 
17.2f (5th ed. 2000)). Deputy Commissioner Pals found that Dr. Stoken’s analysis did not comport 
with this section in two regards. First, Dr. Stoken’s report concluded that Brown’s permanent 
impairment of her right knee was based solely on the limitation to flexion contracture. Cert. Rec. 
359; Arb. Dec. 7. This was only one measurement, rather than the three recommended by the 
AMA. Second, other professionals who evaluated Brown’s knee came to different measurements 
and conclusions. On several occasions, most recently on March 23, 2021, her physical therapist 
took measurements that would place Brown in the “no impairment” class under the AMA Guides. 
Cert. Rec. 266; Arb. Dec. 7. On November 7, 2022, after she moved to Texas, Brown saw Benzel 
MacMaster, MD. Cert. Rec. 419-21. Dr. MacMaster’s measurements of Brown’s knee would place 
her in the “mild impairment” class. Arb. Dec. 7. Dr. Stoken had concluded that Brown was in the 
“moderate impairment” class. Cert. Rec. 359; Arb. Dec. 7. Deputy Commissioner Pals reasoned 
that these different ratings meant that, under the AMA Guides, the results are invalid. Arb. Dec. 7. 
In sum, there is substantial evidence to support both conclusions that led Deputy 
Commissioner Pals to find Dr. Stoken’s report to be unreliable. Having found Brown’s expert 
unreliable, it was reasonable to conclude that Brown had failed to show a permanent disability by 
the preponderance of the evidence. The Commission’s finding on this issue is affirmed. 
Because the finding that Brown does not have a permanent disability is affirmed, the 
Commission’s rulings regarding the Second Injury Fund and costs are also affirmed. 
IV. 
DISPOSITION. 
IT IS THE ORDER OF THE COURT that Decision of the Workers’ Compensation 
Commission is AFFIRMED. The Petition for Judicial Review is dismissed.
