STATEMEN T OF TH E C AS E 
Claimant Regina Ryner seeks workers’ compensation benefits from the 
defendants, employer Tenneco and insurance carrier Corvel. The undersigned presided 
over an arbitration hearing on May 28, 2024. Ryner participated personally and through 
attorney Nicholas G. Pothitakis. The defendants participated by and through attorney 
Michael S. Roling. 
II.  
ISSU ES  
Under Iowa Administrative Code rule 876—4.19(3)(f), the parties jointly 
submitted a hearing report defining the claims, defenses, and issues submitted to the 
presiding deputy commissioner. The hearing report was approved and entered into the 
record via an order because it is a correct representation of the disputed issues and 
stipulations in this case. The parties identified the following disputed issues in the 
hearing report: 
1) Did Ryner sustain an injury arising out of and in the course of her 
employment with Tenneco on August 6, 2021? 
2) Is Ryner entitled to temporary total disability (TTD) or healing period (HP) 
benefits from August 6, 2021, through October 18, 2021? 
3) What is the nature and extent of permanent disability, if any, caused by the 
alleged injury?

STIP U LATION S

On August 6, 2021, Ryner was working a 12-hour shift for Tenneco at the over-
sort table. During a typical shift, Ryner lifted between 60 and 80 trays of spark plugs. 
Lifting trays of spark plugs was part of Ryner’s assigned job duties that day. (Testimony) 
At around 2:00 p.m., Ryner was in a standing position and leaned over a table to 
pick up a tray of spark plugs at slightly higher than shoulder height. As soon as Ryner 
picked up the tray, she felt a pop in the middle of her back, just below her shoulder 
blades, and the onset of pain. She immediately set the tray down. This was the first time 
in her life she had experienced this type of back pain. (Testimony) 
Based on the undersigned’s firsthand observations of Ryner’s demeanor during 
her hearing testimony, she was credible. Ryner credibly testified that she experienced 
no symptoms in her back similar to those she experienced after lifting the tray of 
sparkplugs at Tenneco on August 6, 2021. Reinforcing this conclusion is the lack of any 
evidence to the contrary. The weight of the evidence establishes Ryner’s back was 
asymptomatic until immediately after she felt a pop in it while lifting the tray of 
sparkplugs during her shift at Tenneco. 
The parties dispute the weight of the tray of spark plugs that caused Ryner to 
experience a pop in her back and the onset of pain while lifting it. Initially, Tenneco took 
the position that the tray weighed between five and seven pounds. Later, it changed its 
position, asserting the tray weighed about 12 pounds.

C ON C LU SION S OF LAW. 
In 2017, the Iowa legislature amended the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Act. 
See 2017 Iowa Acts, ch. 23. The 2017 amendments took effect on July 1, 2017. Id. at § 
24(1); see also Iowa Code § 3.7(1). Because the alleged injury at issue in this case 
occurred after July 1, 2017, the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Act, as amended in 2017, 
applies. See Stiles v. Annett Holdings, Inc., Iowa Workers’ Comp. Comm’r No. 5064673, 
2020 WL 6037539 *2 (App., Oct. 2, 2020). The alleged injury in this case took place on 
August 6, 2021, so that year’s edition of the Iowa Code governs. 
A .  
C a u s a t i o n .  
The Iowa Workers’ Compensation Act “covers any and all personal injuries” 
relating to an employee’s work. Hanson v. Dickinson, 188 Iowa 728, 176 N.W. 823, 824 
(1920). Under section 85.3(1), an employer covered by the Act must “provide, secure, 
and pay compensation according to the provisions of this chapter for any and all 
personal injuries sustained by an employee arising out of and in the course of the 
employment.” The legislature has codified a partial definition of “personal injury” in 

RYNER V. TENNECO 
Page 12 
 
section 85.61(4) that is not applicable in the current case. Rather, the expanded 
definition of “personal injury” as articulated by the Iowa Supreme Court in its seminal 
Almquist v. Shenandoah Nurseries, Inc. governs: 
“an injury to the body, the impairment of health, or a disease, not excluded 
by the act, which comes about, not through the natural building up and 
tearing down of the human body, but because of a traumatic or other hurt 
or damage to the health or body of an employee.... The injury to the 
human body here contemplated must be something, whether an accident 
or not, that acts extraneously to the natural processes of nature, and 
thereby impairs the health, overcomes, injures, interrupts, or destroys 
some function of the body, or otherwise damages or injures a part or all of 
the body.”  
Dunlavey v. Econ. Fire & Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 850–51 (Iowa 1995) (quoting 
Almquist, 218 Iowa 724, 732, 254 N.W. 35, 39 (1934)) (citations omitted in Dunlavey). 
“In order for an injury to be compensable in Iowa, there must be ‘a connection 
between the injury and the work.’” Lakeside Casino v. Blue, 743 N.W.2d 169, 173 (Iowa 
2007) (quoting Meyer v. IBP, Inc., 710 N.W.2d 213, 221 (Iowa 2006)). To recover, a 
claimant must prove by a preponderance of the evidence that the injury for which the 
claimant is seeking workers’ compensation arose: 
1) Out of the claimant’s employment; and 
 
2) In the course of the claimant’s employment. St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 
N.W.2d 646, 652 (Iowa 2000) (citing Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 
143, 150 (Iowa 1996)); see also Miedema v. Dial Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309, 311 
(Iowa 1996).  
“‘The two tests are separate and distinct.’” Blue, 743 N.W.2d at 174 (quoting 
Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311). Under Iowa law, “‘both must be satisfied in order for an 
injury to be deemed compensable.’” Id. (quoting Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311). In this 
case, the parties dispute only whether the injury arose out of employment. 
“An injury ‘arises out of’ employment if there is a causal connection between the 
employment and the injury.” Gray, 604 N.W.2d at 652 (citing Bailey v. Batchelder, 576 
N.W.2d 334, 338 (Iowa 1998)). “‘In other words, the injury must not have coincidentally 
occurred while at work, but must in some way be caused by or related to the working 
environment or the conditions of [the] employment.’” Blue, 743 N.W.2d at 174. (quoting 
Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311).  
Taking the words themselves, one is first struck by the fact that in the 
“arising” phrase the function of the employment is passive while in the 
“caused by” phrase it is active. When one speaks of an event “arising out 
of employment,” the initiative, the moving force, is something other than 
the employment; the employment is thought of more as a condition out of 

RYNER V. TENNECO 
Page 13 
 
which the event arises than as the force producing the event in affirmative 
fashion. 
Meyer v. IBP, Inc., 710 N.W.2d 213, 223 (Iowa 2006) (quoting 1 Arthur Larson & Lex K. 
Larson, Larson’s Workers’ Compensation Law (2005) § 3.06, at 3-7 to 3-8 (emphasis in 
original)). 
“[S]ubject to ‘limited exceptions,’” the Iowa Supreme Court has “‘abandoned’ or 
‘discarded’ the increased-risk test in favor of the actual-risk test” when determining 
whether an employee’s injury arises out of employment under the Iowa Workers’ 
Compensation Act. Bluml v. Dee Jay’s Inc., 920 N.W.2d 82, 85–86 (Iowa 2018) (quoting 
Blue, 743 N.W.2d at 177, n.7). The actual risk rule holds: 
If the nature of the employment exposes the employee to the risk of such 
an injury, the employee suffers an accidental injury arising out of and 
during the course of the employment. And it makes no difference that the 
risk was common to the general public on the day of injury. 
Blue, 743 N.W.2d at 174 (quoting Hanson v. Reichelt, 452 N.W.2d 164, 168 (Iowa 
1990)).  
Relatedly, the Iowa Supreme Court has long held the type of movement is not 
dispositive on the question of whether an injury arose out of employment under the Iowa 
Workers’ Compensation Act: 
If in doing his work the employee, in a usual or unusual manner, lifts an 
object, stoops, or takes a step, or makes any movement of his limbs or 
body, and such exertion unexpectedly and unintentionally results in an 
injurious strain upon his nerves, muscles, heart, or any other organ, so as 
to incapacitate him for work, the event or result is an injury as 
contemplated in the act. 
Almquist, 218 Iowa 724, 254 N.W. at 41. 
Thus, in accordance with longstanding caselaw precedent by which the agency is 
bound, the inquiry under the actual risk rule focuses on the circumstances surrounding 
the injurious movement, not the movement itself. The reason for this is commonsense. 
The human body can only move in so many ways, so the type of movement, standing 
alone, cannot dictate whether the injury that results is within the purview of the statute. 
Otherwise, the scope of our workers’ compensation scheme would be limited, which 
would run contrary to the precedent underpinning the Iowa Supreme Court’s adoption of 
the actual risk rule: 
We think the actual risk rule is the better rule and more in line with how we 
construe our Workers' Compensation Act. We construe the Act liberally in 
favor of the employee; we resolve all doubts in favor of the employee.

Hanson, 452 N.W.2d at 168 (citing Teel v. McCord, 394 N.W.2d 405, 406–07 (Iowa 
1986)).  
Dr. Allen, Dr. Boulden, and Dr. Moran suggest that because Ryner’s 
osteoporosis placed her at a greater risk of sustaining a fractured vertebrae, activities of 
daily living outside of work could have caused the fracture. But this is unavailing for two 
reasons. First, as discussed above, the actual risk rule governs in Iowa under binding 
Iowa Supreme Court precedent. Second, as found above, the weight of the evidence 
establishes Ryner’s back was asymptomatic until she attempted to lift the tray of 
sparkplugs while working at Tenneco. 
An expert opinion on causation may not be considered in isolation; rather, it must 
be considered along with all other evidence bearing upon the causal connection 
between the injury and the disability. See Schujter v. Algona Manor Care Ctr., 780 
N.W.2d 549, 560 (Iowa 2010); see also Nellis v. Quealy, 237 Iowa 507, 512, 21 N.W. 
584, 586 (Iowa 1946). Moreover, the Iowa Supreme Court has long held that an injured 
worker’s credible testimony regarding the onset of symptoms “must be given 
considerable weight on the issue of causal connection.” Hinrichs v. Davenport 
Locomotive Works, 203 Iowa 1395, 214 N.W. 585, 586 (Iowa 1927). In the current case, 
while it is possible that Ryner could have fractured her vertebrae while performing an 
activity of daily living outside of work, it is improbable because the evidence establishes 
she experienced no symptoms relating to her fractured vertebrae until after she felt a 
pop in her back while attempting to lift the tray of sparkplugs at work. The 
persuasiveness of the opinions by Dr. Allen, Dr. Moran, and Dr. Boulden is undermined 
by the fact that they give insufficient weight to the undisputed facts regarding the onset 
of Ryner’s symptoms. As a result, their expert opinions are not “consistent with all the 
testimony and of the greater credibility” in this case. Id. 
Further undermining the credibility of the opinions rendered by Dr. Allen, Dr. 
Moran, and Dr. Boulden is their approach to causation as a binary question. In their 
formulation, the question is framed as an either-or proposition: Either Ryner’s 
osteoporosis or lifting the tray of spark plugs caused the injury. This is an incorrect 
proposition under Iowa workers’ compensation law. 
An injured worker is not entitled to workers’ compensation “for the results of a 
pre-existing injury or disease,” but “the mere existence thereof at the time of a 
subsequent injury is not a defense.” Nicks v. Davenport Produce Co., 254 Iowa 130, 
135, 115 N.W.2d 812, 815 (Iowa 1962). In this case, Ryner is not seeking workers’ 
compensation for her osteoporosis; she is seeking benefits for a fractured vertebrae. 
And her osteoporosis is not a defense to her claim. 
“For workers' compensation purposes a cause is proximate if it is a cause; it 
need not be the only cause.” Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 420 (Iowa 
1994). Thus, if a personal health condition such as osteoporosis increases a worker’s 
susceptibility to injury and the injury arises out of and in the course of the workers’ 
employment, it is compensable under the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Act. See St. 
Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 (Iowa 2000); see also Ziegler v. U.S. 

RYNER V. TENNECO 
Page 15 
 
Gypsum Co., 252 Iowa 613, 620, 106 N.W.2d 591, 595 (Iowa 1960); Hanson v. 
Dickinson, 176 N.W. 823, 824–25 (Iowa 1920). “If the employment of claimant resulted 
in a personal injury aggravating her already impaired physical condition, she is entitled 
to compensation to the extent of that injury.” Nicks, 254 Iowa at 135,115 N.W.2d at 815. 
Here, Rendoff is a physician’s assistant and Ryner’s primary care provider for the 
fractured vertebrae. Rendoff’s opinion on causation stands against those of M.D.’s. 
Rendoff’s opinion is nonetheless more persuasive than those of Dr. Allen, Dr. Boulden, 
and Dr. Moran when considered against the competent evidence in the record and 
governing precedent on causation in workers’ compensation claims under Iowa law. A 
doctor’s credentials do not trump the facts underlying the opinion or Iowa law. 
Only Rendoff considered how Ryner’s osteoporosis and the work activity of lifting 
the tray of spark plugs were both causative factors of the compression fracture to her 
vertebrae. Because Rendoff’s opinion is based on a more accurate understanding of the 
facts and application of the causation standard in workers’ compensation cases under 
Iowa law, it is adopted. Ryner has met her burden of proof. The weight of the evidence 
shows that the act of lifting the tray of spark plugs while performing work duties was a 
significant factor in causing Ryner’s fractured vertebrae, need for care, and permanent 
impairment.

P e r m a n e n cy  B e n e f i t s .  
Over a century ago, the Iowa legislature created our workers’ compensation 
system. See 1913 Iowa Acts ch. 147; see also Baker v. Bridgestone/Firestone, 872 
N.W.2d 672, 676–80 (Iowa 2015) (discussing the history of workers’ compensation and 
the policy considerations behind it). Workers’ compensation is “a creature of statute.” 
Darrow v. Quaker Oats Co., 570 N.W.2d 649, 652 (Iowa 1997). This means an injured 
employee’s “right to workers' compensation is purely statutory.” Downs v. A & H Const., 
Ltd., 481 N.W.2d 520, 527 (Iowa 1992).  And “it is the legislature’s prerogative to fix the 
conditions under which the act’s benefits may be obtained.” Darrow, 570 N.W.2d at 652. 
At its inception as now, the Act “provides for work[ers’] compensation for injuries 
received in the course of employment” and “creates a special tribunal,” the 
commissioner, with exclusive jurisdiction “to hear and dispose of such claims.” Des 
Moines Union Ry. Co. v. Funk, 164 N.W. 648, 649–650 (Iowa 1917); see also Tweeten 

RYNER V. TENNECO 
Page 16 
 
v. Tweeten, 999 N.W.2d 270, 275 (Iowa 2023). The legislature codified a mandate that 
in cases before the commissioner, “Process and procedure under this act shall be as 
summary as reasonably may be.” 1913 Iowa Acts ch. 147, § 25.  
The Iowa Supreme Court recognized early in its workers’ compensation 
jurisprudence that “proceedings under the Work[ers’] Compensation Act . . . are 
intended to be simple and informal.” Fischer v. W.F. Priebe & Co., 178 Iowa 512, 160 
N.W. 48, 50 (1916). And in its seminal Flint v. City of Eldon holding, the court 
elaborated: 
The purpose, intent, and scheme of workmen's compensation legislation is 
well understood, and its historical significance has been frequently 
expressed in decisions. 
The fundamental reason for the enactment of this legislation is to avoid 
litigation, lessen the expense incident thereto, minimize appeals, and 
afford an efficient and speedy tribunal to determine and award 
compensation under the terms of this act. 
191 Iowa 845, 183 N.W. 344, 345 (1921). The court then quoted from a New York case 
for the following proposition, which the court has reiterated over the subsequent 
decades: 
It was the purpose of the Legislature to create a tribunal to do rough 
justice--speedy, summary, informal, untechnical. With this scheme of the 
Legislature we must not interfere; for, if we trench in the slightest degree 
upon the prerogatives of the Commission, one encroachment will breed 
another, until finally simplicity will give way to complexity, and informality to 
technicality. 
Id. at 345 (quoting Rhyner v. Hueber Bldg. Co., 171 App. Div. 56, 156 N. Y. Supp. 903); 
see also Cross v. Hermanson Bros., 235 Iowa 739, 742, 16 N.W.2d 616, 618 (1944) 
(quoting Flint, 183 N.W. at 345); Henderson v. Iles, 248 Iowa 847, 852, 82 N.W.2d 731, 
734 (Iowa 1957); Nicks v. Davenport Produce Co., 254 Iowa 130, 134, 115 N.W.2d 812, 
814–15 (Iowa 1962) (quoting Cross, 235 Iowa at 742, 16 N.W.2d at 618); Second Injury 
Fund v. Hodgins, 461 N.W.2d 454, 456 (Iowa 1990) (quoting Nicks, 254 Iowa at 134, 
115 N.W.2d at 814–15); Zomer, 666 N.W.2d at 133 (quoting Flint, 183 N.W. at 345); 
Marovec v. PMX Indus., 693 N.W.2d 779, 787 (Iowa 2005) (quoting Zomer, 666 N.W.2d 
at 133).  
Under Flint and its progeny, “the commissioner has the responsibility for deciding 
cases in an expeditious and timely manner.” Marovec, 693 N.W.2d at 787. The rule that 
simplicity prevails over complexity in workers’ compensation cases has governed 
workers’ compensation procedure for over 100 years. After enactment of the Iowa 
Administrative Procedure Act, Iowa Code chapter 17A, and other legislative changes, 
the statutory mandate of speediness remains expressly in effect today.  

RYNER V. TENNECO 
Page 17 
 
Iowa Code section 86.18(1) provides, “Evidence, process and procedure in 
contested case proceedings or appeal proceedings within the agency under this 
subchapter and chapters 85 and 85A shall be as summary as practicable consistent 
with the requirements of chapter 17A.” And section 86.14(1) states, “In an original 
proceeding, all matters relevant to a dispute are subject to inquiry.” As the Marovec 
court observed, the language the court used in Flint is as true today as it was in 1921. 
693 N.W.2d at 787. 
In cases stemming from a work injury before July 1, 2017, permanent partial 
disability to an unscheduled body party caused by a work injury was “compensated by 
the industrial disability method which takes into account the loss of earning capacity.” Id. 
(citing Mortimer, 502 N.W.2d at 14–15); see also Mannes v. Fleetguard, Inc., 770 
N.W.2d 826, 830 (Iowa 2009) (quoting Oscar Mayer Foods Corp. v. Tasler, 483 N.W.2d 
824, 831 (Iowa 1992)). An industrial disability analysis was used regardless of whether 
the injured employee returned to work with the defendant-employer or the level of 
earnings at the time of hearing relative to the date of injury. See Mannes v. Fleetguard, 
Inc., 770 N.W.2d 826, 830 (Iowa 2009) (quoting Oscar Mayer Foods Corp. v. Tasler, 
483 N.W.2d 824, 831 (Iowa 1992)); see also Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 
158 (Iowa 1996); Arrow-Acme Corp. v. Bellamy, 500 N.W.2d 92, 95 (Iowa App. 1993). 
With the 2017 amendments, the legislature carved out an exception to this general rule 
that created a speedy resolution to disputes regarding the extent of permanent disability 
by creating a new mandatory bifurcated litigation process on the issue of permanent 
disability under certain circumstances. See 2017 Iowa Acts ch. 23, § 8 (now codified at 
Iowa Code § 85.34(2)(v)). This 2017 statutory change is the first instance of the 
legislature rolling back the Flint rule by elongating the litigation process for ultimate 
disposition of a workers’ compensation case before the agency. 
The statute now articulates an exception to when the agency may make a 
determination on an injured employee’s entitlement to industrial disability benefits and 
the circumstances triggering a mandatory bifurcated litigation process as follows: 
If an employee who is eligible for compensation under this paragraph 
returns to work or is offered work for which the employee receives or 
would receive the same or greater salary, wages, or earnings than the 
employee received at the time of the injury, the employee shall be 
compensated based only upon the employee’s functional impairment 
resulting from the injury, and not in relation to the employee’s earning 
capacity. Notwithstanding section 85.26, subsection 2, if an employee who 
is eligible for compensation under this paragraph returns to work with the 
same employer and is compensated based only upon the employee's 
functional impairment resulting from the injury as provided in this 
paragraph and is terminated from employment by that employer, the 
award or agreement for settlement for benefits under this chapter shall be 
reviewed upon commencement of reopening proceedings by the 
employee for a determination of any reduction in the employee's earning 
capacity caused by the employee's permanent partial disability. 

RYNER V. TENNECO 
Page 18 
 
Iowa Code § 85.34(2)(v).  
Thus, the 2017 amendments changed the statute so that its text expressly 
incorporates the agency’s review-reopening process to create a mandatory bifurcated 
litigation process when a criterion identified in the text is met. See, Dungan v. Den 
Hartog Indus., Iowa Workers’ Comp. Comm’r No. 1657969.01, 2023 WL 363118, *15–
*16 (Arb. Sep. 20, 2022), aff’d 2023 WL 363118 (App. Jan. 13, 2023); see also Den 
Hartog Indus. v. Dungan, No. 23-1402, 2025 WL 52449 (Iowa App., Jan. 9, 2025) 
(Table). Under Iowa Code section 86.14(2), review-reopening is a process by which a 
determination of the claimant’s entitlement to compensation is revisited due to a change 
in condition. Green v. N. Cent. Iowa Reg’l Solid Waste Auth., 989 N.W.2d 144, 146–47 
(Iowa 2023). Under the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Act, the “‘commissioner finds the 
facts as they stand at the time of hearing and should not speculate about the future 
course of a claimant’s condition.’” Id. at 147 (quoting Kohlhaas v. Hog Slat, Inc., 777 
N.W.2d 387, 392 (Iowa 2009)). If a party believes the claimant’s condition has changed 
when compared to that at the time of hearing, the party may apply to the agency for 
review-reopening. See id. at 146–47. 
Under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v), an injured employee who returns to work 
with the defendant-employer earning the same or more as the employee earned at the 
time of injury is limited to permanency benefits based solely on the extent of permanent 
impairment and not industrial disability. The only way such an employee may obtain 
permanency benefits based on the extent of industrial disability caused by the work 
injury is via the bifurcated litigation process set forth in the statutory text. Section 
85.34(2)(v) expressly allows a claimant to seek a new agency determination on the 
permanency issue using an industrial disability analysis only if the defendant-employer 
terminates the claimant’s employment after the initial agency award or approval of the 
parties’ agreement for settlement. Presumably, this is because the defendant-
employer’s discharge of the claimant after the award or agreement for settlement 
creates a potential change in the claimant’s condition from that at the time of hearing or 
settlement that could trigger reopening the determination of permanent disability. See 
id. at 146–49; see also Stuart v. Dicketen Masch Plastics, LLC, 998 N.W.2d 192, 2023 
WL 7014183, *2–*4 (Iowa App. 2023) (Table). 
Here, the parties agree that Ryner returned to work with Tenneco after the work 
injury at the same or higher earnings level as on the injury date. Ryner remained 
employed at Tenneco at that earnings level as of the hearing date. Therefore, her 
permanency benefits are determined solely based on the permanent impairment caused 
by the work injury.

by the workers’ compensation commissioner by rule pursuant to chapter

Under Iowa Administrative Code rule 876—2.4, the agency uses the Fifth Edition 
of the Guides. The persuasiveness of an expert’s opinion on the extent of an injured

supports the finding that only one expert determined Ryner’s permanent impairment 
solely by utilizing the Fifth Edition of the Guides. 
As found above, there is an insufficient basis in the evidence from which to 
conclude Dr. Boulden used the Fifth Edition of the Guides. In comparison, Dr. Bansal 
expressly noted using the Fifth Edition of the Guides when rendering his opinion on 
permanent impairment. Therefore, Dr. Bansal’s opinion is more persuasive than Dr. 
Boulden’s. 
Reinforcing this conclusion is the fact that Dr. Boulden misrepresented Dr. 
Bansal’s opinion when critiquing it in his report. Dr. Bansal did not state that Ryner 
sustained a compression fracture with greater than a 50 percent height loss, as Dr.

Ryner has satisfied her burden of proof on the question of permanent

to her vertebrae caused her to sustain a permanent impairment of 18 percent to the

based on 500 weeks. 18 percent multiplied by 500 weeks is 90 weeks. Ryner is 
therefore entitled to 90 weeks of permanency benefits from the stipulated 
commencement date of October 18, 2021.
