STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Manuel Zavala Ramirez, claimant, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from Meridian Manufacturing, Inc., employer, and West Bend 
Mutual Insurance Company, insurance carrier, as defendants.  Hearing commenced via 
Zoom videoconference on December 6, 2024.  The hearing was scheduled for 3 hours.  
Early in the hearing it became apparent that the hearing would exceed the allotted time.  
An agreement was reached to recess the hearing and reconvene on December 17, 
2024.   
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised 
or discussed in this decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.  
Manuel Zavala Ramirez, Brandon Poss, David Chilton, and Ricardo Flores all 
testified live at trial.  The evidentiary record also includes Joint Exhibits 1-5, Claimant’s 
Exhibits 1-10, and Defendants' Exhibits A-K.  All exhibits were received without 
objection.  The evidentiary record closed at the conclusion of the arbitration hearing.       
The parties submitted post-hearing briefs on January 29, 2025, at which time the 
case was fully submitted to the undersigned.

ISSUES 
 
The parties submitted the following issues for resolution: 
1. Whether claimant sustained an injury that arose out of and in the course of 
his employment on December 23, 2022. 
 
2. Whether claimant sustained any permanent partial disability as the result of 
the stipulated work injury.  If so, the nature and extent of permanency he 
sustained.   
 
3. Claimant’s average weekly wages at the time of the alleged injury. 
 
4. Whether claimant’s claim is barred by operation of Iowa Code section 85.23 
for lack of timely notice. 
 
5. Whether defendants should reimburse claimant for the independent medical 
examination. 
 
6. Assessment of costs. 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned, having considered all the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
 
 Claimant, Manuel Zavala Ramirez (“Manuel”) alleged he sustained a respiratory 
injury on December 23, 2022 which arose out of and in the course of his employment as 
a welder with Meridian Manufacturing, Inc. (“Meridian”).  He was 48 years old at the 
time of the hearing. (Transcript, page 9)  
 
Manuel began welding in 2015 when he was working for Midwest Industries.   
(Tr. pp. 12-13) While at Midwest Industries he welded mild steel and aluminum.  (Tr. p. 
14) He was employed at Midwest Industries from 2014-2017 and denied any coughing

Defendants' Exhibit I, deposition pages 36-37)

D.O., with a cough that had been going on for a couple of months. (Joint Exhibit 1, page

Manuel returned to Dr. Schaller on March 26, 2014, with a cough for the past 5 months.  
(JE1, p. 4) He felt as if something was stuck in his throat.  (JE1, p. 4) Manuel did not try 
albuterol as he was instructed.  (JE1, pp. 4-7)

operational in the North plant. (Tr. pp. 134-135) I find that the fans in the North plant 
were operational.

Manuel testified that while welding stainless he was in smaller tanks than what is 
depicted in Defendants’ Exhibit B.  (Tr. pp. 50-52) When welding stainless the tanks 
were approximately six feet in diameter and 10 to 14 feet high or long. (Tr. p. 52) 
Sometimes Manuel would weld in the tanks when they were fully enclosed.  (Tr. p. 53) 
Brandon testified that approximately 30 to 40 percent of a welder’s time is inside the 
tank versus outside.  (Tr. p. 109) Meridian has the Micro Air PM air filtration system that 
they use to extract harmful fumes in stainless.  The system has a hose that goes inside 
the tank that pulls the fumes out of the tank for the welder who is inside the tank for the 
final weld.  (Tr. pp. 109-110) The system has the same filters as the respirators and the 
same efficacy of 99.97 percent.  (Tr. p. 110) Brandon testified that Meridian tests the air 
quality of the stainless area.  (Tr. pp. 105-106) An air pump is located near the welding 
station with a filter that sucks in the fumes.  The filter is sent to a third-party lab for 
testing as required by OSHA.  (Tr. p. 106) OSHA requires additional PPE to protect the 
workers for anything over 5 micrograms.  (Tr. p. 106) Meridian’s air quality has been 
1.56 micrograms per cubic meter which is below the OSHA requirement of 5 
micrograms.  (Tr. p. 106) 
When welding mild steel, Manuel wore a welding helmet, but not a respirator.  
(Tr. p. 50) When welding stainless Manuel wore an air-powered respirator which 
provided oxygen.  (Tr. pp. 51, 82) The respirator is similar to the one depicted in 
Claimant’s Exhibit 3, page 34. Manuel wore the respirator whenever he was welding 
stainless.  His respirator would occasionally get cloudy.  When this occurred he would 
stop welding, step outside of the welding station and use a paper towel to remove the 
cloudiness.  (Tr. pp. 53-54, 81) He also removed his respirator if he needed to talk to 
someone, but he would not be welding during this time.  (Tr. p. 97) 
According to Manuel, his respirator did not have a tight seal and fumes entered 
his respirator.  (Tr. p. 94) Manuel testified that at times he had difficulty with his 
respirator.  (Tr. p. 95) He does not remember ever making any complaints to anyone at 
Meridian that his respirator was not working properly.  (Tr. p. 97)

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury actually occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 
employment. Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 150 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. 
Dial Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309, 311 (Iowa 1996). The words “arising out of” refer to the 
cause or source of the injury. The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 
circumstances of the injury. 2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124, 128 (Iowa 
1995).  An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists 
between the injury and the employment. Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. The injury must 
be a rational consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely 
incidental to the employment. Koehler Elec. v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1, 3 (Iowa 2000); 
Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it 
happens within a period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may 
be when performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties 
or doing an activity incidental to them. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d at 150. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 
621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 
(Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994).

claim if barred by operation of Iowa Code section 85.23. 
Iowa Code section 85.23 requires an employee to give notice of the occurrence 
of an injury to the employer within 90 days from the date of the occurrence, unless the 
employer has actual knowledge of the occurrence of the injury. 
The purpose of the 90-day notice or actual knowledge requirement is to give the 
employer an opportunity to timely investigate the facts surrounding the injury.  The 
actual knowledge alternative to notice is met when the employer, as a reasonably 
conscientious manager, is alerted to the possibility of a potential compensation claim 
through information which makes the employer aware that the injury occurred and that it 
may be work related.  Dillinger v. City of Sioux City, 368 N.W.2d 176, 180 (Iowa 1985); 
Robinson v. Department of Transp., 296 N.W.2d 809, 811 (Iowa 1980). 
Failure to give notice is an affirmative defense which the employer must prove by 
a preponderance of the evidence.  DeLong v. Iowa State Highway Commission, 229 
Iowa 700, 295 N.W. 91, 92 (1940). 
Based on the above findings of fact, I conclude the earliest that claimant provided 
notice to the defendants of his alleged December 23, 2022 was on March 27, 2023.  
Because March 27, 2023 is more than 90 days after December 23, 2022, I conclude 
claimant failed to give notice of the occurrence of an injury to the employer within 90 
days from the date of the occurrence.  As such claimant’s claim is barred.

However, the 2017 changes in the Code require that the date of the occurrence 
of the injury no longer considers a claimant’s knowledge of the nature, seriousness, or 
probable compensable character of the injury. 
Prior to the legislature’s 2017 amendments to Iowa Code chapter 85, Iowa Code 
section 85.23 stated: 
Unless the employer or the employer’s representative shall have actual 
knowledge of the occurrence of an injury received within ninety days from 
the date of the occurrence of the injury, or unless the employee or 
someone on the employee’s behalf or a dependent or someone on the 
dependent’s behalf shall give notice thereof to the employer within ninety 
days from the date of the occurrence of the injury, no compensation shall 
be allowed. 
 
In 2017, the legislature added this definitional sentence to sections 85.23 and 
85.26(1): “For the purposes of this section, ‘date of the occurrence of the injury’ means 
the date that the employee knew or should have known that the injury was work-
related.” 
The Iowa Supreme Court in Tweeten v. Tweeten, 999 N.W. 2d 270, 281 (Iowa 
2023) noted that prior to the 2017 amendments, the commencement of the limitation 
period in section 85.26(1) had been tied to the claimant’s discovery of the nature, 
seriousness, and probable compensable character of the injury. The Court held that, by 
adding the definition of “date of the occurrence of the injury” to section 85.26(1), the 
legislature changed the commencement of the limitation period to tie it to the 
occurrence of a specific event, namely the date “the employee knew or should have 
known that the injury was work related.” Tweeten, 999 N.W. 2d at 281. The court 
explained:  
The general assembly has chosen to limit the discovery rule to knowledge 
that an injury is work-related, not to knowledge of the compensable nature 
of the injury as we had previously recognized. Continuing to apply our 
prior common law discovery rule to further toll the limitation period until the 
employee is also aware of the permanent adverse impact of an injury 
would add words to the statute that the general assembly did not—
something we cannot do. 
Id. 
In Tyler v. Tyson Fresh Meats, No. 23-0393, February 7, 2024 (Iowa Ct. App.), 
unpublished 4 N.W. 3d 721 (Table), the Court of Appeals held that:  
Because the legislature added identical language to sections 85.23 and 
85.26(1), coupled with the fact that we apply the discovery rule the same 
under both sections… we find Tweeten controls the outcome in this case. 
Consistent with the holding in Tweeten, we conclude the ninety-day notice 
period set by section 85.23 starts on the date the claimant knew or should

have known the injury was work-related, without regard to a claimant’s 
knowledge of the nature, seriousness, or probable compensable character 
of the injury. 
Tyler, slip op. at 6. 
Claimant testified that while he was welding stainless he had a sudden onset of 
coughing attacks that felt like he was drowning.  (Tr. pp. 54-55, 77) The attacks would 
last approximately five to twenty minutes, one to two times per day during elite training.
