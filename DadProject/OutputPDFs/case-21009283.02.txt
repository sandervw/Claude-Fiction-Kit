STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Tyrone Mitchell, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from M J Daly Construction, Inc. (MJ), employer, and Acuity, 
insurer, both as defendants.  This matter was heard on August 12, 2024, with a final 
submission date of September 13, 2024. 
The record in this case consists of Joint Exhibits 1 through 10, Claimant’s 
Exhibits 1 through 13, Defendants’ Exhibits A through M, and the testimony of claimant. 
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised 
or discussed in this decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.  
ISSUES 
1. The extent of claimant’s entitlement to permanent partial disability benefits. 
 
2. Commencement date of benefits. 
 
3. Credit.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The first issue to be determined is the extent of claimant’s entitlement to 
permanent partial disability benefits. 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e). 

MITCHELL V. M J DALY CONSTRUCTION, INC. 
Page 8 
 
If claimant has an impairment to the body as a whole, an industrial disability has 
been sustained.  Industrial disability was defined in Diederich v. Tri-City Ry. Co. of Iowa, 
219 Iowa 587, 591, 258 N.W. 899, 902 (1935) as follows:  "It is therefore plain that the 
Legislature intended the term 'disability' to mean 'industrial disability' or loss of earning 
capacity and not a mere 'functional disability' to be computed in terms of percentages of 
the total physical and mental ability of a normal man." 
Functional impairment is an element to be considered in determining industrial 
disability which is the reduction of earning capacity, but consideration must also be 
given to the injured employee's age, education, qualifications, experience, motivation, 
loss of earnings, severity and situs of the injury, work restrictions, inability to engage in 
employment for which the employee is fitted and the employer's offer of work or failure 
to so offer.  McSpadden v. Big Ben Coal Co., 288 N.W.2d 181, 192 (Iowa 1980); Olson 
v. Goodyear Service Stores, 255 Iowa 1112, 1120, 125 N.W.2d 251, 256 (1963); Barton 
v. Nevada Poultry Co., 253 Iowa 285, 110 N.W.2d 660, 663 (1961). 
Compensation for permanent partial disability shall begin when it is medically 
indicated that maximum medical improvement from the injury has been reached and 
that the extent of loss or percentage of permanent impairment can be determined by the 
use of the guides to the evaluation of permanent impairment, published by the American 
Medical Association, as adopted by the Commissioner. Iowa Code section 85.34(2)   
Under the Iowa Workers' Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is 
compensated either for a loss or loss of use of a scheduled member under Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(a)-(u) or as an unscheduled injury pursuant to the provisions of section 
85.34(2)(v). The extent of scheduled member disability benefits to which an injured 
worker is entitled is determined by using the functional method. Functional disability is 
"limited to the loss of the physiological capacity of the body or body part.” Mortimer v. 
Fruehauf Corp., 502 N.W.2d 12, 15 (Iowa 1993); Sherman v. Pella Corp., 576 N.W.2d 
312, 320 (Iowa 1998).   
Iowa Code section 85.34(x) states:  
x. In all cases of permanent partial disability described in 
paragraphs “a” through “u”, or paragraph “v” when determining functional 
disability and not loss of earning capacity, the extent of loss or percentage 
of permanent impairment shall be determined solely by utilizing the guides 
to the evaluation of permanent impairment, published by the American 
medical association, as adopted by the workers' compensation 
commissioner by rule pursuant to chapter 17A. Lay testimony or agency 
expertise shall not be utilized in determining loss or percentage of 
permanent impairment pursuant to paragraphs “a” through “u”, or 
paragraph “v” when determining functional disability and not loss of 
earning capacity.  
Iowa Code section 85.34 (x).

This agency has adopted the Guides to the Evaluation of Permanent Impairment, 
Fifth Edition, published by the American Medical Association for determining the extent 
of loss or percentage of impairment for permanent partial disabilities.  See 876 IAC 2.4.  
A loss of earning capacity due to voluntary choice or lack of motivation to return 
to work is not compensable. Malget v. John Deere Waterloo Works, File No. 5048441 
(Remand Dec. May 23, 2018); Rus v. Bradley Puhrmann, File No. 5037928 (App. 
December 16, 2014); Gaffney v. Nordstrom, File No. 5026533 (App. September 7, 
2011); Main v. Quaker Oats, File No. 5017903 (App. December 19, 2007); Snow v. 
Chevron Phillips Chemical Co., File No. 5016619 (App. October 25, 2007);  Copeland v. 
Boone Book and Bible Store, File No. 1059319 (App. November 6, 1997).  See also 
Brown v. Nissen Corp., 89-90 IAWC 56, 62 (App. 1989) (no prima facie showing that 
claimant is unemployable when claimant did not make an attempt for vocational 
rehabilitation). 
Both parties agree that claimant has a permanent impairment to the left ankle.  
Dr. Kuhnlein opined that claimant has a 44 percent permanent impairment to the lower 
extremity.  (Ex. 4, p. 22)  Dr. Willey treated claimant for an extended period of time and 
performed surgery on claimant.  He believed that claimant had a 41 percent permanent 
impairment to the left lower extremity.  (JE 9, pp. 271-272) 
Dr. Willey last evaluated claimant in June of 2023.  Dr. Kuhnlein saw claimant in 
February of 2024.  Dr. Kuhnlein evaluated claimant closer to the time of hearing, and as 
a result of his evaluation, this is a much more accurate and up-to-date picture of 
claimant’s permanent impairment at the time of hearing.  Based on this, it is found that 
Dr. Kuhnlein’s permanent impairment rating to claimant’s ankle is more convincing than 
that of Dr. Willey.  It is found that claimant has a 44 percent permanent impairment to 
the left lower extremity for his ankle injury.

Dr. Milani appears to believe that claimant does have an altered gait due to his

Second, Dr. Milani opined, in treatment records, that claimant’s gait was

the permanent impairment of claimant’s back and hip conditions are found not 
convincing.

Given these issues as detailed above, it is found that Dr. Schmitz’s opinion 
regarding permanent impairment to claimant’s hip and back pain are found not 
convincing. 
Dr. Kuhnlein did not find claimant had permanent impairment to his lower back.  
(Ex. 4, p. 22)  He did find that claimant had a 3 percent permanent impairment for his 
left ankle, knee and hip pain.  (Ex. 4, p. 22) 
Drs. Minard and Moses-Nanthan both opine that claimant had permanent 
impairment to his lower back.  Dr. Kuhnlein examined claimant, took range of motion 
measurements, and gave a detailed report regarding claimant’s permanent impairment.  
Drs. Minard and Moses-Nanthan did not.  Based on this, it is found the opinions of Dr. 
Kuhnlein regarding claimant’s permanent impairment to his hip and back are more 
convincing than those of Drs. Minard and Moses-Nanthan. 
Given this record, claimant has carried his burden of proof he has a permanent 
impairment to both his left lower extremity and his hip. 
Claimant has also alleged that he has a mental injury caused or aggravated by 
his July of 2021 work injury. 
Claimant testified at hearing that he had depression and anxiety prior to the July 
of 2021 work injury.  The record indicates that claimant has a pre-existing history of 
mental health disorders.  
Two experts have opined regarding claimant’s alleged mental health injury.  Dr. 
Carpenter is a board-certified psychiatrist.  (Ex. G)  He opined that claimant does not

have PTSD and does not have depression or any other mental health disorder caused

Dr. Moses-Nanthan, in her statement, believed that claimant has PTSD,

Claimant was 51 years old at the time of hearing.  Claimant graduated from high 
school.  He has a CDL.  Claimant has worked a number of physically demanding jobs.  
Claimant’s unrebutted testimony is that his prior work before MJ, and his work with MJ,
