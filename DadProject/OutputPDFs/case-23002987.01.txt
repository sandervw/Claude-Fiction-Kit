STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Jody Wenzel, claimant, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from Housby Mack, Inc., employer and Travelers Property 
Casualty Company of America, insurance carrier, as defendants.  Hearing was held via 
Zoom videoconference on December 5, 2024.     
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.  
Jody Wenzel was the only witness to testify live at trial.  The evidentiary record 
also includes joint exhibits 1-5, claimant’s exhibits 1-7, and defendant’s exhibits A-D.  
Defendants objected to claimant’s exhibit 5, 6, and 7 on the basis that the exhibits are 
not relevant and are hearsay.  Claimant’s exhibit 5 is a report authored by Christopher 
Brigham, M.D. Defendants point out that the report is incomplete and does not involve 
the claimant in this case.  Claimant’s exhibit 6 is a portion of an agreement for 
settlement and attached report authored by Linda Cocchiarella, M.D.  Claimant’s exhibit 
7 is the Curriculum Vitae of Linda A. Cocchiarella, M.D.  Defendants’ objection was 
overruled and claimant’s exhibits 5, 6, and 7 were admitted.  All other exhibits were 
received without objection.  The evidentiary record closed at the conclusion of the 
arbitration hearing.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony.  The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question.  The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances.  The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); 
Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995).  Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994).  Unrebutted expert medical 
testimony cannot be summarily rejected.  Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 
N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 1994).  
Under the Iowa Workers' Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is 
compensated either for a loss or loss of use of a scheduled member under Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(a)-(u) or as an unscheduled injury pursuant to the provisions 
of section 85.34(2)(v). The extent of scheduled member disability benefits to which an 
injured worker is entitled is determined by using the functional method. Functional 
disability is “limited to the loss of the physiological capacity of the body or body 
part.” Mortimer v. Fruehauf Corp., 502 N.W.2d 12, 15 (Iowa 1993); Sherman v. Pella 
Corp., 576 N.W.2d 312 (Iowa 1998).   
Iowa Code section 85.34(x) permanent disabilities states: 
 
x. In all cases of permanent partial disability described in paragraphs “a” 
through “u”, or paragraph “v” when determining functional disability and 
not loss of earning capacity, the extent of loss or percentage of permanent 
impairment shall be determined solely by utilizing the guides to the 
evaluation of permanent impairment, published by the American medical 
association, as adopted by the workers' compensation commissioner by 
rule pursuant to chapter 17A. Lay testimony or agency expertise shall not

The parties stipulated that claimant sustained an injury to his right shoulder which 
arose out of and in the course of his employment on October 23, 2022.  The central 
dispute in this case is the extent of permanent impairment claimant sustained as the 
result of the work injury.  There is no dispute that the claimant in this case underwent a 
distal clavicle excision.  This agency has held that when a distal clavicle excision is 
performed, the AMA Guides require application of a 25 percent multiplier as set forth in 
table 16-18.  See Jay v. Archer Skid Loader Service, LLC, File No. 19003586.01 (App. 
August 23, 2022).   
Claimant argues that the Commissioner’s decision in Jay, was unreasonable, 
arbitrary, capricious, and an abuse of discretion.  Additionally, claimant contends the 
decision was an error of law because it was an improper use of agency expertise and 
the Commissioner took notice of a medical opinion not in evidence.   I am bound by 
agency precedent and will not rule on the claim that the Commissioner’s decision in Jay 
should be overturned.  However, the parties were allowed to make their records 
regarding the issue in this case.  
As noted, the undersigned is bound by this agency’s appeal decision in Jay.  
Therefore, Dr. Smidt’s impairment rating carries greater weight.  I conclude claimant has 
demonstrated that he sustained 3 percent functional impairment to his upper extremity 
as the result of the October 23, 2022 injury to his shoulder. 
The parties have stipulated that claimant’s injury should be compensated as a

weeks (400 weeks x .03) of permanent partial disability benefits commencing on the 
stipulated date of July 6, 2023.

Next, we turn to the issue of the appropriate weekly workers’ compensation rate.  
Iowa Code section 85.36 states “[t]he basis of compensation shall be the weekly 
earnings of the injured employee at the time of the injury.” Weekly earnings are defined 
as the gross salary, wages, or earnings of an employee had the employee worked the 
customary hours for the full pay period in which the employee was injured as the 
employer regularly required for work of employment. Id. The subsections of Iowa Code 
section 85.36 set forth methods for computing weekly earnings depending upon the 
type of earnings and employment. If the employee is paid on a weekly pay period basis, 
the weekly gross earnings are the basis of compensation. Iowa Code section 85.36(1). 
If the employee is paid on a biweekly basis, the weekly earnings are one-half of the 
biweekly gross earnings. Iowa Code section 85.36(2). 
If an employee is paid on a daily, or hourly basis, or based upon output, weekly 
earnings are computed by dividing by thirteen (13) the earnings over the thirteen (13) 
week period immediately preceding the injury. However, any week that does not fairly 
reflect the employee's customary earnings shall be replaced by the closest previous 
week that is a fair representation of the employee's customary earnings. Iowa Code 
85.36(6). The calculation shall include shift differential pay, but not overtime or premium 
pay in the calendar weeks immediately preceding the injury. Id. If the employee was 
absent during the time period subject to calculation for personal reasons, the weekly 
earnings are the amount the employee would have earned had the employee worked 
when work was available to other employees in a similar occupation for the employer. 
Id. 
Under Iowa law, gross earnings are defined as, “recurring payments by the 
employer to the employee for employment, before any authorized or lawfully required 
deduction or withholding of funds by the employer, excluding irregular bonuses, 
retroactive pay, overtime, penalty pay, reimbursement of expenses, expense 
allowances, and the employer's contribution for welfare benefits.” Iowa Code section 
85.61(3) 
Iowa Code section 85.61(3) allows for inclusion of regular bonuses in the 
calculation of gross earnings. Some factors considered by this body in determining 
whether a bonus is regular is whether the bonus payment is subject to a condition 
precedent, varied in amount, and fixed in terms of entitlement or amount. Noel v. 
Rolscreen Co., 475 N.W.2d 666 (Iowa Ct. App. 1991).  
Based on the above findings of fact, I found that the weeks claimant included in 
his rate calculation accurately reflects his customary weekly earnings.  Therefore, I 
conclude claimant’s average gross weekly earnings should be based on the weeks 
included in claimant’s rate calculation.  (Cl. Ex. 3, p. 19)  However, I found that the 
bonus claimant included in his calculation should not be included.  I conclude that a 
bonus that varies in amount and is subject to a condition precedent is not a regular 
bonus.  Therefore, I conclude Wenzel’s calculation of his gross earnings, less the

$269.95 bonus he included, accurately reflects his customary earnings.  Thus, I 
conclude claimant’s gross weekly earnings at the time of the injury were one thousand

parties stipulated that claimant was married and entitled to 2 exemptions.  This results

Next, claimant seeks reimbursement for an IME under Iowa Code section 85.39.  
The Iowa Supreme Court has provided a literal interpretation of the plain language of 
Iowa Code section 85.39, stating that section only allows the employee to obtain an 
independent medical evaluation at the employer's expense if an evaluation of 
permanent disability has already been made by an employer retained physician. Des 
Moines Area Reg'l Transit Auth. v. Young, 867 N.W.2d 839, 847 (Iowa 2015). Under the 
Young decision, an employee can only obtain an IME at the employer's expense if an 
evaluation of permanent disability has been made by an employer-retained physician. 
Id. 
In this instance, defendants do not dispute claimant’s entitlement to an IME.  
Rather, the dispute centers on whether the fee charged by Dr. Rondinelli for the IME is 
reasonable under Iowa Code section 85.39.   
Iowa Code section 85.39(2) provides claimant is entitled to reimbursement for the 
IME examination if the charges are reasonable and “based on the typical fee charged 
by a medical provider to perform an impairment rating in the local area where the 
examination is conducted.” Defendants do not argue that the fee is not reasonable for 
this local area, rather, defendants seem to contend that certain portions of the exam 
were unnecessary.   
Iowa Code section 85.39(2), as amended, allows an employee with a 
compensable injury to be reimbursed the reasonable cost of an examination. See Iowa 
Code § 85.39(2)(2017); Mid American Construction LLC v. Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d 838, 849 
(Iowa 2024). Under the statute, an examination may include reviewing medical records, 
conducting a physical exam, opining on causation, assessing permanent impairment, 
assigning restrictions, and recommending further care. Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d at 851. 
The burden is on the claimant to show the reasonableness of his or her chosen 
physician's IME fee. Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d at 852; see also Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e) 
(stating “Ordinarily, the burden of proof on an issue is upon the party who would suffer 
loss if the issue were not established.”); Pexa v. Auto Owners Ins. Co., 686 N.W.2d 150, 
156 (Iowa 2004)(stating “the plaintiff has the burden to prove the reasonable value of 
the [medical] service rendered”). The reasonableness of an IME fee is a question of fact 
to be decided by the commissioner. See Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 154 
(Iowa 1996). The reasonableness of an IME fee must be based upon the typical fee 
charged by a medical provider performing an impairment rating in the local area where
