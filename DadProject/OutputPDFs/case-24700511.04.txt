STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
On September 19, 2025, the claimant filed a petition for alternate medical care 
pursuant to Iowa Code 85.27(4) and 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.48.  The 
defendants filed an answer admitting liability. 
 
The undersigned presided over the hearing held electronically and recorded 
digitally on September 30, 2025.  That recording constitutes the official record of the 
proceeding pursuant to 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.48(12).  The claimant 
participated through his attorney, Emily Schott Hood. The defendants participated 
through their attorney, Julie Burger. 
   
Prior to the hearing, the claimant submitted six pages of exhibits, marked as 
Exhibits 1 and 2.  The defendants submitted no exhibits.  The claimant’s exhibits were 
received into the record without objection. 
 
 
On July 21, 2023, the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commissioner issued an 
order delegating authority to deputy workers’ compensation commissioners, such as the 
undersigned, to issue final agency decisions on applications for alternate care.  
Consequently, this decision constitutes final agency action, and there is no appeal to 
the commissioner.  Judicial review in a district court pursuant to Iowa Code Chapter 17A 
is the avenue for an appeal.

ISSUE 
 
The issue under consideration is whether the defendants should be ordered to 
authorize and provide the claimant with cortisone injections into his shoulder at a 
location within a reasonable distance of his home in Grinnell, Iowa. 
 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
 
Edward Davidson is the claimant in this matter.  The claimant alleges that he 
injured his left shoulder and left upper extremity while working on November 1, 2022.  
According to the defendants’ answer, Dr. Eyberg in Cedar Rapids, Iowa, is an 
authorized treating physician for Mr. Davidson’s ongoing complaints.  Dr. Eyberg has 
ordered maintenance care via cortisone injections into the claimant’s left shoulder.  The 
defendants argue that Dr. Eyberg ordered that these injections be performed by an 
orthopedic surgeon.   
 
 
Mr. Davidson visited with Blade Eyberg, M.D., at Physicians’ Clinic of Iowa, P.C., 
in Cedar Rapids, Iowa on August 4, 2025.  (Claimant’s Exhibit 2:5-6).  Dr. Eyberg noted 
that he examined Mr. Davidson for a “work comp evaluation of his left shoulder pain…”  
(CE 2:5).  Dr. Eyberg noted previous injections to the claimant’s left shoulder that were 
“helpful but not durable.”  (CE 2:5).  Dr. Eyberg reviewed the results of an MRI, which 
showed “full-thickness retracted tears of supraspinatus and subscap [sic] with some 
associated glenohumeral arthritis.”  (CE 2:6).  The tears atrophied and according to Dr. 
Eyberg were “irreparable cuff tears.”  (CE 2:6).  Dr. Eyberg recommended conservative 
care until cuff tear arthropathy developed.  (CE 2:6).  Conservative care consisted of 
cortisone injections on an as needed basis.  (CE 2:6).    
  
On August 6, 2025, counsel for the claimant sent defendants’ counsel a letter 
indicating that Dr. Eyberg ordered cortisone shots for Mr. Davidson.  (CE 1:1).  Dr. 
Eyberg ordered that these cortisone shots occur in Cedar Rapids, Iowa.  (CE 1:1).  
Claimant’s counsel noted that Mr. Davidson lives in Grinnell, Iowa, and that it takes him 
one hour and twenty minutes one-way to travel to Cedar Rapids, Iowa, for these 
injections.  (CE 1:1).  Claimant’s counsel requested that the defendants authorize a 
provider closer to Mr. Davidson’s home.  (CE 1:1-2).  
  
 
Claimant’s counsel sent another letter to defendants’ counsel on August 19, 
2025.  (CE 1:3).  Claimant’s counsel again noted the distance which Mr. Davidson 
would need to travel in order to have the cortisone shots as recommended by Dr. 
Eyberg.  (CE 1:3).  Claimant’s counsel requested for a second time that the defendants 
authorize a provider closer to Mr. Davidson’s home.  (CE 1:3).  Claimant’s counsel 
noted that Mr. Davidson wished to avoid missing work as much as he could.  (CE 1:3).  
  
 
On September 17, 2025, claimant’s counsel emailed defendants’ counsel noting 
the claimant’s dissatisfaction with the care offered by the defendants via cortisone 
injections at a facility that is one hour and twenty minutes on a one-way basis from the

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
Iowa Code 85.27(4) provides, in relevant part: 
 
For purposes of this section, the employer is obligated to furnish reasonable 
services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has the right to choose the 
care….  The treatment must be offered promptly and be reasonably suited to treat 
the injury without undue inconvenience to the employee. If the employee has 
reason to be dissatisfied with the care offered, the employee should communicate 
the basis of such dissatisfaction to the employer, in writing if requested, following 
which the employer and the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably 
suited to treat the injury. If the employer and employee cannot agree on such 
alternate care, the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable proofs of 
the necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
 
Iowa Code 85.27(4). See Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 433 (Iowa 
1997).   
 
“Iowa Code section 85.27(4) affords an employer who does not contest the 
compensability of a workplace injury a qualified statutory right to control the medical 
care provided to an injured employee.”  Ramirez-Trujillo v. Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 
N.W.2d 759, 769 (Iowa 2016) (citing R.R. Donnelly & Sons v. Barnett, 670 N.W.2d 190, 
195, 197 (Iowa 2003)).  “In enacting the right-to-choose provision in section 85.27(4), 
our legislature sought to balance the interests of injured employees against the 
competing interests of their employers.”  Ramirez, 878 N.W.2d at 770-71 (citing Bell 
Bros., 779 N.W.2d at 202, 207; IBP, Inc. v. Harker, 633 N.W.2d 322, 326-27 (Iowa 
2001)).   
 
The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except where the 
employer has denied liability for the injury.  Iowa Code section 85.27; Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening, October 16, 1975).  An employer’s right to select 
the provider of medical treatment to an injured worker does not include the right to 
determine how an injured worker should be diagnosed, evaluated, treated, or other

matters of professional medical judgment.  Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 
866389 (Declaratory Ruling, May 19, 1988).  Reasonable care includes care necessary 
to diagnose the condition, and defendants are not entitled to interfere with the medical 
judgment of its own treating physician.  Pote v. Mickow Corp., File No. 694639 (Review-
Reopening Decision, June 17, 1986).   
 
The employer must furnish “reasonable medical services and supplies and 
reasonable and necessary appliances to treat an injured employee.”  Stone Container 
Corp. v. Castle, 657 N.W.2d 485, 490 (Iowa 2003) (emphasis in original).  Such 
employer-provided care “must be offered promptly and be reasonably suited to treat the 
injury without undue inconvenience to the employee.”  Iowa Code section 85.27(4).  
More specifically in Long, the court noted that “the employer’s obligation under the 
statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.”  Long v. Roberts 
Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122, 128 (Iowa 1995).   
 
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See e.g. 
Iowa R. App. P. 14(f)(5); Bell Bros. Heating and Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d 
193, 209 (Iowa 2010); Long, 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  More specifically in Long, 
the court noted that “the employer’s obligation under the statute turns on the question of 
reasonable necessity, not desirability.”  Long, 528 N.W.2d at 128.   
 
An injured employee dissatisfied with the employer-furnished care (or lack 
thereof) may share the employee’s discontent with the employer and if the parties 
cannot reach an agreement on alternate care, “the commissioner may, upon application 
and reasonable proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and order the care.”  Id.  
“Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.”  Long, 528 
N.W.2d at 123; Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 436.  As the party seeking 
relief in the form of alternate care, the employee bears the burden of proving that the 
authorized care is unreasonable.  Id. at 124; Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d at 209; Pirelli-
Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 436.  Because “the employer’s obligation under the 
statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability,” an injured 
employee’s dissatisfaction with employer-provided care, standing alone, is not enough 
to find such care unreasonable.  Id.  However, if substantial evidence exists that the 
care authorized by the employer was ineffective, it may be found that the care was not 
reasonable.  Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 437.   
 
In this case, the claimant would like to proceed with the cortisone injections 
ordered by Dr. Eyberg.  The defendants indicated that they have authorized the 
cortisone injections as ordered by authorized treating physician Dr. Eyberg.  The 
question is whether it is reasonable for the claimant to have to drive over 80 miles, 
and/or one hour and twenty minutes in each direction, in order to receive the injections 
in Cedar Rapids, Iowa.   
 
It is longstanding precedent that medical care needs to be provided within a 
reasonable distance from the residence of the claimant.  See Trade Professionals, Inc.

v. Shriver, 661 N.W.2d 119, 124 (Iowa 2003)(indicating that more than 100 miles is 
undue inconvenience); Schulte v. Vocational Services of Area Residential Care, File No. 
1134342 (Alt. Care. Sept. 6, 1996)(offering care more than 70 miles away is 
unreasonable).  Previous decisions have also held that a fifty-mile radius is “generally 
considered a reasonable distance to travel for treatment in workers’ compensation 
cases…” but that “…what is reasonable depends on the circumstances in a particular 
case.”  Ickes v. Great River Medical Center, File No. 5063028 (Alt. Care Feb. 28, 
2019)(citing Warner v. City of Hawarden, File No. 5039478 (Arb. June 27, 2013); Bitner 
v. Cedar Falls Constr. Co., File No. 5013852 (Alt. Care Sept. 24, 2004)).   
 
Both parties seemed to agree that requiring Mr. Davidson to drive one hour and 
twenty minutes one-way for his maintenance cortisone injections was unreasonable.  
The defendants indicated that they would provide injections from a closer provider 
should Dr. Eyberg order them.  The circumstances in this case, including that these are 
seemingly regular maintenance injections and the distance from the claimant’s home, 
indicate that requiring him to travel to Cedar Rapids, Iowa, from his home in Grinnell, 
Iowa, is unreasonable.  Therefore, I find that it is unreasonable to require the claimant to 
drive 81 miles and/or one hour and twenty minutes one-way from his home to Cedar 
Rapids, Iowa, in order to have maintenance cortisone injections on his left shoulder.   
 
Counsel for the claimant indicated that they found several orthopedic physicians 
in Grinnell, Iowa, and nearby Newton, Iowa.  Claimant’s counsel also indicated that Des 
Moines, Iowa, which is about 50 miles away, would be reasonable, should providers in 
Grinnell, Iowa, or Newton, Iowa, refuse to treat the patient.   
 
As the undersigned noted during the hearing, I do not possess the ability to order 
a doctor to take on the claimant as a patient.  Therefore, my order is that the defendants 
shall attempt to schedule the claimant for the maintenance cortisone injections with an 
orthopedic provider in Grinnell, Iowa, Newton, Iowa, and/or Des Moines, Iowa.  The 
defendants shall diligently attempt to arrange for treatment in the aforementioned cities 
from the closest city to the claimant to the furthest city from the claimant (i.e. 1. Grinnell, 
Iowa; 2. Newton, Iowa; and, 3. Des Moines, Iowa).  There are also smaller cities which 
are in closer proximity to Grinnell, Iowa, such as Marshalltown, Iowa, Pella, Iowa, or 
Oskaloosa, Iowa.  These cities are all about 30 miles from Grinnell, Iowa, and it would 
be reasonable for the claimant to travel to these cities for his maintenance cortisone 
injections, should no provider be found in the aforementioned cities. 
 
IT IS THEREFORE ORDERED: 
 
1. The claimant’s petition for alternate care is granted. 
  
2. The defendants shall arrange for maintenance cortisone injections for the 
claimant as ordered by Dr. Eyberg in a nearby city in closer proximity to the 
claimant than Cedar Rapids, Iowa.
