STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A.  On 
October 31, 2025, claimant, Willard Street, filed an application for alternate care under 
Iowa Code section 85.27, invoking the expedited procedure rule 876 IAC 4.48.  In the 
petition claimant requested defendants authorize a referral to Sandeep Bhangoo, M.D., 
or another spine specialist for a second opinion, for an alleged work-related injury to his 
low back on February 27, 2025. (See Petition).  Defendants did not file an answer prior 
to hearing.   
 
The undersigned presided over an alternate care hearing held via telephone on 
November 12, 2025.  Claimant appeared personally and through his attorney Nick Platt.  
Defendants appeared through their attorney Thomas Joensen.  The proceedings were 
digitally recorded.  That recording constitutes the official record of this proceeding. The 
hearing record consists of: 
 
▪ Claimant’s Exhibits 1 and 2 
▪ Defendants’ Exhibits A through C 
 
Claimant testified at the hearing.  Counsel also offered oral argument to support 
their positions.  During the hearing, defendants verbally accepted liability for an injury to 
claimant’s low back on February 27, 2025. Initially defendants’ counsel stated that 
defendants did not think the need for the requested referral was causally related to the 
February 27, 2025 date of injury. (Testimony).  However, defendants’ counsel later

FINDINGS OF FACT 
On February 27, 2025, claimant sustained a work-related injury to his low back.   
(See Petition; Testimony).  Defendants accepted liability for the injury and directed 
claimant’s medical treatment. (Testimony; Def Ex. A).  According to the medical records 
in evidence, claimant had an MRI of his spine. (Id. at 1).  This took place on April 15, 
2025. (See Ex. C, p. 4).  It showed mild retrolisthesis of L5 and S1, diffuse disc bulging, 
posterior disc protrusion, no significant central spinal narrowing, disc material contacting 
the bilateral S1 nerve roots, and mild left and right foraminal narrowing. (Ex. A, p. 1).   
After the imaging, claimant’ was referred to Christian Ledet, M.D., an 
interventional pain specialist. (Ex. A, p. 1).  Claimant indicated he saw Dr. Ledet 
approximately three times. (Testimony).  Dr. Ledet performed an epidural steroid 
injection to claimant’ lumbar spine. (Ex. A, p. 1).  The injection did not decrease 
claimant’s symptoms or pain complaints. (Id.).  Claimant’s care was then transferred to 
Trevor Schmitz, M.D. (Id.).  Dr. Schmitz is an orthopedic surgeon specializing in spinal 
surgery.  (Id.).  From the record available, it is unclear how many times claimant treated 
with Dr. Schmitz.   
Claimant had an appointment with Dr. Schmitz on July 16, 2025. (See Ex. A, p. 
1).  Dr. Schmitz ordered flexion-extension films. (Id.).  They showed no obvious

retrolisthesis. (Id.).  Dr. Schmitz also reviewed claimant’s April 15, 2025 MRI. (Ex. C, p. 
4).  He indicated the MRI demonstrated a disc bulge at L4-L5, degenerative disc 
disease at L5-S1, but no evidence of high-grade central or foraminal stenosis. (Id.).  Dr. 
Schmitz diagnosed claimant with axial low back pain with bilateral leg symptoms. (Id.).  
He mentioned that claimant complained of stool incontinence with activity. (Id.).  He, 
however, also noted claimant “had virtually every Waddell sign known.”  (Id.).  Dr. 
Schmitz noted claimant had normal age-related changes on his MRI with no evidence of 
impingement. (Id.).  He saw nothing that would explain claimant’s symptoms. (Id.).  He

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Under Iowa law, an employer who has accepted compensability for a workplace 
injury has a right to control the care provided to the injured employee.  Ramirez-Trujillo 
v. Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 N.W.2d 759, 769 (Iowa 2016).  The relevant statute provides 
as follows: 
For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish 
reasonable services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has the 
right to choose the care. . . . The treatment must be offered promptly and 
be reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the

Page 6 
 
employee. If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the care 
offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such dissatisfaction 
to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the employer and 
the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited to treat the 
injury. If the employer and employee cannot agree on such alternate care, 
the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable proofs of the 
necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
Iowa Code § 85.27(4).   
Defendants’ “obligation under the statute is confined to reasonable care for the 
diagnosis and treatment of work-related injuries.” Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 
N.W.2d 122, 124 (Iowa 1995) (emphasis in original). In other words, the “obligation 
under the statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.”  Id. An 
application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because claimant is 
dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving. Mere dissatisfaction with the medical 
care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical care. Rather, 
the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not reasonably 
suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the claimant. See 
Iowa Code § 85.27(4). By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment and seeking 
alternate care, claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is 
unreasonable. See Iowa R. App. P 6.904(3)(e); Long, 528 N.W.2d at 124.   
An employee’s desire for a different “reasonable” treatment plan does not make 
the employer-authorized care unreasonable.  See Long, 528 at 124.  A finding that the 
treatment requested by the claimant is reasonable does not result in an implicit finding 
that the authorized treatment is unreasonable. Id.  The employee must prove the care 
being offered by the employer is unreasonable to treat the work injury, not that another 
treatment plan is reasonable. Id.; See also Lynch v. Bursell, 870 N.W.2d 274 (Table) 
(Iowa Ct. App. 2015).  Ultimately, determining whether care is reasonable under the 
statute is a question of fact.  Long, 528 N.W.2d at 123.  
According to the record before me, defendants are not currently offering the 
claimant any treatment at all.  They argue this is because Dr. Schmitz and Dr. Mooney, 
two of claimant’s authorized treating physicians for this claim, have opined that claimant 
does not require any additional treatment for his work-related back complaints.  To be 
fair to the defendants, it appears they have previously authorized a plethora of care for 
the February 27, 2025, date of injury including approximately four months of physical 
therapy, x-rays, an EMG, an MRI, several visits with a pain specialist, an epidural 
steroid injection, treatment with an occupational medicine specialist, and an evaluation 
with a neurosurgeon.  Claimant, however, continues to complain of significant pain and 
debilitating symptoms in his back, legs and bowels.  (Testimony).  Given this, 
defendants failure to offer any treatment, whether that be palliative or otherwise, is 
unreasonable.   
Claimant’s request for a second opinion with a spine specialist is granted.  That 
spine specialist, however, does not have to be Dr. Bhangoo.  Under the workers’

compensation statute, defendants have a right to direct claimant’s causally-related care.  
There is nothing in the hearing record demonstrating defendants should lose that right 
in this case.  Additionally, the referral submitted by claimant is from a family physician, 
not an occupational doctor or a spine specialist.  That physician only evaluated the 
claimant one time for his back complaints and it is not clear whether he had access to 
claimant’s prior imaging when he made the referral.  Finally, Dr. Bhangoo’s name never 
actually appears in Dr. Standing’s referral, so it is unclear how exactly he was chosen to 
provide a second opinion.  Claimant is entitled to additional treatment for his ongoing 
back complaints.  However, given the above facts, defendants remain entitled to chose 
the specialist that provides claimant with a second opinion on his back condition and 
any appropriate treatment options.   
ORDER 
THEREFORE, IT IS ORDERED: 
Within fourteen (14) days of filing of this decision, defendants shall authorize and 
provide claimant with a date for an appointment with a spine specialist for a second 
opinion on claimant’s back complaints.  
However, claimant’s request for that second opinion to take place with Dr. 
Bhangoo is denied. Defendants retain the right to direct claimant’s causally-related care.
