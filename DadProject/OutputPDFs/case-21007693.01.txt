RULING ON JUDICIAL REVIEW 
 
 
This judicial review action came on for hearing on March 29, 2024.  Tom Drew 
represented petitioner Heriberto Hernandez-Garcia.  Jeff Margolin represented respondent Metro 
Salvage Poole, Inc. and Western National Mutual Insurance Company (jointly referred to as 
“Metro”).   
STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
Petitioner is a 43 year old man who was born in Mexico and came to the United States in 
1999.  Petitioner estimated that he is approximately 25 percent proficient in English.  Prior to 
2009, he worked janitorial, labor, landscaping, and roofing jobs.  In 2009, he started working for 
Metro, which is an auto salvage business.  A friend and co-worker helped with translation.  
Petitioner’s job duties included conducting an inventory of cars.  His job duties required him to 
climb, stoop, and lift.  (Arb. Dec., p. 2; Tr. 11-15).  
 
Petitioner suffered a non-job-related back injury in 2019.  He was diagnosed with a left 
L4-5 disc herniation that was treated by a microdiscectomy and decompression of the L5 nerve 
root.  His doctors initially placed some work limits that were lifted over time.  He returned to 
full-time duties at Metro following his recovery.  (Arb. Dec., p. 3; Tr. 21-23).

On January 4, 2021, petitioner was injured at Metro after he fell into a hole that was 
covered by snow.  He reported a “bolt” of pain moving down his left leg.  Metro referred him to 
a clinic that subsequently referred him to Dr. Trevor Schmitz, an orthopedic spine surgeon.  An 
MRI showed a disc herniation.  Following a series of less-invasive treatment attempts, Dr. 
Schmitz recommended surgery.  Petitioner agreed and the surgery was conducted on July 29, 
2021.  (Arb. Dec. p. 3; Tr. 24-26; Exh. JE5). 
 
Petitioner reported that the pain returned 5-6 days following surgery.  He attempted 
physical therapy but reported it made the pain worse.  Petitioner returned to Dr. Schmitz who 
conducted additional MRIs and testing.  On November 19, 2021, Dr. Schmitz found petitioner 
reached maximum medical improvement (MMI).  He found petitioner showed normal strength 
and sensation.  He recommended no further medical interventions.  Dr. Schmitz stated that 
petitioner could return to work without restrictions.  (Arb. Dec. p. 3; Tr. 29-31; Exh. JE5).   
 
Because petitioner continued to complain of pain, Dr. Schmitz recommended a functional 
capacity evaluation (FCE).  An FCE was performed on November 30, 2021, by a physical 
therapist.  The FCE measured petitioner’s lifting ability in multiple capacities.  Petitioner was 
able to lift up to 77 pounds from floor to waist.  The FCE determined petitioner could return to 
“heavy category work.”  Dr. Schmitz reviewed and accepted the FCE, which was consistent with 
his prior recommendation.  He assessed petitioner with a 10 percent permanent functional 
impairment of the whole person.  (Arb. Dec. p. 4; Exh. JE5). 
 
During treatment, petitioner attempted to return to work at Metro but the business had 
been sold and no longer had a position open for him.  He later obtained employment with Alter 
Recycling where he was assigned to sweep floors.  After three days, he went to the emergency 
room where he complained of increasing pain in his lower back radiating down his left leg.  The

Both parties hired vocational experts.  Petitioner’s expert based his review on the second 
FCE, Dr. Sassman’s IME, and his interview with petitioner.  Petitioner’s expert found that he 
had sustained an 86 percent loss of access to the labor market.  Metro’s expert reviewed both 
FCEs and the restrictions offered by Drs. Sassman, Schmitz, and Mooney.  She opined that 
petitioner was capable of working full-time in several jobs in the light to heavy work range.  
(Arb. Dec. pp. 7-8; Exh. 3, C). 
 
Petitioner’s workers’ compensation hearing took place on November 1, 2022, before a 
deputy workers’ compensation commissioner.  The deputy found that petitioner has an 18 
percent permanent functional impairment and that the 2021 injury constituted a “substantial 
aggravation of his preexisting condition.”  The deputy also found that petitioner was capable of 
working at the heavy demand level based on the recommendations of the first FCE, Dr. Schmitz, 
and Dr. Mooney.  The deputy found those opinions to be more credible than those of the second 
FCE and Dr. Sassman.  (Arb. Dec. pp. 6-8). 
 
The deputy also considered whether petitioner qualified for enhanced benefits pursuant to 
the odd-lot doctrine.  He found that petitioner presented a prima facie case of odd-lot status, but 
Metro met its burden of production on the same issue.  He ultimately found that petitioner did 
not carry the burden of persuasion because he remains capable of working in the labor market.  
The deputy calculated petitioner’s loss of earning capacity at 30 percent based on the impairment 
rating and other factors including his limited ability to speak English, that his work history is 
limited to physical labor, and that he may experience back symptoms performing work.  This 
decision was affirmed in its entirety by the Workers’ Compensation Commissioner on Appeal.    
(Arb. Dec. pp. 8-9; App. Dec.).

STANDARD OF REVIEW 
Iowa Code chapter 17A governs judicial review of administrative agency action. The 
district court acts in an appellate capacity to correct errors of law on the part of the agency. Meyer 
v. IBP, Inc., 710 N.W.2d 213, 219 (Iowa 2006).  The court “may grant relief if the agency action 
has prejudiced the substantial rights of the petitioner, and the agency action meets one of the 
enumerated criteria contained in section 17A.19(10)(a) through (n).” Burton v. Hilltop Care Cntr., 
813 N.W.2d 250, 256 (Iowa 2012) (quoting Evercom Sys., Inc. v. Iowa Utilities Bd., 805 N.W.2d 
758, 762 (Iowa 2011)). Where an agency has been “clearly vested” with a fact-finding function, 
the appropriate “standard of review [on appeal] depends on the aspect of the agency's decision that 
forms the basis of the petition for judicial review”—that is, whether it involves an issue of 1) 
findings of fact, 2) interpretation of law, or 3) application of law to fact. Burton, 813 N.W.2d at 
256.  
Review of findings of fact:  The courts use a substantial evidence standard when 
considering challenges to findings of fact in agency decisions.  A reviewing court can only disturb 
factual findings if they are “not supported by substantial evidence in the record before the court 
when that record is reviewed as a whole.”  Burton, 813 N.W.2d at 256 (quoting Iowa Code § 
17A.19(10)(f)).  The Iowa Supreme Court has outlined the court’s guidelines when reviewing 
substantial evidence claims under the 17A.19 standard as follows: 
When reviewing a finding of fact for substantial evidence, we judge the finding in 
light of all the relevant evidence in the record cited by any party that detracts from 
that finding as well as all of the relevant evidence in the record cited by any party 
that supports it.  Our review of the record is fairly intensive, and we do not simply 
rubber stamp the agency finding of fact. 
 
Evidence is not insubstantial merely because different conclusions may be drawn 
from the evidence. To that end, evidence may be substantial even though we may 
have drawn a different conclusion as fact finder. Our task, therefore, is not to 
determine whether the evidence supports a different finding; rather, our task is to

Cedar Rapids Cmty. Sch. Dist. v. Pease, 807 N.W.2d 839, 845 (Iowa 2011) (internal citations and

Application of law to fact:  When a party challenges the ultimate conclusion reached by 
the agency, then the challenge is to the agency's application of the law to the facts.  In that event, 
the question on review is whether the agency abused its discretion by, for example, employing 
wholly irrational reasoning or ignoring important and relevant evidence.  Meyer, 710 N.W.2d at 
219.  The court will only reverse the agency’s application of law to the facts if it is irrational, 
illogical, or wholly unjustifiable. Neal v. Annett Holdings, Inc., 814 N.W.2d 512, 518 (Iowa 2012).  
CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Petitioner’s primary argument concerns the agency’s application of the odd-lot doctrine.  
A worker becomes an odd-lot employee when “an injury makes the worker incapable of 
obtaining employment in any well-known branch of the labor market.”  Guyton v. Irving Jensen 
Co., 373 N.W.2d 101, 105 (Iowa 1985).  In other words, “[a]n odd-lot worker is thus totally 
disabled if the only services the worker can perform are so limited in quality, dependability, or 
quantity that a reasonably stable market for them does not exist[.]”  Id. (cites and internal quotes 
omitted).   
 
The odd-lot doctrine has a multi-faceted burden shifting test.  First, the worker must 
make a prima facie case of total disability by “producing substantial evidence that the worker is 
not employable in the competitive labor market[.]”  Id. at 106.  If the worker makes a prima facie 
case, the burden shifts to the employer to produce evidence of suitable employment.  Id.  If the 
employer produces such evidence, the ultimate burden of persuasion falls back on the employee.  
Id. at 105; See also Michael Eberhart Const. v. Curtin, 674 N.W.2d 123, 126 (Iowa 2004).

The odd-lot doctrine is premised on the concept that an employee can be injured out of 
his or her respective job market.   The argument made sense in this case.  Petitioner has limited 
job options.  He is minimally educated, is not fluent in English, and had worked labor-type jobs 
his whole career.  If excluded from heavy demand jobs, he would have a good case. 
 
The problem is that some of the medical experts found that petitioner was not excluded 
from heavy demand jobs.  His treating physician, Dr. Schmitz made that finding and reaffirmed 
it upon later visits.  The physical therapist who performed the FCE came to the same conclusion.  
Dr. Schmitz reviewed that FCE and adopted it.  Dr. Mooney came to the same conclusions as 
well.  So did Metro’s vocational expert.  These findings constitute substantial evidence to 
support the finding on odd-lot and industrial disability. 
 
Petitioner produced experts who came to different conclusions.  However, the deputy did 
a nice job explaining credibility decisions in support of his decision.  For example, he found 
Metros vocational expert more credible because she considered information presented by both 
parties as opposed to petitioner’s expert, who was only provided the second FCE and Dr. 
Sassman’s opinion.  The deputy found Dr. Mooney more credible than Dr. Sassman in another 
instance because he provided more of an explanation for the difference in the impairment ratings.  
These credibility findings made sense and support the reasonableness of the decision-making. 
 
Because the court must accept the agency finding that petitioner can perform heavy 
demand work, the court must likewise find that he is not excluded from the job market.  He has 
worked a variety of labor jobs over his career.  There are jobs available in the general area he has 
previously worked.  The agency’s finding as to petitioner’s odd-lot status is not irrational, 
illogical, or wholly unjustifiable.  Based on the legal standards that apply to review of agency 
action, the decision must be affirmed.
