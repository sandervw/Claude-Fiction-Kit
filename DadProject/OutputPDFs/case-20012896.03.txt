STATEMENT OF THE CASE   
This is a contested case action to determine whether, and to what extent, 
Attorney Mark Sullivan should be awarded in attorney’s fees for his representation of 
respondent, Angela Fuller, in a workers’ compensation contested case litigation against 
Fuller’s former employer, Bimbo Bakeries, and insurance carrier, Indemnity Insurance 
Company of North America.   
Counsel filed the petition in this case on November 15, 2023.  Shortly after filing, 
claimant terminated Mr. Sullivan’s representation of her.  An Order was entered on 
December 20, 2023, allowing Mr. Sullivan to withdraw as counsel of record.  A 
prehearing conference (PHC) was held on January 4, 2024.  Counsel for Bimbo 
Bakeries in the underlying case intervened.  Respondent was served notice of the PHC 
by the agency both by certified mail and email.  Respondent responded via phone call 
on December 21, 2023 at 10:55 A.M. to the agency and acknowledged receipt of the 
notice.  Respondent, however, chose not to participate in the PHC.  At the PHC, it was 
determined the best way to proceed was to schedule a quick hearing to determine 
appropriate attorney fees under Iowa Code section 10A.327.  Hearing was scheduled 
for January 30, 2024.  Notice was sent to the Respondent again via certified mail and 
email, which included the text of the statute in question.  Unfortunately, Ms. Fuller has 
never registered on WCES, nor responded in any meaningful way to any of the notices 
delivered by the agency. 
A contested case hearing was held on January 30, 2024, in Des Moines, Iowa, 
via Zoom.  The hearing was recorded on the agency’s recording software, Quality 
Management Suite.  The Petitioner participated, as did the Intervenors. The 
Respondent did not appear or even acknowledge any efforts by the agency to 
communicate with her.  This is interpreted as a conscious decision not to participate in 
the hearing given the extraordinary efforts to secure her participation.  The record in this 
case consists of petitioner’s exhibits 1 through 10, and the testimony of Mr. Sullivan.  In

ISSUE   
The only issue presented for resolution is whether the attorney fees sought by 
Petitioner are appropriate under Section 10A.327.   
FINDINGS OF FACT   
The deputy workers’ compensation commissioner, having heard the testimony of 
the witnesses and considered the evidence in the record, finds that: 
Attorney Sullivan began representing Ms. Fuller on or about July 28, 2021.  Ms. 
Fuller had previous attorneys.  She signed Mr. Sullivan’s standard fee contract on that 
date.  (Petitioner’s Exhibit 3)  The fee agreement was for one-third of the gross amount 
recovered for Ms. Fuller or 40 percent if the matter proceeded through intra-agency 
appeal. 
The matter proceeded to an arbitration hearing on July 15, 2022, before the 
undersigned deputy.  The matter was briefed and fully submitted on September 23, 
2022.  I entered an arbitration decision on January 26, 2023, awarding Ms. Fuller a 65 
percent industrial disability (325 weeks of benefits), with interest, past medical 
expenses, future alternate medical care expenses, reimbursement of the independent 
medical examination and costs.  (Pet. Ex. 1, pp. 9-10)  Attorney Sullivan sought no fees 
for the past or future medical expenses. 
The defendants appealed this decision, and an Appeal Decision was entered on 
September 12, 2023, affirming the relevant portions of the arbitration decision.1   
Having reviewed the entire file, I find there is no question that this recovery was 
secured based upon Attorney Sullivan’s zealous, diligent, and persistent representation 
of Ms. Fuller.  In other words, I find at the time she secured his legal representation, the 
defendants were not going to pay her any additional compensation.  As a direct result of 
Mr. Sullivan’s legal representation, Ms. Fuller secured an award of 325 weeks of 
compensation (an additional 290 weeks beyond what the defendants had voluntarily 
paid). 
In October 2023, Ms. Fuller filed a lawsuit against Mr. Sullivan and the 
defendants, as well as an ethics complaint against Mr. Sullivan.  (Pet. Exs., 9 and 10)  
In the lawsuit (which has never been served), the petitioner’s primary complaint seems 
to be that neither Mr. Sullivan nor her previous attorneys tried to help her keep her job 
with Bimbo Bakeries; she also complained about Mr. Sullivan’s attorney fees.  Other

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW   
Iowa Code section 10A.327 provides that all fees or claims for legal services, and 
certain other services provided in workers’ compensation litigation, to be reimbursed 
under chapters 85, 85A, 85B and 87, are subject to the approval of the workers’ 
compensation commissioner.  Section 10A.327(2) states: 
An attorney shall not recover fees for legal services based on the amount 
of compensation voluntarily paid or agreed to be paid to an employee for 
temporary or permanent disability under this subchapter, or chapter 85, 
85A, 85B, or 87.  An attorney shall only recover a fee based on the 
amount of compensation that the attorney demonstrates would not have 
been paid to the employee but for the efforts of the attorney.  Any disputes 
over the recovery of attorney fees under this subsection shall be resolved 
by the workers’ compensation commissioner.   
Under Disciplinary Rule 2-106(B) of the Iowa Code of Professional Responsibility 
for Lawyers, factors to be considered in determining the reasonableness of fees are 
time and labor required, fees customarily charged in locality for similar services, the 
amounts involved and the results obtained, and the experience, reputation, and ability of 
the lawyer involved.   
An attorney is entitled to a fee if the amount of compensation due on a scheduled 
injury is increased.  Lee v. John Deere Waterloo Tractor Works, 34 Biennial Rep., Iowa 
Industrial Comm’r 185 (App. Order 1978); Kobliska v. John Deere Waterloo Tractor 
Works, 32 Biennial Rep., Iowa Industrial Comm’r 110 (Review Dec. 1976).

This agency has approved one-third contingency fee arrangements in the past, 
when appropriate.  Francis v. Ryder Truck Rental, IV Iowa Industrial Comm’r Report 
129 (App. September 30, 1983) (Dist. Ct. Aff’d.); Schmidt v. Keim Transportation, File 
No. 1198459 (Arb. Dec. January 24, 2003); Butler v. Quik Trip Corporation, File No. 
1185376 (Arb. Dec. March 24, 2003). 
At hearing, Attorney Sullivan proved by a preponderance of evidence that Ms. 
Fuller’s recovery was based upon the legal services he provided.  He is entitled to the 
fee that he and Ms. Fuller agreed upon at the commencement of his representation of 
her which is 40 percent of the recovery. 
Given that payments are ongoing, and Attorney Sullivan has not been paid a fee 
on a number of the weekly checks that have been issued, and that Ms. Fuller has 
terminated his legal representation, the Petitioner has requested specific and somewhat 
novel relief.  He has requested that the award be directed to his law firm with an Order 
that he distribute the proceeds in accordance with the attorney fee contract.  (Pet. Brief)  
The Intervenor participated in this hearing, primarily to ensure that it is in compliance 
with the agency’s directives in this regard. 
While I have found that the Petitioner’s fees are appropriate under Section 
10A.327(2), I conclude that I should afford the Respondent a further opportunity to be 
heard before applying this novel or unusual relief.  Specifically, I am concerned that the 
Respondent, Ms. Fuller, will claim in the future that she has not had proper notice or 
opportunity to be heard.  Upon review of the entire record, it is my judgment that 
Petitioner should personally serve this decision on the claimant, which includes the next 
hearing date.  If the claimant is alleging that she has not received adequate notice or 
opportunity to be heard, she is required to participate in the next hearing to articulate 
these arguments.    
ORDER   
THEREFORE, IT IS ORDERED:   
That attorney Mark Sullivan is entitled to the fees set forth in the Attorney Fee 
contract, Petitioner’s Exhibit 3, in the amount of forty (40) percent of claimant’s gross 
recovery. 
That insurance carrier may disburse Attorney Sullivan’s fee directly to him, 
deducted from Respondent's recovery.
