STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Roger Abrams, claimant, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from Lennox Industries, Inc., employer and Indemnity Insurance 
Company of North America, insurance carrier, as defendants.  Hearing was held via 
Zoom videoconference on July 10, 2024.     
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised 
or discussed in this decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.  
Roger Abrams was the only witness to testify live at trial.  The evidentiary record 
also includes joint exhibits 1-5, claimant’s exhibits 1-8, and defendant’s exhibits A-I.  All 
exhibits offered at the time of hearing were received without objection.  The evidentiary 
record closed at the conclusion of the arbitration hearing.       
The parties submitted post-hearing briefs on August 12, 2024, at which time the 
case was fully submitted to the undersigned.     
ISSUES 
The parties submitted the following issues for resolution: 
1. Whether claimant sustained an injury that arose out of and in the course of 
employment on February 24, 2023. 
2. Whether claimant sustained permanent partial disability as the result of the 
February 24, 2023 injury.  If so, the extent of disability he sustained to his 
right shoulder. 
3. The appropriate commencement date for any permanent partial disability 
benefits.

There are several medical experts who have provided their opinions in this case.  
Roger relies on the opinions of Dr. Fish in this case.  Dr. Fish’s opinions are set forth 
above.  Unfortunately, it appears that Dr. Fish’s opinions are based on an inaccurate or 
incomplete history.  (JE5) 
When Dr. Fish issued his initial opinion, he based his opinion on the premise that 
Roger did not have any problems with his right shoulder before February 24, 2023.  The 
evidence demonstrates that this history is not accurate.  (JE5, p. 76) It is not clear if Dr. 
Fish was ever made aware of Roger’s prior problems with his right shoulder, including 
his treatment in 2020.  Dr. Fish issued another opinion letter after meeting with Roger’s 
attorney.  (JE5, pp. 80) When claimant’s attorney met with Dr. Fish on September 11, 
2023, it appears he only shared the emergency room notes from February 27, 2023.  
(JE5, p. 78)  In his September 14, 2023 missive Dr. Fish felt it was significant that Roger 
was able to work after the February 22 ice incident.  However, it is not clear if Dr. Fish 
was aware that after the alleged February 24, 2023 injury, Roger completed his shift on 
Friday and worked his entire overtime shift on Saturday.  Dr. Fish appears to have been 
under the impression that Roger’s pain on February 24 was so severe that he had to 
stay out of work immediately.  (JE5, p. 80) Additionally, Dr. Fish does not address the 
fact that Roger initially denied any type of incident or injury and waited approximately 
five months before he reported any type of incident with pulling a coil.  The record is 
void of evidence to demonstrate that Dr. Fish had the complete history, including the 
notes from the Lennox nurse, Dr. Jew, or the physical therapy records. (JE5)  I find that 
the opinions of Dr. Fish are based on incomplete and inaccurate histories and therefore, 
his opinions are not probative and cannot be relied upon.

causally connected to the alleged February 24, 2023 work injury.   
Given the numerous inconsistencies in the reporting of the onset of symptoms, 
the mechanism of injury, and whether the location of the symptoms changed after the 
alleged February 24, 2023 incident, I find that the preponderance of the evidence does 
not support a finding that Roger sustained an injury at Lennox on February 24, 2023.  
As such, I find that claimant has failed to prove by a preponderance of the evidence that 
he sustained an injury that arose out of and in the course of his employment with 
Lennox on February 24, 2023. 
Because claimant has failed to prove by a preponderance of the evidence that he 
sustained an injury that arose out of and in the course of his employment with Lennox 
on February 24, 2023, all remaining issues, other than costs, are rendered moot. 
CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.14(6) 
(2015). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury actually occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 
employment.  Xenia Rural Water Dist. v. Vegors, 786 N.W.2d 250, 254 (Iowa 2010).  
The words “arising out of” referred to the cause or source of the injury.  The words “in 
the course of” refer to the time, place, and circumstances of the injury.  2800 Corp. v. 
Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124, 128 (Iowa 1995).  An injury arises out of the employment 
when a causal relationship exists between the injury and the employment.  Miedema v. 
Dial Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309, 312 (Iowa 1996).  The injury must be a rational 
consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely incidental to 
the employment.  Koehler Electric v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1, 3 (Iowa 2000); Miedema, 551 
N.W.2d at 311.  An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it happens within 
a period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may be when 
performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties or doing 
an activity incidental to them.  Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 150 (Iowa 
1996). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  Sanchez v.

Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa App. 1996); George A. Hormel & Co. v. 
Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 152 (Iowa 1997).  A cause is proximate if it is a substantial 
factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only cause.  A preponderance of 
the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable rather than merely possible.  
Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa App. 1997). 
When an expert's opinion is based upon an incomplete history, it is not 
necessarily binding on the commissioner or the court. It is then to be weighed, together 
with other facts and circumstances, the ultimate conclusion being for the finder of the 
fact. Musselman v. Central Telephone Company, 154 N.W.2d 128, 133 (Iowa 
1967); Bodish v. Fischer, Inc., 257 Iowa 521, 522, 133 N.W.2d 867 (1965). 
The commissioner as trier of fact has the duty to determine the credibility of the 
witnesses and to weigh the evidence, together with the other disclosed facts and 
circumstances, and then to accept or reject the opinion. Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and 
Casualty Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995). 
Based on the above findings of fact, I conclude that claimant has failed to carry 
his burden of proof to demonstrate by a preponderance of the evidence that he 
sustained an injury that arose out of and in the course of his employment with Lennox 
on February 24, 2023.  The record is replete with inconsistencies.  Furthermore, the 
only medical opinion which supports claimant’s allegations is based on an incomplete or 
inaccurate medical history.  As such, I conclude that claimant has failed to establish that 
he sustained a work-related injury to his right shoulder at Lennox on February 24, 2023.   
Claimant is seeking an assessment of costs as set forth in his exhibit 9.  Costs 
are to be assessed at the discretion of the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commissioner 
or the deputy hearing the case.  876 IAC 4.33.  I find that the claimant was not 
successful in his claim and exercise my discretion to not assess costs against the 
defendants in this case.  Therefore, each party shall bear their own costs. 
Because claimant failed to carry his burden of proof that he sustained a work-
related injury on February 24, 2023, all other issues are rendered moot.
