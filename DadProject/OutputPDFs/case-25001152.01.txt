STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
On March 19, 2025, the claimant filed a petition for alternate medical care 
pursuant to Iowa Code 85.27(4) and 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.48.  The 
defendants filed an answer admitting liability for injuries to the claimant’s left shoulder, 
left arm, and for contusions to the bilateral knees.  The defendants denied liability for 
the claims relating to the claimant’s right shoulder, right arm, hips, bilateral knee 
osteoarthritis, back, collarbone, and neck.  As such, the undersigned dismissed without 
prejudice any claims relating to the right shoulder, right arm, hips, bilateral knee 
osteoarthritis, back, collarbone, and neck, at the outset of the hearing. 
The undersigned presided over the hearing held electronically and recorded 
digitally on March 31, 2025.  That recording constitutes the official record of the 
proceeding pursuant to 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.48(12).  Claimant participated 
through her attorney, MaKayla Augustine.  The defendants participated through their 
attorney, Stephanie Techau.   
Prior to the hearing, the claimant submitted ten pages of exhibits, marked as 
Exhibits 1-10.  The defendants submitted eight pages of exhibits, marked as Exhibits 1-
8.  All of the exhibits were admitted with no objection.     
 
On July 21, 2023, the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commissioner issued an 
order delegating authority to deputy workers’ compensation commissioners, such as the 
undersigned, to issue final agency decisions on applications for alternate care.  
Consequently, this decision constitutes final agency action, and there is no appeal to

ISSUE 
The issue under consideration is whether the defendants should be ordered to 
provide the claimant with a wheelchair and for in-home care subsequent to an upcoming 
surgery on April 7, 2025.1 
FINDINGS OF FACT

bilateral upper extremities, bilateral shoulders, bilateral lower extremities, bilateral hips,

arm, hips, bilateral knee osteoarthritis, back, collarbone, and neck, the undersigned will 
not consider allegations relating to these body parts as the petition relating to these

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Iowa Code 85.27(4) provides, in relevant part: 
For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish reasonable 
services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has the right to 
choose the care….  The treatment must be offered promptly and be 
reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the 
employee.  If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the care 
offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such dissatisfaction 
to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the employer and 
the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited to treat the 
injury.  If the employer and employee cannot agree on such alternate care, 
the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable proofs of the 
necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
Iowa Code 85.27(4). See Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 433 (Iowa 
1997).   
“Iowa Code section 85.27(4) affords an employer who does not contest the 
compensability of a workplace injury a qualified statutory right to control the medical

care provided to an injured employee.”  Ramirez-Trujillo v. Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 
N.W.2d 759, 769 (Iowa 2016) (citing R.R. Donnelly & Sons v. Barnett, 670 N.W.2d 190, 
195, 197 (Iowa 2003)).  “In enacting the right-to-choose provision in section 85.27(4), 
our legislature sought to balance the interests of injured employees against the 
competing interests of their employers.”  Ramirez, 878 N.W.2d at 770-71 (citing Bell 
Bros., 779 N.W.2d at 202, 207; IBP, Inc. v. Harker, 633 N.W.2d 322, 326-27 (Iowa 
2001)).   
The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except where the 
employer has denied liability for the injury.  Iowa Code section 85.27; Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening, October 16, 1975).  An employer’s right to select 
the provider of medical treatment to an injured worker does not include the right to 
determine how an injured worker should be diagnosed, evaluated, treated, or other 
matters of professional medical judgment.  Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 
866389 (Declaratory Ruling, May 18, 1988).  Reasonable care includes care necessary 
to diagnose the condition, and defendants are not entitled to interfere with the medical 
judgment of its own treating physician.  Pote v. Mickow Corp., File No. 694639 (Review-
Reopening Decision, June 17, 1986).   
The employer must furnish “reasonable medical services and supplies and 
reasonable and necessary appliances to treat an injured employee.”  Stone Container 
Corp. v. Castle, 657 N.W.2d 485, 490 (Iowa 2003)(emphasis in original).  Such 
employer-provided care “must be offered promptly and be reasonably suited to treat the 
injury without undue inconvenience to the employee.”  Iowa Code section 85.27(4).  
More specifically in Long, the court noted that “the employer’s obligation under the 
statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.”  Long v. Roberts 
Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122, 128 (Iowa 1995).   
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment - and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See e.g. 
Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e); Bell Bros. Heating and Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 
N.W.2d 193, 209 (Iowa 2010); Long, 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  More specifically in 
Long, the court noted that “the employer’s obligation under the statute turns on the 
question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.”  Long, 528 N.W.2d at 128.   
An injured employee dissatisfied with the employer-furnished care (or lack 
thereof) may share the employee’s discontent with the employer and if the parties 
cannot reach an agreement on alternate care, “the commissioner may, upon application 
and reasonable proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and order the care.”  Id.  
“Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.”  Long, 528 
N.W.2d at 123; Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 436.  As the party seeking 
relief in the form of alternate care, the employee bears the burden of proving that the 
authorized care is unreasonable.  Id. at 124; Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d at 209; Pirelli-
Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 436.  Because “the employer’s obligation under the 
statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability,” an injured 
employee’s dissatisfaction with employer-provided care, standing alone, is not enough

to find such care unreasonable.  Id.  However, if substantial evidence exists that the 
care authorized by the employer was ineffective, it may be found that the care was not 
reasonable.  Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 437.   
The question in this case is whether the defendants should be ordered to provide 
the claimant with in-home care and/or an aide.   
Ms. Burris was injured while traveling in New Jersey for work.  The defendants 
authorized emergency care in New Jersey, and then allowed Ms. Burris to be seen by 
her primary care physician following her return from New Jersey.  Dr. Vo saw the 
claimant upon her return from New Jersey and recommended that she see orthopedic 
physicians.  As such, the defendants referred Ms. Burris to Drs. Holm and Nemanich.  
Subsequent to these referrals, Dr. Holm recommended that Ms. Burris undergo a left 
shoulder surgery.  This surgery and postsurgical physical therapy were authorized by 
the defendants.  The issue is whether the recommendation of Dr. Vo for the claimant to 
have an “in-home nurse” due to her “current medical conditions,” is reasonable.   
The claimant contends that this recommendation was made by an authorized 
treating provider, Dr. Vo.  The defendants contend that Dr. Vo was only authorized on a 
limited basis to treat Ms. Burris following her return from New Jersey.  The defendants 
did not revoke their authorization of Dr. Vo as a treating physician until a letter dated 
March 28, 2025, so his recommendation on March 6, 2025, would normally carry the 
day.  However, Dr. Vo made referrals to orthopedic physicians, Drs. Holm and 
Nemanich.  Dr. Holm treated the claimant’s shoulder issues.  Dr. Nemanich treated the 
claimant’s bilateral knees.  Dr. Holm’s office took no position as to the necessity of a 
home health aide, but explicitly noted that it was not due to the claimant’s shoulder 
issues.  Dr. Nemanich recommended a wheelchair but made no comment as to the 
claimant’s need for a home health aide.  He made no recommendations regarding a 
home health aide.  The recommendations, or silence, of the specialist physicians carries 
more weight than the recommendations of Dr. Vo.  Additionally, Dr. Vo’s 
recommendation for a home health aide is insufficiently detailed to indicate whether it 
relates solely to the shoulder injury or to the claimant’s bilateral knee contusions, or the 
claimant’s bilateral knee osteoarthritis.  Dr. Vo’s order is also unclear as to whether the 
need for an “in-home nurse” was due to her work injury or personal health conditions.   
Iowa Code section 85.27(1) indicates that employers “shall furnish reasonable 
surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, 
nursing, ambulance, and hospital services and supplies therefor…”  Dr. Vo’s order 
indicates that Ms. Burris should be provided with an “in-home nurse...” but further notes 
that this individual would be to help Ms. Burris with “activities of daily living and chores 
as needed.”  The claimant’s application for alternate care refers to “in-home care.”  
Certainly, nursing care following a surgery would be a reasonable request pursuant to 
the statute in certain cases.   
However, the medical necessity of this request is unclear from the record.  Dr. Vo 
is the only provider who makes mention of this need.  His record is simply a conclusory 
three-sentence recommendation.  It is not clear whether or not this is a reasonable

request or, in the alternative, that the care provided by the defendants is unreasonable.  
Two other providers make no mention of the need for a home-health aide or nurse. 
The claimant also made mention during the hearing that the defendants were 
interfering in the judgment of a treating physician.  I reject this argument, as two other 
providers assumed care for the body parts which Ms. Burris allegedly injured on 
January 16, 2025.  Dr. Vo is the claimant’s family physician.  He is not a specialist.  
There is no evidence that he practices occupational medicine.  Therefore, there is no 
evidence that he is more qualified than the treating orthopedic physicians to offer 
proposed restrictions.   
Based on the foregoing, I find that the claimant has not proven that the care 
offered by the defendants is unreasonable.   
 
IT IS THEREFORE ORDERED: 
1. The claimant’s petition for alternate care is dismissed without prejudice as it 
relates to allegations concerning the claimant’s right shoulder, right arm, hips, 
bilateral knee osteoarthritis, back, collarbone, and neck.   
  
2. If claimant seeks to recover the charges incurred in obtaining care for the 
conditions for which defendants denied liability, the defendants are barred 
from asserting lack of authorization as a defense to those charges during the 
period of their denial.   
 
3. The claimant’s petition for alternate care is denied.
