On October 3, 2024, claimant filed a petition for alternate medical seeking 
treatment with Holly McClintock including Spravato therapy be authorized. Defendants 
filed an answer admitting responsibility for the injury that was pled but denying that the 
care requested should be authorized.  
A hearing was held on October 14, 2024, where the parties’ representatives 
presented argument. The record consists of Claimant’s Exhibit A, pages 1-10, and 
Defendants’ Exhibit 1, pages 1-8.  
On or about April 3, 2013, claimant sustained a work-related injury arising out of 
and in the course of his employment. This work injury resulted, in part, of the need for 
care for a mental health issue.  
On January 16, 2019, the parties agreed to a settlement of a workers’ 
compensation suit pertaining to the work injury of April 3, 2013. (Claimant’s Exhibit A, 
pages 7-9) In the settlement agreement, the parties agreed that, “treatment with 
Claimant’s current providers for his upper extremities, knees, back pain, and mental 
health/PTSD will continue at the providers’ discretion and will not be disturbed by this 
settlement nor interfered with by Defendants.” (CE A:8)  
At the time of the hearing, claimant’s primary mental health provider is Timi 
Jordison. Ms. Jordison was managing claimant’s mental health care and remains the 
overseer of that health concern today. In the fall of 2022, Ms. Jordison referred claimant 
to Holly McClintock, PMHNP, a nurse practitioner specializing in psychiatry, at 
Community Health Center of Fort Dodge Medical. (CE A:1) NP McClintock

recommended claimant seek an evaluation at Van Diest and consult with Dr. Tulp for 
Spravato therapy due to claimant’s treatment-resistant depression. (CE A:2) This plan 
was approved by the Center’s in-house doctor, Dr. Elizabeth Stoebe, on July 18, 2024.  
Dr. Stoebe did not see claimant but reviewed the treatment records authored by NP 
McClintock.  
Spravato is a new nasal spray medication which is available only through a 
certified doctor’s office or clinic. (CE A:10) It is available only through a restricted 
distribution system, under a Risk Evaluation and Mitigation Strategy. (CE A:10) It does 
not appear that NP McClintock is authorized to prescribe Spravato. 
Defendants argue that they should be permitted to have a doctor of their choice 
examine claimant to determine the proper future course of treatment. They have 
requested claimant be seen by Dr. Fields at Waverly Clinic in Waverly, Iowa. 
(Defendants’ Exhibit 1, page 1) Defendants expressed concern that the recent 
treatment and evaluations are from a physician’s assistant and nurse practitioner and 
desire to have claimant evaluated by a licensed medical doctor. (DE 1:3)  Defendants 
also wanted Dr. Fields to take over care completely. (DE 1:4)  
Claimant resists this for six reasons. First, the settlement agreement requires no 
interference by defendants. Second, longstanding precedent requires referrals by 
authorized treating providers be honored. Third, Waverly is unduly inconvenient to the 
claimant with a nearly two-hour drive time from home to the clinic and back again. 
Further, Dr. Fields is not reasonably suited as a treater as he is an occupational doctor 
with a specialty in preventative medicine, not a mental health professional. Fourth, a 
nurse practitioner is not necessarily underqualified compared to a medical doctor. Fifth, 
Dr. Stoebe, a doctor licensed in the state of Iowa, has reviewed the referral to Van 
Diest. Finally, if Dr. Fields disagrees with treatment recommendations, defendants could 
wrongly terminate care.  
As claimant is seeking relief in this case, claimant bears the burden of proof to 
show by a preponderance of the evidence that the offered medical treatment is not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the employee.   
Under Iowa law, the employer is required to provide care to an injured employee 
and is permitted to choose the care.  Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 
N.W.2d 433 (Iowa 1997).   
Iowa Code section 85.27 provides, in relevant part: 
For purposes of this section, this employer is obliged to furnish 
reasonable services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has 
the right to choose the care.  The treatment must be offered promptly and 
be reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the 
employee.  If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the care 
offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such 
dissatisfaction to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the

employer and the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited 
to treat the injury.  If the employer and employee cannot agree on such 
alternate care, the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable 
proofs of the necessity therefore, allow and order other care. 
The question of reasonable care is a question of fact.  An application for alternate 
medical care is not granted simply because the employee is dissatisfied with the care 
the employer has chosen.  Mere dissatisfaction with the care is not sufficient grounds to 
grant an application for alternate medical care.  The employee has the burden of 
proving that the care chosen by the employer is unreasonable.  Unreasonableness can 
be established by showing that the care was not offered promptly, was not reasonably 
suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the claimant. West 
Side Transport v. Cordell, 601 N.W.2d 691 (Iowa 1999);  Long v. Roberts Dairy 
Company, 528 N.W. 2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  Unreasonableness can be established by 
showing that the care authorized by the employer has not been effective and is “inferior 
or less extensive” than other available care requested by the employee.  Pirelli-
Armstrong, at 437. 
An employer’s statutory right is to select the providers of care and the employer 
may consider cost and other pertinent factors when exercising its choice.  Long, at 
124.  An employer (typically) is not a licensed health care provider and does not 
possess medical expertise.  Accordingly, an employer does not have the right to control 
the methods the providers choose to evaluate, diagnose and treat the injured 
employee.  An employer is not entitled to control a licensed health care provider’s 
exercise of professional judgment.  Assman v. Blue Star Foods, Declaratory Ruling, File 
No. 866389 (May 18, 1988).  An employer’s failure to follow recommendations of an 
authorized physician in matters of treatment is commonly a failure to provide reasonable 
treatment.  Boggs v. Cargill, Inc., File No. 1050396 (Alt. Care, January 31, 1994) 
(Settlement agreement requires no interference by defendants). 
At hearing, claimant argued that the language of the settlement agreement 
controls the course of care. The language of the agreement states that claimant is 
entitled to treatment with “current providers” without interference from defendants. The 
question is then what is “current providers.” Claimant argued that current providers 
includes anyone that is recommended or referred to by a treating physician. This 
definition is derived from case law interpreting alternate care law in the State of Iowa. A 
more common interpretation of current providers would not include any and all providers 
referred or recommended by the existing treating health professional.  
When asked under what scenario defendants would have a right for an 
examination of claimant by a doctor of their choosing, claimant suggested that if Timi 
Jordison was no longer able to direct claimant’s care, that would be an appropriate 
opportunity. Otherwise, so long as claimant was following the recommendations of an 
authorized physician, the treatment recommended should be paid for by the defendants 
without interference.

In this scenario, it appears that claimant wants to avoid the statutory right of the 
defendants to control care by arguing that right was surrendered in the settlement 
agreement while also applying the alternate care case law interpretation to the 
contractual clause instead of using contract law to interpret the meaning of an undefined 
term.  
The agreement stated “current providers,” not “providers recommended by the 
current providers.” At the time of the agreement, the current provider was Timi Jordison. 
Ms. Jordison is not authorized to prescribe medication. At the time of the settlement 
agreement, the outlet Ms. Jordison relied upon to provide that service was Berryhill. As 
of October 5, 2022, Berryhill was not a viable alternative. Thus, the current providers 
were no longer available but for Ms. Jordison.  
Defendants requesting claimant see an alternate medical provider does not 
violate the settlement agreement.  
However, longstanding precedent does require referrals by authorized treating 
providers to be honored. When a designated physician refers a patient to another 
physician, that physician acts as the defendant employer’s agent.  Permission for the 
referral from defendant is not necessary.  Kittrell v. Allen Memorial Hospital, Thirty-
fourth Biennial Report of the Industrial Commissioner, 164 (Arb. November 1, 1979) 
(aff’d by industrial commissioner).  See also Limoges v. Meier Auto Salvage, I Iowa 
Industrial Commissioner Reports 207 (1981). There is no disagreement that Ms. Timi 
Jordison is an authorized treating provider. Ms. Jordison made the referral to Holly 
McClintock. Ms. McClintock specializes in treatment of mental health, whereas Dr. 
Fields does not. Further, Dr. Fields is almost two hours from claimant’s residence.  
Care should be provided within a reasonable distance from claimant’s 
residence.   Trade Professionals, Inc. v. Shriver, 661 N.W.2d 119 (Iowa 2003), (more 
than 100 miles and 3 hours driving time is an undue inconvenience to the injured 
worker);  Schrock v. Corkery Waste Disposal, Inc., File No. 1133725, Alt. Care, June 
26, 1996 (120 mile round trip excessive);  Cordero v. Florilli Corp., File No. 1134333, 
Alt. Care, September 5, 1996 (care ordered within 50 mile radius of claimant’s 
home);  Schulte v. Vocational Services of Area Residential Care, File No. 1134342, Alt. 
Care, September 6, 1996 (care more than 70 miles away unreasonable). 
Dr. Fields’ location is unduly inconvenient for claimant and he is not a mental 
health specialist like Ms. McClintock. For these reasons, and due to the referral by an 
authorized treating health care provider, Ms. Jordison, it is found that the care proferred 
by defendants in Dr. Fields is not reasonable care.  
It is appropriate for Ms. McClintock to be authorized as a mental health provider 
for claimant. However, Ms. McClintock is not qualified to prescribe or administer 
Spravato. There is no showing by claimant that Spravato is a reasonable form of 
treatment. Claimant’s request was specifically that Holly McClintock be authorized as 
well as Spravato therapy be authorized. The latter request is denied, as claimant has 
not carried his burden that the alternative—which is no authorization of Spravato

treatment—is unreasonable. While Spravato is an FDA approved treatment for 
treatment-resistant depression, there is no showing that claimant is a good candidate 
for such treatment or that the benefits outweigh the risks.  
Claimant’s petition for alternate medical care is GRANTED in part and DENIED 
in part.  
ORDER 
THEREFORE, the following is ordered: 
1. Claimant’s request that Holly McClintock be authorized to treat claimant’s 
mental health is GRANTED.  
2. Claimant’s request to authorize treatment with Spravato is DENIED.
