compensation benefits from Tyson Foods, Inc. (Tyson), self-insured employer.  This 
matter was heard on November 14, 2024, with a final submission date of January 17, 
2025.

Defendant’s Exhibits A-F, and the testimony of claimant, Michael Shay, and Ashley

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The first issue to be determined is the extent of claimant’s entitlement to 
permanent partial disability benefits. 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e). 
Since claimant has an impairment to the body as a whole, an industrial disability 
has been sustained.  Industrial disability was defined in Diederich v. Tri-City Ry. Co. of 
Iowa, 219 Iowa 587, 591, 258 N.W. 899, 902 (1935) as follows:  "It is therefore plain that 
the Legislature intended the term 'disability' to mean 'industrial disability' or loss of 
earning capacity and not a mere 'functional disability' to be computed in terms of 
percentages of the total physical and mental ability of a normal man." 
Functional impairment is an element to be considered in determining industrial 
disability which is the reduction of earning capacity, but consideration must also be 
given to the injured employee's age, education, qualifications, experience, motivation, 
loss of earnings, severity and situs of the injury, work restrictions, inability to engage in 
employment for which the employee is fitted and the employer's offer of work or failure 
to so offer.  McSpadden v. Big Ben Coal Co., 288 N.W.2d 181, 192 (Iowa 1980); Olson 
v. Goodyear Service Stores, 255 Iowa 1112, 1120, 125 N.W.2d 251, 256 (1963); Barton 
v. Nevada Poultry Co., 253 Iowa 285, 110 N.W.2d 660, 663 (1961). 
Compensation for permanent partial disability shall begin when it is medically 
indicated that maximum medical improvement from the injury has been reached and 
that the extent of loss or percentage of permanent impairment can be determined by the 
use of the guides to the evaluation of permanent impairment, published by the American 
Medical Association, as adopted by the Commissioner. Iowa Code section 85.34(2)   
In Guyton v. Irving Jensen Co., 373 N.W.2d 101 (Iowa 1985), the Iowa court 
formally adopted the “odd-lot doctrine.”  Under that doctrine a worker becomes an 
odd-lot employee when an injury makes the worker incapable of obtaining employment 
in any well-known branch of the labor market.  An odd-lot worker is thus totally disabled 
if the only services the worker can perform are “so limited in quality, dependability, or 
quantity that a reasonably stable market for them does not exist.”  Id., at 105.

Under the odd-lot doctrine, the burden of persuasion on the issue of industrial 
disability always remains with the worker.  Nevertheless, when a worker makes a prima 
facie case of total disability by producing substantial evidence that the worker is not 
employable in the competitive labor market, the burden to produce evidence showing 
availability of suitable employment shifts to the employer.  If the employer fails to 
produce such evidence and the trier of facts finds the worker does fall in the odd-lot 
category, the worker is entitled to a finding of total disability.  Guyton, 373 N.W.2d at 
106.  Factors to be considered in determining whether a worker is an odd-lot employee 
include the worker’s reasonable but unsuccessful effort to find steady employment, 
vocational or other expert evidence demonstrating suitable work is not available for the 
worker, the extent of the worker’s physical impairment, intelligence, education, age, 
training, and potential for retraining.  No factor is necessarily dispositive on the issue.  
Second Injury Fund of Iowa v. Nelson, 544 N.W.2d 258, 266 (Iowa 1995).  Even under 
the odd-lot doctrine, the trier of fact is free to determine the weight and credibility of 
evidence in determining whether the worker’s burden of persuasion has been carried, 
and only in an exceptional case would evidence be sufficiently strong as to compel a 
finding of total disability as a matter of law.  Guyton, 373 N.W.2d at 106. 
Claimant contends she is permanently and totally disabled.  (Claimant’s Brief, 
page 23)  Defendant argues that claimant is only entitled to permanent partial disability 
benefits that have been paid already.  (Defendant’s Post-Hearing Brief, page 9) 
Claimant was 64 years old at the time of hearing.  Claimant graduated from high 
school.  She worked on a farm and at a Baskin-Robbins.  Claimant began working at 
Tyson in 1987.  Claimant has always worked on the processing side of the plant.  
Claimant has worked in jobs that required repetitive use of both of her upper 
extremities.   
Claimant has had reverse total arthroplasties on both the left and right shoulders.   
Two experts have opined regarding claimant’s permanent impairment to her 
shoulders.  Dr. Bissell performed both of claimant’s surgeries.  He found that claimant 
had a 23 percent permanent impairment on the left upper extremity, converting to a 14 
percent permanent impairment to the body as a whole.  He also found that claimant had 
a 16 percent permanent impairment to the right upper extremity, converting to a 10 
percent permanent impairment to the body as a whole.  Based on the combined values 
chart found in the Guides at page 604, this results in a 23 percent permanent 
impairment to the body as a whole.   
Dr. Bansal evaluated claimant once for an IME.  He found that claimant had a 37 
percent permanent impairment to the left upper extremity, converting to a 22 percent 
permanent impairment to the body as a whole.  He also found that claimant had a 29 
percent permanent impairment to the right upper extremity, converting to a 17 percent 
permanent impairment to the body as a whole.  Based upon the combined values chart 
found in the Guides at page 604, this results in a 35 percent permanent impairment to 
body as a whole.

I recognize Dr. Bissell performed both of claimant’s shoulder surgeries and 
provided long-term care for the claimant.  However, Dr. Bansal’s report is far more 
detailed than that of Dr. Bissell.  I am able to follow how Dr. Bansal arrived at his 
opinions regarding claimant’s permanent impairment for her left and right upper 
extremities.  Given this record, it is found that Dr. Bansal’s opinion regarding permanent 
impairment are more convincing than those of Dr. Bissell.  Claimant is found to have a 
permanent impairment of 35 percent to the body as a whole. 
Regarding permanent restrictions, claimant was returned to light duty with 
restrictions from Dr. Bissell as detailed in Joint Exhibit 3, pages 80, 83 and 86.  It is true 
Dr. Bansal assigned far more detailed restrictions than those given by Dr. Bissell.  
However, as a practical matter, the permanent restrictions given to claimant by Dr. 
Bansal were never applied in the work place.  Claimant was able to perform light duty at 
Tyson under the restrictions as provided by Dr. Bissell.  Based on this, it is found that 
the permanent restrictions given by Dr. Bissell, limiting claimant to light duty, and as 
detailed in Joint Exhibit 3, pages 80, 83 and 86, are more convincing than those of Dr. 
Bansal. 
The record is clear that Tyson was able to accommodate claimant’s restrictions 
and allowed her to return to light duty jobs.  The record is also clear that claimant left 
her job at Tyson to take possession of her sister’s home in Idaho.  The reason for 
claimant terminating her employment with Tyson was not due to her work injuries, but 
was due to her need to move to Idaho to take possession of her sister’s home.   
Ms. Sellner opined that claimant had job opportunities in her geographic region in 
Idaho.  Mr. Audet opined, given Dr. Bissell’s permanent restrictions, that claimant had a 
potential loss of earning capacity as high as 69 percent. (Ex. 5) 
Claimant testified she has asked a few store managers about employment.  
Other than this testimony, there is no evidence claimant has looked for work since 
leaving Tyson.  Claimant has not filed job applications.  No doctor has restricted 
claimant from returning to work. 
A loss of earning capacity due to voluntary choice or lack of motivation to return 
to work is not compensable. Copeland v. Boone Book and Bible Store, File No. 
1059319 (App. November 6, 1997).  Snow v. Chevron Phillips Chemical Co., File No. 
5016619 (App. October 25, 2007). See also Brown v. Nissen Corp., 89-90 IAWC 56, 62 
(App. 1989) (no prima facie showing that claimant is unemployable when claimant did 
not make an attempt for vocational rehabilitation). 
When all factors are taken into consideration, it is found claimant has a 60 
percent loss of earning capacity or an industrial disability.   
Using the same factors as detailed above, it is found that claimant is not 
permanently and totally disabled.  It is also found that claimant is not an odd-lot 
employee.

The next issue to be determined is the commencement date of permanent partial 
disability benefits.  Dr. Bissell opined that claimant had reached MMI on the right 
shoulder as of November 9, 2023.  (JE 3, p. 69)  He found claimant at MMI on the left

Commencement of permanent partial disability benefits shall commence as of July 2,

The next issue to be determined is whether claimant is entitled to reimbursement 
for Dr. Bansal’s IME.   
An employer is only liable to reimburse an employee for the cost of an 
examination conducted pursuant to this subsection if the injury for which the employee 
is being examined is determined to be compensable.  Iowa Code § 85.39(2) (2017).  
“An employer is not liable for the cost of such examination if the injury for which the 
employee is being examined is determined not to have a compensable injury. A 
determination of the reasonableness of a fee for an examination made pursuant to this 
subsection, shall be based on the typical fee charged by a medical provider to perform 
an impairment rating in the local area where the examination is conducted.”  Iowa Code 
§ 85.39(2); Tweeten v. Tweeten, 999 N.W.2d 270, 282 (Iowa 2023).   
Iowa Code section 85.39(2), as amended, allows an employee with a 
compensable injury to be reimbursed the reasonable cost of an examination. See Iowa 
Code § 85.39(2)(2017); Mid American Construction LLC v. Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d 838, 849 
(Iowa 2024). Under the statute, an examination may include reviewing medical records, 
conducting a physical exam, opining on causation, assessing permanent impairment, 
assigning restrictions, and recommending further care. Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d at 851.  
The burden is on the claimant to show the reasonableness of his or her chosen 
physician’s IME fee. Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d at 852; see also Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e)(stating “Ordinarily, the burden of proof on an issue is upon the party who 
would suffer loss if the issue were not established.”); Pexa v. Auto Owners Ins. Co., 686 
N.W.2d 150, 156 (Iowa 2004)(stating “the plaintiff has the burden to prove the 
reasonable value of the [medical] service rendered”).  The reasonableness of an IME 
fee is a question of fact to be decided by the commissioner. See Quaker Oats Co. v. 
Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 154 (Iowa 1996). The reasonableness of an IME fee must be 
based upon the typical fee charged by a medical provider performing an impairment 
rating in the local area where the IME is conducted. Iowa Code § 85.39 (2); Sandlin, 2 
N.W.3d at 852.   
In Des Moines Area Reg’l Transit Auth. v. Young, 867 N.W.2d 839, 847 (Iowa 
2015), the Supreme Court noted that in cases where Iowa Code section 85.39 IME 
reimbursement is not triggered, a claimant can still be reimbursed expenses associated 
with the preparation of a written report as a cost under administrative rule 876 IAC 4.33 
because the report is being used as evidence in lieu of the doctor’s testimony.  Id. at

846-47.  However, only the cost of the report can be assessed, not the underlying exam 
or treatment. Id. 
Dr. Bissell issued his permanent impairment rating for claimant on August 29, 
2024.  (JE 3, p. 93)  Dr. Bansal issued his permanent impairment rating in a report 
dated October 14, 2024.  (Ex. 2)  Dr. Bansal indicated his charges for his IME were 
typical and reasonable for charges of an IME in this geographic area.  (Ex. 3)  There is 
no evidence disputing this claim.  Based on this record, claimant has carried her burden 
of proof that she is due reimbursement for charges associated with Dr. Bansal’s IME.  
The final issue to be determined is costs. Claimant seeks costs including costs 
associated with Mr. Audet's report.  
876 IAC 4.33 indicates, in relevant part:   
Costs taxed by the workers’ compensation commissioner or a deputy 
commissioner shall be (1) attendance of a certified shorthand reporter or 
presence of mechanical means at hearings and evidential depositions, (2) 
transcription costs when appropriate, (3) costs of service of the original 
notice and subpoenas, (4) witness fees and expenses as provided by 
Iowa Code sections 622.69 and 622.72, (5) the costs of doctors’ and 
practitioners’ deposition testimony, provided that said costs do not exceed 
the amounts provided by Iowa Code sections 622.69 and 622.72, (6) the 
reasonable costs of obtaining no more than two doctors’ or practitioners’ 
reports, (7) filing fees when appropriate, (8) costs of persons reviewing 
health service disputes. 
The costs of Mr. Audet’s report are not considered a reimbursable cost under 
rule 876 IAC 4.33.
