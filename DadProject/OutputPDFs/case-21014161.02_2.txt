I. 
BACKGROUND FACTS AND PROCEEDINGS. 
Hayes started working as a part-time dishwasher at Christian Retirement Homes in July of 
2021. On September 12, 2021, tripped over a metal strip on the floor while taking out some 
garbage. Approximately a week after his fall, Hayes first treated for his injury. He went to the 
emergency department on September 20, 2021 with complaints of lower back pain radiating down 
his right leg related to his injury. D0005 at 60. The ER doctor noted Hayes’ chronic low back pain 
and a prescription for tramadol. D0005 at 61-62.  
Hayes reported the injury to his employer, who referred him to physical therapy. D0003,

II. 
SCOPE AND STANDARD OF REVIEW. 
In workers’ compensation cases, the scope of the court’s review is governed by the Iowa 
Administrative Procedure Act, Iowa Code chapter 17A. Iowa Code § 86.26; Meyer v. IBP, Inc., 
710 N.W.2d 213, 218 (Iowa 2006). “Under the Act, we may only interfere with the commissioner’s 
decision if it is erroneous under one of the grounds enumerated in the statute, and a party’s 
substantial rights have been prejudiced.” Meyer, 710 N.W.2d p. 218. The party challenging an 
agency decision bears the burden of demonstrating the action’s invalidity and resulting prejudice. 
Iowa Code § 17A.19(8)(a). The district court acts in an appellate capacity to correct errors of law 
on the part of the agency. Grundmeyer v. Weyerhaeuser Co., 649 N.W.2d 744, 748 (Iowa 2002).  
“If the claim of error lies with the agency's findings of fact, the proper question on review 
is whether substantial evidence supports those findings of fact” when the record is viewed as a 
whole. Meyer, 710 N.W.2d at 219. Substantial evidence is evidence of the quality and quantity 
“that would be deemed sufficient by a neutral, detached, and reasonable person, to establish the 
fact at issue when the consequences resulting from the establishment of that fact are understood to 
be serious and of great importance.” Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(f)(1); Mycogen Seeds v. Sands, 686 
N.W.2d 457, 464. The application of workers’ compensation laws to the facts is vested in the 
Commissioner. Midwest Ambulance Serv. v. Ruud, 754 N.W.2d 860, 864 (Iowa 2008). District 
courts sitting in judicial review “may reverse the commissioner’s application of the law to the facts 
only if it is ‘irrational, illogical, or wholly unjustifiable.’” Id. (quoting Iowa Code § 
17A.19(10)(m)). The Court must affirm the Commissioner’s factual findings if they 
are supported by substantial evidence. Mid Am. Constr. LLC v. Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d 838, 
846 (Iowa 2024). Evidence may be substantial even if “different conclusions may be 
drawn from the evidence”. Cedar Rapids Cmty. Sch. Dist. v. Pease, 807 N.W.2d 839, 845 (Iowa

III. 
MERITS. 
Petitioner makes two legal arguments that are somewhat intertwined.  First, he points out 
that the parties stipulated that he sustained an injury arising out of and in the course of employment 
on September 12, 2021. They further stipulated that the injury was a cause of temporary disability 
during a period of recovery and that the injury was industrial in nature. Based on these stipulations, 
he claims that his only burden was to prove he sustained a permanent disability from the injury, 
not that it was work related. Second, petitioner points out that defendants selected Dr. Dolphin as 
the treating physician prior to denying liability. He claims the employer is bound to provide the 
treatment recommended by its own treating physician even if another consulting physician offers 
an opposing opinion. 
The deputy noted the stipulation and that defendants were bound to it.  She did not 
specifically explain why the stipulation to a temporary work injury did not preclude her ultimate 
finding that the injury resulting in surgery was not causally connected to the fall at work. She 
likewise did not address the issue whether defendants were bound to the treatment 
recommendation of Dr. Dolphin. Rather, the deputy’s evaluation of the case essentially consists of 
three segments: 1) discussing petitioner’s lengthy and significant medical history, 2) discussing 
petitioner’s credibility as to information he provided to his employer and to medical providers, and 
3) weighing the opinions of the medical experts.  That led to her conclusion that the back injury 
that resulted in the surgery was preexisting and not caused by the workplace fall. 
While the deputy did not directly confront petitioner’s arguments, her decision is not 
unreasonable. Most of the case law relevant to petitioner’s arguments comes from penalty or bad 
faith denial decisions. Those cases consider whether an employer has improperly denied or delayed

paying benefits. The usual standard is whether the denial or delay is: 1) necessary to investigate 
the claim, and 2) whether the employer had a reasonable basis to contest benefits. McIlravy v. N. 
River Ins. Co., 653 N.W.2d 323, 328 (Iowa 2002). Defendants also argue that case law supports 
changing positions if it receives more information in a case. Normally, the additional information 
is held against the employer. Squealer Feeds v. Pickering, 530 N.W.2d 678, 683 (Iowa 1995), 
abrogated on other grounds by Wells Dairy, Inc. v. Am. Indus. Refrigeration, Inc., 690 N.W.2d 38 
(Iowa 2004). In Squealer Feeds, the court held that a denial of benefits might be supported early 
in an investigation, but might be unreasonable based on subsequent information. Id. In this case, 
defendants make the reverse argument, that is, that an initial decision to approve benefits and/or 
medical care might be unjustified after receiving additional information during an investigation. 
Defendants’ argument is rational. It is uncontested that petitioner had a workplace fall. It 
is uncontested that he had a back injury. Defendants were initially receptive to considering the 
claim as a workplace injury. As the investigation proceeded, they discovered more information, 
including that petitioner was not completely forthcoming about his lengthy and significant medical 
history. It took time to collect the history from several providers. Defendants then sought 
additional medical opinions starting with a record review from Dr. Boulden. Based on the 
additional information, defendants veered. They requested an IME from Dr. Igram, which 
ultimately led them to deny the claim. 
Certainly, this was not an ideal process. Petitioner was in limbo while defendants 
determined whether they would pay for the surgery that their own selected doctor recommended. 
Still, defendants had a right to continue to evaluate the information as it was collected. 
Once those arguments are resolved, the case can be resolved on substantial evidence. The 
deputy’s lengthy and detailed decision accurately recounts the extensive evidence provided in this

CONCLUSION AND DISPOSITION. 
 
The decision of the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commission is AFFIRMED. Costs are 
assessed to petitioner.
