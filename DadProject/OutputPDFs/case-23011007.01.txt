STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Teresa Liddell-Bailey, claimant, filed two petitions for arbitration against Nestle 
Purina Petcare Company, as the employer, and Indemnity Insurance Company of North 
America, as the insurance carrier.  This case came before the undersigned for an 
arbitration hearing on May 20, 2024.  Pursuant to an order from the Iowa Workers’ 
Compensation Commissioner, this case was heard via a Zoom videoconference.   
The parties filed a hearing report prior to the commencement of the hearing.  On 
the hearing report, the parties entered into numerous stipulations.  Those stipulations 
were accepted and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be 
made or discussed.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations. 
The evidentiary record includes Joint Exhibits 1 through 4, Claimant’s Exhibits 1 
through 7, and Defendants’ Exhibits A through J.  Claimant objected to introduction of 
Defendants’ Exhibit H.  The objection was overruled, and Exhibit H is considered for 
whatever relevance or weight it may have to these proceedings. 
Claimant testified on her own behalf.  No other witnesses testified live at the 
hearing.  The evidentiary record closed at the conclusion of the arbitration hearing and 
the case was considered fully submitted at that time.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned, having considered all of the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
Teresa Liddell-Bailey, claimant, a 55-year-old woman, who lives in Davenport, 
filed these workers’ compensation claims against her former employer, Nestle Purina 
Petcare Company (hereinafter referred to as “Nestle”).  Ms. Liddell-Bailey asserts an 
injury occurring on December 6, 2022.  Defendants stipulate that claimant sustained a 
left leg injury that arose out of and in the course of her employment on that date.  
However, defendants dispute any additional claim that Ms. Liddell-Bailey sustained an 
unscheduled injury as a result of a gait disturbance. 
Claimant asserts a second injury claim, contending that she sustained a right leg 
injury on July 28, 2023.  Defendants dispute that claimant was employed as of this 
injury date and further dispute whether claimant sustained an injury that arose out of 
and in the course of her employment on July 28, 2023. 
Ms. Liddell-Bailey started her employment with Nestle in December 2011.  
Claimant worked as a line operator for Nestle.  On December 6, 2022, while performing 
typical job duties, claimant stepped off a platform and slipped on some spilled feed.  
She immediately felt pain and heard something pop in her left knee.  She immediately

treatment for claimant’s left knee injury. 
Claimant first sought medical care on December 7, 2022.  (Joint Exhibit 1, page 
1)   She was initially treated conservatively, and an MRI was ordered.  The radiologist 
interpreted the MRI to demonstrate a partially torn MCL ligament in claimant’s left knee.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury actually occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 
employment. Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 150 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. 
Dial Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309, 311 (Iowa 1996). The words “arising out of” refer to the 
cause or source of the injury. The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 
circumstances of the injury. 2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124, 128 (Iowa 
1995).  An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists 
between the injury and the employment. Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. The injury must 
be a rational consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely 
incidental to the employment. Koehler Elec. v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1, 3 (Iowa 2000); 

LIDDELL-BAILEY V. NESTLE PURINA PETCARE CO. 
Page 9 
 
 
Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it 
happens within a period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may 
be when performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties 
or doing an activity incidental to them. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d at 150. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa App. 1997); 
Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa App. 1996). 
A possibility of causation is not sufficient; a probability is necessary. Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996) (citing Holmes v. Bruce 
Motor Freight, Inc., 215 N.W.2d 296, 297 (Iowa 1974). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 
621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 
(Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa App. 1994). 
A personal injury contemplated by the workers’ compensation law means an 
injury, the impairment of health or a disease resulting from an injury which comes about, 
not through the natural building up and tearing down of the human body, but because of 
trauma.  The injury must be something that acts extraneously to the natural processes 
of nature and thereby impairs the health, interrupts or otherwise destroys or damages a 
part or all of the body.  Although many injuries have a traumatic onset, there is no 
requirement for a special incident or an unusual occurrence.  Injuries which result from 
cumulative trauma are compensable.  Increased disability from a prior injury, even if 
brought about by further work, does not constitute a new injury, however.  St. Luke’s 
Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 650-51 (Iowa 2000); Ellingson v. Fleetguard, Inc., 599 
N.W.2d 440, 444 (Iowa 1999); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 
845, 850-51 (Iowa 1995); McKeever Custom Cabinets v. Smith, 379 N.W.2d 368, 373-
74 (Iowa 1985).  An occupational disease covered by chapter 85A is specifically 
excluded from the definition of personal injury.  Iowa Code section 85.61(4)(b); Iowa 
Code section 85A.8; Iowa Code section 85A.14.

While a claimant is not entitled to compensation for the results of a preexisting 
injury or disease, its mere existence at the time of a subsequent injury is not a defense.  
Rose v. John Deere Ottumwa Works, 247 Iowa 900, 908, 76 N.W.2d 756, 760-61 
(1956).  If the claimant had a preexisting condition or disability that is materially 
aggravated, accelerated, worsened or lighted up so that it results in disability, claimant 
is entitled to recover.  Nicks v. Davenport Produce Co., 254 Iowa 130, 135, 115 N.W.2d 
812, 815 (1962); Yeager v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co., 253 Iowa 369,374-75, 112 
N.W.2d 299, 302 (1961). 
The initial disputed issue in the July 28, 2023 injury date is whether claimant 
proved by a preponderance of the evidence that she sustained an injury that arose out 
of and in the course of employment, or a material aggravation of a pre-existing condition 
as a result of her work activities on or about July 28, 2023.  Ultimately, claimant was not 
an employee of this employer on July 28, 2023.  However, more importantly, I found 
that claimant failed to prove her right knee injury and condition arose out of and in the 
course of her employment or was permanently aggravated by the left knee injury.  
Therefore, I conclude claimant failed to carry her burden of proof to establish a 
compensable injury for the alleged July 28, 2023 injury date.  Claimant’s July 28, 2023 
injury claim in File No. 23011007.01 is dismissed without award of further benefits. 
With respect to File No. 23700157.01, the parties stipulated claimant sustained a 
work-related injury and that she sustained permanent disability as a result of that injury.  
With respect to the issue of permanent disability, I found that claimant’s December 6, 
2022 injury was limited to her left knee. 
Under the Iowa Workers' Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is 
compensated either for a loss or loss of use of a scheduled member under Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(a)-(u) or for loss of earning capacity under section 85.34(2)(v).  The 
extent of scheduled member disability benefits to which an injured worker is entitled is 
determined by using the functional method.  Functional disability is "limited to the loss of 
the physiological capacity of the body or body part.”  Mortimer v. Fruehauf Corp., 
502 N.W.2d 12, 15 (Iowa 1993); Sherman v. Pella Corp., 576 N.W.2d 312 (Iowa 1998). 
Having found that claimant’s December 6, 2022 injury is limited to a left knee 
injury, I note that Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(p) provides that an injury to the leg should 
be compensated on a scheduled member basis using a 220-week schedule.  
Accordingly, I conclude that claimant has proven a permanent scheduled injury to the 
left leg that should be compensated pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(p). 
Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(x) provides: 
In all cases of permanent partial disability described in paragraphs “a” 
through “u,” or paragraph “v” when determining functional disability and 
not loss of earning capacity, the extent of loss or percentage of permanent 
impairment shall be determined solely by utilizing the guides to the 
evaluation of permanent impairment, published by the American medical 
association, as adopted by the workers’ compensation commissioner by

Page 11 
 
 
rule pursuant to chapter 17A.  Lay testimony or agency expertise shall not 
be utilized in determining loss or percentage of permanent impairment 
pursuant to paragraphs “a” through “u,” or paragraph “v” when determining 
functional disability and not loss of earning capacity. 
This agency complied with the requirement in Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(x) by 
enacting agency rule 876 IAC 2.4.  Rule 2.4 adopts the fifth edition of the AMA Guides 
for purposes of determining permanent functional disability of scheduled member 
injuries. 
I found the opinions of Dr. Hussain to be the most credible and convincing in this 
record.  Dr. Hussain specifically referenced and applied the AMA Guides, Fifth Edition, 
in rendering his permanent impairment rating, making it an appropriate method to

As noted above, a leg injury is compensated using a 220-week schedule.  Iowa 
Code section 85.34(2)(p).  Ten percent of 220 weeks totals 22 weeks.  Therefore, I 
conclude claimant proved entitlement to 22 weeks of permanent partial disability 
benefits.  Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(p), (w). 
The parties also disputed the applicable weekly rate at which benefits should be 
paid in this case.  The basis of compensation is the weekly earnings of the employee at 
the time of the injury.  Iowa Code § 85.36 (2015).  Weekly earnings are the gross salary, 
wages, or earnings to which an employee would have been entitled had the employee 
worked the customary hours for the full pay period in which the employee was injured 
as the employer regularly required for the work or employment.  Id.  The various 
subsections of section 85.36 set forth methods of computing weekly earnings 
depending upon the type of earnings and employment.  Id. 
If the employee is paid on a daily or hourly basis or by output, weekly earnings 
are computed by dividing by thirteen the earnings over the thirteen week period 
immediately preceding the injury.  Iowa Code § 85.36(6) (2015).  Any week that does 
not fairly reflect the employee’s customary earnings is excluded, however.  Id. 
In this case, I found claimant excluded the earnings for the week ending 
September 18, 2022.  I found that week was atypical, and I conclude claimant 
appropriately excluded that week of earnings and substituted the next available week of 
earnings to calculate the gross earnings. 
Iowa Code section 85.36(6) provides that shift differential earnings should be 
included in the gross earnings but that overtime premium payments should not be 
included in the gross earnings calculations.  I found that claimant’s calculations in
