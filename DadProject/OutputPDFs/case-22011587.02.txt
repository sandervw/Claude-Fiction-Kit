STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A. The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Shakeyla Blue.

appeared personally and through her attorney Joseph Lyons.  Defendants appeared 
through their attorney Peter Thill.  The proceedings were digitally recorded. That 
recording constitutes the official record of this proceeding.

delegated authority to issue a final agency decision in this alternate medical care 
proceeding. Therefore, this ruling is designated final agency action, and any appeal of

ISSUES 
The issues presented for resolution are whether the claimant is entitled to

Authorization to see her primary care provider to obtain a prescription for

FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned having considered all the evidence in the record finds: 
On October 12, 2022, claimant sustained a work-related injury to her right 
shoulder. (See Petition; Hearing Testimony).  Defendants admitted liability for the injury 
and authorized medical care.  (Answer; Hearing Testimony).  Claimant originally 
received treatment from Steven Olsen, D.O., at Allen Occupational Health. (Hearing 
Testimony).  At some point, claimant’s care was transferred to Matthew Bollier, M.D., an 
orthopedic surgeon at the University of Iowa Hospitals and Clinics. (Hearing Testimony; 
Ex. B, pp. 2-10).   
On October 18, 2023, Dr. Bollier performed a right shoulder arthroscopy, 
posterior labral repair, and cyst excision. (See Ex. B).  Following surgery, claimant 
received follow-up treatment from Dr. Bollier’s office, as well as attended physical 
therapy. (Id.).  Claimant continued to have some pain complaints post-operatively. (Id. 
at 3).  Dr. Bollier prescribed meloxicam, gabapentin, and Tylenol. (Id. at 3-4).   On 
February 13, 2024, she underwent a subacromial injection to the right shoulder. (Id. at 
8).  According to Dr. Bollier’s treatment notes, it did not improve her pain levels. (Id.).  
Dr. Bollier determined her pain was not originating from her shoulder joint and was 
myofascial in nature. (Id. at 10).  
On April 12, 2024, Dr. Bollier placed claimant at maximum medical improvement 
(MMI) for her right shoulder injury. (Ex. B, p. 10).  He provided a permanent impairment 
rating of 10 percent of the right upper extremity due to loss of range of motion in the 
shoulder joint and provided temporary restrictions until an FCE could be completed. 
(Id.).  In his discharge instructions, Dr. Bollier indicated that if claimant needed ongoing 
medication management, it should be provided locally. (Cl Ex. 3, p. 10).   
In May 2024, claimant contacted Dr. Bollier’s office requesting a refill of her 
meloxicam. (Cl Ex. 2, p. 9).  She was again instructed to have the prescription refilled 
locally by her primary care provider (PCP).  (Id.).  On Friday, May 3, 2024, claimant’s 
counsel contacted defendants’ counsel requesting they reach out to Dr. Bollier and get 
the prescription refilled. (Cl Ex. 1, p. 8).  The email makes no mention of Dr. Bollier’s 
instructions to claimant regarding the medication. (Id.).

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Under Iowa law, an employer who has accepted compensability for a workplace 
injury has a right to control the care provided to the injured employee.  Ramirez-Trujillo 
v. Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 N.W.2d 759, 769 (Iowa 2016).  The relevant statute provides 
as follows: 
For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish 
reasonable services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has the 
right to choose the care. . . . The treatment must be offered promptly and 
be reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the 
employee. If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the care 
offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such dissatisfaction 
to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the employer and 
the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited to treat the 
injury. If the employer and employee cannot agree on such alternate care, 
the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable proofs of the 
necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
Iowa Code § 85.27(4).   
Defendant’s “obligation under the statute is confined to reasonable care for the 
diagnosis and treatment of work-related injuries.” Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 
N.W.2d 122, 124 (Iowa 1995) (emphasis in original).  In other words, the “obligation 
under the statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.”  (Id.). 
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  See Iowa Code § 85.27(4). By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment 
and seeking alternate care, claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care 
is unreasonable.  See Iowa R. App. P 6.904(3)(e); Long, 528 N.W.2d at 124.

Ultimately, determining whether care is reasonable under the statute is a question of 
fact.  Long, 528 N.W.2d at 123. 
Under Iowa Code section 85.27, claimant bears the burden of providing 
“reasonable proofs of the necessity” for an order of alternate care.  Defendants have 
already authorized a one-time appointment with claimant’s PCP to refill her meloxicam, 
as well as ongoing treatment with either Allen Occupational Health or UIHC.  Because 
defendants have already authorized the care claimant seeks, that issue is moot and 
need not be ruled upon.  Claimant appears to allege the authorization to her PCP is 
insufficient.  She is still requesting authorization to treat with a pain management 
specialist.  No provider has recommended claimant treat with a pain management 
specialist.  However, defendants have agreed to authorize pain care with either Allen 
Occupational Health or UIHC.  An employee’s desire for a different “reasonable” 
treatment plan does not make the employer-authorized care unreasonable. See Long, 
528 at 124. A finding that the treatment requested by the claimant is reasonable does 
not result in an implicit finding that the authorized treatment is unreasonable. (Id.). The 
employee must prove the care being offered by the employer is unreasonable to treat 
the work injury, not that another treatment plan is reasonable. (Id.); See also Lynch 
Livestock, Inc. v. Bursell, 870 N.W.2d 274 (Table) (Iowa Ct. App. 2015). There is no 
evidence in the record showing that the care being offered by defendants is 
unreasonable.  Given this, claimant’s request for authorization to treat with a pain 
management specialist is denied.  
ORDER 
THEREFORE, IT IS ORDERED: 
Claimant's petition for alternate medical care is denied.
