I.

will be recounted.

which was unrelated to her employment. She had surgery to repair a medial meniscus tear on 
March 13, 2019, and she was released to full duty the following month.  
On May 24, 2019, Eslinger slipped and fell while delivering mail as part of her job duties.

II. 
SCOPE AND STANDARDS OF REVIEW. 
The Iowa Administrative Procedure Act, Iowa Code chapter 17A, governs the scope of the 
Court’s review in workers’ compensation cases. Iowa Code § 86.26; Meyer v. IBP, Inc., 710 
N.W.2d 213, 218 (Iowa 2006). “Under the Act, we may only interfere with the commissioner’s 
decision if it is erroneous under one of the grounds enumerated in the statute, and a party’s 
E-FILED                    CVCV065947 - 2024 JAN 16 02:20 PM             POLK    
CLERK OF DISTRICT COURT                    Page 3 of 12

4 
 
substantial rights have been prejudiced.”  Meyer, 710 N.W.2d p. 218. A party challenging agency 
action bears the burden of demonstrating the action’s invalidity and resulting prejudice. Iowa Code 
§ 17A.19(8)(a). The district court acts in an appellate capacity to correct errors of law on the part 
of the agency. Grundmeyer v. Weyerhaeuser Co., 649 N.W.2d 744, 748 (Iowa 2002).  
“If the claim of error lies with the agency’s findings of fact, the proper question on review 
is whether substantial evidence supports those findings of fact” when the record is viewed as a 
whole. Meyer, 710 N.W.2d at 219. Factual findings regarding an award of workers’ compensation 
benefits are within the Commissioner’s discretion, so the court is bound by the Commissioner’s 
findings of fact if they are supported by substantial evidence. Mycogen Seeds v. Sands, 686 
N.W.2d 457, 464-65 (Iowa 2004). Substantial evidence is defined as evidence of the quality and 
quantity “that would be deemed sufficient by a neutral, detached, and reasonable person, to 
establish the fact at issue when the consequences resulting from the establishment of that fact are 
understood to be serious and of great importance.” Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(f)(1); Mycogen, 686 
N.W.2d p. 464. The application of workers’ compensation laws to the facts as found by the 
Commissioner is vested in the Commissioner. Midwest Ambulance Serv. v. Ruud, 754 N.W.2d 
860, 864 (Iowa 2008). District courts sitting in judicial review “may reverse the commissioner’s 
application of the law to the facts only if it is ‘irrational, illogical, or wholly unjustifiable.’” Id. 
(quoting Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(m)). 
III. 
MERITS. 
Petitioners make three main arguments on appeal: 1) the finding that Eslinger’s left knee 
injury was caused by her work injury is not backed by substantial evidence; 2) even if the left knee 
injury was compensable, Eslinger is not entitled to alternate medical care; and 3) the Commissioner 
erred in the assignment of costs.

A. Finding that the Left Knee Injury was Causally Connected to the Work Injury 
Petitioners first challenge the finding that Eslinger’s left injury was causally related to her 
fall at work. “Medical causation presents a question of fact that is vested in the discretion of the 
workers’ compensation commission.” Cedar Rapids Cmty. Sch. Dist. v. Pease, 807 N.W.2d 839, 
844-45 (Iowa 2011) (citing Dunlavey v. Econ. Fire & Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995)). As the finder of fact, the agency determines the weight to assign an expert opinion, 
assessing the accuracy of the facts provided to the expert as well as other surrounding 
circumstances. Sherman v. Pella Corp., 576 N.W.2d 312, 321 (Iowa 1998). The agency may reject 
or accept the expert evidence entirely or in part. Pease, 807 N.W.2d p. 850. This Court is “not at 
liberty to accept contradictory opinions of other experts in order to reject the finding of the 
commissioner.” Id. (citation omitted). A determination of fact can only be overturned on judicial 
review if it “is not supported by substantial evidence in the record before the court when that record 
is viewed as a whole.” Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(f). 
In sum, the law is clear; the Court cannot reweigh the evidence in this case. Both the Deputy 
Commissioner and the Commissioner found Eslinger’s experts were stronger than Petitioners’ 
experts, particularly when considered in conjunction with the available medical records. As a 
result, both factfinders agreed that the left knee injury was caused by her fall at work. Reviewing 
the record as a whole, there is substantial evidence in the form of multiple expert opinions and 
medical records that support this finding. The Commissioner’s finding on this issue is affirmed. 
B. Award of Alternate Medical Care 
After determining causation, the Deputy Commissioner then turned to the matter of 
expenses. He found that Eslinger was not entitled to past medical expenses, but she was entitled 
to future medical expenses. Cert. Rec. Pt. 1 p. 110. He also granted Eslinger’s request for alternate

medical care. Id. Given the finding that the left knee injury was a compensable injury, the Court 
agrees that Petitioners are responsible for future medical expenses; however, the award of alternate 
medical care was premature. 
Iowa Code section 85.27 governs what medical expenses an employer must pay for a 
compensable injury under Iowa workers’ compensation law. Normally, the employer must pay all 
reasonable medical expenses related to the injury, but the employer also has the right to choose 
the medical provider. Iowa Code § 85.27(4). The statute also contemplates that this arrangement 
can create conflicts and provides a process to address any issues. 
The treatment must be offered promptly and be reasonably suited to treat the injury 
without undue inconvenience to the employee. If the employee has reason to be 
dissatisfied with the care offered, the employee should communicate the basis of 
such dissatisfaction to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the 
employer and the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited to treat 
the injury. If the employer and employee cannot agree on such alternate care, the 
commissioner may, upon application and reasonable proofs of the necessity 
therefor, allow and order other care. 
 
Id. The regulations implementing this section as it applies to applications for alternate care 
specifically state that an “[a]pplication cannot be filed under this rule if the liability of the employer 
is an issue.” Iowa Admin. Code r. 876-4.48(6). Clearly the liability of the employer was part of 
the dispute in this case. Therefore, the application for alternate care should have been dismissed 
without prejudice. See id.; see also R.R. Donnelly & Sons v. Barnett, 670 N.W.2d 190, 197 (Iowa 
2003) (“[I]f a compensability issue arises in the course of an alternate care dispute, the 
commissioner cannot order that the alternate care sought by the employee be furnished by the 
employer prior to a determination of the compensability of the injury in a contested case 
proceeding or some other proceeding.”). This does not preclude Eslinger from requesting alternate 
medical care in the future should the need arise, but she must at least give Petitioners the chance

to offer suitable care now that her injury has been deemed compensable. The Commissioner erred 
as a matter of law on the issue of alternate medical care. 
C. Award of Costs 
Finally, Petitioners state that the Commissioner erred in ordering them to pay the costs for 
all services provided by Dr. Kuhnlein and Dr. Mahoney. Dr. Kuhnlein conducted a physical 
examination of Eslinger on January 26, 2022, and he finished his report on April 18, 2022. The 
17-page report took into account both his examination of Eslinger and a review of 187 pages of 
Eslinger’s medical records. Cert. Rec. Pt. 3 pp. 26-27. The full bill from Dr. Kuhnlein amounted 
to $4,828.50.1 Cert. Rec. Pt. 4 p. 2. This total included $1684 for the medical exam, $323 for 
imaging of both knees, and $2,821.50 for the preparation of the report. Id. Dr. Mahoney also wrote 
a one-page report at the request of Eslinger’s counsel after reviewing a small subset of Eslinger’s 
medical records specifically relating to MRIs taken of her left knee before and after her fall. Cert. 
Rec. Pt. 2 p. 112. Dr. Mahoney charged $275 for an hour of record review and $300 for the report. 
Cert. Rec. Pt. 4 p. 3. 
Petitioners make a series of truncated arguments regarding this award of this expense. They 
first claim that they should not be responsible for any of the costs. If any costs are awarded, 
Petitioners state it should only be for the cost of the reports and not the cost of Dr. Kuhnlein’s 
physical exam. Finally, they assert that if the cost of the report is awarded to Eslinger, the hours 
spent reviewing Eslinger’s medical records should not be included in the cost of the reports.

The Deputy Commissioner cited two separate statutes when awarding costs. The first is 
Iowa Code section 85.39, which has to do with independent medical examinations (IMEs). The 
issue is this section only authorizes reimbursement when “an evaluation of permanent disability 
has been made by a physician retained by the employer and the employee believes this evaluation 
to be too low . . .” Iowa Code § 85.39(2). The parties do not dispute that there is not currently an 
issue of permanent disability, because Eslinger has not yet reached maximum medical 
improvement as is required for such an evaluation. While Eslinger sought Dr. Kuhnlein’s services 
independent of her employer and Dr. Kuhnlein conducted a medical examination, this was not an 
“independent medical examination” as described in section 85.39. 
The other section cited by the Deputy Commissioner was Iowa Code section 86.40, which 
states that “[a]ll costs incurred in the hearing before the commissioner shall be taxed in the 
discretion of the commissioner.” The Iowa Administrative Code defines these costs with more 
specificity. 
Costs taxed by the workers’ compensation commissioner or a deputy workers’ 
compensation commissioner shall be (1) attendance of a certified shorthand 
reporter or presence of mechanical means at hearings and evidential depositions, 
(2) transcription costs when appropriate, (3) costs of service of the original notice 
and subpoenas, (4) witness fees and expenses as provided by Iowa Code sections 
622.69 and 622.72, (5) the costs of doctors’ and practitioners’ deposition testimony, 
provided that said costs do not exceed the amounts provided by Iowa Code sections 
622.69 and 622.72, (6) the reasonable costs of obtaining no more than two doctors’ 
or practitioners' reports, (7) filing fees when appropriate, including convenience 
fees incurred by using the WCES payment gateway, and (8) costs of persons 
reviewing health service disputes. 
 
Iowa Admin. Code r. 876—4.33. 
The interaction and limits of these two provisions were explored by the Iowa Supreme 
Court in Des Moines Area Regional Transit Authority v. Young, 867 N.W.2d 839 (Iowa 2015). In 
that case, the claimant underwent a medical examination without the authorization of her

employer, and the resulting report was offered as evidence at the agency hearing. Id. p. 841. The 
deputy commissioner acknowledged that the cost of the examination was not reimbursable under 
section 85.39, because the examination occurred before the employer had conducted their own 
evaluation of the claimant’s disability. Id. p. 843. The deputy instead awarded the cost of the 
examination under section 86.40, with the reasoning that the examination was necessary to write 
the report, which is an expressly authorized cost under Rule 876—4.33. Id. The Supreme Court 
disagreed.  
We conclude section 85.39 is the sole method for reimbursement of an examination 
by a physician of the employee’s choosing and that the expense of the examination 
is not included in the cost of a report. Further, even if the examination and report 
were considered to be a single, indivisible fee, the commissioner erred in taxing it 
as a cost under administrative rule 876—4.33 because the section 86.40 discretion 
to tax costs is expressly limited by Iowa Code section 85.39. 
 
Id. pp. 846-47. It is clear that the Deputy Commissioner and the Commissioner erred as a matter 
of law by taxing the cost of Dr. Kuhnlein’s medical examination to the Petitioners.  
 
While Young addressed the costs of the physical examination, it appears Iowa courts have 
not yet explicitly addressed whether review of medical records is also a cost that can only be 
awarded under section 85.39. Petitioners assert that the Commissioner’s decision in this case is in 
conflict with another agency decision, Kirkendall v. Cargill Meat Solutions Corp., No.: 5055494, 
2018 WL 6735762 (Iowa Workers’ Comp. Com’n Dec. 17, 2018). In that decision, the 
Commissioner wrote: 
Joint Exhibit 26 provides that Dr. Bansal's “Physical Examination” totaled $560.00, 
and his “Record Review and Report” totaled $3,390.00. As mentioned, the court in 
[Young] held the “underlying medical expenses associated with the examination do 
not become costs of a report needed for a hearing, just as they do not become costs 
of the testimony or deposition.” . . . I conclude the expenses for a physician’s review 
of medical records are expenses associated with an examination and therefore 
cannot be taxed under rule 876 IAC 4.33(6).

In this case, Dr. Bansal did not break down his bill to establish which portion of the 
$3,390.00 was associated with his report versus his records review. Because 
claimant did not offer any evidence regarding what was charged solely for Dr. 
Bansal's report, I conclude no portion of Dr. Bansal's $3,390.00 IME charge can be 
taxed as a cost under rule 876—4.33.  
 
Id. p. *10. 
 
The Court finds that the Commissioner’s award of the full costs for Dr. Mahoney was 
appropriate, but that none of the costs for Dr. Kuhnlein should have been awarded.  
 
Dr. Mahoney’s review of records is distinguishable from the review at issue in Kirkendall. 
The records review in Kirkendall could not have been a precursor to an examination because Dr. 
Mahoney did not conduct an examination. Although rule 876—4.33 does not explicitly mention 
reports, it allows for “the reasonable costs of obtaining no more than two doctors’ or practitioners’ 
reports.” Iowa Admin. Code r. 876—4.33. In the absence of an examination, it would have been 
impossible for Dr. Mahoney to draft a report without reviewing records. Thus, such a review is a 
reasonable, and necessary, cost of procuring the report.  
 
Dr. Kuhnlein reviewed 187 pages of records, as noted at the end of his report. The Court 
cannot determine whether this review was a precursor to his examination or if it occurred along 
with drafting the report. Further, the report itself notes the purpose was for IME (which, as 
discussed, cannot be assessed as a cost). The report goes into detail about the examination and 
offers diagnoses.  
The claimant bears the burden of showing that an expense is compensable. Based on Dr. 
Kuhnlein’s failure to itemize the records review and his inclusion of the findings of the 
Independent Medical Examination in the broader report, the Court cannot determine what portion 
of the report fee may appropriately be awarded. As in Kirkendall, when an improper cost is 
indivisible from a proper cost, neither should be awarded. Accordingly, the Court finds the

IV. 
CONCLUSION AND DISPOSITION. 
 
Petitioners have not shown that the factual findings of the Workers’ Compensation 
Commission are unsupported by substantial evidence. The Court therefore affirms the finding that 
Eslinger’s left knee injury was causally connected to her work injury, and as a result, Petitioners 
are responsible for Eslinger’s future medical expenses with respect to that injury. Unless a petition 
for alternate medical care is brought at a future date, Petitioners shall maintain control of Eslinger’s 
physicians consistent with Iowa Code section 85.27. Finally, Petitioners are responsible for the 
full cost of $575 for Dr. Mahoney’s record review and report, but Eslinger is responsible for the 
full cost of Dr. Kuhnlein’s services. 
IT IS THE ORDER OF THE COURT that the Appeal Order of the Iowa Workers’ 
Compensation Commission is AFFIRMED in part and REVERSED in part. The Commissioner’s 
finding on causation is affirmed. The Commissioner’s award of alternative medical care is 
reversed. The Commissioner’s award of all costs related to Dr. Mahoney is affirmed. The 
Commissioner’s award of any costs related to Dr. Kuhnlein is revered.
