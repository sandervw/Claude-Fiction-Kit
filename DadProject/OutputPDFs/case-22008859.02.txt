STATEMENT OF THE CASE   
 
Claimant, Jessica Padilla, has filed two original notices and petitions for alternate 
medical care against Mercy Medical Center, self-insured employer, defendant.  These 
petitions are filed under Iowa Code section 85.27, invoking the provisions of rule 876 
IAC 4.48.  Claimant appeared personally and through her attorney, Mark Chipokas. 
Defendant appeared through their attorney, Thomas Wolle.   
 
The alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on June 21, 2024.  The 
proceedings were digitally recorded.  That recording constitutes the official record of this 
proceeding.  Pursuant to the Commissioner’s July 21, 2023 Order, the undersigned has 
been delegated authority to issue a final agency decision in this alternate medical care 
proceeding.  Therefore, this ruling is designated final agency action and any appeal of 
the decision would be to the Iowa District Court pursuant to Iowa Code section 17A.   
 
The evidentiary record consists of Claimant’s Exhibit 1-3, Defendant’s Exhibit A 
and B, and claimant’s testimony during the telephonic hearing.  During the course of the 
hearing defendant accepted liability for the July 28, 2022, and the December 4, 2022, 
work injuries and for the conditions that for which claimant is seeking treatment.  The 
petitions in this case seek treatment for pain management and mental health care.  At 
the outset of the hearing the parties advised the undersigned that the issue of mental 
health care was moot.  Therefore, the portion of the alternate care proceeding 
pertaining to mental health care is hereby dismissed as moot.         
ISSUE   
The issue for resolution is whether the claimant is entitled to alternate medical 
care consisting of pain management.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
Claimant, Jessica Padilla, was involved in two work-related accidents wherein 
defendant admits she sustained injuries which include her left knee, neck, and mental.  
Through this proceeding Padilla is seeking pain management as recommended by 
authorized treating physician, Robert John Struthers, M.D.  (Petitions, numbered 
paragraph 8)  On September 11, 2023, Dr. Struthers’ diagnoses included migraine and 
cervicalgia.  Dr. Struthers stated, “I think you would benefit from seeing a PMR doc 
(possibly Dr. Matthews [sic])” (Cl. Ex. 1, p. 1)  Padilla would like to receive treatment for 
her neck pain with a pain management doctor, specifically Dr. Mathew.   
 
Padilla testified that she experiences constant neck pain which limits her 
activities.  She recently started a new job and has had to miss work due to her neck 
pain.  (Testimony)  At defendant’s request, Padilla underwent an independent medical 
evaluation (IME) with Christopher Iverson, M.D. in February 2024.  In his report, Dr. 
Iverson noted that Dr. Kim provided treatment to Padilla.  On December 5, 2023 Dr. Kim 
placed Padilla at maximum medical improvement (MMI) for her neck.  Despite placing 
Padilla at MMI, Dr. Kim recommended additional treatment, including a series of 
Lightforce to the cervical spine to enhance pain relief.  (Def. Ex. A, p. 3) Padilla testified 
that she is willing to try anything, including Lightforce, to decrease her pain.  However, 
Dr. Iverson told her that it was unlikely Lightforce would be beneficial to her.  Padilla 
seeks treatment with a pain management doctor as recommended by Dr. Struthers.  
(Testimony)   
 
Defendant argues that Dr. Kim placed Padilla at MMI for her neck and defendant 
does not believe that a neurologist’s suggestion that a PMR doctor might help is 
sufficient to justify an order of alternate medical care.  Currently, Padilla is receiving 
treatment for her headaches with Dr. Struthers, but is not receiving treatment for her 
neck.  I find defendant’s refusal to provide pain management treatment, as 
recommended by an authorized treating physician, is not reasonable.   
REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW   
Under Iowa law, the employer is required to provide care to an injured employee 
and is permitted to choose the care.  Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 
N.W.2d 433 (Iowa 1997).   
[T]he employer is obliged to furnish reasonable services and supplies to 
treat an injured employee, and has the right to choose the care. . . .  The 
treatment must be offered promptly and be reasonably suited to treat the 
injury without undue inconvenience to the employee.  If the employee has 
reason to be dissatisfied with the care offered, the employee should 
communicate the basis of such dissatisfaction to the employer, in writing if 
requested, following which the employer and the employee may agree to 
alternate care reasonably suited to treat the injury.  If the employer and 
employee cannot agree on such alternate care, the commissioner may,

upon application and reasonable proofs of the necessity therefor, allow 
and order other care.   
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See Iowa 
R. App. P. 5.904(3)(e); Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995). 
Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.  Id.  The 
employer’s obligation turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.  Id.; 
Harned v. Farmland Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983).  In Pirelli-Armstrong Tire 
Co., 562 N.W.2d at 433, the court approvingly quoted Bowles v. Los Lunas Schools, 
109 N.M. 100, 781 P.2d 1178 (App. 1989):   
[T]he words “reasonable” and “adequate” appear to describe the same 
standard.   
[The New Mexico rule] requires the employer to provide a certain 
standard of care and excuses the employer from any obligation to provide 
other services only if that standard is met.  We construe the terms 
"reasonable” and “adequate” as describing care that is both appropriate to 
the injury and sufficient to bring the worker to maximum recovery.   
The commissioner is justified in ordering alternate care when employer-
authorized care has not been effective and evidence shows that such care is “inferior or 
less extensive” care than other available care requested by the employee.  Long; 528 
N.W.2d at 124; Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co.; 562 N.W.2d at 437.   
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance, and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Section 85.27.  Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening October 16, 1975).   
Reasonable care includes care necessary to diagnose the condition and 
defendants are not entitled to interfere with the medical judgment of its own treating 
physician.  Pote v. Mickow Corp., File No. 694639 (Review-Reopening Decision June 
17, 1986).  
Based on the above findings of fact, I conclude claimant has demonstrated by a 
preponderance of the evidence that the lack of authorized pain management care as 
recommended by an authorized treating physician is unreasonable.  I conclude that 
claimant’s petition for alternate medical care, as it pertains to pain management, should 
be granted.  Defendant is hereby ordered to provide pain management treatment with a 
doctor of their choosing.
