STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
The claimant, Jose Wilson, filed a petition for arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from employer Arconic, Inc. (“Arconic”) and its insurer, Indemnity 
Insurance Company of North America.  Rocco Motto appeared on behalf of the 
claimant.  Troy Howell appeared on behalf of the defendants.   
 
The matter came on for hearing on October 14, 2024, before Deputy Workers’ 
Compensation Commissioner Andrew M. Phillips.  Pursuant to an order of the Iowa 
Workers’ Compensation Commissioner, the hearing occurred electronically via Zoom.  
The hearing proceeded without significant difficulty.  
 
The record in this case consists of Joint Exhibits 1-11, Claimant’s Exhibits 1-4, 
and Defendants’ Exhibits A-Q.  All of the exhibits were received into evidence without 
objection.  The undersigned took administrative notice that the claimant’s original notice 
and petition in File Number 23700914.01 was electronically filed with the Agency on 
October 31, 2023.   
The claimant testified on his own behalf.  Heidi Krafka was appointed the official 
reporter and custodian of the notes of the proceeding.  The record was held open for 45 
days following the conclusion of the hearing in order to receive a rebuttal report from the 
claimant, and the matter was considered fully submitted following briefing by the parties 
on January 3, 2025.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned, having considered all of the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
Jose Wilson, the claimant, was 45 years old at the time of the hearing.  
(Testimony).  He resides in Clinton, Iowa.  (Defendants’ Exhibit P:91).  He was married 
and lived with his wife and two minor children.  (DE P:91-92).  At the time of the 
incidents in question in this matter, two additional children did not reside with him.  (DE 
P:92).  He obtained a GED in 2017 or 2018, and completed some college courses.  (DE 
P:92).  He also obtained a certificate in machine maintenance repair from Eastern Iowa 
Community College.  (Testimony).   
 
In May of 2011, Mr. Wilson began working for Arconic.  (Testimony; DE P:97).  
He worked for them for about 11 years as an equipment operator.  (Testimony).  As an 
equipment operator, he waited for metal coils to arrive on the production line.  
(Testimony).  He would then cut the ends of the coils with a trimmer.  (Testimony).  He 
also took calculations on the coils as instructed.  (Testimony).  In February of 2019, Mr. 
Wilson earned about twenty-six and 00/100 dollars ($26.00) per hour.  (Testimony).   
 
On February 20, 2019, Mr. Wilson was working and “running coils like normal.”  
(Testimony).  He cut the front part of an ingot, which then got stuck in the machine.  
(Testimony).  He proceeded to the other side of the machine using a catwalk located in 
a tunnel.  (Testimony).  He freed the ingot and began to return to his workstation.  
(Testimony).  He walked down three stairs into the tunnel and continued across the 
catwalk when he fell on some loose grating.  (Testimony).  He then fell forward into a 
pit.  (Testimony).  As a result of his fall, he felt pain in his right knee, right leg, and right 
shoulder.  (Testimony).  He also had some scrapes on his right hand, which required 
bandaging.  (Testimony).   
 
After his fall, Mr. Wilson was taken to an onsite medical facility, where he was 
bandaged, and provided with ice and ibuprofen.  (Testimony; Joint Exhibit 8:105-108).  
He continued to follow-up with the onsite medical facility for several weeks prior to 
starting his shift.  (Testimony).

File No. 23700914.01 – Alleged Date of Injury: June 1, 2019 
 
The claimant alleges that he injured his right hand, right arm, right knee and right 
shoulder on June 1, 2019, as the result of a fall as he attempted to flee a fire at his 
workstation.  In their posthearing brief, claimant’s counsel noted:

he only injured his right knee as a result of the June 1, 2019[,] work injury.  
As such, claimant’s argument will be limited to the February 20, 2019[,] work

See Claimant’s Posthearing Brief, pages 9-10.  This does not amount to a waiver or 
dismissal of the claimant’s allegations regarding the June 1, 2019, date of injury.  
Therefore, I will take a brief moment to address the affirmative defense asserted by the 
defendants.   
 
Specifically, the defendants assert that the claimant’s allegations regarding the 
June 1, 2019, work injury should be barred by the statute of limitations pursuant to Iowa 
Code section 85.26.   
Before engaging in any analysis as to any of the other disputed issues in this 
claim, it is important to determine whether or not the claim can even stand based upon 
an analysis of the facts and applicable law.  Therefore, I begin my review of this case 
with an analysis of the defendants’ asserted affirmative defense.  The alleged date of 

WILSON V. ARCONIC, INC. 
Page 34 
 
injury in this matter is June 1, 2019.  In 2017, significant changes were applied to Iowa 
Code Chapter 85.  These changes became effective on July 1, 2017.   
Iowa Code 85.26(1) states: 
An original proceeding for benefits under chapter 10A, subchapter III, this 
chapter, or chapter 85A or 85B, shall not be maintained in any contested 
case unless the proceeding is commenced within two years from the date 
of the occurrence of the injury for which benefits are claimed or, if weekly 
compensation benefits are paid under section 10A.315, within three years 
from the date of the last payment of weekly compensation benefits.  For 
purposes of this section, “date of the occurrence of the injury” means the 
date that the employee knew or should have known that the injury was work-
related. 
See Iowa Code section 85.26(1).  Prior to the 2017 statutory changes, the common law 
discovery rule was often applied when analyzing an affirmative defense under Iowa 
Code section 85.26.  The Iowa Supreme Court clarified the application of Iowa Code 
section 85.26 in a recent case in noting that, “[t]he general assembly has chosen to limit 
the discovery rule to knowledge that an injury is work-related, not to knowledge of the 
compensable nature of the injury…”  Tweeten v. Tweeten, 999 N.W.2d 270, 281 (Iowa 
2023).  Therefore, “[f]or purposes of section 85.26, the two-year statutory period begins 
to run when the employee knows or should know that an injury is work-related, without 
regard to whether the injury is also serious enough to be compensable.”  Id. at 282.   
 
In this case, the claimant alleged that he was injured on June 1, 2019, when he 
attempted to flee a fire at his workstation.  He noted that he used crutches at the time to 
assist in ambulation.  An Arconic report of injury was completed on June 1, 2019, 
indicating Mr. Wilson alleged that he reinjured his right shoulder and felt a pop in his 
right knee.  Mr. Wilson then went to the emergency room at UnityPoint Health Trinity on 
June 2, 2019, complaining of an injury to his right shoulder and right knee when he fled 
a fire on a work machine.  On June 3, 2019, Arconic medical placed a phone call to Mr. 
Wilson to check in on him following the June 1, 2019, incident.  On June 5, 2019, Mr. 
Wilson told Dr. Chelli that he experienced severe pain in his right leg, and that he could 
not perform the functions of his job.  Mr. Wilson continued to seek care for his right 
knee. 
 
During the hearing, I took judicial or administrative notice that the claimant filed 
an original notice and petition alleging a June 1, 2019, date of injury on October 31, 
2023.  As part of the agreed upon and approved hearing report in this matter, the 
parties stipulated that the claimant was paid zero (0) weeks of benefits related to the 
June 1, 2019, date of injury.  There is no evidence that the discovery rule as modified by 
Tweeten applies in this case based upon the claimant’s immediate treatment and 
knowledge of his work-related injury.   
Considering the fact that the parties stipulated that no workers’ compensation 
benefits were paid that were related to the June 1, 2019, date of injury, a two-year 

WILSON V. ARCONIC, INC. 
Page 35 
 
statute of limitations would apply.  Two years from June 1, 2019, would be June 1, 
2021.  The claimant did not file his original notice and petition relating to the June 1, 
2019, work injury until October 31, 2023.  This is more than two years after the 
expiration of the two-year statute of limitations.  Finally, no evidence was presented that 
a condition existed to toll the statute of limitations.  Therefore, the claimant’s claim 
related to the June 1, 2019, injury was barred by the statute of limitations imposed by 
Iowa Code section 85.26(1).  The claimant shall take nothing regarding the June 1, 
2019, date of injury.   
File No. 19003589.03 
Permanent Disability 
There is a dispute as to whether or not the claimant’s right arm injury was a 
cause of permanent disability or simply a temporary exacerbation.  More specifically, 
the claimant alleges that his right carpal tunnel syndrome caused permanent disability 
to his right arm.  The defendants dispute this issue.  There are also disputes as to the 
extent of permanent disability, the potential application of Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(t), 
and the commencement date for permanent partial disability benefits.   
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is 
probable, rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 
148, 153 (Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. 
App. 1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1996).    
A possibility of causation is not sufficient; a probability is necessary. Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996) (citing Holmes v. Bruce 
Motor Freight, Inc., 215 N.W.2d 296, 297 (Iowa 1974)). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 
621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 
(Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994).

Next, I turn to the extent of permanent impairment related to the claimant’s right 
knee and right shoulder injuries.  The claimant argues that he should be compensated 
based upon the provisions of Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(t).  The defendants dispute 
this contention.   
Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(t) provides, in pertinent part: 
…For all cases of permanent partial disability compensation shall be paid 
as follows: 
(t) The loss of both arms, or both hands, or both feet, or both legs, or both 
eyes, or any two thereof, caused by a single accident, shall equal five

See Iowa Code section 85.34(2).  I previously determined that the claimant did not 
prove that the February 20, 2019, work injury to his right wrist caused permanent 
disability to his right wrist or arm due to his carpal tunnel syndrome.  Therefore, the only 
two body parts to which the claimant sustained permanent disability were his right knee 
and right shoulder.  The shoulder is not listed as one of the body parts entitling up to 
500 weeks of permanent disability benefits.  The statute only lists the arms, hands, feet, 
legs, and eyes.  Since the shoulder is not listed, compensation is not appropriate 
pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(t).

Under the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is 
compensated either for a loss of use of a scheduled member under Iowa Code 
85.34(2)(a)-(u) or for loss of earning capacity under Iowa Code 85.34(2)(v).  The extent 
of scheduled member disability benefits to which an injured worker is entitled is 
determined by using the functional method.  Functional disability is “limited to the loss of 
the physiological capacity of the body or body part.”  Mortimer v. Fruehauf Corp., 502 
N.W.2d 12, 15 (Iowa 1993); Sherman v. Pella Corp., 576 N.W.2d 312 (Iowa 1998).   
An injury to a scheduled member may, because of after effects or compensatory 
change, result in permanent impairment of the body as a whole.  Such impairment may 
in turn be the basis for a rating of industrial disability.  It is the anatomical situs of the 
permanent injury or impairment which determines whether the schedules in Iowa Code 
85.34(2)(a) – (u) are applied.  Lauhoff Grain Co. v. McIntosh, 395 N.W.2d 834, 835 
(Iowa 1986); Blacksmith v. All-American, Inc., 290 N.W.2d 348 (Iowa 1980); Dailey v. 
Pooley Lumber Co., 233 Iowa 758, 10 N.W.2d 569 (1943); Soukup v. Shores Co., 222 
Iowa 272, 268 N.W. 598 (1936).  
There is a question as to whether the claimant should be compensated for 
permanent partial disability pursuant to an industrial disability analysis pursuant to Iowa 
Code section 85.34(2)(v) or for each injury as a scheduled member.  In Bridgestone 
Americas, Inc. v. Anderson, the Iowa Supreme Court confronted a similar issue and 
provided valuable statutory interpretation that is helpful in this case.  Bridgestone 
Americas, Inc. v. Anderson, 4 N.W.3d 676 (Iowa 2024).  In Anderson, the claimant 
sustained an injury to his right shoulder and his right arm.  Id. at 680.  A deputy 
commissioner determined that Mr. Anderson’s injuries to his right shoulder and right 
arm should be treated as unscheduled injuries pursuant to Iowa Code section 
85.34(2)(v).  Id.  The Commissioner affirmed the deputy’s decision, and the decision 
was appealed to the Iowa Supreme Court.  Id.   
The court held that Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v) only applies to “…injuries 
‘other than those … described or referred to in paragraphs ‘a’ through ‘u’.’”  Id. at 683.  
Since Mr. Anderson’s injuries were to his shoulder and arm, body parts which are 
“‘described or referred to in the schedule composed of ‘paragraphs ‘a’ through ‘u’…’” the 
court determined that they should be considered scheduled injuries and not 
unscheduled injuries.  Id. at 683.  The court reasoned further that “…paragraph (v) 
generally doesn’t apply to injuries to fingers, arms, hands, shoulders, or other parts 
‘described or referred to’ in the schedule.”  Id. at 685.   
The facts of this case are similar in some respects to Anderson.  The claimant in 
this case sustained injuries to his right shoulder and his right knee.  Both of these are 
considered scheduled members under our workers’ compensation statute.  More 
specifically, Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(n) provides for compensation for the loss of a 
shoulder totaling 400 weeks.  Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(p) provides for compensation 
for loss of the leg (or lower extremity) totaling 220 weeks.  Therefore, the claimant shall 
be compensated based upon a scheduled member basis for each body part.

I next turn to the issue of the extent of the claimant’s permanent partial disability 
to his right lower extremity and right shoulder.  There are a number of impairment 
opinions to consider from various medical experts.   
The weight to be given to an expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact 
and may be affected by the accuracy of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other 
surrounding circumstances. The expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole 
or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 (Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 
N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 
(Iowa 1994). Unrebutted expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula 
v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
As it relates to the claimant’s right lower extremity, the claimant underwent an 
arthroscopic surgery in September of 2019, as performed by Dr. Hussain.  Dr. Hussain

chondroplasty of the patella.  Mr. Wilson underwent a course of physical therapy, and

the claimant sustained a 10 percent permanent impairment to his right lower extremity.  
Based upon 220 weeks of compensation, the claimant would be entitled to 22 weeks of 
compensation for this impairment.

The claimant urges the adoption of Dr. Taylor’s impairment rating.  The 
defendants argue that Dr. Taylor’s impairment rating to the right shoulder does not 
properly comply with the Guides.  More specifically, the defendants argue that Dr. 
Taylor improperly assigned impairment due to a weakness in supination. 
The Guides state that strength measurements are “influenced by subjective 
factors,” and as such “further research is needed before loss of grip and pinch strength 
is given a larger role in impairment evaluation.”  See Guides, page 507.  Further, 
Section 16.8a of the Guides provides that it is only in a rare case that a loss of strength 
represents an impairing factor.  See Guides, page 508.  Therefore, loss of strength 
should only be considered if impairment has not been considered adequately by other 
methods in the Guides.  Id.  Furthermore, strength should only be combined with other 
impairments if it is based upon unrelated etiologic or pathomechanical causes, 
“[o]therwise, the impairment ratings based on objective anatomic findings take 
precedence.”  Id.  The Guides conclude that, “[d]ecreased strength cannot be rated in 
the presence of decreased motion … that prevent effective application of maximal force 
in the region being evaluated.”  Id. (emphasis in original).  Dr. Taylor does not provide 
an explanation as to why permanent impairment based upon a loss of range of motion 
is not adequate.  Therefore, I reject Dr. Taylor’s additional 1 percent impairment for 
“mild weakness of supination” in the right shoulder. 
Additionally, Dr. Taylor provided a 10 percent impairment based upon the distal 
clavicle resection based upon Table 16-27 on page 506 of the Guides.  A distal clavicle 
resection is listed under Section 16.7 of the Guides, which is titled, “Impairment of the 
Upper Extremities Due to Other Disorders.”  The Guides notes that: 
The criteria described in this section should be used only when the other 
criteria have not adequately encompassed the extent of the impairments.  
Some of the conditions described in this section can be concurrent with 
each other and with decreased motion because they share overlapping 
pathomechanics.  The evaluator must have good understanding of 
pathomechanics of deformities and apply proper judgment to avoid 
duplication of impairment ratings. 
Guides, page 499.  Dr. Taylor did not indicate why impairment based solely on a loss of 
range of motion did not adequately encompass the extent of Mr. Wilson’s impairment.  
Additionally, the Guides provides that a 10 percent impairment for a distal clavicle 
resection should be multiplied by a 25 percent maximum value noted in Table 16-18.  
Dr. Taylor failed to do this in considering his impairment rating.  Based upon his 
misapplication of the Guides, I find that Dr. Taylor’s impairment rating holds no value in

Iowa Code 85.39(2) states:   
If an evaluation of permanent disability has been made by a

Iowa Code section 85.39(2).

The Iowa Supreme Court determined that an “examination” required “to perform 
an impairment rating” must include certain steps as required under the AMA 
Guides.  Mid American Construction, LLC, v. Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d 838 (Iowa 2024).  
These steps include: determining whether an injured worker achieved MMI, reviewing 
past medical records to ascertain knowledge as to any past injuries to the injured 
worker, opining as to whether the work injury caused the impairment, conducting a 
physical examination of the claimant in order “to assess the employee’s status and 
perform accurate measurements…”, and determining a diagnosis.  Id.   
Defendants are responsible only for reasonable fees associated with claimant’s 
independent medical examination.  The claimant has the burden of proving the 
reasonableness of the expenses incurred for the examination.  See Schintgen v. 
Economy Fire & Casualty Co., File No. 855298 (App. April 26, 1991).  This burden may 
be met either by “…evidence of the fees typically charged in that locality to support or 
challenge the fee charged in a particular case…” or by agency expertise in adjudicating 
claims in the local area where the examination was conducted.  Mid American 
Construction, LLC v. Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d 838 (Iowa 2024).   
An opinion finding a lack of causation is tantamount to a zero percent impairment 
rating.  Kern v. Fenchel, Doster & Buck, P.L.C., 2021 WL 3890603 (Iowa App. 2021).     
It is well accepted Iowa law that a claimant is only entitled to reimbursement for 
one IME pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.39.  Larson Mfg. Co., Inc. v. Thorson, 763 
N.W.2d 842, 861 (Iowa 2009).  
The claimant argues that Dr. Taylor’s IME was not a “routine IME” insofar as Dr. 
Taylor examined several of Mr. Wilson’s body parts, reviewed 348 pages of records, 
and reviewed video surveillance.  The claimant also notes that Dr. Taylor took one hour 
and 40 minutes in reviewing medical records and met with Mr. Wilson for two hours.  
The claimant points to the IME fee for Dr. Ebinger’s IME as further proof that Dr. 
Taylor’s IME fees were reasonable.  The defendants point to Dr. Hussain’s letter in 
arguing that Dr. Taylor’s IME fees were not reasonable.  They also point to Dr. Garrels’ 
IME fees of one thousand seven hundred fifty and 00/100 dollars ($1,750.00) as further 
proof that Dr. Taylor’s IME fees were unreasonable.   
The Iowa Supreme Court allows for the claimant to meet their burden of proving 
the reasonableness of expenses through showing “…evidence of the fees typically 
charged in that locality to support or challenge the fee charged in a particular case…” or 
by agency expertise in adjudicating claims in the local area where the examination was 
conducted.  Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d 838.  Based upon my experience adjudicating claims in 
the local area where the examination was conducted, I find that both the claimant and 
the defendants are correct.  The claimant has failed to prove that an IME fee of six 
thousand three hundred twenty and 00/100 dollars was reasonable.  However, only 
reimbursing the claimant four thousand dollars was not reasonable.  Dr. Taylor 
performed an examination and provided impairment ratings for three body parts.  Dr. 
Taylor reviewed over three hundred pages of medical records.  He also met with Mr. 

WILSON V. ARCONIC, INC. 
Page 45 
 
Wilson for at least two hours.  I find that the defendants shall reimburse the claimant an 
additional one thousand and 00/100 dollars ($1,000.00) for Dr. Taylor’s IME fees.   
Costs 
Claimant seeks an award of costs.  Costs are to be assessed at the discretion of 
the deputy commissioner hearing the case.  See 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.33; 
Iowa Code 10A.328.  876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.33(86) provides:  
[c]osts taxed by the workers’ compensation commissioner or a deputy 
commissioner shall be (1) attendance of a certified shorthand reporter or 
presence of mechanical means at hearings and evidential depositions, (2) 
transcription costs when appropriate, (3) costs of service of the original 
notice and subpoenas, (4) witness fees and expenses as provided by Iowa 
Code sections 622.69 and 622.72, (5) the costs of doctors’ and 
practitioners’ deposition testimony, provided that said costs do not exceed 
the amounts provided by Iowa Code sections 622.69 and 622.72, (6) the 
reasonable costs of obtaining no more than two doctors’ or practitioners’ 
reports, (7) filing fees when appropriate, including convenience fees 
incurred by using the WCES payment gateway, and (8) costs of persons 
reviewing health service disputes.   
Pursuant to the holding in Des Moines Area Regional Transit Authority v. Young, 
867 N.W.2d 839 (Iowa 2015), only the report of an IME physician, and not the 
examination itself, can be taxed as a cost according to 876 IAC 4.33(6).  The Iowa 
Supreme Court reasoned, “a physician’s report becomes a cost incurred in a hearing 
because it is used as evidence in lieu of the doctor’s testimony,” while “[t]he underlying 
medical expenses associated with the examination do not become costs of a report 
needed for a hearing, just as they do not become costs of the testimony or 
deposition.”  Id.  (noting additionally that “[i]n the context of the assessment of costs, the 
expenses of the underlying medical treatment and examination are not part of the costs 
of the report or deposition”).  The commissioner has found this rationale applicable to 
expenses incurred by vocational experts.  See  Kirkendall v. Cargill Meat Solutions 
Corp., File No. 5055494 (App. December 17, 2018); Voshell v. Compass Group, USA, 
Inc., File No. 5056857 (App. September 27, 2019).   
The claimant requests reimbursement of costs totaling one thousand seventeen 
and 50/100 dollars ($1,017.50) in costs.  These include filing fees for both contested 
cases and for the rebuttal report of Dr. Taylor.  Based upon my discretion, I award the 
claimant one hundred twenty-eight and 75/100 dollars ($128.75) in costs.
