On October 24, 2025, the above-captioned matter came before this Court for hearing. 
Petitioner Jeffrey Springer was represented by attorney Zeke McCartney, and Respondent Holy 
Family Catholic Schools (HFCS) was represented by attorney Christopher Fry. After hearing the 
arguments of Counsel and reviewing the court file, including the briefs filed by the parties and the 
Certified Administrative Record, the Court enters this Order. 
I. 
BACKGROUND FACTS AND PROCEEDINGS. 
 
This case has an extensive factual history, which need not be repeated in full here. Only 
those facts relevant to the Court’s findings will be recounted. 
 
On August 1, 2017, Springer slipped and fell on his left shoulder while working as a 
custodian for HFCS. D0010, Certified Agency R. Part 1 at 143 (06/02/2025). The fall resulted in 
“a massive rotator cuff tear and a ruptured long head of the left biceps tendon.” Id. After less 
drastic treatment failed to address the issue, Springer underwent a reverse total shoulder 
arthroplasty procedure on June 28, 2018.1 Id. On December 5, 2018, his doctor placed him at 
maximum medical improvement and assigned permanent restrictions for his left arm, including no

II. 
SCOPE AND STANDARDS OF REVIEW. 
The Iowa Administrative Procedure Act, Iowa Code chapter 17A, governs the scope of the 
Court’s review in workers’ compensation cases. Iowa Code § 10A.322; Meyer v. IBP, Inc., 710 
N.W.2d 213, 218 (Iowa 2006). “Under the Act, we may only interfere with the commissioner’s 
decision if it is erroneous under one of the grounds enumerated in the statute, and a party’s 
substantial rights have been prejudiced.” Meyer, 710 N.W.2d at 218. A party challenging agency 
action bears the burden of demonstrating the action’s invalidity and resulting prejudice. Iowa Code 
§ 17A.19(8)(a).  
The district court acts in an appellate capacity to correct errors of law on the part of the 
agency. Grundmeyer v. Weyerhaeuser Co., 649 N.W.2d 744, 748 (Iowa 2002). “If the claim of 
error lies with the agency's findings of fact, the proper question on review is whether substantial 
evidence supports those findings of fact” when the record is viewed as a whole. Meyer, 710 
N.W.2d at 219. The application of workers’ compensation laws to the facts as found by the 
Commissioner is vested in the Commissioner. Midwest Ambulance Serv. v. Ruud, 754 N.W.2d 
860, 864 (Iowa 2008). District courts sitting in judicial review “may reverse the commissioner’s 
application of the law to the facts only if it is ‘irrational, illogical, or wholly unjustifiable.’” Id. 
(quoting Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(m)). However, “interpretation of workers’ compensation 
statutes and related case law has not been clearly vested within the discretion of the agency, so this 
court is free to substitute its judgment de novo for the agency’s interpretation of law.” Id. 
III. 
MERITS. 
Springer raise two issues on appeal: 1) whether the finding that the injury was a scheduled 
injury was an erroneous interpretation of the law and 2) whether the finding that Springer is not 
permanently and totally disabled is supported by substantial evidence.

A. Is the injury scheduled or unscheduled? 
Iowa Code section 85.34(2) lists specific—i.e., scheduled—injuries that are compensated 
at set rates for the specified number of weeks. “In 2017, the legislature amended section 85.34(2) 
to add ‘shoulder’ to the list of scheduled injuries and set the benefits schedule for ‘the loss of a 
shoulder’ to ‘weekly compensation during four hundred weeks,’ but it did not define ‘shoulder.’” 
Chavez v. MS Tech. LLC, 972 N.W.2d 662, 667 (Iowa 2022) (citing 2017 Iowa Acts ch. 23, § 7). 
The Court in Chavez addressed if a rotator cuff injury constituted “the loss of a shoulder” under 
the statute. It concluded that it did, adopting a functional definition of shoulder rather than a strict 
anatomical one. 
Viewing section 85.34(2) in its entirety, it is apparent that the legislature did not 
intend to limit the definition of “shoulder” solely to the glenohumeral joint. 
Notably, the legislature refers to the joints of certain body parts in other subsections, 
including the “shoulder joint,” yet it chose not to include the term “joint” when 
adding “shoulder” to the list of scheduled injuries. . . . Compare Iowa Code § 
85.34(2)(n), with id. § 85.34(2)(m). If the legislature only wanted to encompass the 
glenohumeral joint under section 85.34(2)(n), it could have expressly stated so as 
it did when referring to joints in other subsections. Yet, it chose to list “the loss of 
a shoulder” as a scheduled injury under section 85.34(2)(n) instead. 
 
Moreover, we have previously explained that the “loss” referenced in section 
85.34(2) includes the “loss of the use of a scheduled member.” . . . In other words, 
the loss of function. “The functional shoulder is ... not confined to the single 
anatomical joint known as the ‘shoulder’ or glenohumeral joint, but is a system 
which in its entirety has the largest range of motion of any joint in the human body.” 
43 Am. Jur. Proof of Facts 3d 201, § 4. Simply put, the shoulder cannot function to 
its fullest extent without the muscles that comprise the rotator cuff. 
 
Id. at 668-69. The Commissioner in his appeal decision applied the functional test in Chavez and 
found that a shoulder replacement surgery is fundamentally for the improvement of the function 
of the shoulder. D0010 at 35. He therefore found that Springer’s injury was a scheduled injury.  
Springer offers two arguments for why the Commissioner’s decision should be reversed. 
First, he argues that the Commissioner improperly disregarded Dr. Hart’s medical opinion that the

Second, Springer argues that his reverse shoulder replacement is distinct from the shoulder 
replacement at issue in Chavez, as well as in agency precedent classifying a shoulder replacement 
as a scheduled injury. A reverse shoulder replacement requires the drilling of several long screws 
into the scapula, and Springer argues that the scapula is part of the back as well as part of the 
shoulder. In particular, he cites to Lauhoff Grain Co. v. McIntosh, in which the Iowa Supreme 
Court held that the hip joint was not included in the definition of “leg” under section 85.34(2). 395 
N.W.2d 834, 839 (Iowa 1986). Given that the shoulder is the only other location where a limb 
attaches to the torso, he argues that if one substitutes the femur for the humerus and the pelvis for 
the scapula, then the same conclusion should be reached. However, the Iowa Supreme Court in 
Chavez specifically considered this argument regarding the application of Lauhoff to the shoulder 
and disregarded it due to the addition of the shoulder as a scheduled injury. Chavez, 972 N.W.2d 
at 670. It is also worth noting that Springer has offered no evidence that the screws in his scapula 
affected the functioning of his back or any other non-shoulder body part. 
The court in Chavez specifically cited the definition of the functional shoulder articulated 
in American Jurisprudence Proof of Facts 3d, which specifically includes “the upper end of the 
humerus, the clavicle, the scapula, and the surrounding soft tissues as well as the joints between 
the three bones.” 43 Am. Jur. Proof of Facts 3d 201 § 2. In fact, that scapula is the origin of the 
muscles that compose the rotator cuff, id. at § 4, which was specifically at issue in Chavez. In

B. Is Springer permanently and totally disabled? 
Springer also asserts that there is not substantial evidence to support the agency’s finding 
that he was not permanently and totally disabled as an odd-lot employee. It is true that “a person 
may suffer a permanent total disability as a result of some scheduled injury” due to “age, lack of 
training, or other condition peculiar to the person.” Mortimer v. Fruehauf Corp., 502 N.W.2d 12, 
15 (Iowa 1993). 
Iowa adopted the odd-lot doctrine in Guyton v. Irving Jensen Co., stating that “an odd-lot 
worker is thus totally disabled if the only services the worker can perform are ‘so limited in 
quality, dependability, or quantity that a reasonably stable market for them does not exist....’” 
373 N.W.2d 101, 105 (Iowa 1985) (quoting Lee v. Minneapolis Street Railway Co., 230 Minn. 
315, 320, 41 N.W.2d 433, 436 (1950)). In such cases, the employee must demonstrate “a prima 
facie case of total disability by producing substantial evidence that the worker is not employable 
in the competitive labor market,” at which point the burden then shifts to the employer to rebut 
this case with evidence of suitable, available work. Id. at 106. 
In this particular case, the Deputy Commissioner concluded that Springer failed to 
demonstrate a prima facie case that he is an odd-lot worker. The Commissioner affirmed this 
finding and, upon review of the hearing transcript, the Court agrees that this finding is supported 
by substantial evidence. It may not be the only conclusion that could be drawn from the 
evidence, but “the question on appeal is not whether the evidence supports a different finding 
than the finding made by the commissioner, but whether the evidence ‘supports the findings

Springer has a high school education. D0010 at 290. His right arm is still fully functional, 
and he can still perform various activities including driving, vacuuming, sweeping, and doing 
dishes. Id. at 345-47. He listed four jobs that he applied for before stopping his job search. Id. at 
331. Springer agreed that he stopped his job search because of his intention to retire at age 66, id. 
at 346, which a reasonable factfinder could conclude cuts against his claim that he is an odd-lot 
employee. See Bennett v. Dexter Co., 734 N.W.2d 487 (Table), 2007 WL 1062846 at *2 (Iowa 
Ct. App. 2007). None of the medical experts opined that he was incapable of gainful 
employment, and there was no occupational expert or similar opinion to support his odd-lot 
claim. See id. at *5. Taken together, there is substantial evidence to conclude that Springer failed 
to demonstrate a prima facie case that he is an odd-lot worker, and the agency’s finding must 
therefore be affirmed.

DISPOSITION. 
IT IS THE ORDER OF THE COURT that the Appeal Order of the Iowa Workers’ 
Compensation Commission is AFFIRMED.
