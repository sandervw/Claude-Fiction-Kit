STATEMENT OF THE CASE   
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A.  The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Dayna Struve. 
Claimant appeared personally and through attorney, Connor Mulholland.  Defendants, 
Deep Lake Outsourcing, LLC, employer, and Technology Insurance Company, 
insurance carrier, appeared through their attorney, Nicholas Pellegrin.   
The alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on June 5, 2025.  The 
proceedings were digitally recorded.  That recording constitutes the official record of this 
proceeding.  Pursuant to the Commissioner’s July 31, 2023 Order, the undersigned has 
been delegated authority to issue a final agency decision in this alternate medical care 
proceeding.  Therefore, this ruling is designated final agency action and any appeal of 
the decision would be to the Iowa District Court pursuant to Iowa Code section 17A.   
The evidentiary record consists of Claimant’s Exhibit 1, Defendants’ Exhibit 1, 
and the claimant’s testimony.  Prior to the hearing, the defendants filed an answer which 
accepted liability for the December 8, 2022, work injury and for the hearing loss 
condition that claimant is seeking treatment for.            
ISSUE   
The issue for resolution is whether the claimant is entitled to alternate medical 
care.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
On May 23, 2025, claimant, Dayna Struve, filed an application for alternate 
medical care.  She seeks “authorization and scheduling for the recommended audiology 
appointment made by the treating physician, Dr. Emery.”  (Application, numbered 
paragraph 9)  
Ms. Struve testified that in the late winter/early spring of 2024 she was tested and 
fitted for hearing aids at New Sound Hearing Centers.  She wore them for approximately 
one week.  After one week she returned to New Sound and was advised that they did 
not have a response from workers’ compensation regarding authorization.  New Sound 
informed her that she would either have to return the hearing aids or pay cash and seek 
reimbursement from the workers’ compensation carrier. She would like to have the 
treatment.  (Testimony)   
On June 5, 2025, defendants filed an answer and exhibit.  Defendants’ Exhibit 1 
contains a June 2, 2025, missive from defendants which states in pertinent part: 
Please note that l have spoken with my clients and they have informed me 
that they have agreed to authorize the recommended treatment with New 
Sounds Hearing Center and are in the process of scheduling a return 
evaluation. New Sounds was contacted directly by my client and provided 
authorization. I anticipate we will have an appointment scheduled shortly, 
and it is likely Ms. Struve learns about this first. l will notify you as soon as 
an appointment date and time has been scheduled, if we hear first. 
However, if Ms. Struve would prefer to contact the provider and schedule 
the appointment on her own, my clients are agreeable to this as well, and 
have already provided New Sounds Hearing Center with the necessary 
contact, billing, and claim information.  
Based on the above, please advise if you will be dismissing your petition for 
alternate medical care at this time as the hearing would appear an inefficient 
use of administrative resources. 
(Defendants’ Exhibit 1, p. 2)  
I find the record is void of any evidence that claimant expressed dissatisfaction of 
care and the basis for the dissatisfaction to the employer.  I further find that the 
defendants have authorized all of the treatment that claimant is seeking in her 
application for alternate medical care.   
Further, I find defendants have conveyed to claimant in writing that the treatment 
she seeks is authorized.  Defendants have advised her that New Sounds Hearing will 
contact her to schedule the appointment or the claimant is also free to contact New 
Sounds directly to schedule her own appointment.  I find that the treatment defendants 
have authorized is not unreasonable.

REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW   
Under Iowa law, the employer is required to provide care to an injured employee 
and is permitted to choose the care.  Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 
N.W.2d 433 (Iowa 1997).   
[T]he employer is obliged to furnish reasonable services and supplies to 
treat an injured employee, and has the right to choose the care. . . .  The 
treatment must be offered promptly and be reasonably suited to treat the 
injury without undue inconvenience to the employee.  If the employee has 
reason to be dissatisfied with the care offered, the employee should 
communicate the basis of such dissatisfaction to the employer, in writing if 
requested, following which the employer and the employee may agree to 
alternate care reasonably suited to treat the injury.  If the employer and 
employee cannot agree on such alternate care, the commissioner may, 
upon application and reasonable proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and 
order other care.   
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See Iowa 
R. App. P. 6.904(3); Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  
Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.  Id.  The 
employer’s obligation turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.  Id.; 
Harned v. Farmland Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983).  In Pirelli-Armstrong Tire 
Co., 562 N.W.2d at 433, the court approvingly quoted Bowles v. Los Lunas Schools, 
109 N.M. 100, 781 P.2d 1178 (App. 1989):   
[T]he words “reasonable” and “adequate” appear to describe the same 
standard.   
[The New Mexico rule] requires the employer to provide a certain 
standard of care and excuses the employer from any obligation to provide 
other services only if that standard is met.  We construe the terms 
"reasonable” and “adequate” as describing care that is both appropriate to 
the injury and sufficient to bring the worker to maximum recovery.   
The commissioner is justified in ordering alternate care when employer-
authorized care has not been effective and evidence shows that such care is “inferior or 
less extensive” care than other available care requested by the employee.  Long; 528 
N.W.2d at 124; Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co.; 562 N.W.2d at 437.   
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance, and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Section 85.27.  Holbert v.

Based on the above findings of fact, I conclude claimant failed to prove she 
expressed dissatisfaction of the care offered to the employer prior to filing her 
application for alternate medical care.  See Iowa Code section 85.27(4).  Iowa law 
requires the notice of dissatisfaction prior to filing the application; this requirement was 
not met and therefore the application must be denied.   
Even if the claimant were able to show that she had met the statutory 
dissatisfaction requirement, her application would fail.  In the present case, prior to the 
hearing, the defendants authorized the care the claimant is seeking.  (Defendants’ 
Exhibit 1, page 2) Defendants have conveyed to claimant in writing that the treatment 
she seeks is authorized.  Defendants have advised her that New Sounds Hearing will 
contact the claimant or the claimant is also free to contact New Sounds directly to 
schedule her own appointment.  I find that the treatment defendants have authorized is 
not unreasonable.  I conclude claimant has failed to carry her burden of proof to 
demonstrate by a preponderance of the evidence that the authorized care is 
unreasonable 
ORDER   
THEREFORE IT IS ORDERED:   
Claimant’s petition for alternate medical care is denied.
