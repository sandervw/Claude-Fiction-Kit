STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A.  On 
September 5, 2025, claimant, Rogena Jones, filed an application for alternate care 
under Iowa Code section 85.27, invoking the expedited procedure rule 876 IAC 4.48.  In 
the petition claimant requested defendants authorize a repeat MRI and a diagnostic 
injection recommended by Mark Gorsche, M.D., for a work-related injury to her right 
arm/elbow on September 12, 2023.  (See Petition).  On September 10, 2025, 
defendants, Aramark d/b/a Vestis and Indemnity Insurance Company of North America, 
filed an answer accepting liability for claimant’s right elbow injury on September 12, 
2023. (See Answer).   
The undersigned presided over an alternate care hearing held via telephone on 
September 17, 2025.  Claimant appeared through her attorney Gary Nelson. 
Defendants appeared through their attorney Sara Smalley.  The proceedings were 
digitally recorded.  That recording constitutes the official record of this proceeding. The 
hearing record consists of: 
 Claimant’s Exhibits 1 and 2 
 Defendants’ Amended Exhibits A and B

ISSUE 
The issue presented for resolution is whether the claimant is entitled to alternate 
medical care in the form of: 
 Authorization for a diagnostic cortisone injection to check for radial tunnel 
syndrome as recommended by Mark Gorsche, M.D., defendants’ IME 
physician.   
FINDINGS OF FACT 
On September 12, 2023, claimant sustained a work-related injury to her right 
arm/elbow.  (See Petition; Defendants’ Answer).  According to the medical records, 
claimant began experiencing right elbow and forearm pain in March 2023. (Ex. 1, p. 2).  
She went to the ER, where she was diagnosed with epicondylitis. (Id.). In September 
2023, she was sent to Occupational Health. (Id.).  Her diagnosis was changed to right 
carpal tunnel syndrome and olecranon bursitis and she was prescribed physical 
therapy. (Id.).  An EMG was performed. (Id.).  It was abnormal with concerns for carpal 
tunnel syndrome and ulnar neuropathy. (Id.).  Claimant was referred to Dr. Naylor for 
further care. (Id.).  
Dr. Naylor recommended surgery. (Ex. 1, p. 2).  On January 24, 2024, he 
performed a cubital tunnel release with anterior subcutaneous nerve transposition and a 
carpal tunnel injection. (Id. at 2-3).  Claimant’s symptoms improved after the surgery, 
but she still had decreased sensation in her ulnar digits. (Id. at 3).  In March 2024, 
Claimant had worsening right arm pain. (Id.).  There was concern she might be 
developing RSD. (Id.).  Dr. Naylor prescribed Neurontin, a steroid taper, and Meloxicam. 
(Id.).  In April 2023, Dr. Naylor performed another cortisone injection and a repeat EMG. 
(Id.).  The EMG showed improved carpal tunnel syndrome. (Id.).  Dr. Naylor ordered an 
MRI of her elbow. (Id.).  It showed lateral epicondylitis. (Id).  Dr. Naylor recommended a 
second surgery. (Id.).   
On September 4, 2024, Dr. Naylor performed a radiofrequency tenotomy with 
cortisone injection and grafting with Bioinductive graft with Regeneten. (Ex. 1, p. 3).

Claimant continued to complain of pain following the second surgery. (Id.).  A third EMG 
was performed. (Id.). It showed improvement of her carpal tunnel syndrome and no 
involvement of her ulnar nerve. (Id.).  In December 2024, Dr. Naylor recommended a 
repeat MRI of her elbow and a possible referral to a peripheral nerve specialist. (Id.).   
On February 5, 2025, claimant attended an independent medical exam (IME) 
with Mark Gorsche, M.D., at the defendants’ request. (Ex. 1, p. 1).  Following that exam, 
Dr. Gorsche diagnosed claimant with right elbow pain secondary to lateral epicondylitis 
after surgery, weakness of the right upper extremity secondary to lateral epicondylitis 
after surgery, resolved carpal tunnel syndrome after surgery and cortisone injection with 
remaining finger abduction weakness and possibly radial tunnel syndrome. (Id. at 6).  
Dr. Naylor recommended a repeat MRI of her right elbow to confirm that the lateral 
epicondylitis has improved, as well as a diagnostic cortisone injection to determine 
whether claimant’s ongoing symptoms were caused by radial tunnel syndrome. (Id. at 6-
7).  Dr. Gorsche indicated that if the injection improved her symptoms, claimant might 
be a candidate for radial tunnel release. (Id.).  If both the MRI and the diagnostic 
injection were unremarkable, Dr. Gorsche recommended a functional capacity 
evaluation (FCE).   
On March 14, 2025, claimant’s counsel sent defendants’ counsel a letter 
requesting an appointment at the University of Iowa Hospitals and Clinics. (Ex. 2, p. 10).  
The letter states “the treating physician has recommended that Rogena be seen at the 
University of Iowa for ongoing nerve issues with her elbow. Please confirm this is being 
authorized.”  (Id.).   
On April 16, 2025, Dr. Gorsche sent a supplemental report at defendants’ 
request. (Ex. A, p. 1).  In that letter he indicates that on March 4, 2025, a new MRI was 
taken of claimant’s right elbow.  (Id.).  According to Dr. Gorsche, the MRI showed stable 
tendinopathy at the common extensor tendon with some degenerative changes at the 
elbow joint. (Id.).  Dr. Gorsche’s letter also indicates that claimant had a follow-up 
appointment with Dr. Naylor on March 13, 2025. (Id.).  Dr. Naylor recommended a 
referral to a peripheral nerve specialist. (Id.). Once again, Dr. Gorsche recommended a 
“diagnostic/therapeutic injection to rule out radial tunnel syndrome.” (Id. at 2-3).  Based 
upon the results of claimant’s October 2024 EMG and her normal hand sensation, Dr. 
Gorsche opined that a referral to a peripheral nerve specialist was unwarranted. (Id.).   
Claimant had an appointment with Dr. Naylor on June 6, 2025. (Ex. B, p. 1).  
Following that appointment, Dr. Naylor issued a report placing claimant at maximum 
medical improvement (MMI) for the right elbow injury.  (Id.).  He stated “I do not think 
she will have any future medical needs.” (Id.).  He also provided her with a permanent 
impairment rating, permanent work restrictions, and indicated she should return to see 
him as needed. (Id.).  
 
On September 5, 2025, claimant filed a petition for alternate medical care 
requesting a “repeat MRI of right elbow and injections, as recommended by defense 
IME.” (See Petition).  At the hearing, claimant’s counsel acknowledged that defendants 
had already approved and performed an updated MRI of the right elbow.  (Testimony).

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Under Iowa law, an employer who has accepted compensability for a workplace 
injury has a right to control the care provided to the injured employee.  Ramirez-Trujillo 
v. Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 N.W.2d 759, 769 (Iowa 2016).  The relevant statute provides 
as follows: 
For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish 
reasonable services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has the 
right to choose the care. . . . The treatment must be offered promptly and 
be reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the 
employee. If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the care 
offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such dissatisfaction 
to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the employer and 
the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited to treat the 
injury. If the employer and employee cannot agree on such alternate care, 
the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable proofs of the 
necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
Iowa Code § 85.27(4).   
Defendants’ “obligation under the statute is confined to reasonable care for the 
diagnosis and treatment of work-related injuries.” Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 
N.W.2d 122, 124 (Iowa 1995) (emphasis in original). In other words, the “obligation 
under the statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.”  Id. An 
application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because claimant is 
dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving. Mere dissatisfaction with the medical 
care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical care. Rather, 
the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not reasonably 
suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the claimant. See 
Iowa Code § 85.27(4). By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment and seeking 
alternate care, claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is 
unreasonable. See Iowa R. App. P 6.904(3)(e); Long, 528 N.W.2d at 124.  Ultimately,

determining whether care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact. Long, 
528 N.W.2d at 123. 
An employer's right to select the provider of medical treatment for the injured 
worker does not include the right to determine how an injured worker should be 
diagnosed, evaluated, treated, or other matters of professional medical judgment. 
Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 866389 (Declaratory Ruling, May 19, 1988).  An 
employer is not entitled to interfere with the medical judgment of a treating 
physician. Pote v. Mickow Corp., File No. 694639 (Review-Reopening Decision June 
17, 1986).  When a designated physician refers a patient to another physician, that 
physician acts as the defendant employer's agent. Permission for the referral from 
defendant is not necessary. Kittrell v. Allen Memorial Hospital, Thirty-fourth Biennial 
Report of the Industrial Commissioner, 164 (Arb. November 1, 1979) (aff'd by industrial 
commissioner). See also Limoges v. Meier Auto Salvage, I Iowa Industrial 
Commissioner Report 207 (1981). 
Both parties agreed that Dr. Naylor is the authorized treating physician, not Dr. 
Gorsche.  Dr. Gorsche is a designated expert that evaluated the claimant once.  Dr. 
Naylor has treated the claimant for approximately two years - performing two surgeries, 
multiple MRIs, EMGs, cortisone injections, and prescribing medications. After receiving 
Dr. Gorsche’s recommendations, defendants returned claimant to Dr. Naylor, the 
authorized treating physician.  Since February 2025, Dr. Naylor has examined the 
claimant at least twice and ordered additional MRI testing.  His last appointment with the 
claimant took place on June 6, 2025. At that appointment, Dr. Naylor placed claimant at 
MMI and opined that she did not need any additional treatment for the work injury.  
Under Iowa Code section 85.27, claimant bears the burden of providing 
“reasonable proofs of the necessity” to order alternate care.  After receiving Dr. 
Gorsche’s recommendations, defendants directed her to Dr. Naylor for additional 
evaluation/care. This care was reasonable. An employee’s desire for a different 
“reasonable” treatment plan does not make the employer-authorized care 
unreasonable. See Long, 528 at 124. A finding that the treatment requested by the 
claimant is reasonable does not result in an implicit finding that the authorized treatment 
is unreasonable. Id. The employee must prove the care being offered by the employer is 
unreasonable to treat the work injury, not that another treatment plan is reasonable. Id.; 
See also Lynch Livestock, Inc. v. Bursell, 870 N.W.2d 274 (Table) (Iowa Ct. App. 2015). 
There is no evidence in the record showing that the care being offered by defendants is 
unreasonable.   
Additionally, Iowa Code section 85.27(4) requires an employee to notify 
defendants of his or her dissatisfaction with care before applying to the agency for 
alternate medical care.  According to the statute, this requirement is intended to give the 
parties an opportunity to reach an understanding on future care for the employee, 
before asking the agency to intervene.  There is no evidence in the hearing record 
showing claimant requested an updated MRI and a new diagnostic injection before filing 
this application for alternate care.  On March 14, 2025, claimant’s counsel sent a letter 
requesting an appointment at the University of Iowa “that the treating physician

recommended.” (Ex. 2, p. 10).  There, however, is nothing in the hearing record 
showing claimant requested defendants authorize and provide Dr. Gorsche’s IME 
recommendations before filing this action.  Claimant’s request for a new diagnostic 
injection is denied.  
Claimant has not met her burden to prove the care offered by defendants is 
unreasonable.  Claimant’s petition for alternate care is denied.
