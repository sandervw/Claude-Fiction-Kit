RULING ON JUDICIAL REVIEW 
 
This is a judicial review action of a decision by the Iowa Workers’ Compensation 
Commission regarding alternative medical care. The court held a hearing on November 1, 2024. 
Caitlin Kilburg represented petitioners Hastings Mutual Insurance Company & LJ and J 
Corporation (jointly referred to as the employer). Zeke McCartney represented respondent John 
Henry. 
STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
Mr. Henry sustained a work-related posterior lateral meniscus tear injury to his right knee 
on September 11, 2019. The employer accepted liability and the Worker’s Compensation 
Commission approved a settlement on May 15, 2022.  The employer agreed to pay future 
medical care. Mr. Henry underwent right meniscus surgery with Dr. David Field in 2019. Since 
the surgery, his right knee required drainage several times.  
After Mr. Henry’s right knee worsened and his pain increased, he sought medical care at 
the Adult Hip and Knee Clinic, CU Orthopedic Surgery – Inverness Clinic on March 4, 2024. 
PA-C Jordyn Roddy diagnosed Mr. Henry with moderate right knee osteoarthritis. Based upon 
this diagnosis, PA-C Roddy recommended a total right knee replacement procedure as a 
treatment option. On April 2, 2024, Mr. Henry asked the employer to authorize the surgery. The 
employer failed to respond. Again on April 25, 2024, Mr. Henrey asked the employer to

Deputy Commissioner James Christenson held an alternative medical care hearing on

employer was judicially estopped from disputing liability. Specifically, Deputy Commissioner 
Christenson stated, 
Defendants agreed to provide claimant with future medical care for the accepted 
injury of September 11, 2019. A provider has indicated claimant had a work-
related right knee injury that now requires a total knee replacement. Defendants 
have denied that recommendation. Defendants have not provided any new 
evidence there is a significant change in the facts since the admission of liability. 
Given this record, it is found defendants’ denial of the requested medical care is 
unreasonable. Claimant has carried his burden of proof he is entitled to the 
requested medical care. 
D0004, Certified Agency Record at 16 (6/17/24). For these reasons, Deputy Commissioner 
Christenson ordered the employer to authorize and pay for Mr. Henry’s right total knee 
replacement procedure.

STANDARD OF REVIEW 
 
The judicial review of an agency’s decision is governed by Iowa Code Chapter 17A. New 
Midwest Rentals, LLC v. Iowa Dep’t of Commerce, Alcoholic Beverages Div., 910 N.W.2d 643, 
648 (Iowa Ct. App. 2018). Judicial review is available to “a person or party who has exhausted 
all adequate administrative remedies and who is aggrieved or adversely affected by any final 
agency action.” Iowa Code § 17A.19(1). The district court acts in an appellate capacity to review 
the agency’s actions. New Midwest Rentals, LLC, 910 N.W.2d at 648. Upon review, “the court 
may affirm the agency action or remand to the agency for further proceedings.” Iowa Code § 
17A.19(10). “The court shall reverse, modify, or grant other appropriate relief from agency 
action, equitable or legal and including declaratory relief, if it determines that substantial rights 
of the person seeking judicial review have been prejudiced because the agency action” fits any of 
the criteria outlined in Iowa Code section 17A.19(10)(a)-(n). Id. 
 
A party challenging agency action bears the burden of demonstrating the action’s 
invalidity and resulting prejudice. Iowa Code § 17A.19(8)(a). The party may satisfy its burden in 
several ways, including proving the agency’s action was based upon an irrational, illogical, or 
wholly unjustifiable interpretation of law to fact. Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(l). In cases involving 
an error “of fact, we must determine if the commissioner’s findings are supported by substantial 
evidence.” Lynch Livestock, Inc. v. Bursell, 870 N.W.2d 274 (Table), 2015 WL 2394143 at *2 
(Iowa Ct. App. 2015). In this case, where “the error is one of interpretation of law, we will 
determinate whether the commissioner’s interpretation is erroneous and substitute our judgment 
for that of the commissioner.” Id.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The parties’ arguments in this case resemble a chicken or the egg argument.  The claimant 
makes the chicken argument.  He argued that the issue of liability for the right knee injury was 
decided in 2022 and the employer agreed to pay future medical care for the knee.  The claimant 
thus claims that his motion for alternate care was warranted after the employer refused to 
authorize the recommended surgery.  The employer makes the egg argument.  It argued that the 
settlement does not matter in this instance.  It claimed that it can deny liability for the subsequent 
injury or condition that caused the need for the knee replacement surgery.  If so, it argues that 
claimant cannot file an application for alternate medical care because liability is at issue.  See 
876 IAC 4.48(6) (although allowing petitions for alternate care pursuant to rule 4.1). 
Most of the case law is not quite on point.  The most helpful case is Huntzinger v. Moore 
Business Forms, Inc., 320 N.W.2d 545 (Iowa 1982).  In Huntzinger, the claimant suffered a back 
injury at work and received benefits including ongoing medical treatment.  Id. at 546.  Seven 
years later, his back “went out” while vacationing in Florida, leading to back surgery and four 
months off work.  Id.  He filed a workers’ compensation action and the employer raised a statute 
of limitations defense.  Id. at 547.  The court agreed with the employer as to disability benefits, 
but denied the employer’s motion as to medical benefits.  Id.  The court cited to Iowa Code 
section 85.26(2), which provides that once an award for payments or agreement for settlement 
has been made, the commission may “at any time upon proper application” make a determination 
and order concerning the entitlement for benefits under section 85.27.  Id. at 547-48.  The court 
characterized the statute as setting “unlimited lifetime medical benefits[.]”  Id. at 547. 
Mr. Henry makes a similar claim here.  In 2022, he reached a settlement with his 
employer regarding a right knee injury.  The agreement included future medical benefits.  He has

had continuing problems with the knee since 2022.  Those problems led to the recommendation 
for a knee replacement.  He sought authorization from the employer, who ignored him.  He then 
filed his petition for alternate medical care.  Claimant’s position is consistent with the statute and 
the Huntzinger decision. 
 
This conclusion is supported by other principles set forth in the case law.  For example, 
the case law is clear that an employer retains the right to direct an employee’s care if it admits 
that an injury is compensable even if disputes the nature and extent of the disability.  Bell Bros. 
Heating & Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d 193, 207 (Iowa 2010).  The claimant is bound 
to the employer’s choice of care unless the commissioner orders alternate care.  Id.  That is what 
occurred in this case.  The employer continues to admit the original workplace injury that 
resulted in the 2022 settlement was a compensable event.  The employer then denied any medical 
care, leading to the application for alternate care.  This dispute is more related to the nature and 
extent of the injury, as opposed to its compensability. 
 
The judicial estoppel cases also support the result.  Judicial estoppel is a “common sense” 
rule designed to prevent a party from making inconsistent assertions in succeeding tribunals.  
Winnebago Indus., Inc. v. Haverly, 727 N.W.2d 567, 573 (Iowa 2006).  Once a party has 
admitted liability for the purpose of maintaining control over medical care, it cannot 
simultaneously deny liability for workers’ compensation benefits.  Id. at 575.  In 2022, the 
employer admitted liability and entered into a settlement of Mr. Henry’s claim.  That settlement 
provided for future medical care.  Now the employer does not want to pay for a surgery that has 
been recommended by Mr. Henry’s health care provider.  The employer should be estopped from 
contesting liability absent a showing that there is a “significant change in the facts[.]”  Id.

The alternate medical care process still gives an employer an opportunity to defend a 
claimant’s requested relief.  The administrative rules provide for due process.  They allow 
prehearing discovery.  876 IAC 4.48(9).  All written evidence must be filed with the agency 
before the hearing.  Id.  The employer has the right to an in-person hearing if it chooses.  876 
IAC 4.48(12).  The employer may respond to the claimant’s request at the hearing.  Through this 
process, the employer can make its case that it should not be responsible to pay for the requested 
treatment.  See Haverly, 727 N.W.2d at 575. 
 
In this case, the employer did not take advantage of the process allowed by the rules.  It 
decided to defend solely on the theory that it could deny liability and cut off claimant’s 
application for alternate care.  The deputy commissioner denied that theory and considered the 
application on the evidence presented.  Mr. Henry submitted evidence.  The employer did not.  
The deputy commissioner found that the evidence supported Mr. Henry’s claim for treatment.  
The deputy’s decision is reasonable and supported by substantial evidence.   
RULING 
 
The decision by the Workers’ Compensation Deputy Commissioner is affirmed.  The 
employer is responsible for any court costs.
