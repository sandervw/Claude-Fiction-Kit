STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
The claimant, Melissa Denham, filed a petition for arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from self-insured employer City of Des Moines (“City”).  
Christopher Spaulding appeared on behalf of the claimant.  Michelle Mackel-
Wiederanders appeared on behalf of the defendant.   
 
The matter came on for hearing on April 1, 2025, before Deputy Workers’ 
Compensation Commissioner Andrew M. Phillips.  Pursuant to an order of the Iowa 
Workers’ Compensation Commissioner, the hearing occurred electronically via Zoom.  
The hearing proceeded without difficulty.  
 
The record in this case consists of Joint Exhibits 1-9, Claimant’s Exhibits 1-6, and 
Defendant’s Exhibits A-C.  The claimant filed an objection as to Defendant’s Proposed 
Exhibit C.  At the outset of the hearing, the claimant agreed to withdraw their objection, 
as the defendant declined to object to certain of the claimant’s exhibits.  Therefore, the 
exhibits were received into the record without objection. 
 
Ms. Denham testified on her own behalf.  Theresa Kenkel was appointed the 
official reporter and custodian of the notes of the proceeding.  The matter was 
considered fully submitted following briefing by the parties on May 15, 2025.     
STIPULATIONS 
 
Through the hearing report, the parties stipulated and/or established the 
following: 
1. There was an employer-employee relationship at the time of the alleged injury. 
  
2. That, if the injury is found to be a cause of permanent disability, the disability is 
a scheduled member disability to the right knee.

3. That, at the time of the alleged injury, the claimant’s gross earnings were one

ISSUES 
The parties submitted the following issues for determination: 
1. Whether the claimant sustained an injury which arose out of, and in the 
course of, her employment on July 9, 2021. 
  
2. Whether the alleged injury is a cause of temporary total disability, temporary 
partial disability, or healing period benefits. 
 
3. Whether the claimant is entitled to temporary disability and/or healing period 
benefits from July 9, 2021, to the present.   
 
4. Whether the claimant was off work from July 9, 2021, to the present.   
 
5. Whether the alleged injury is a cause of permanent disability. 
  
6. The extent of permanent disability benefits, should any be awarded.   
 
7. Whether the disability is an industrial disability. 
  
8. The proper commencement date for permanent partial disability benefits, if 
any are awarded. 
 
9. Whether the claimant is entitled to a specific taxation of costs, the amount of 
those costs, and whether they have been paid.   
The parties also noted in Section 10 of the Hearing Report:  
The parties agree that Ms. Denham sustained an injury to her right knee on 
July 9, 2021.  The City has paid the full rating for that injury, as well as 
medical costs.  Ms. Denham’s position is that she has experienced a 
back/hip injury as a result of her knee injury.  This is the issue in dispute.  
Claimant is also seeking authorization for treatment with Jolene Smith, 
D.O., Claimant’s treating pain physician.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned, having considered all of the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
Melissa Denham, the claimant, was 56 years old at the time of the hearing.  
(Testimony).  She is single, with two adult children.  (Testimony).  She completed two 
years of high school in Ottumwa, Iowa, and later obtained a G.E.D.  (Testimony).   
Ms. Denham began working for the City in 2016.  (Testimony).  By 2021, she 
worked as a sweeper operator.  (Testimony).  This involved driving a street sweeper.  
(Testimony).   
There are some medical records in evidence that predate the alleged date of the 
claimant’s injury on July 9, 2021.  Those records will be reviewed in their chronological 
order.   
On July 1, 2020, Ms. Denham visited with Angela Monthei, D.C., at Complete 
Wellness Chiropractic.  (Joint Exhibit 1:1-6).  She complained of left-sided neck, 
trapezius, mid-thoracic, lower thoracic, lumber, sacroiliac, and buttock pain.  (JE 1:1).  
She described the pain as intolerable aching, annoying, deep, heavy, and sharp.  (JE 
1:1).  Her pain radiated to the back of the left side of her head and into her left glute.  
(JE 1:1).  X-rays performed showed mild to moderate degenerative joint disease.  (JE 
1:2).  She was provided with chiropractic care.  (JE 1:1-6).   
Ms. Denham continued to follow-up with Dr. Monthei six more times through 
August 13, 2020, for her ongoing left sided complaints.  (JE 1:7-19).  Throughout her 
chiropractic care, she reported improvement to Dr. Monthei.  (JE 1:7-19).  Dr. Monthei 
recommended continued care, but Ms. Denham did not return at that time.  (JE 1:19).   
On March 7, 2021, Ms. Denham returned to Dr. Monthei’s offices with different 
complaints than her previous visit.  (JE 1:20-23).  She felt right sided pain in her mid 
thoracic region, lumbar, posterior trapezius, lower thoracic, sacroiliac, buttock, posterior 
thigh, posterior knee, upper thoracic, and neck.  (JE 1:20).  These began after she 
stepped out of a truck and fell on a curb.  (JE 1:20).  Since having a surgery on her 
knee in October of 2021, she “has really been messed up with walking in her gluts [sic] 
and hamstring, but her middle back is just like a knife constantly [throughout] the day 
and up into the neck.”  (JE 1:20).  She also reported frequent headaches.  (JE 1:20).  
Ms. Denham expressed frustration, and told Dr. Monthei that she wished to return to 
work but could not due to her pain.  (JE 1:20).  She also experienced sleep 
disturbances due to her pain.  (JE 1:20).  She rated her pain 8 out of 10.  (JE 1:20).  Dr. 
Monthei felt Ms. Denham would make good progress, and provided her with chiropractic 
care.  (JE 1:20-23).

knee on a curb, causing her immediate pain.  (Testimony).  She continued working her

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa Rule of Appellate 
Procedure 6.904(3).   
Arising out of and in the Course of Employment  
 
The parties agree that the claimant’s right knee injury arose out of, and in the 
course of her employment with the City.  There is a dispute as to whether the claimant 
sustained a back and hip injury as a result of her knee injury.  It could be argued that 
this is the claimant alleging that her back and hip issues are a sequela of her knee 
injury.   
To receive workers’ compensation benefits, an injured employee must prove, by 
a preponderance of the evidence, that the employee’s injuries arose out of, and in the 
course of the employee’s employment with the employer.  2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 
528 N.W.2d 124, 128 (Iowa 1995).  The words “arising out of” refer to the cause or 
source of the injury.  The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place and 
circumstances of the injury.  Id.  An injury arises out of employment when a causal 
relationship exists between the employment and the injury.  Quaker Oats v. Ciha, 552 
N.W.2d 143, 151 (Iowa 1996).  The injury must be a rational consequence of a hazard 

DENHAM V. CITY OF DES MOINES 
Page 18 
connected with the employment and not merely incidental to the employment.  Koehler 
Elec. v. Willis, 608 N.W.2d 1, 3 (Iowa 2000).  The Iowa Supreme Court has held that an 
injury occurs “in the course of employment” when: 
it is within the period of employment at a place where the employee 
reasonably may be in performing his duties, and while he is fulfilling those 
duties or engaged in doing something incidental thereto.  An injury in the 
course of employment embraces all injuries received while employed in 
furthering the employer’s business and injuries received on the employer’s 
premises, provided that the employee’s presence must ordinarily be 
required at the place of the injury, or, if not so required, employee’s 
departure from the usual place of employment must not amount to an 
abandonment of employment or be an act wholly foreign to his usual 
work.  An employee does not cease to be in the course of his employment 
merely because he is not actually engaged in doing some specifically 
prescribed task, if, in the course of his employment, he does some act which 
he deems necessary for the benefit or interest of his employer.   
Farmers Elevator Co. v. Manning, 286 N.W.2d 174, 177 (Iowa 1979).   
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
A possibility of causation is not sufficient; a probability is necessary. Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996) (citing Holmes v. Bruce 
Motor Freight, Inc., 215 N.W.2d 296, 297 (Iowa 1974)). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 
621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 
(Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994).

While a claimant is not entitled to compensation for the results of a preexisting 
injury or disease, its mere existence at the time of a subsequent injury is not a defense. 
Rose v. John Deere Ottumwa Works, 247 Iowa 900, 908, 76 N.W.2d 756, 760-61 
(1956). If the claimant had a preexisting condition or disability that is materially 
aggravated, accelerated, worsened or lighted up so that it results in disability, claimant 
is entitled to recover. Nicks v. Davenport Produce Co., 254 Iowa 130, 135, 115 N.W.2d 
812, 815 (1962); Yeager v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co., 253 Iowa 369, 374-75, 112

As previously noted, Ms. Denham’s low back and hip issues did not arise out of, 
and in the course of, her employment with the City.  The parties stipulated that Ms. 
Denham sustained an injury to her right knee and that the City paid for her medical care

transcription costs when appropriate, (3) costs of service of the original

the amounts provided by Iowa Code sections 622.69 and 622.72, (6) the

reports, (7) filing fees when appropriate, including convenience fees 
incurred by using the WCES payment gateway, and (8) costs of persons 
reviewing health service disputes.   
Pursuant to the holding in Des Moines Area Regional Transit v. Young, 867 N.W.2d 839 
(Iowa 2015), only the report of an IME physician, and not the examination itself, can be 
taxed as a cost according to 876 IAC 4.33(6).  The Iowa Supreme Court reasoned, “a 
physician’s report becomes a cost incurred in a hearing because it is used as evidence 
in lieu of the doctor’s testimony,” while “[t]he underlying medical expenses associated 
with the examination do not become costs of a report needed for a hearing, just as they 
do not become costs of the testimony or deposition.”  Id.  (nothing additionally that “[i]n 
the context of the assessment of costs, the expenses of the underlying medical 
treatment and examination are not part of the costs of the report or deposition”).  The 
commissioner has found this rationale applicable to expenses incurred by vocational 
experts.  See  Kirkendall v. Cargill Meat Solutions Corp., File No. 5055494 (App. Dec., 
December 17, 2018); Voshell v. Compass Group, USA, Inc., File No. 5056857 (App. 
Dec., September 27, 2019).   
 
In my discretion, I decline to award the claimant costs.
