STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
On October 16, 2025, the claimant filed a petition for alternate medical care 
pursuant to Iowa Code 85.27(4) and 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.48.  The 
defendants filed an answer admitting liability for the claim relating to the left shoulder 
rotator cuff tear, left elbow contusion, left wrist TFCC sprain, and a temporary bilateral 
knee contusion.  The defendants’ answer denied liability for the claim relating to the 
right shoulder, bilateral hips, upper and/or lower back, head, “generalized lower 
extremities,” nervous system, and right nerve or brachial plexus injury.  As such, the 
undersigned dismissed without prejudice any claims relating to those body parts at the 
outset of the hearing. 
The undersigned presided over the hearing held electronically and recorded 
digitally on October 27, 2025.  That recording constitutes the official record of the 
proceeding pursuant to 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.48(12).  The claimant 
participated personally and through her attorney, MaKayla Augustine.  The defendants 
participated through their attorney, Stephanie Techau.   
Prior to the hearing, the claimant submitted nine pages of exhibits, marked as 
Exhibits 1 through 9.  The defendants submitted seven pages of exhibits, marked as 
Exhibits D1 through D7.  The exhibits were received into the record without objection. 
 
On July 21, 2023, the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commissioner issued an 
order delegating authority to deputy workers’ compensation commissioners, such as the 
undersigned, to issue final agency decisions on applications for alternate care.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
Cynthia “Cindy” Burris is the claimant in this matter.  (Testimony).  She resides in

failure to tolerate “submaximal proximal stimulation of the left median motor CMAP due

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Iowa Code 85.27(4) provides, in relevant part: 
For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish reasonable 
services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has the right to 
choose the care….  The treatment must be offered promptly and be 
reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the 
employee.  If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the care 
offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such dissatisfaction 
to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the employer and 
the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited to treat the 
injury.  If the employer and employee cannot agree on such alternate care, 
the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable proofs of the 
necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
Iowa Code 85.27(4). See Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 433 (Iowa 
1997).   
“Iowa Code section 85.27(4) affords an employer who does not contest the 
compensability of a workplace injury a qualified statutory right to control the medical 
care provided to an injured employee.”  Ramirez-Trujillo v. Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 
N.W.2d 759, 769 (Iowa 2016) (citing R.R. Donnelly & Sons v. Barnett, 670 N.W.2d 190, 
195, 197 (Iowa 2003)).  “In enacting the right-to-choose provision in section 85.27(4), 
our legislature sought to balance the interests of injured employees against the 
competing interests of their employers.”  Ramirez-Trujillo, 878 N.W.2d at 770-71 (citing 
Bell Bros., 779 N.W.2d at 202, 207; IBP, Inc. v. Harker, 633 N.W.2d 322, 326-27 (Iowa 
2001)).   
The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except where the 
employer has denied liability for the injury.  Iowa Code section 85.27; Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening, October 16, 1975).  An employer’s right to select 
the provider of medical treatment to an injured worker does not include the right to 
determine how an injured worker should be diagnosed, evaluated, treated, or other 
matters of professional medical judgment.  Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 
866389 (Declaratory Ruling, May 18, 1988).  Reasonable care includes care necessary

to diagnose the condition, and defendants are not entitled to interfere with the medical 
judgment of its own treating physician.  Pote v. Mickow Corp., File No. 694639 (Review-
Reopening Decision, June 17, 1986).   
The employer must furnish “reasonable medical services and supplies and 
reasonable and necessary appliances to treat an injured employee.”  Stone Container 
Corp. v. Castle, 657 N.W.2d 485, 490 (Iowa 2003)(emphasis in original).  Such 
employer-provided care “must be offered promptly and be reasonably suited to treat the 
injury without undue inconvenience to the employee.”  Iowa Code section 85.27(4).  
More specifically in Long, the court noted that “the employer’s obligation under the 
statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.”  Long v. Roberts 
Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122, 128 (Iowa 1995).   
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment - and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See e.g. 
Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3); Bell Bros. Heating and Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d 
193, 209 (Iowa 2010); Long, 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  More specifically in Long, 
the court noted that “the employer’s obligation under the statute turns on the question of 
reasonable necessity, not desirability.”  Long, 528 N.W.2d at 128.   
An injured employee dissatisfied with the employer-furnished care (or lack 
thereof) may share the employee’s discontent with the employer, and if the parties 
cannot reach an agreement on alternate care, “the commissioner may, upon application 
and reasonable proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and order the care.”  Id.  
“Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.”  Long, 528 
N.W.2d at 123; Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 436.  As the party seeking 
relief in the form of alternate care, the employee bears the burden of proving that the 
authorized care is unreasonable.  Id. at 124; Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d at 209; Pirelli-
Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 436.  Because “the employer’s obligation under the 
statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability,” an injured 
employee’s dissatisfaction with employer-provided care, standing alone, is not enough 
to find such care unreasonable.  Id.  However, if substantial evidence exists that the 
care authorized by the employer was ineffective, it may be found that the care was not 
reasonable.  Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 437.   
The claimant requests an order requiring the defendants to continue authorized 
visits with authorized treating physicians Drs. Bhatt and Holm.  The claimant also 
requests an order that the defendants provide the claimant with round-trip transportation 
to these appointments.  The defendants wish to transfer the claimant’s care back to 
Iowa.  They argue that the initial authorization of care in Minnesota was at the 
claimant’s request due to her prior relationship with Dr. Holm.  The defendants also 
argue that they are obtaining second opinions from doctors in Iowa. 
As noted in Assmann and Pote, a defendant does not have the right to interfere 
in the medical judgment of an authorized treating physician.  Based upon the evidence 
in the record, Dr. Bhatt is an authorized treating physician.  There is no evidence to 
indicate that this authorization has been revoked by the defendants.  Therefore, the

claimant’s petition is granted as to this issue, and Dr. Bhatt remains an authorized 
treating physician for the claimant.   
It should be noted that this does not prevent the defendants from obtaining 
second opinions or independent medical examinations pursuant to Iowa Code section 
85.39 from other doctors while Ms. Burris continues to treat with her authorized treating 
physicians, Drs. Bhatt and Holm, in Minnesota.   
As noted in the above footnote, the defendants have authorized an upcoming 
visit with Dr. Holm on November 11, 2025.  Therefore, I need not decide that specific 
issue.   
The second issue is whether the defendants are required to provide the claimant 
with transportation to and from her appointments in Minnesota.  Iowa Code section 
85.27(1) provides that a defendant-employer “shall allow reasonably necessary 
transportation expenses incurred for such services.”  876 Iowa Administrative Code 8.1 
defines transportation expenses as: 
1. The cost of public transportation if tendered by the employer or insurance 
carrier.   
2. All mileage incident to the use of a private auto.  The per mile rate shall be 
the rate allowed by the Internal Revenue Service for the business standard 
mileage rate in effect on July 1 of each year.   
3. Meals and lodging if reasonably incident to the examination. 
4. Taxi fares or other forms of local transportation if incident to the use of public 
transportation. 
5. Ambulance service or other special means of transportation if deemed 
necessary by competent medical evidence or by agreement of the parties. 
See 876 Iowa Administrative Code 8.1(1)-(5).   
In cases where an injured worker cannot drive, this often manifests as the 
defendant-employer providing the injured worker with transportation to and from medical 
appointments, as has previously been done in this case.  It also may manifest as the 
defendant-employer reimbursing the injured worker for their transportation expenses, in 
the form of payment for medical mileage at the rate mandated in 876 Iowa 
Administrative Code 8.1.  As noted in 876 Iowa Administrative Code 8.1(5), “…other 
special means of transportation…” shall be used “…if deemed necessary by competent 
medical evidence or by agreement of the parties.”  It appears in this case that the 
parties previously had an agreement to transport the claimant using “special means of 
transportation” to and from her medical appointments.  However, the defendants now do 
not wish to engage in this agreement and dispute whether there is “competent medical 
evidence” for the claimant to require “special means of transportation” rather than 
transporting herself to her appointments and submitting for medical mileage costs.   
There is no “competent medical evidence” in the record before me to indicate 
that the claimant required “special means of transportation” and/or that she cannot

transport herself via personal conveyance to her ongoing medical care in Minnesota.  
The only evidence before me is the testimony of the claimant.  The medical records 
submitted to me are silent as to whether the claimant can, or should, drive.  Therefore, 
as to this issue, the claimant’s alternate care petition is denied.  The claimant may 
transport herself to and from her appointments with her authorized providers and submit 
for medical mileage, unless and until the parties have an agreement pertaining to 
“special means of transportation.”   
IT IS THEREFORE ORDERED: 
1. The claimant’s petition for alternate care is granted as it relates to Drs. Bhatt 
and Holm remaining authorized treating physicians. 
  
2. The defendants shall reimburse the claimant for her medical mileage 
pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.27(1) and 876 Iowa Administrative Code 
8.1.   
 
3. The defendants are not required, at this time, to provide “special means of 
transportation” for the claimant to attend her appointments with her authorized 
treating providers.
