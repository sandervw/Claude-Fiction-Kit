STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Mardy Benson, filed petitions in arbitration seeking workers' 
compensation benefits against defendant Nordstrom, Inc., a self-insured employer.  The 
hearing occurred before the undersigned on March 7, 2025, via Zoom.   
The parties filed two hearing reports at the commencement of the hearing. On 
the hearing reports, the parties entered into numerous stipulations. Those stipulations 
were accepted and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties' stipulations will be 
made or discussed. The parties are now bound by their stipulations. 
The evidentiary record consists of Joint Exhibits 1 through 5, Claimant's Exhibits 
1 through 4, and Defendant's Exhibits A and B. Claimant testified on her own behalf. No 
other witnesses were called to testify. The evidentiary record closed at the conclusion of 
the evidentiary hearing. All parties filed their post-hearing briefs on or before April 22, 
2025, at which time the case was deemed fully submitted to the undersigned. 
ISSUES

2. The nature and extent of permanent disability; 
 
3. The proper commencement date for permanent partial disability benefits; and

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence. Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the 
evidence that the alleged injury actually occurred and that it both arose out of and in the 

BENSON V. NORDSTROM, INC. 
Page 9 
course of the employment. Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 150 (Iowa 1996); 
Miedema v. Dial Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309, 311 (Iowa 1996). 
The words “arising out of” refer to the cause or source of the injury. The words “in 
the course of” refer to the time, place, and circumstances of the injury. 2800 Corp. v. 
Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124, 128 (Iowa 1995). An injury arises out of the employment 
when a causal relationship exists between the injury and the employment. Miedema, 
551 N.W.2d at 311. The injury must be a rational consequence of a hazard connected 
with the employment and not merely incidental to the employment. Koehler Elec. v. 
Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1, 3 (Iowa 2000); Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311.  
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability. 
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. St. Luke's Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); 
Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995). Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994).  
When an injury occurs in the course of employment, the employer is liable for all 
the consequences that “naturally and proximately flow from the accident.” Iowa Workers' 
Compensation Law and Practice, Lawyer and Higgs, section 4-4. The Supreme Court 
has stated: “If the employee suffers a compensable injury and thereafter suffers further 
disability which is the proximate result of the original injury, such further disability is 
compensable.” Oldham v. Scofield & Welch, 222 Iowa 764, 767, 266 N.W. 480, 481 
(1936). The Oldham Court opined that a claimant must present sufficient evidence that 
the disability was naturally and proximately related to the original work injury. 
An employer may be liable for a sequela of an original work injury if the employee 
sustained a compensable injury and later sustained further disability that is a proximate 
result of the original injury. Mallory v. Mercy Medical Center, File No. 5029834 (Appeal 
February 15, 2012). A sequela can be an after-effect or secondary effect of an injury. 
Lewis v. Dee Zee Manufacturing, File No. 797154, (Arb. September 11, 1989). One 
form of sequela is an adverse effect from medical treatment for the original injury. 
Where treatment rendered with respect to a compensable injury itself causes further 
injury, the subsequent injury is also compensable. Young v. United Fire & Casualty Co., 
256 Iowa 813, 129 N.W.2d 75 (1964).  
A sequela can also take the form of a secondary effect on the claimant's body 
stemming from the original injury. For example, where a leg injury causing shortening of 
the leg in turn alters the claimant's gait, causing mechanical back pain, the back 
condition can be found to be a sequela of the leg injury. Fridlington v. 3M, File No. 
788758, (Arb. November 15, 1991).  A sequela can also take the form of a later injury 
that is caused by the original injury. For example, where a leg injury leads to the 
claimant's knee giving out in a grocery store, the resulting fall is compensable as a 

BENSON V. NORDSTROM, INC. 
Page 10 
sequela of the leg injury. Taylor v. Oscar Mayer & Co., Ill Iowa Ind. Comm. Rep. 257, 
258 (1982). 
Defendant argues the right shoulder injury is not a sequela of the earlier left 
shoulder injury.  Instead, defendant asserts that Benson sustained a separate, 
cumulative injury to the right shoulder.  I do not find the defendant's argument 
convincing.  Benson’s first injury was the result of a traumatic incident in which she 
injured her left shoulder while reaching up to lift a few boxes of shoes off a stack.  The 
contemporaneous medical records note that the second injury to her right shoulder 
developed gradually as a result of overuse and overcompensation from her left shoulder 
pathology.  Moreover, Benson credibly testified that nothing specific happened on 
March 11, 2021, and she was simply using her right arm to carry out her job duties 
because her left arm was weak. (Hr. Tr., p. 24)  Benson has proven that she sustained 
a sequela injury to her right shoulder. 
Benson asserts she is entitled to industrial disability benefits as she sustained 
permanent impairment to four scheduled members.  Specifically, Benson asserts she 
sustained permanent injuries to her left arm, left shoulder, right arm, and right shoulder. 
The Iowa Supreme Court has considered and defined the limits and 
expansiveness of the “shoulder” within the definition or usage of Iowa Code section 
85.34(2)(n). Chavez v. MS Technology, L.L.C., 972 N.W.2d 662 (Iowa 2022)  The 
injuries involved in Chavez included a full-thickness rotator cuff tear, severe AC 
arthrosis, tendonitis, and tearing of the biceps tendon.  The Iowa Supreme Court 
interpreted Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(n) and defined the shoulder “to include the 
glenohumeral joint as well as all of the muscles, tendons, and ligaments that are 
essential for the shoulder to function.”  In this case, Benson’s injuries are clearly 
included within the injuries considered by the Court in Chavez and determined to 
constitute a scheduled member shoulder injury.  I reject the claimant’s assertion that 
she sustained injuries to four scheduled members.  Instead, I conclude that Benson has 
proven only injuries to her bilateral shoulders. 
When a worker sustains cumulative injuries to two scheduled members not listed 
in section 85.34(2)(t), the injured worker is entitled to compensation for the functional 
disability caused to each member individually. See Bridgestone Americas, Inc. v. 
Anderson, 4 N.W.3d 676, 683 (Iowa 2024). In the wake of Anderson, the Iowa Court of 
Appeals held that the rule applies to bilateral cumulative shoulder injuries. Nordstrom, 
Inc. v. Carmer, 8 N.W.3d 198 (Iowa Ct. App. 2024). Under Anderson and Carmer, a 
worker who sustains bilateral cumulative shoulder injuries is entitled to compensation 
for functional disability out of 800 weeks by applying the 400-week statutory schedule 
under section 85.34(2)(n), instead of industrial disability out of 500 weeks under section 
85.34(2)(u).

solely by utilizing the guides to the evaluation of permanent impairment, published by 
the American Medical Association, as adopted by the workers' compensation 
commissioner by rule pursuant to chapter 17A. Lay testimony or agency expertise shall

The weight of the evidence established Benson sustained 10 percent permanent 
impairment resulting from her left shoulder injury.  Taking 10 percent multiplied by 400 
weeks equals 40 weeks.  Benson is entitled to 40 weeks of permanent partial disability 
benefits for the left shoulder injury.  The evidence further established Benson sustained 
12 percent permanent impairment resulting from her right shoulder injury.  Taking 12 
percent multiplied by 400 weeks equals 48 weeks.  Benson is entitled to 48 weeks of 
permanent partial disability benefits for the right shoulder injury. 
The next issue to be considered in this case is the proper commencement date 
for permanent partial disability benefits. 
Under Iowa Code section 85.34(2), the commencement  date for permanent 
partial disability benefits occurs when it is medically indicated that maximum medical 
improvement from the injury has been reached and that the extent of loss or percentage 
of permanent impairment can be determined by using of the Guides.  For purposes of 
multiple injuries stemming from the same event, permanent disability benefits 
commence upon the termination of the initial healing period. Evenson, 881 N.W.2d 360 
373-374 (Iowa 2016). 
Dr. Kennedy placed the claimant at maximum medical improvement for her right 
shoulder on April 4, 2022.  Under Iowa Code section 85.34(1), placement at MMI 
independently terminates the healing period.  Although the claimant continued to 
experience right shoulder pain and soreness after April 4, 2022, the persistence of pain 
does not preclude a finding that the healing period has ended if the underlying condition 
is stable.  As such, I accept Dr. Kennedy’s opinion that the claimant initially reached 
maximum medical improvement on April 4, 2022.   
The claimant asserts that the defendant’s credit for benefits paid should be 
reduced by the amount of temporary benefits it failed to pay. 
The claimant’s argument presumes that she remained in a healing period after 
April 4, 2022, and that the defendant therefore owed temporary benefits during the 
period in which permanent partial disability benefits were volunteered.  However, the 
parties stipulated in both hearing reports that entitlement to temporary disability and/or 
healing period benefits was no longer in dispute. By accepting that stipulation, the 
undersigned cannot make findings regarding whether the claimant remained in a 
healing period after April 4, 2022, or whether additional temporary benefits were owed.  
Under the circumstances of this case, the undersigned cannot reach any reduction of 
the defendant’s credit without first deciding entitlement to and duration of 
temporary/healing period benefits.  Absent such findings, there is no basis to 
recharacterize properly paid permanent partial disability benefits or to reduce the 
defendant’s credit for those payments. 
Even if the claimant’s argument was properly before the undersigned, it would fail 
on the merits.

It is well settled in Iowa that a healing period may be intermittent. Waldinger 
Corp. v. Mettler, 817 N.W.2d 1 (Iowa 2012); Teel v. McCord, 394 N.W.2d 405 (Iowa 
1986). Healing period may terminate and then begin again. Willis v. Lehigh Portland 
Cement Co., I-2 Iowa Ind. Comm'r Decisions 485 (Review Reopening 1984); Clemens 
v. Iowa Veterans Home, I-1 Iowa Industrial Comm'r Decisions 35 (Review Reopening 
1984); Riesselman v. Carroll Health Center, III Iowa Ind. Comm'r Report 209 (App. 
1982); Junge v. Century Engineering Corp., II Iowa Industrial Comm'r Report 219 (App. 
1981).  
The Iowa Supreme Court has specifically noted that a claimant's healing period 
terminates whenever the first of three factors in Iowa Code section 85.34(1) is met. 
Evenson v. Winnebago Industries, Inc., 881 N.W.2d 360 (Iowa 2016). The factors are 
whether (1) “the employee has returned to work,” (2) “it is medically indicated that 
significant improvement from the injury is not anticipated” (MMI), or (3) “the employee is 
medically capable of returning to employment substantially similar to the employment in 
which the employee was engaged at the time of injury.” Iowa Code § 85.34(1).  Even 
when there are multiple  healing periods, like in the instant case, the healing period lasts 
only until one of the events in Iowa Code section 85.34(1) occurs. 
Dr. Kennedy placed the claimant at maximum medical improvement for her right 
shoulder on April 4, 2022.  Under Iowa Code section 85.34(1), placement at MMI 
independently terminates the healing period, regardless of whether the claimant is 
medically capable of returning to work substantially similar to the work she was 
engaged in at the time of injury.  Thereafter, the defendant appropriately paid 56 weeks 
of permanency benefits at the rate of $762.46 per week pursuant to Dr. Kennedy’s right 
shoulder impairment rating. 
Although the claimant continued to experience right shoulder pain after April 4, 
2022, the persistence of pain does not preclude a finding that the healing period has 
ended if the underlying condition is stable.  The record does not reflect instability or a 
recommendation for additional curative treatment until December 2022.   
The claimant continued working until April 20, 2023, when she underwent repeat 
right shoulder surgery.  That surgery marks the beginning of a second healing period, 
which ended when Dr. Westpheling placed the claimant at MMI on October 10, 2023. 
Accordingly, even if the issue were properly before the undersigned, the claimant 
has not shown that the healing period continued beyond April 4, 2022. The defendant’s 
payments beginning at that time were therefore properly made as permanent partial 
disability benefits, and there is no basis to reduce the defendant’s credit for those 
payments.
