On January 30, 2024, the undersigned filed an arbitration decision in this case.  
Later that day, defendants filed a motion for order nunc pro tunc.   
Defendants correctly contend that the undersigned made a scrivener’s error with 
respect to claimant’s weekly benefits rate.  In the arbitration decision, the undersigned 
concluded that claimant’s weekly rate was $628.84; however, in the Order, the 
undersigned provided that all weekly benefits shall be paid at the rate of $717.64.  It is 
appropriate to grant defendants’ motion for order nunc pro tunc in this regard.   
Defendants next request an order be entered entitling defendants to a credit for 
benefits previously paid as stipulated on the Hearing Report. 
The phrase, “nunc pro tunc” means “now for then.”  See:  Black’s Law Dictionary, 
page 1218 (Revised 4th Edition 1968).  The definition in Black’s Law Dictionary further 
provides:  “A phrase applied to acts allowed to be done after the time when they should 
be done, with a retroactive effect, i.e., with the same effect as if regularly done.”  Black’s 
at 1218.  A nunc pro tunc order “is not for the purpose of correcting judicial thinking, a 
judicial conclusion, or a mistake of law.”  Headley v. Headley, 172 N.W.2d 104, 108 
(Iowa 1969).  The nunc pro tunc order can be employed to correct obvious errors or to 
make an order conform to the judge’s original intent.  Graber v. Iowa Dist. Court for 
Washington County, 410 N.W.2d 224, 229 (Iowa 1987).  Brinson v. Spee Dee Delivery 
Service, No. 8-754/06-2074 (Iowa App. 11/13/2008) (Iowa App., 2008). 
On the hearing report, the parties stipulated that prior to hearing claimant was 
paid 71 weeks of compensation at the rate of $634.49 per week.

In the “Statement of the Case” on page one of the January 30, 2024, arbitration 
decision, the undersigned noted: 
     The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the hearing.  
On the hearing report, the parties entered into numerous stipulations.  
Those stipulations were accepted, and no factual or legal issues relative to 
the parties’ stipulations will be made or discussed.  The parties are now 
bound by their stipulations. 
Given the above statement and acceptance of the parties' stipulations, no further 
order was required in the arbitration decision. However, it is obvious that the 
undersigned intended for the parties to be bound by their stipulations, as noted in the 
hearing report. Therefore, it is appropriate to grant defendants’ motion for 
order nunc pro tunc in this regard. 
Lastly, defendants assert the undersigned made a scrivener’s error when 
summarizing Dr. Segal’s impairments assessment.  The arbitration decision provides, 
“Dr. Segal assigned 7 percent whole person impairment for cognitive impairment, 7 
percent whole person impairment for post-traumatic headache.”  Defendants contend 
that the second 7 percent whole person impairment rating was duplicative and 
accidental.  Defendants further contend the current language in the arbitration decision 
might be misinterpreted as concluding there are two 7 percent whole person impairment 
ratings for different head conditions.   
The undersigned disagrees with defendants’ assertion that Dr. Segal assigned 7 
percent whole person impairment to claimant’s closed head injury.  Dr. Segal’s report 
provides, 
Mr. Stover has different areas of impairment due to his traumatic brain 
injury. These will be rated individually and combined as per the 
instructions given in the AMA Guides, Fifth Edition, Chapter 13. Note that 
these areas of impairment are not overlapping or duplicative, but each are 
independently impairing for Mr. Stover. 
(Ex. 2, p. 125)  Dr. Segal’s report goes on to discuss the separate impairment ratings for 
cognitive impairment, post-concussive migrainous and trigeminal dysfunction features, 
emotional and behavioral impairment, and vestibular dysfunction/gait and station. (Ex. 
2, pp. 125-128)  Then, on page 53 of his report, Dr. Segal provides that the combined 
impairment for claimant’s traumatic brain injury and post-concussive syndrome is 18 
percent whole person impairment. (Ex. 2, p. 128)   
Directly under the combined impairment rating, Dr. Segal notes that he attributed 
7 percent whole person impairment to claimant’s cognitive impairment, 7 percent whole 
person impairment to claimant’s post-traumatic headache, and 5 percent whole person 
impairment to claimant’s vestibular dysfunction. (Id.)  Admittedly, it is somewhat unclear 
as to how Dr. Segal calculated these ratings, as his preceding paragraphs discuss 
slightly different ratings. (See Ex. 2, pp. 125-128)

The undersigned ultimately accepted the 7 percent whole person impairment 
rating attributable to claimant’s post-traumatic headaches as accurate.  I did not accept 
or mention the 7 percent whole person impairment rating for cognitive impairment as 
claimant provided insufficient evidence causally connecting the alleged word-finding 
issues, memory deficits, tinnitus, vestibular dysfunction, sleep disturbances, and 
emotional sequela to the work injury.  However, given the apparent confusion, it is 
appropriate to grant defendants’ motion for order nunc pro tunc in this regard. 
THEREFORE, IT IS ORDERED:  
Defendants’ motion for order nunc pro tunc is sustained. 
The arbitration decision filed on January 30, 2024, shall be modified to include 
the following order: 
All weekly benefits shall be paid at the weekly rate of six hundred 
twenty-eight and 84/100 dollars ($628.84) per week. 
Pursuant to the stipulation of the parties as reflected on the hearing 
report, defendants are entitled to a credit of seventy-one (71) weeks 
(2/9/18 – 6/20/19) of benefits paid at the rate of six hundred thirty-four and 
49/100 dollars ($634.49) per week. 
The fifth paragraph on page 22 of the arbitration decision shall be modified to 
state: 
Dr. Segal assigned 7 percent whole person impairment for post-
traumatic headache. (Ex. 2, p. 128)  Dr. Segal utilized the AMA Guides to 
the Evaluation of Permanent Impairment, Fifth Edition, when assessing 
claimant’s permanent impairment. Dr. Segal is the only physician to assign 
impairment to claimant’s closed head injury. I accept the 7 percent whole 
person impairment rating as accurate. 
The remainder of the January 30, 2024, arbitration decision stands as issued.
