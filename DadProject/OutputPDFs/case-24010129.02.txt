STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A. The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Monica Cameron. 
Claimant appeared through her attorney, MaKayla Augustine. 
Defendants did not file an answer or appearance, and did not participate in the 
hearing. The proof of service attached to the petition indicates the petition was sent via 
certified mail to Amazon.com Services, LLC and LM Insurance Corporation on 
December 16, 2024.  The certified mail was delivered to the insurance carrier on 
December 23, 2024.  Additionally, claimant’s attorney produced an electronic copy of 
the original notice and petition to her contact with the Third Party Administrator on 
December 16, 2024.  This agency sent a notice of hearing via US mail to both the 
employer and insurance carrier on December 17, 2024. Service was proper. As such, 
the hearing proceeded without defendants' participation. 
The alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on December 27, 2024. 
The proceedings were digitally recorded. That recording constitutes the official record of 
this proceeding. Pursuant to the Commissioner's July 21, 2023 Order, the undersigned 
has been delegated authority to issue a final agency decision in this alternate medical 
care proceeding. Therefore, this ruling is designated final agency action and any appeal 
of the decision would be to the Iowa District Court pursuant to Iowa Code section 17A. 
The record consists of Claimant's Exhibit 1, consisting of ten pages. No 
witnesses were called, but claimant's attorney made a statement for the record.

ISSUE 
The issue presented for resolution is whether the claimant is entitled to alternate 
medical care consisting of the ability to direct her own medical care due to defendants’ 
failure to approve and pay for care that was ordered in the December 4, 2024, decision 
and sanctions. 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
A brief recitation of the docket in this matter is relevant regarding the problems 
claimant has had in getting medical care from defendants. 
Claimant sustained an injury to her bilateral shoulders, back, left hip, and left 
lower extremity arising out of and in the course of employment on June 11, 2024. 
Defendants authorized treatment for claimant's bilateral shoulders. (Claimant's Exhibit 
1, pp. 1-2) However, claimant's requests for treatment for her back and left leg were 
ignored. 
On November 15, 2024, claimant's attorney sent a letter to the claims adjuster, 
Helyn Cole, advising of claimant's dissatisfaction with care, and asking for authorization 
to see a qualified physician for her back and left leg symptoms. (Cl. Ex. 1, pp. 1-4) No 
response has been received to date.  Claimant has attempted to see her primary care 
physician for treatment, but the physician declined to see her since the case involves a 
workers' compensation claim.  
On November 20, 2024, claimant filed a petition for alternate medical care 
seeking authorization and scheduling of an appointment with a qualified physician to 
treat claimant's back and left lower extremity injuries.  A December 4, 2024, decision 
ordered defendants to authorize and pay for the requested care. Cameron v. 
Amazon.com Services, LLC, File No. 24010129.01 (December 4, 2024) 
On December 13, 2024, claimant’s attorney inquired as to whether an 
appointment with a qualified physician had been scheduled consistent with the 
December 4, 2024, decision.  No response has been received to date. 
On December 16, 2024, claimant filed her second petition for alternate medical 
care in this matter.  The petition requested claimant be granted the ability to direct her 
own medical care due to defendants’ failure to approve and pay for the care that was 
ordered in the December 4, 2024, decision.   
In the December 4, 2024, decision, defendants were ordered to authorize and 
schedule an appointment for treatment with a qualified physician for claimant’s back and 
left lower extremity symptoms within ten (10) days of the decision.  The decision also 
provided that, “Failure to comply with this order may result in the termination of 
defendants’ ability to direct care for the work injury.”

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence. Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3). 
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law. The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services. The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury. Section 85.27. Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening October 16, 1975). 
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable. See Iowa 
R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e); Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995). 
Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact. Id. The 
employer’s obligation turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability. Id.; 
Harned v. Farmland Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983). 
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving. Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care. Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant. Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995). 
Defendants were ordered to authorize and schedule an appointment for 
treatment with a qualified physician for claimant's back and left lower extremity 
symptoms in the December 4, 2024, alternate medical care decision.  The record 
indicates the care ordered by the December 4, 2024, decision, to be provided to 
claimant by December 14, 2024, was still not provided as of the December 27, 2024, 
hearing.  The record also indicates claimant has tried to resolve this matter with 
defendants without filing a second alternate medical care petition, without success.   
Given the claimant's numerous requests for care, this agency's order for 
defendants to provide care, and defendants continued delay and noncompliance of said 
order, I find that defendants have not offered prompt medical care that is reasonably 
suited to treat the claimant's work injury.  I find that defendants' failure to offer 
authorized medical care, defendants' delay of authorized medical care, and defendants' 
noncompliance with an order from the Iowa Workers' Compensation Commission is 
unreasonable. 
Claimant has carried her burden of proving she is entitled to alternate medical 
care.  Claimant may direct her own medical care at the defendants’ expense due to the 
delay and failure to provide care as ordered by this agency.

Claimant has also requested sanctions in this case given defendants’ failure to 
comply with the December 4, 2024, order. 
Rule 876 IAC 4.36 states in relevant part that: 
… any party to a contested case or an attorney representing such party 
shall fail to comply with these rules or any order of a deputy workers’ 
compensation commissioner or the workers’ compensation commissioner, 
the deputy workers’ compensation commissioner or workers’ 
compensation commissioner may impose sanctions which may include 
dismissing the action without prejudice, excluding or limiting evidence, 
assessing costs or expenses, and closing the record in whole or in part to 
further activity by the party.  
Given the noncompliance with the agency order in the December 4, 2024, 
alternate medical care decision, defendants shall pay claimant’s attorney’s costs and 
fees involved with preparing for and presenting the case for alternate medical care.  
Defendants may be required to pay claimant’s attorney fees in the future, if future 
noncompliance with agency orders occurs.   
ORDER 
Claimant may direct her own medical care at defendants’ expense. 
Defendants shall pay claimant's attorney's costs and fees involved with preparing 
for and presenting this case.
