STANDARD OF REVIEW 
On judicial review of an agency action, the district court functions in an appellate capacity.  
Greater Cmty. Hosp. v. Pub. Emp’t Relations Bd., 553 N.W.2d 869, 871 (Iowa 1996).  Judicial 
review of a final agency action is governed by the standards set out in Iowa Code section 17A.19.  
The district court’s review is limited to corrections of errors of law and is not de novo.  Second

Injury Fund v. Klebs, 539 N.W.2d 178, 179-80 (Iowa 1995).  The court has no original authority 
to declare the rights of the parties. Office of Consumer Advocate v. Iowa State Commerce Comm’n, 
432 N.W.2d 148, 156 (Iowa 1988).  Nearly all administrative disputes are won or lost at the agency 
level.  Iowa-Illinois Gas & Elect. Co. v. Iowa State Commerce Comm’n, 412 N.W.2d 600, 604 
(Iowa 1987).  Judgment calls are generally left to the agency.  Burns v. Bd. of Nursing, 495 N.W.2d 
698, 699 (Iowa 1993).   
The district court may reverse, modify or grant appropriate relief when an agency 
determination of fact clearly vested in the discretion of the agency is not supported by substantial 
evidence in the record.  Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(f).  The court must review the record as a whole 
when reviewing an agency’s witness veracity determination and the agency’s explanation of why 
the relevant evidence in the record supports its material findings of fact.  Iowa Code § 
17A.19(10)(f)(3).  It is the agency’s role to determine the credibility of witnesses and the weight 
to be given to any evidence, and it may accept or reject an expert opinion in whole or in part.  
Sherman v. Pella Corp., 576 N.W.2d 312, 321 (Iowa 1998); Dunlavey v. Econ. Fire & Cas. Co., 
526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 1995). 
In deciding whether substantial evidence exists, the court must consider  
the quantity and quality of evidence that would be deemed sufficient by a neutral, 
detached, and reasonable person, to establish the fact at issue when the 
consequences resulting from the establishment of the fact are understood to be 
serious and of great importance.   
 
Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(f)(1).  The substantial evidence standard only applies to factual findings.  
Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(f). 
While courts cannot simply rubber stamp an agency’s fact finding without engaging in a 
fairly intensive review of the record to ensure that the fact finding is itself reasonable, evidence is 
not insubstantial merely because it could support contrary inferences.  Wal-Mart Stores, Inc. v.

Caselman, 657 N.W.2d 493, 499 (Iowa 2003) (quoting Arthur E. Bonfield, Amendments to Iowa 
Administrative Procedure Act (1998) at 68 (1998)); Univ. of Iowa Hosp. & Clinics v. Waters, 674 
N.W.2d 92, 95-96 (Iowa 2004).  Where the evidence is in conflict or where reasonable minds 
might disagree about the conclusion to be drawn from the evidence, the court must give appropriate 
deference to the agency’s findings.  Freeland v. Emp’t Appeal Bd., 492 N.W.2d 193, 197 (Iowa 
1992).   The ultimate question is not whether the evidence could support a different finding, but 
whether the evidence supports the finding actually made.  Munson v. Iowa Dep’t of Transp., 513 
N.W.2d 722, 723 (Iowa 1994).  The possibility of drawing two inconsistent conclusions from the 
evidence does not mean the agency’s decision is not supported by substantial evidence.  
Robbennolt v. Snap-On Tools Corp., 555 N.W.2d 229, 233 (Iowa 1996).

D(1). Substantial evidence – maximum penalty assessed to East Penn for lack of a 
reasonable basis to deny Krystal temporary total disability benefits from January 25, 2018, 
to April 12, 2018.  Krystal urged, and the agency agreed, that penalty benefits should be assessed 
against East Penn for two reasons.  First, there was no reasonable basis for denying her benefits 
from January 25, 2018, through April 12, 2018.  Second, East Penn failed to timely communicate 
to her any basis for the denial.  The agency assessed the maximum penalty of 50% after finding 
substantial evidence supported both arguments asserted by Krystal.  (Arb. Dec. at p. 12, ¶¶ 3-5.). 
Assessment of penalties is governed by Iowa Code section 86.13 and is mandatory if any 
ground under the statute is proven.  Section 86.13(4) relevantly provides: 
   86.13  Compensation payments 
. . . . 
   (4) a.  If a denial, a delay in payment, or a termination of benefits occurs without 
reasonable or probable cause or excuse known to the employer or insurance carrier 
at the time of the denial, delay in payment, or termination of benefits, the workers’ 
compensation commissioner shall award benefits in addition to those benefits 
payable under this chapter, or chapter 85, 85A, or 85B, up to fifty percent of the 
amount of benefits that were denied, delayed, or terminated without reasonable or 
probable cause or excuse. 
   b.  The workers’ compensation commissioner shall award benefits under this 
subsection if the commissioner finds both of the following facts: 
   (1)  The employee has demonstrated a denial, delay in payment, or termination 
of benefits. 
   (2)  The employer has failed to prove a reasonable or probable cause or excuse     
for the denial, delay in payment, or termination of benefits. 
   c.  In order to be considered a reasonable or probable cause or excuse under 
paragraph “b,” an excuse shall satisfy all of the following criteria: 
   (1)  The excuse was preceded by a reasonable investigation and evaluation by the 
employer or insurance carrier into whether benefits were owed to the employee. 
   (2)  The results of the reasonable investigation and evaluation were the actual    
basis upon which the employer or insurance carrier contemporaneously relied to    
deny, delay payment of, or terminate benefits. 
   (3)  The employer or insurance carrier contemporaneously conveyed the basis for 
the denial, delay in payment, or termination of benefits to the employee at the time 
of the denial, delay, or termination of benefits.

Iowa Code § 86.13(4) (emphasis added). 
 
 
Section 86.13(4) requires the agency to award a penalty to the claimant if the employer 
fails to prove a reasonable cause for the denial.  Up to January 25, 2018, (1) Krystal’s claim was 
accepted, (2) all medical treatment was authorized and paid for by East Penn, and (3) temporary 
total disability benefits were paid to Krystal by East Penn.  (Tr. at pp. 13-17.).   
 
On January 25, 2018, Dr. Goding took Krystal off work.  (JEx. 4 at pp. 21-22; Tr. at pp. 
17-18; App. Dec. at p. 8, ¶ 2, p. 12, ¶¶ 2, 4.).  Krystal was released by Dr. Goding to return to work 
with restrictions on April 12, 2018. (JEx. 4 at p. 32; App. Dec. at p. 8, ¶ 2, p. 12 ¶¶ 2, 4.).  She was 
entitled to temporary total disability benefits once she was taken off work on January 25, 2018. 
Iowa Code §§ 85.33(1), 85.33(2); Thorson, 763 N.W.2d at 857.  She should have received 
temporary total disability benefits from January 25, 2018, through April 12, 2018, when Dr. 
Goding released her to return to work with restrictions.  (JEx. 4 at p. 32; Arb. Dec. at p. 8, ¶ 2.).   
 
East Penn argued it needed more time to investigate the February 8, 2018, surgery request. 
(Tr. at p. 28.).  On this issue the Arbitration Decision says: 
Defendants contend they required time to investigate claimant’s claim for a 
second shoulder injury. There is no evidence in the record defendants contacted Dr. 
Goding regarding claimant’s need for surgery.  There is no evidence defendants 
contacted Dr. Kirkland, the expert they previously hired to provide an IME.  
Defendants continued to no pay claimant any temporary benefits even after 
receiving a letter from Dr. Goding indicating the need for claimant’s second surgery 
was caused by the November of 2016 work accident.  (Jt. Ex. 4, pp. 28-34).  The 
only evidence of any investigation is a May 31, 2018 letter sent to Dr. Boulden 
asking for a causation opinion. (Ex. E, p. 1).  It was not until June 11, 2018, when 
defendants actually communicated to claimant they were denying claimant 
temporary benefits.  (Ex. H, p. 2). 
 
 
The authorizing treating physician took claimant off work from January 25, 
2018 through April 12, 2018. The authorized treating physician indicated 
claimant’s need for surgery was related to the November 2016 work injury.  There 
is little evidence defendants performed any investigation to determine if the claim 
for temporary benefits was compensable.  The only record of any investigation is 
Dr. Boulden’s IME based on defense counsel’s letter of May 31, 2018.  Defendants

for imposing a mandatory penalty.  In Terhark v. Hope Haven, File No. 5031853 (Arb. Dec. Mar. 
20, 2011), the agency in analyzing assessment of a penalty said: 
[t]here is evidence in the file that the employer investigated the claim.  There is no

In Christensen v. Snap-on Tools Corp., 554 N.W.2d 254 (Iowa 1996), the Iowa Supreme 
Court discussed when an employee is entitled to penalty benefits under the statute: 
Based on the plain language of section 86.13, we hold an employee is entitled to 
penalty benefits if there has been a delay in payment unless the employer proves a 
reasonable cause or excuse. A reasonable cause or excuse exists if either (1) the 
delay was necessary for the insurer to investigate the claim or (2) the employer had 
a reasonable basis to contest the employee’s entitlement to benefits.   
 
(Id. at 260.).

denial was timely communicated to Krystal as required by section 86.13(4).  (Arb. Dec. at p. 12, 
¶¶ 3-4.).

Dr. Boulden’s record review opinion sent to East Penn’s counsel. (Resp. Ex. E at p. 1.).  East 
Penn’s counsel sent Krystal’s counsel a follow-up letter on June 11, 2018, referencing Dr. 
Boulden’s opinion.  (Resp. Ex. H at p. 2.).  The Deputy found and the Commissioner affirmed that 
“[d]efendants did not communicate any reason for the denial of temporary benefits until June 11, 
2018, or approximately six months [sic] when benefits should have commenced.”  (Arb. Dec. at 
p. 12, ¶¶ 3-4.). 
If East Penn wanted another medical opinion regarding the temporary total disability and 
surgery request, it should have contemporaneously communicated to Krystal (1) the reason for the 
delay in payment, and (2) why East Penn believed another opinion was necessary to fairly adjust 
Krystal’s claim.  Even if this record could support a finding that East Penn acted reasonably from 
January 25, 2018, up to June 1 or June 11, 2018—a finding this record does not support—East 
Penn did not communicate the basis for its denial to Krystal during the interim timeframe between 
January and June.  Under the contemporaneous timing requirement in section 86.13(4), giving 
Krystal notice in June 2018, does not comply with this requirement—as the agency reasonably 
found.  (Arb. Dec. at p. 12, ¶¶ 3-4.).  
 
When this record is considered as a whole, the final agency decision finding that East Penn 
failed to contemporaneously communicate any denial of benefits to Krystal and assessing the 
maximum 50% penalty to East Penn is supported by substantial evidence and should be affirmed.  
(Arb. Dec. at pp. 12-13.).   
                                                                  CONCLUSION 
 
When this record is considered as a whole, substantial evidence supports the agency’s final 
decision on all issues raised.  The final agency decision should be affirmed, the Petition should be 
denied and dismissed, and costs should be assessed equally to Petitioners.
