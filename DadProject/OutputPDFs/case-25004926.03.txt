STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A.  The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Corrie Brown.  On 
April 23, 2025, claimant filed an alternate medical care petition against Dollar Tree and 
Safety National Casualty Corp.   
The alternate medical care claim came on for telephone hearing on May 2, 2025.  
The proceedings were digitally recorded.  That recording constitutes the official record 
of this proceeding.   
Pursuant to the Commissioner’s July 21, 2023, Order, the undersigned has been 
delegated authority to issue a final agency decision in this alternate medical care 
proceeding.  Therefore, this ruling is designated final agency action and any appeal of 
the decision would be to the Iowa District Court pursuant to Iowa Code section 17A. 
The record consists of Claimant’s Exhibit 1 and Defendants’ Exhibit A.  All 
exhibits were offered without objection and received into evidence.  Corrie Brown 
testified on her own behalf.  Argument of counsel was heard and considered.  The 
evidentiary record closed at the conclusion of the hearing.

ISSUE 
The issue presented for resolution is whether the claimant is entitled to 
authorization of an orthopedic evaluation. 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
Ms. Brown sustained a work-related injury on November 10, 2024. (See Petition; 
Answer)  Two days later, defendants attempted to contact Ms. Brown regarding her 
claim.  Megan Stanley, a claims adjuster with the third-party administrator, left Ms. 
Brown a voicemail and requested a return call to discuss her need for medical 
treatment. (Exhibit A, page 1)  Ms. Stanley followed up with another voicemail to Ms. 
Brown on November 14, 2024. (Ex. A, p. 2) 
Ms. Brown eventually returned defendants’ calls on December 3, 2024. (Ex. A, p. 
3)  She relayed that she had been busy with work and had not had time to call Ms. 
Stanley back. (Id.)  Ms. Brown informed Ms. Stanley that she was treating her injury with 
her primary care physician, Bruce Pehl, M.D. (Id.; Claimant’s Testimony)  Ms. Stanley 
informed Ms. Brown that Dr. Pehl was not an authorized physician and offered to 
schedule her for an evaluation with a different provider.  Although she still intended to 
treat with Dr. Pehl, Ms. Brown accepted Ms. Stanley’s offer to present for an initial 
evaluation with a different physician. (Id.)  Ms. Stanley told Ms. Brown that she would 
review her file and find a provider for her to see as soon as possible. (Id.) 
On or about December 17, 2024, Ms. Stanley left Ms. Brown a voicemail 
explaining that defendants had authorized her to present to MercyOne Clinton UC, a 
walk-in clinic, for an evaluation of her right shoulder. (Ex. A, p. 5)   Ms. Stanley 
subsequently e-mailed Ms. Brown her contact information, the claim number, and 
defendants’ billing information. (Ex. A, pp. 5-6)   
While the claim notes in evidence do not provide a definitive timeline, it appears 
Ms. Brown attempted to present to the walk-in clinic on December 17, 2024.  After 
waiting a considerable amount of time to be seen, she was informed that the walk-in 
clinic no longer accepted workers’ compensation patients. (Claimant’s Testimony; see 
Ex. A, p. 9)  She was further informed that all workers’ compensation claims are 
handled at the MercyOne Clinton North Health Plaza. (See Ex. A, p. 9)  Ms. Brown 
relayed this information to Ms. Stanley who in turn authorized her to present to the 
MercyOne Clinton North Health Plaza. (Ex. A, p. 9) 
On or about December 20, 2024, Ms. Brown presented to the MercyOne Clinton 
North Health Plaza.  Unfortunately, she was informed that the clinic did not have a 
doctor on staff that particular day and she would need to come back the following 
Monday. (Ex. A, p. 7)  She was also informed that Ms. Stanley would need to call the 
walk-in clinic and authorize the visit over the phone in order for her to be seen. (See id.)

Ms. Stanley talked to Ms. Brown on the phone shortly thereafter and agreed to call the 
clinic. (Id.) 
Ms. Brown asserts that defendants failed to provide the medical staff at the North 
Health Plaza with the necessary authorization and billing information before her 
attempted visit on or about December 20, 2024. The record suggests this was a 
miscommunication between the parties. Ms. Brown clearly believed that defendants 
would furnish the required information directly to the medical staff prior to her arrival. In 
contrast, defendants expected Ms. Brown to supply the contact and billing information 
previously emailed to her by Ms. Stanley. Further complicating the situation, the claim 
notes indicate that Ms. Brown no longer had access to the email and asked Ms. Stanley 
to resend the information, as it had been deleted from her inbox. 
It is unclear whether Ms. Brown returned to the occupational health clinic on 
Monday, December 23, 2024. 
Ultimately, Ms. Brown asserts defendants did not timely communicate with her 
regarding authorized care.  She asserts that after waiting two weeks to hear back from 
defendants, she elected to seek medical care from Bruce Pehl, M.D., her primary care 
physician. (Claimant’s Testimony)  While the medical records are not in evidence, Ms. 
Brown testified that she received conservative care from Dr. Pehl, which included a 
steroid injection.  When the injection failed to alleviate Ms. Brown’s pain, Dr. Pehl 
ordered an MRI of the right shoulder.  It is unclear when the right shoulder MRI 
occurred; however, it revealed a rotator cuff tear.  The only medical record in evidence 
is an April 17, 2025, opinion letter from Dr. Pehl, noting the rotator cuff tear and 
recommending surgery.  (Exhibit 1, p. 1) 
Defendants dispute Ms. Brown’s claim that they did not timely communicate with 
her regarding authorized treatment.  In support of their argument, defendants introduced 
the previously mentioned claim notes from Ms. Brown’s file. (Exhibit A)  The claim notes 
do not support Ms. Brown’s assertions regarding a lack of communication. 
 
Defendants further dispute that they have not timely authorized medical care to 
address Ms. Brown’s condition.  At hearing, defense counsel asserted that care was 
authorized through MercyOne; however, the occupational health clinic at MercyOne 
declined to treat Ms. Brown as she was already treating with Dr. Pehl, who is also part 
of the MercyOne system.  In response, defendants arranged for Ms. Brown to present to 
Ryan Dunlay, M.D., of ORA Orthopedics for in independent medical examination to 
determine whether surgery is necessary.  At hearing, defense counsel stated that Dr. 
Dunlay is prepared to evaluate Ms. Brown; however, he will not schedule an 
appointment until he has received and reviewed Ms. Brown’s right shoulder MRI.  
Defense counsel further provided that defendants have requested the imaging in 
question.

REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence. Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3). 
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Iowa Code section 85.27 (2013). 
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See 
Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  Determining what care is 
reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.  Id.  The employer’s obligation turns 
on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.  Id.; Harned v. Farmland 
Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983).   
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995). 
An employer’s statutory right is to select the providers of care and the employer 
may consider cost and other pertinent factors when exercising its choice. Long, at 124. 
An employer (typically) is not a licensed health care provider and does not possess 
medical expertise. Accordingly, an employer does not have the right to control the 
methods the providers choose to evaluate, diagnose and treat the injured employee. An 
employer is not entitled to control a licensed health care provider’s exercise of 
professional judgment. Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 866389 (Declaratory 
Ruling, May 19, 1988). An employer’s failure to follow recommendations of an 
authorized physician in matters of treatment is commonly a failure to provide reasonable 
treatment. Boggs v. Cargill, Inc., File No. 1050396 (Alt. Care January 31, 1994). 
Through her April 23, 2025, petition, Claimant seeks an order directing 
defendants to promptly authorize and schedule an orthopedic evaluation of her right 
shoulder based on Dr. Pehl’s recommendation.  Claimant does not request to be seen 
by a specific specialist. 
Defendants argue that their actions have been reasonable.  Defendants further 
assert that they are not refusing to provide causally connected authorized care, and

they have been trying to provide care with a physician of their choosing since November 
12, 2024. 
At this point in time, it would be difficult to attribute any significant delay in 
claimant’s medical treatment to defendants.  The evidentiary record indicates that 
defendants reached out to claimant to arrange authorized medical treatment two days 
after the date of injury.  A claims adjuster with the third-party administrator left claimant 
voicemails requesting a return call to discuss her need for medical treatment on 
November 12, 2024, and November 14, 2024.  Claimant did not respond until 
December 3, 2024 – more than two weeks later.   
During the December 3, 2024, call, Claimant told the claims adjuster that she 
was treating her condition with her primary care physician.  Despite being informed that 
Dr. Pehl was not authorized to treat her work injury – and that such treatment would not 
be covered – Ms. Brown continued to pursue care through Dr. Pehl.  While Ms. Brown 
has every right to seek unauthorized care, this ultimately undermined defendants’ right 
to direct care and complicated matters given that both Dr. Pehl and the preferred 
authorized treating physician were located at the same facility. 
While Ms. Brown bears primary responsibility for the initial delay, defendants are 
not without fault, particularly in the later stages of the timeline.  Defendants responded 
promptly to the claim and made efforts to arrange treatment once contact with Ms. 
Brown was established.  However, there is little evidence demonstrating a continued 
effort by defendants to identify an alternative provider once the occupational health 
clinic declined to see Ms. Brown due to her ongoing treatment with Dr. Pehl.  It is 
possible defendants were attempting to secure an independent medical examination 
with Dr. Dunlay between January and April 2025; however, there is no documentation or 
evidence to support the same.     
Ultimately, defendants have the right to direct care under Iowa Code section 
85.27 and an obligation to investigate claimant's request for medical treatment. 
Defendants are not obligated to accept the surgical recommendation of an unauthorized 
physician.  It is the claimant's burden to show that the care offered by defendants is not 
reasonable or presents an undue inconvenience. To date, defendants have attempted 
to provide claimant with reasonable and necessary care. Defendants are in the process 
of securing an independent medical examination with an orthopedic surgeon to evaluate 
the need for the surgery recommended by Dr. Pehl.  It appears likely that the 
examination will occur in the near future, assuming Dr. Dunlay is able to obtain the 
claimant’s recent imaging.   
Defendants’ actions in seeking what is essentially a second opinion are 
reasonable.  I find that the claimant's petition for alternate medical care does not state 
sufficient grounds at this time for the relief requested. Defendants maintain the right to 
identify, select, and authorize a provider of their choosing, provided they do so timely.
