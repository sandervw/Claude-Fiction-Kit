This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A. The 
expedited procedures of rule 876 IAC 4.48, the “alternate medical care” rule, are 
invoked by claimant, Joseph Bass.  
This alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on December 1, 2025. The 
proceedings were recorded digitally and constitute the official record of the hearing.  By 
an order filed by the Workers’ Compensation Commissioner, this decision is designated 
final agency action. Any appeal would be by petition for judicial review under Iowa Code 
section 17A.15(1).  
The record in this case consists of Claimant’s Exhibits 1-2 and Defendants’ 
Exhibits A-B. 
ISSUE 
The issue presented for resolution in this case is whether claimant is entitled to 
alternate medical care consisting of authorization and payment for an appointment with 
a neurologist. 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
Defendants accept liability for an injury occurring on February 17, 2025, to 
claimant’s head and neck. 
On September 16, 2025, claimant was evaluated by Zachary Ries, M.D.  
Claimant had a board fall on his head in February while at work.  Claimant had a 10-

On October 16, 2025, claimant’s attorney asked defendants’ attorney if

In a November 7, 2025, email, defendants reiterated they had authorized 
claimant to see a neurologist but were having difficulty setting up the appointment.  That 
email indicated that on October 21, 2025, defendants faxed a referral to MercyOne 
Neurology.  The email indicated that on October 24, 2025, defendants sent claimant’s 
records to MercyOne Neurology.  On November 3, 2025, November 4, 2025, November 
5, 2025, and November 7, 2025, defendants followed up with MercyOne for a status on 
the referral.  MercyOne staff indicated that on November 7, 2025, they had all the 
records, but did not yet have an appointment date for claimant.  (Exhibit A, p. 1) 
In a professional statement, defendants’ counsel indicated his client has 
contacted nine different providers to get a neurological consult for claimant.  At the time 
of hearing, defendants did not have an appointment for claimant with a neurologist.  
In a professional statement, claimant’s attorney indicated the first time he was 
aware of defendants contacting nine different providers was at hearing.  Claimant’s 
counsel requested he be provided with evidence of all nine contacts and that 
defendants provide his client with weekly updates regarding the status of a neurologist 
appointment.  Defendants’ counsel agreed to that request. 
CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance, and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law. The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services. The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury. Section 85.27. Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening, October 16, 1975).  
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable. See Iowa 
Rule of Appellate Procedure 6.904(3)(e); Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 
(Iowa 1995). Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of 
fact. Id. The employer’s obligation turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not

desirability. Id.; Harned v. Farmland Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983). In Pirelli-
Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 433 (Iowa 1997), the court approvingly 
quoted Bowles v. Los Lunas Schools, 109 N.M. 100, 781 P.2d 1178 (App. 1989):  
[T]he words “reasonable” and “adequate” appear to describe the same 
standard.  
[The New Mexico rule] requires the employer to provide a certain 
standard of care and excuses the employer from any obligation to provide 
other services only if that standard is met. We construe the terms 
"reasonable” and “adequate” as describing care that is both appropriate to 
the injury and sufficient to bring the worker to maximum recovery.  
An authorized provider recommended claimant see a neurologist in mid-
September of this year.  At the time of hearing, an appointment had not been made for 
claimant with a neurologist. 
I appreciate claimant’s frustration regarding the delay in care.  However, the 
record indicates defendants have authorized care.  The record also suggests 
defendants have contacted nine different providers for a neurological consult, but have 
not yet been able to secure an appointment. Because defendants have authorized care, 
and because the record suggests defendants have tried to schedule care with nine 
different providers, it is found defendants’ care is not unreasonable, at this time.  As a 
result, claimant has failed to carry his burden of proof he is entitled to alternate medical 
care, at this time.  As per the agreement at hearing, defendants will provide claimant 
with evidence, on or before December 5, 2025, they have attempted to schedule care 
with nine different providers.  Beginning on or before December 5, 2025, defendants 
shall also provide claimant with weekly status reports on the attempts to schedule 
claimant with a neurologist. 
ORDER 
Therefore, it is ordered that claimant’s petition for alternate medical care is 
denied at this time.  Defendants will provide claimant with evidence, on or before 
December 5, 2025, they have attempted to schedule care with nine different providers.  
Beginning on or before December 5, 2025, defendants shall also provide claimant with 
weekly status reports on the attempts to schedule claimant with a neurologist.
