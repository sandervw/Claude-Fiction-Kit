On February 2, 2024, this matter came before the court for oral argument on 
the petition for judicial review. Petitioner Sheryl Hermanstorfer (“Hermanstorfer”) 
was represented by attorney James M. Ballard. Respondents Lennox Industries, 
Inc. (“Lennox”) and Indemnity Insurance Company of North America were 
represented by attorney Robert C. Gainer. After hearing the arguments of counsel 
and reviewing the court file, including the briefs filed by the parties and the 
certified administrative record, the court reverses the commissioner’s decision and 
remands the case to the commissioner. 
BACKGROUND FACTS 
Hermanstorfer sustained a work injury to her head, neck, and vision on 
August 21, 2019, while working at Lennox Industries, Inc. She filed her petition 
for arbitration and medical benefits on October 11, 2021. Hearing commenced on

The issues presented for hearing were: 1) whether Hermanstorfer was 
entitled to permanent partial disability, 2) whether the permanent disability should 
be compensated on a functional impairment basis or under an industrial disability 
analysis, 3) her correct average weekly wage (AWW) and weekly workers’ 
compensation rate (WWCR), 4) her entitlement to reimbursement of her section 
85.39 independent medical examination,1 and 5) taxation of her costs.  
The deputy commissioner filed his arbitration decision on April 4, 2023. In 
that decision he found Hermanstorfer sustained a 7% permanent functional 
impairment of the whole person. He determined that Hermanstorfer’s AWW at the 
time of her injury was $880.89 and her applicable WWCR at $580.56. Based upon 
her AWW the deputy commissioner found Hermanstorfer’s post-injury earnings 
were slightly more than she earned at the time of her injury. Accordingly, the 
deputy commissioner found Hermanstorfer’s permanent partial disability recovery 
was limited to the functional rating assigned by Dr. Wolfe. He ordered respondents 
to reimburse her costs at $350.33.

Calculation of AWW

work between 32 and 51 hours per week immediately before her work injury.2 He 
further found that she “earned $11,451.62 during the 13 earliest typical, or 
representative, weeks immediately preceding her work injury.”3 Dividing this 
amount by 13 weeks he established her gross AWW at $880.89.4

ANALYSIS  
 
In reviewing an agency decision the court is required to consider the 
evidence that supports the challenged finding and the evidence that detracts from 
it.17 In reviewing the record the court is not to determine whether there is evidence 
to support a decision the commissioner did not make but to determine if there is 
substantial evidence to support the decision made by the commissioner.18 If the 
court could have reached a different conclusion the court is not to reverse unless 
the commissioner’s decision is not supported by substantial evidence.19 If the 
challenge is the commissioner misinterpreted the language of the statute the court 
must determine whether the commissioner’s interpretation is erroneous, and if so, 
substitute its judgment for that of the commissioner.20  
When we interpret a statute, we attempt to give effect to the general 
assembly's intent in enacting the law. IBP, Inc. v. Harker, 633 N.W.2d 
322, 325 (Iowa 2001). Generally, this intent is gleaned from the 
language of the statute. State v. Casey's Gen. Stores, Inc., 587 N.W.2d 
599, 601 (Iowa 1998). To ascertain the meaning of the statutory 
language, we consider the context of the provision at issue and strive 
to interpret it in a manner consistent with the statute as an integrated 
whole. McSpadden v. Big Ben Coal Co., 288 N.W.2d 181, 188 (Iowa 
1980). With respect to the workers' compensation statute in particular, 
we keep in mind that the primary purpose of chapter 85 is to benefit

If the challenge is the commissioner misapplied the law to the facts the district

Hermanstorfer asserts that the deputy commissioner misinterpreted section 
85.36(6) and misapplied the law to the facts. The deputy commissioner 
misinterpreted section 85.36(6) by including weeks she missed time from work 
while on FMLA leave due to a personal medical condition which altered her 
customary work week. By including these weeks, she contends the deputy 
commissioner’s application of the law to the facts was erroneous because it did not 
follow the plain language of the statute.  
The court does not find the deputy commissioner misinterpreted section 
85.36 as to the standard he was to apply in determining earnings. As the deputy 
commissioner noted he was to use what were the “usual or typical” earnings for the 
employee.23 He specifically stated, “[t]he standard is whether the earnings are

Hermanstorfer’s criticism is that the deputy commissioner should not have 
included the weeks when she took FMLA because section 85.36(6) based upon the 
last two sentences of the statute, which provides:  
If the employee was absent from employment for reasons personal to 
the employee during part of the thirteen calendar weeks preceding the 
injury, the employee’s weekly earnings shall be the amount the 
employee would have earned had the employee worked when work 
was available to other employees of the employer in a similar 
occupation. A week which does not fairly reflect the employee's 
customary earnings shall be replaced by the closest previous week 
with earnings that fairly represent the employee's customary 
earnings.25 
 
This portion of the statute was added to section 85.36(6) with a 2000 
amendment.26 Initially, our supreme court determined the amendment did not 
change the law rather it “clarified the legislature’s intent that a nonrepresentative 
week be excluded from the calculation of an employee’s compensation rate.”27 The 
court affirmed this conclusion later when it stated:

Thus, in our interpretation of section 85.36, both before and after the 
addition of the language at issue in this case, we have determined that 
one must look to the earnings themselves to see if they are customary. 
The reason for the variance in earnings is not determinative of 
whether a week's earnings should be replaced because they are not 
customary.28  
 
The court elucidated upon this statement in a footnote. 
The reason for nontypical wages is relevant if the employee was 
absent from work for reasons personal to the employee. As previously 
noted, section 85.36(6) provides for a different method of addressing 
a nontypical week of earnings due to personal reasons. In that case, 
the weekly wages must be replaced with the wages the employee 
“would have earned had the employee worked when work was 
available” to the employer's workers performing in a similar 
occupation. Iowa Code § 85.36(6).29 
 
 
Thus, while normally the reason for the variance is not important, the reason 
becomes important when the personal reason causes the atypical earnings. If 
personal reasons cause the earnings to be atypical, the commissioner is to disregard 
that week and replace it with a representative week. A review of her work records 
demonstrate her health condition which caused her to use FMLA caused her 
workweek hours (earnings) to be atypical.

In reaching his conclusion about Hermanstorfer’s AWW, the deputy 
commissioner ignored the second and third sentences of section 85.36(6) when he 
found:  
I find that claimant established a pattern of work prior to the injury 
date in which she required medical leave and did not work 50 hours 
per week during at least the six months immediately preceding her 
injury. I find that it was customary for claimant to take this leave and 
work less than 50 hours in a week quite frequently.33 
 
These statements demonstrate the deputy commissioner did not consider the 
personal that caused reason Hermanstorfer not to work her typical week. This 
conclusion is contrary to the plain language of section 85.36(6) and the court’s

Likewise, the deputy commissioner’s conclusion is contrary to the court’s 
statement in Griffin Pipe. The court there in approving the commissioner’s 
conclusion stated: 
We agree with the agency that the issue under section 85.36 “is 
whether the hours of work in any particular workweek are 
representative of the hours typically or customarily worked by an 
employee during a typical or customary full week of work.” (Emphasis 
added.) This interpretation is most consistent with the language of the 
statute, which directs that the basis for computation of the weekly rate 
is to be the earnings that the “employee would have been entitled to 
had the employee worked the customary hours for the full pay period 
in which the employee was injured.” Iowa Code § 85.36 (emphasis 
added); accord James R. Lawyer & Judith A. Higgs, Iowa Workers' 
Compensation Law and Practice § 12–4, at 123 (3d ed.1999) (stating 
weeks that contain “absences due to illness, vacation or other causes 
... are not representative of [the worker's] earnings” and are 
routinely excluded from the rate calculation).35 
 
Based upon the plain language of section 85.36(6) and the previous 
decisions construing section 85.36 the court finds the deputy commissioner 
misapplied the law to the facts and the weeks of April 28, May 19, June 2, June 9, 
and June 16, 2019, should not have been included in Hermanstorfer’s AWW. Their

Hermanstorfer also contends that the deputy commissioner erred by 
compensating her based upon a functional impairment. This was derived primarily 
based upon his determination that Hermanstorfer was making more in her present 
position with Lennox by comparing her AWW with her present earnings.36 The 
court finds that since the AWW was not calculated correctly, the commissioner 
must reevaluate its conclusion on this issue once Hermanstorfer’s AWW is 
calculated correctly. Accordingly, the court finds this issue must likewise be 
reexamined upon remand. 
IT IS THEREFORE ORDERED the decision of the commissioner is 
reversed. The court remands the case to the commissioner to determine 
Hermanstorfer’s AWW in accordance with this opinion and to determine whether 
her compensation should be based upon a functional impairment or an industrial 
disability once her AWW is determined in accordance with section 85.36(6).
