On January 10, 2025, claimant filed two original notices and petitions for 
alternate medical care under Iowa Code section 85.27, invoking the provisions of rule 
876 IAC 4.48.  These petitions came on for hearing before the undersigned on January 
21, 2025, at 10:30 a.m.  The proceedings were recorded digitally and constitute the 
official record of the hearing.   
 
Pursuant to the Commissioner’s July 21, 2023, Order, the undersigned has been 
delegated authority to issue a final agency decision in this alternate medical care 
proceeding. Therefore, this ruling is designated final agency action and any appeal of 
the decision would be to the Iowa District Court pursuant to Iowa Code section 17A. 
 
The record consists of Claimant’s Exhibits 1 through 5 and Defendant’s Exhibit A.  
All exhibits were offered without objection and received into evidence.  Ms. Padilla was 
the only witness to provide testimony.  Counsel for both parties provided argument.  The 
evidentiary record closed at the conclusion of the hearing.   
 
ISSUE 
 
The first issue presented for resolution is whether the defendant is barred from 
denying liability based upon the principle of judicial estoppel.  The second issue is 
whether the claimant is entitled to alternate medical care.

REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence. Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3). 
 
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers’ compensation law. The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services. The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury. Section 85.27. Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening October 16, 1975). 
 
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment - and seeking alternate care - 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable. See Iowa 
R. App. P 6.04(3)(e); Bell Bros. Heating and Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d 
193, 209 (Iowa 2010); Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995). 
Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact. Long v. 
Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995). The employer’s obligation turns on the 
question of reasonable necessity, not desirability. Id.; Harned v. Farmland Foods, Inc., 
331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983). 
 
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving. Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care. Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant. Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).

An employer does not have the right to control the methods the providers choose 
to evaluate, diagnose and treat the injured employee. An employer is not entitled to 
control a licensed healthcare provider's exercise of professional judgment. Assmann v. 
Blue Star Foods, File No. 866389 (Declaratory Ruling, May 18, 1988). An employer's 
failure to follow recommendations of an authorized physician in matters of treatment is 
commonly a failure to provide reasonable treatment. Boggs v. Cargill, Inc., File No. 
1050396 (Alt. Care, January 31, 1994). 
 
The Iowa Supreme Court has applied the principles of judicial estoppel to 
alternate medical care proceedings in the workers' compensation context. See 
Winnebago Industries v. Haverly, 727 N.W.2d 567 (Iowa 2006); Tyson Foods v. 
Hedlund, 740 N.W.2d 192 (Iowa 2007); Judicial estoppel is “a commonsense doctrine 
that prohibits a party who has successfully and unequivocally asserted a position in one 
proceeding from asserting an inconsistent position in a subsequent proceeding.” 
Hedlund, 740 N.W.2d at 196. Its purpose is “to protect the integrity of the judicial 
process.” Id. For judicial estoppel to apply, the party's inconsistent position must be 
“judicially accepted by the commissioner at the first alternate medical care hearing.” 
Judicial acceptance means that the “previous, inconsistent assertion was material to the 
holding in the first proceeding.” Id. at 197. 
 
One court has summarized the principle of judicial estoppel in the context of 
alternate medical care proceedings as follows:   
 
Indeed, to allow an employer to control the medical care of an employee 
while simultaneously allowing that same employer to free itself of any 
obligation to later pay for medical care or benefits is a contravention of 
justice and fairness. Such a result, as the Iowa Supreme Court related, 
“simply does not advance the policy goal of avoiding inconsistent and 
misleading results.” Hedlund, 740 N.W.2d at 198. It does not respect judicial 
integrity. Generally, once an employer admits liability for a claim in an 
alternate medical care proceeding, it is bound by that assertion. See 
Winnebago, 727 N.W.2d at 575 (explaining it would take a “significant 
change in the facts after the admission of liability” in order to later deny 
liability); Hedlund, 740 N.W.2d at 198-99 (describing the critical role that 
admitting liability plays in an alternate medical care proceeding). This is 
because “[l]iability is normally an important component of the course of an 
alternate medical care proceeding.” Hedlund 740 N.W.2d at 198. Without 
first admitting liability, the workers' compensation commissioner cannot 
hear the case. Iowa Admin. Code r. 876-48.7. 
 
Spencer v. Annett Holdings, Inc., 905 F.Supp.2d 953, 985 (S.D. Iowa 2012). 
 
I find that the principles of judicial estoppel apply in this case. The defendant 
admitted liability for these work injuries on two separate occasions, and those 
admissions were judicially accepted and relied upon by this agency. Once liability is 
judicially accepted, the defendant is barred from changing its position unless there is a

“significant change in the facts after the admission of liability.” Winnebago, 727 N.W.2d 
at 575. The burden rests on the defendant to demonstrate such a change by a 
preponderance of the evidence. 
 
On November 5, 2024, Deputy Pals issued the second decision ordering the 
defendant to authorize and provide reasonable and necessary pain management 
treatment for Ms. Padilla’s conditions. Before Ms. Padilla was evaluated by Dr. Mathew, 
the defendant sought a supplemental opinion from Dr. Iverson. While Dr. Iverson 
reviewed updated medical records, he did not reexamine Ms. Padilla. His February 
2024 and December 2024 reports reached nearly identical conclusions, providing no 
new analysis regarding maximum medical improvement or the need for additional care. 
There is no evidence Dr. Iverson reviewed or considered Dr. Mathew’s 
recommendations before issuing his supplemental opinion. 
 
The defendant now denies liability for the treatment recommendations of Dr. 
Mathew and Dr. Kim based on Dr. Iverson’s unchanged opinions. In his February 2024 
report, Dr. Iverson concluded that Ms. Padilla had reached maximum medical 
improvement and did not require ongoing treatment for her neck condition. His 
December 2024 report reiterated these conclusions, despite the defendant’s prior 
admissions of liability and decisions to provide care. The defendant had Dr. Iverson’s 
February 2024 opinion long before it admitted liability in two alternate medical care 
hearings but chose not to deny liability at that time. 
 
The agency relied on the defendant’s formal admissions to proceed with 
alternate medical care hearings. Judicial estoppel requires a change in the underlying 
facts to justify a subsequent denial of liability. Here, no such change has occurred. The 
defendant has presented no evidence showing that Ms. Padilla’s need for treatment is 
unrelated to her work injuries, nor has it shown any significant change in facts since its 
admissions of liability or the prior orders to provide care. 
 
This case exemplifies an unreasonable denial of care. The defendant accepted 
liability on two separate occasions and was ordered both times to provide the same 
care. While the claimant was in the process of obtaining that care, the defendant sought 
and relied on an outdated opinion from its independent medical examiner, which merely 
reiterated the same conclusions he had provided a year earlier.  The defendant’s 
reliance on a stale and recycled medical opinion, without considering updated treatment 
recommendations or providing new evidence, reflects an improper attempt to deny care. 
To allow the defendant to reverse its position would contravene the principles outlined 
in Winnebago and undermine the claimant’s right to appropriate medical care. 
 
Because the defendant has failed to demonstrate a significant change in the 
facts after admitting liability, it is estopped from denying liability for the requested 
medical treatment. Hormel Foods Corp. v. Tamayo-Perez, 2 N.W.3d 66 (Iowa Ct. App. 
2023).

On November 5, 2024, Deputy Pals found that Ms. Padilla could seek reasonable 
and necessary pain management treatment with a doctor of her choosing at the 
expense of the defendant.  Dr. Mathew, now an authorized treating physician, has 
provided a number of treatment recommendations.  Defendant has denied liability for 
the treatment recommendations.  Defendant has not offered any new evidence to 
indicate that there has been a significant change in the facts since its admission of 
liability.  I conclude that defendant’s denial of the requested medical care is 
unreasonable.  Claimant has carried her burden of proof that she is entitled to the 
requested medical care. 
 
ORDER 
 
The claimant’s petition for alternate medical care is granted.  Defendant shall 
authorize and pay for the treatment recommendations as recommended by Dr. Mathew.
