STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A.  The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Christina Sheets.  
Claimant appeared personally and through her attorney, Reid Nennig.  Defendants 
appeared through their attorney, Stephen Babe. 
 
The alternate medical care claim came on for a telephonic hearing on February 
6, 2025.  The proceedings were digitally recorded.  That recording constitutes the 
official record of this proceeding.  Pursuant to the Commissioner’s July 21, 2023 Order, 
the undersigned has been delegated authority to issue a final agency decision in this 
alternate medical care proceeding.  Therefore, this ruling is designated final agency 
action and any appeal of the decision would be to the Iowa District Court pursuant to 
Iowa Code section 17A. 
 
Claimant offered exhibit 1, which includes 1 page.  Defendants submitted 
Exhibits A through E, which consist of 10 pages.  All exhibits were received without 
objection.  Claimant testified on her own behalf.  No other witnesses were called to 
testify and the evidentiary record closed at the conclusion of the alternate medical care 
hearing.

ISSUE 
 
The issue presented in this alternate medical care proceeding is limited to 
whether claimant’s authorized treating physical therapist should be changed to a 
therapist closer to her home. 
 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
The undersigned having considered all the evidence in the record finds: 
 
Claimant, Christina Sheets, sustained a work-related injury on July 10, 2024.  For 
purposes of this alternate medical care proceeding, defendants admitted claimant 
sustained injuries to her right upper extremity and/or right shoulder on the above date 
and that the current treatment sought is causally related to the work injury.  Ms. Sheets 
testified that she initially treated for this injury in Iowa.  However, she currently lives in 
Richmond, Texas. 
 
Defendants have transferred care and authorized both orthopaedic care and 
physical therapy in Texas.  Gerard T. Gable, M.D. is claimant’s current treating 
orthopaedic surgeon in Texas.  Dr. Gable referred claimant to Innovate Physical 
Therapy for treatment in Sugar Land, Texas.  Innovate Physical Therapy’s office is 
approximately 16 miles from her home.   
 
Ms. Sheets testified that it is about a 22-minute drive from her home to Innovate 
Physical Therapy.  However, due to personal issues with work, childcare, and other 
medical appointments, she testified that she can only schedule therapy appointments to 
occur at 5:00 p.m.  At that time of day, she testified that the drive to and from Innovate 
Physical Therapy is 30-40 minutes due to traffic. 
 
Ms. Sheets testified that she is a single mother.  She further testified that she 
works for a different employer now and uses many of her lunch breaks to attend other 
medical appointments, including her orthopaedic appointments, cardiac appointments, 
and appointments for her son.  She testified that it is difficult for her to find childcare for 
her son during a 5:00 p.m. appointment and that those appointments make it difficult for 
her to timely prepare an evening meal for her son. 
 
Claimant prefers and requests that physical therapy be transferred to another 
provider closer to her home.  She identified three physical therapy providers closer to 
her home and testified that the closest of those providers would only require a 15-
minute drive or less.  Therefore, due to the inconvenience of having to drive 30-40 
minutes one-way for physical therapy, Ms. Sheets is requesting an order transferring 
care to one of the physical therapy clinics closer to her home. 
 
On the other hand, defendants introduce a medical note from the authorized 
treating orthopaedic surgeon, which suggests that his referral to Innovate Physical 
Therapy was intentional, though further from claimant’s home.  In his February 5, 2025

office note, Dr. Gable indicates, “We are trying to get, as far as the therapy, the best 
location for that and the closest to her is going to be there in Sugar Land at Innovate.  
The therapist there is a certified upper extremity therapist who is going to provide the

Claimant confirmed that she had reviewed Innovate Physical Therapy’s website and

I understand claimant’s testimony and sympathize with her situation.  She has 
practical difficulties attending physical therapy at Innovate Physical Therapy.  She will 
definitely have to select alternate times to treat, miss time from work, or seek childcare 
for her son and have difficulties with parental duties while trying to attend these therapy 
sessions.  Her concerns are legitimate and understandable. 
 
On the other hand, defendants are following the recommendation of the 
authorized treating orthopaedic surgeon.  Dr. Gable has clearly indicated that the best 
available care would be through a certified upper extremity therapist.  Dr. Gable has 
already determined that the closest qualified therapist to provide the best care for 
claimant is at Innovate Physical Therapy. I find that defendants are offering reasonable, 
appropriate physical therapy.  Weighing the need for the best possible care with the 
inconvenience claimant will experience, I find that claimant is not unduly 
inconvenienced and that the defendants’ offer of physical therapy at Innovate Physical 
Therapy is reasonable. 
 
REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Section 85.27.  Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening October 16, 1975). 
 
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See Iowa 
R. App. P 6.904(3)(e); Bell Bros. Heating & Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d 193, 
209 (Iowa 2010); Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  Determining 
what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.  Long v. Roberts Dairy 
Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  The employer’s obligation turns on the question of 
reasonable necessity, not desirability.  Id.; Harned v. Farmland Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 
98 (Iowa 1983).   
An employer’s right to select the provider of medical treatment to an injured 
worker does not include the right to determine how an injured worker should be

diagnosed, evaluated, treated, or other matters of professional medical judgment.  
Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 866389 (Declaratory Ruling, May 19, 1988).   
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care she has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).   
 
Acknowledging that claimant will experience some inconvenience in seeking 
therapy at Innovate Physical Therapy, I found that the recommendation of Dr. Gabel 
and defendants’ offer of care with a certified upper extremity specialist for the “best 
care” for claimant is reasonable.  Given that defendants are offering and authorizing 
therapy with a specially certified therapist that was specifically recommended by the 
orthopaedic surgeon and that the location of this care is only 16 miles from her home, I 
found that defendants are offering reasonable medical care and that the offered care 
does not unduly inconvenience claimant.  Therefore, I conclude that defendants have 
met their burden to offer reasonable medical care and that the petition for alternate 
medical care should be denied at this time. 
 
ORDER 
 
THEREFORE, IT IS ORDERED: 
 
The claimant's petition for alternate medical care is denied and dismissed.
