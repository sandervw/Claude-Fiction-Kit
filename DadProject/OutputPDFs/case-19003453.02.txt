STANDARD OF REVIEW 
 
On judicial review of agency action, the district court functions in an appellate 
capacity to apply the standards of Iowa Code section 17A.19.  Iowa Planners Network 
v. Iowa State Commerce Comm’n, 373 N.W.2d 106, 108.  (Iowa 1985).  The District 
Court’s review is limited to corrections of errors of law and is not de novo.  Second 
Injury Fund of Iowa v. Klebs, 539 N.W.2d 178, 180 (Iowa 1995); Harlan v. Iowa Dept. of 
Job Serv., 350 N.W.2d 192, 193 (Iowa 1984).   
The court shall reverse, modify, or grant other appropriate relief from agency 
action if the agency action was based upon a determination of fact clearly vested by a 
provision of law in the discretion of the agency that is not supported by substantial 
evidence in the record before the court when the record is viewed as a whole.  Iowa 
Code § 17A.19(10)(f).  “Substantial evidence” means the quantity and quality of 
evidence that would be deemed sufficient by a neutral, detached, and reasonable 
person to establish the fact at issue when the consequences resulting from the 
establishment of that fact are understood to be serious and of great importance.  Iowa 
Code § 17A.19(10)(f)(1). 
Evidence is substantial when a reasonable person could accept it as 
adequate to reach the same findings.  Conversely, evidence is not 
insubstantial merely because it would have supported contrary inferences.

The ultimate question is not whether the evidence supports a different 
finding but whether the evidence supports the findings actually made.   
 
Reed v. Iowa Dept. of Transp., 478 N.W.2d 844, 846 (Iowa 1992). 
 
The Court shall also reverse, modify, or grant other appropriate relief from agency 
action if such action was based upon action other than a rule that is inconsistent with the 
agency’s prior practice or precedents, unless the agency has justified that inconsistency 
by stating credible reasons sufficient to indicate a fair and rational basis for the 
inconsistency.  Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(h).   
 
A reviewing court must also reverse, modify, or grant other appropriate relief when 
the agency’s decision is otherwise unreasonable, arbitrary, capricious, or an abuse of 
discretion.  Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(n).   
 
Finally, a reviewing court must also reverse, modify, or grant other appropriate 
relief when the decision is based upon an erroneous interpretation of a provision of law 
whose interpretation has not clearly been vested by a provision of the law in the discretion 
of the agency.  Iowa Code § 17A.9(10)(c).  See also Iowa Code 17A.(10)(a)-(n) 
(describing other grounds which mandate reversal, modification, or other appropriate 
relief from agency action).  In making the determinations required by subsection 10, 
paragraphs “a” through “n,” the court shall not give deference to the view of the agency 
with respect to particular matters that have not been vested by a provision of law in the 
discretion of the agency.  Iowa Code § 17A.19(11)(b).  The burden of demonstrating the 
required prejudice and the invalidity of the agency is on the party asserting the invalidity.  
Iowa Code § 17A.19(8)(a).

DISCUSSION 
1. Did the Commissioner properly sustain Deputy Pals’ ruling that Seliger failed 
to prove he sustained an injury to his left hip as a result of the work injury 
incident on June 22, 2019? 
 
 Seliger alleges that Commissioner Cortese II erred when he adopted Deputy 
Pals’ findings and affirmed Pals’ ruling that Seliger failed to prove by a preponderance 
of the evidence that he sustained an injury to his left hip as a result of the work injury 
incident on June 22, 2019.  Seliger argues that Pals’ findings were based on an 
incomplete review of Dr. Bansal’s report, Bansal’s misinterpretation of Seliger’s 
statements to him regarding causation, and that Pals incorrectly found Bansal’s report 
to be less credible than the reports of Dr. Rice and Dr. Moody. 
 
Respondent disagrees and argues that Commissioner Cortese II correctly 
adopted Deputy Pals’ decision and that Deputy Pals’ decision was based on a thorough 
examination of the medical evidence, Seliger’s testimony, and non-medical exhibits 
submitted by both parties.  Respondent submits that Pals’ decision specifically 
discusses the medical and expert advice she relied upon in reaching her conclusion that 
Seliger failed to meet his burden of proof that he sustained a permanent injury to his left 
hip or the need for replacement surgery.   
Deputy Pals found that Seliger failed to prove his left hip injury did not arise out 
of and in the course of his employment, but rather was attributable to his pre-existing

osteoarthritis.  Commissioner Cortese II relied upon the findings of Deputy Pals.  In 
reaching her conclusion, Deputy Pals made a finding that Seliger failed to carry his 
burden of proof to show that the workplace injury caused any permanent impairment or 
the need for hip replacement surgery.   
“It is well settled in Iowa that for an injury to be compensable, it must occur both 
in the course of and arise out of employment.”  Miedema v. Dial Corp, 551 N.W.2d 309, 
311 (Iowa 1996).  “An injury occurs ‘in the course of employment’ when it is ‘within the 
period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may be in performing 
his duties, and while he is fulfilling those duties or something incidental thereto.’”  St 
Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 (Iowa 2000) (citations omitted).  An injury 
“arises out of” employment when there is a causal connection between the employment 
and the injury.  Id.   
There is no dispute in this present case that Seliger sustained an injury.  Rather, 
the fighting issue is whether the claimed injury rose out of Seliger’s employment or if the 
left hip injury was because of a preexisting condition in his left hip.    As the claimant in 
this matter, Seliger bears the burden of proving the existence of causal connection 
between “the conditions of [his] employment and the injury” by a preponderance of the 
evidence.  Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. 
Dr. Bansal, unlike Dr. Mooney and Dr. Rice, found that the left hip injury to 
Seliger was as a result of his employment and not due to an underlying preexisting 
condition, and understandably Seliger agrees.  As to this issue, Pals did not find Dr. 
Bansal’s opinion to be more persuasive than those of Dr. Rice and Dr. Mooney because

according to Pals, Dr. Bansal based his finding on an incorrect history.   
The incorrect history, according to Pals, is Dr. Bansal’s notations in his records 
that Seliger aggravated his left hip osteoarthritis from jumping off his semi-trailer rather, 
than as Seliger reported and testified, that it was the step and subsequent twist that 
caused the injury.  Seliger denies reporting to Dr. Bansal that he jumped off the trailer, 
and he argues Pals ignored the rest of Dr. Bansal’s report which addresses the twisting 
of the knee and hip, which is consistent with the facts in Dr. Mooney and Dr. Rice’s 
reports.  Seliger argues error occurred when Deputy Commissioner Pals and 
Commissioner Cortese II, discounted Dr. Bansal’s report due to what Pals characterized 
as Dr. Bansal’s conclusions basis in incorrect history.  
This Court disagrees and finds that Deputy Commissioner Pals and 
Commissioner Cortese II did not err in their weighing of the expert testimony of Dr. 
Bansal, Dr. Mooney, or Dr. Rice.  Deputy Commissioner Pals, and as ratified by 
Commissioner Cortese II, provided a justified reason for her finding regarding Dr. 
Bansal’s determination as to causation.  Even if this Court were to assess Dr. Bansal’s 
opinion differently and conclude that his opinion was not based on incorrect history, 
Seliger’s claims still fail because there is substantial evidence supporting the opposite 
conclusion—that Seliger’s hip injury was caused by a preexisting condition.  Smith v. 
TPI Iowa, LLC., 965 N.W.2d 621 (Table), 2021 WL 3074501 *2-3. 
Respondent asserts that Seliger fails to support his position that reliance on Dr. 
Rice’s and Dr. Mooney’s opinion that Seliger’s preexisting degenerative condition was 
going to someday require him to undergo hip surgery is contrary to current law.  The

Court agrees.  As the Court understands Seliger’s argument, he contends that by 
considering such opinions, Commissioner Cortese II and Deputy Commissioner Pals 
are essentially creating a preexisting condition defense to any claim.  In this case, the 
Court disagrees.   
The record in this case is clear that Seliger had preexisting problems with his left 
hip.  This fact is supported by the historical medical records, testimony of reviewing 
experts, reports by Seliger’s own treating physician and Seliger’s own testimony.   
The Court hereby finds that Deputy Commissioner Pals’ and Commissioner 
Cortese II’s conclusions, including that Seliger failed to meet his burden of proof to 
establish a compensable left hip injury are supported by substantial evidence in the 
record and are affirmed in their entirety.   
The Court further finds that Seliger has failed to show that the action taken herein 
is inconsistent with the agency’s prior practices and precedents or that the agency 
action herein was unreasonable, arbitrary, capricious, or an abuse of discretion.     
The Court further finds that Deputy Commissioner Pals’ and Commissioner 
Cortese II’s conclusions are rational, logical, and justifiable application of the law to 
facts. 
RULING AND ORDER 
For the reasons stated herein, the Appeal Decision of Commissioner Cortese II, 
adopting the Arbitration Decision of the Deputy Commissioner Pals should be and is 
hereby affirmed.  Petitioner’s Petition for Judicial Review is hereby dismissed.  
Petitioner shall pay the court costs.
