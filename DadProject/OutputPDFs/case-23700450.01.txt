STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Michael Lee Phillips, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits against Qwest, employer, and Reliance, insurance carrier, both 
as defendants.  This case was heard on August 16, 2024, and considered fully 
submitted on October 15, 2024, upon the simultaneous filing of briefs.  
The record consists of Joint Exhibits 1-8; Claimant’s Exhibits 1-9 and 12-18; 
Defendants’ Exhibits A-O, along with the testimony of claimant, Danielle Kent and 
George Bykowski.  
ISSUES 
1. Whether claimant’s Parkinson’s disease arose out of and in the course of his 
employment. 
 
2. Whether the claim is barred by the statute of repose of Iowa Code section 
85A.12. 
 
3. Whether the claim is barred due to untimely notice under Iowa Code section 
85.23 and/or Iowa Code section 85A.18. 
 
4. Whether the claim is barred by the statute of limitations in Iowa Code section  
85.26.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
Claimant, Michael Lee Phillips, was an 80-year-old person at the time of the 
hearing. Following his graduation from high school, claimant joined the Army. He was 
stationed in Fort Knox, Fort Benning, and Korea. He was honorably discharged in 1967. 
He then moved to Davenport where he lived until he moved to Des Moines in 1996 and 
then to Arizona following his retirement in 2003. He then returned to Davenport.  
His educational background includes completion of the 11th grade and 
subsequent GED.  
For employment, claimant primarily worked for defendant employer or one of 
defendant employer’s predecessors from 1968 to 2003. He retired in 2003 and in the 
following year he was diagnosed with Parkinson’s disease. He alleges that he 
contracted this disease from the years of exposure to trichloroethylene (TCE). His

clean off the icky-pic from the cables as well as from his own hands. He testified that he

The MSDS Data sheet released in 1999 by OSHA regarding TCE requires the 
use of safety goggles or full facepiece respirator, skin protection such as gloves and an 
apron, safety shoes, safety shower, and eyewash. (CE 12:49-50)  
A 1973 Health Hazard Evaluation Report issued by OSHA noted that Western 
Electric Company in Dublin, California, was found to have TCE levels above the federal 
standards. (CE 17) A study was conducted which found that of the 24 workers tested, 
aged 21-52, 33.3 percent experienced dizziness, 25 percent experienced throat 
irritation, nose irritation, fatigue and drowsiness. (CE 17:106)  
In 1975, an occupational health meeting between medical directors and 
telephone company engineers was held to discuss new work safety concerns. (CE 
13:53) The meeting minutes included new health standard proposals regarding TCE 
use. (CE 13:55) The proposed standards recommended that the maximum exposure 
level to TCE be limited to 150 ppm due to exposures causing visual disturbances, 
mental confusion, fatigue, tremors, dizziness, nausea and vomiting. (CE 14:59, 62)  
A 1978 Health Hazard Evaluation Determination Report acknowledged that 
employees have exposure to Quick Fix (another term for icky-pic) and that some 
employees could experience discomfort at or below the time weighted average for 
exposure. (CE 16:87) The document noted TCE exposure causes central nervous 
system depression and defatting of the skin. Acute exposure symptoms include 
dizziness, incoordination, drowsiness, increased reaction time, unconsciousness and 
death. (CE 16:89) Because of these health dangers, employees should use protective 
equipment. (CE 16:90)  
A 1989 US West Communications document identifies polychlorinated biphenyls 
as a hazardous substance to which employees are exposed. (CE 15:79) The document 
directs employees who work with those substances to wear proper protective clothing, 
including appropriate goggles and gloves, and prolonged contact with TCE should be 
avoided. (CE 16:72-83)

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW  
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury actually occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 
employment. Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 150 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. 
Dial Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309, 311 (Iowa 1996). The words “arising out of” refer to the 
cause or source of the injury. The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 
circumstances of the injury. 2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124, 128 (Iowa 
1995).  An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists 
between the injury and the employment. Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. The injury must 
be a rational consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely 
incidental to the employment. Koehler Elec. v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1, 3 (Iowa 2000); 
Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it 
happens within a period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may 
be when performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties 
or doing an activity incidental to them. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d at 150. 
A possibility of causation is not sufficient; a probability is necessary. Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996) (citing Holmes v. Bruce 
Motor Freight, Inc., 215 N.W.2d 296, 297 (Iowa 1974)).

The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 
621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 
(Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
The majority of Claimant’s brief focuses on the exposures of TCE and the 
timeline of those exposures as well as when the employer would have known of the 
hazardous quality of TCE. As stated in the Findings of Fact, Claimant was exposed to 
TCE throughout his career with defendant employer. In 1995, Claimant moved to an 
office position for Defendant and was no longer exposed to TCE in a work setting.  
Claimant relies on the expert testimony of Dr. Harker, a family practice doctor, 
who initially opined to a reasonable degree of medical certainty that Claimant’s 
Parkinson’s disease was caused by his work exposure to TCE, but then later could not 
state to a reasonable degree of medical certainty that Claimant’s Parkinson’s was the 
result of his workplace exposure, military exposure or home life exposure. While 
Claimant’s exposure to TCE was greatest while he was working, there is no medical 
opinion that serves as a causal link between the workplace TCE and Claimant’s 
Parkinson’s. While defendants knew or should have known TCE exposure could lead to 
health problems, this alone cannot provide the causal link between TCE exposure and 
the development or acceleration of the development of Parkinson’s.  
Dr. Harker’s final opinion was only that Claimant’s exposure to TCE in the 
workplace was consistent with Claimant’s development of Parkinson’s, but that she 
could not state to a reasonable degree of medical certainty that the Parkinson’s was 
caused by the workplace exposure.  
The lack of a medical opinion supporting the causal connection is fatal to 
Claimant’s case. Because of this, Claimant’s claim is not compensable.
