On May 18, 2020, the undersigned issued an arbitration decision in this matter. 
The defendants appealed to the Commissioner, who remanded the matter on 
December 21, 2020, to the undersigned for consideration of whether an order nunc pro 
tunc is appropriate. On December 22, 2020, in an email from claimant’s counsel, the 
parties notified the agency that they felt an order nunc pro tunc is appropriate and the 
parties waived their opportunity to submit additional filings on the issue. 
The phrase, “nunc pro tunc” means “now for then.”  See:  Black’s Law Dictionary, 
page 1218 (Revised 4th Edition 1968).  The definition in Black’s Law Dictionary further 
provides:  “A phrase applied to acts allowed to be done after the time when they should 
be done, with a retroactive effect, i.e. with the same effect as if regularly done.”  Black’s 
at 1218.  A nunc pro tunc order “is not for the purpose of correcting judicial thinking, a 
judicial conclusion, or a mistake of law.”  Headley v. Headley, 172 N.W.2d 104, 108 
(Iowa 1969).  The nunc pro tunc order can be employed to correct obvious errors or to 
make an order conform to the judge’s original intent.  Graber v. Dist. Court for 
Washington Cty., 410 N.W.2d 224, 229 (Iowa 1987);  Brinson v. Spee Dee Delivery 
Service, No. 8-754/06-2074 (Iowa App. November 13, 2008). 
On pages 28 and 29 of the arbitration decision, the undersigned concluded 
claimant is entitled to receive healing period benefits from June 15, 2014, through 
September 2, 2014, in the amount of $933.55 per week.  Paragraph 2 of the Order 
section, on page 34 of the arbitration decision, states the wrong number of weeks for 
which healing period benefits are owed. Thus, Paragraph 2 of the Order section should 
be changed to accurately reflect the conclusion regarding healing period benefits stated 
on pages 28 and 29 of the arbitration decision.

The table, which is the ninth paragraph on page 28 of the arbitration decision, 
details the amounts of temporary partial disability (TPD) benefits and dates during which 
claimant is entitled to receive such benefits.  Paragraph 3 of the Order section, on page 
34 of the arbitration decision, states the wrong numbers of weeks and amounts of 
benefits owed.  Consequently, paragraph 3 of the Order section should be changed to 
accurately reflect the conclusion regarding the dates and amounts of TPD benefits 
stated on page 28 of the arbitration decision. 
IT IS THEREFORE ORDERED, an order nunc pro tunc is appropriate in this 
matter and the “Order” section of the arbitration decision filed on May 18, 2020, is 
hereby amended to state as follows: 
2)  The defendants shall pay claimant healing period benefits for the 
period beginning on June 15, 2014, and ending on September 2, 2014, in 
the amount of nine hundred thirty-three and 55/100 dollars ($933.55) per 
week. 
3)  The defendants shall pay claimant TPD benefits as follows: 
a.  From May 15, 2013, through December 14, 2013, at the rate of 
twenty and 03/100 dollars ($20.03) per week; 
b.  From December 29, 2013, through March 22, 2014, at the rate 
of twenty-five and 36/100 dollars ($25.36) per week; 
c.  From March 23, 2014, to June 14, 2014, at the rate of two 
hundred and seventy-one and 43/100 dollars ($271.43) per week; 
and 
 
d.  From September 21, 2016, to October 19, 2016, at the rate of 
five hundred sixteen and 33/100 dollars ($516.33) per week. 
There are no other changes to the arbitration decision filed in this matter on May 
18, 2020.
