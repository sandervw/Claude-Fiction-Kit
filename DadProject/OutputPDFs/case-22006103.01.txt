STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Richard Irish, claimant, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from UniFirst Corporation, employer and Indemnity Insurance 
Company of North America, insurance carrier, as defendants.  Hearing was held via 
Zoom videoconference on June 2, 2025.     
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.  
Richard Irish was the only witness to testify live at trial.  The evidentiary record 
also includes joint exhibits 1-5, claimant’s exhibit 1, and defendants’ exhibits A-G.  
Claimant’s proposed exhibit 2 was a first report of injury (FROI).  Because notice is not 
an issue in this case, the FROI was excluded pursuant to Iowa Code 10A.313.  
Claimant objected to defendants’ exhibit G, pages 13 and 14 on the basis that it was 
identified as an exhibit less than 30 days before the hearing.  (Transcript pages 9-10) 
Defendants’ exhibit G, pages 13 and 14 contain excerpts from claimant’s personnel file 
regarding his pay.  Defendants admitted the records were just identified as exhibits 
merely days before the hearing; however, this was because the weekly rate was only 
identified as a disputed issue days before the hearing.  (Tr. pp. 9-11) Although the 
documents in question were not identified as exhibits 30 days before the hearing, they 
were provided to the claimant in April in discovery responses.  I found that claimant 
failed to prove that he was unfairly prejudiced by the exhibits.  The objection was

ISSUES 
The parties submitted the following issues for resolution: 
1. Claimant’s gross weekly earnings at the time of the injury. 
2. Whether claimant is entitled to any additional weekly benefits as the result of 
an underpayment of the weekly rate. 
3. Whether claimant is entitled to healing period benefits from May 9, 2022 
through August 31, 2022.   
4. Whether defendants should reimburse claimant for the full amount of the 
independent medical examination. 
5. Whether an assessment of costs against the defendants is appropriate. 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned, having considered all the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
 
 Richard Irish is a resident of Iowa.  (Tr. p. 18) He sustained a work-related injury 
to his left leg on May 9, 2022 while working at UniFirst Corporation (“UniFirst”).  (Tr. pp. 
20-21) He worked at UniFirst for approximately one month prior to his injury.  (Tr. pp. 
18-19) UniFirst provides laundry services for uniforms and rugs.  They also bring in 
toiletries and have first-aid stations.  (Tr. p. 19) Richard was hired as a route sales 
representative.  (Tr. p. 19)  
 
Richard sustained his injury on May 9, 2022.  (Tr. p. 20) He and another 
employee were unloading a truck at the dock when a cart rolled off and landed on him.  
(Tr. pp. 20-21) He injured his left leg.  (Tr. p. 21) Defendants authorized medical 
treatment for the injury.  (Tr. p. 21) He treated primarily with Robert Bartelt, M.D. at 
Cedar Valley Orthopedic Surgery.  (Tr. p. 21; Joint Exhibit 5) Dr. Bartelt’s assessment 
was closed fracture of left tibial plateau.  (JE5, p. 53) He was placed on restrictions and 
underwent physical therapy.  (JE5, p. 55)  
 
On September 12, 2022, Dr. Bartelt authored a missive. (JE5, p. 591) Dr. Bartelt 
had treated Richard for a left tibial plateau fracture which was treated nonsurgically.  He 
last saw Richard on September 1, 2022 and placed him at MMI at that time.  He stated 
that Richard had no permanent restrictions.  Dr. Bartelt assigned “5% impairment to the 
left lower extremity as per the AMA guides (5th ed.)”.  (JE5, p. 59)  
 
At the request of his attorney, Richard saw Sunil Bansal, M.D. for an IME on April 
17, 2025.  (Claimant’s Exhibit 1) As the result of the examination and review of records

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 
Claimant sustained a work-related injury to his left lower extremity on May 9, 
2022.  There is a dispute surrounding what his gross earnings were at the time of the 
injury.  The parties have stipulated that claimant was single and entitled to one 
exemption.  (Hearing Report, numbered paragraph 6) The dispute regarding the weekly 
earnings seems to center on which subsection of Iowa Code 85.36 should apply in this 
case. 
Iowa Code section 85.36 governs the basis for computation of claimant’s weekly 
rate.  The first paragraph of that statute states,  
The basis of compensation shall be the weekly earnings of the injured 
employee at the time of the injury. Weekly earnings means gross salary, 
wages, or earnings of an employee to which such employee would have 
been entitled had the employee worked the customary hours for the full 
pay period in which the employee was injured, as regularly required by the 
employee's employer for the work or employment for which the employee 
was employed, computed or determined as follows and then rounded to 
the nearest dollar: 
Iowa Code section 85.36. 
 
Following the initial paragraph, the subsections of the Code address how the 
weekly earnings are to be calculated depending on how an employee is paid.  In his 
post-hearing brief claimant cites to subsection 1 of Iowa Code 85.36 which addresses 
employees who are paid on a weekly pay period basis.  (Claimant’s post-hearing brief, 
p. 2) Subsection 1 states, “[i]n the case of an employee who is paid on a weekly pay 
period basis, the weekly gross earnings.”  Iowa Code section 85.36(1). Claimant seems 
to argue that because he received a paycheck once a week he was paid on a weekly 
basis.  Claimant contends that his weekly wages at the time of the injury were 
$1,000.00.  However, a review of claimant’s earnings demonstrates that the only time 
claimant’s gross pay was $1000.00 or greater was after the injury and when he was in a 
different job.  (Def. Ex. A) Therefore, I find that from the time claimant was hired until the 
date of his injury his weekly earnings were not $1,000.00 or greater. 
Claimant also argues that certain weeks should not be included if that week does 
not fairly reflect the employee’s customary earnings and any such week should be 
replaced by the closest previous week which is representative.  (Brief, pp. 2-3) 
However, the approach of replacing unrepresentative weeks with representative weeks 
is typically used for rate calculations made pursuant to subsection 6 which is for

Page 6 
 
employees who are paid on a daily or hourly basis, not on a weekly basis.  It may be 
that claimant is attempting to rely on subsection 7.  Iowa Code 85.36(7) addresses how 
to calculate the rate when the injured worker has worked less than 13 weeks before the 
injury.  The Code states,  
In the case of an employee who has been in the employ of the employer 
less than thirteen calendar weeks immediately preceding the injury, the 
employee's weekly earnings shall be computed under subsection 6, taking 
the earnings, including shift differential pay but not including overtime or 
premium pay, for such purpose to be the amount the employee would 
have earned had the employee been so employed by the employer the full 
thirteen calendar weeks immediately preceding the injury and had worked, 
when work was available to other employees in a similar occupation. If the 
earnings of other employees cannot be determined, the employee's 
weekly earnings shall be the average computed for the number of weeks 
the employee has been in the employ of the employer. 
Iowa Code section 85.36(7). 
Regardless, of which subsection claimant is relying on for his calculation, I 
conclude that basing his weekly workers’ compensation rate on salary or wages he 
earned after the date of the injury is not consistent with Iowa Code section 85.36. I also 
conclude that the evidentiary record is void of any reliable evidence of the earnings of 
other employees at the time of claimant’s injury. 
 
Claimant also argues that the check associated with the date of May 6, 2022 on 
page one of exhibit A reflects the claimant’s earnings for one week of work prior to the 
injury and therefore, that is the only representative week that should be used to 
calculate his rate.  He contends that exhibit A shows claimant’s “gross earnings were 
$1,000, as he worked a total of 50 hours (40 hours regular time = $800; 10 hours 
overtime = $200) for one week prior to his date of injury, and was single and entitled to 
one exemption, therefore, $623.06 should be adopted as the correct rate.”  (Cl. Brief, p. 
3) However, exhibit A does not support claimant’s contention that his gross earnings 
associated with the date of May 6, 2022 were $1,000.  As noted above, claimant’s gross 
earnings for that week were $810.00.   
 
Defendants contend claimant’s weekly earnings should be calculated pursuant to 
Iowa Code subsection 85.36(5) and based on his annual salary of $41,600.00, which 
amounts to $800.00 per week. (Defendants’ post-hearing brief, p. 6) 
Based on the above findings of fact, I conclude the preponderance of the 
evidence demonstrates that claimant was paid on the basis of his annual salary. I 
further conclude that claimant’s weekly earnings should be calculated pursuant to Iowa 
Code section 85.36(5). I conclude his annual salary was $41,600.00.  (Def. Ex. G, p. 13) 
Claimant failed to demonstrate by a preponderance of the evidence what, if any, 
commission he would have earned on his route. 
Under Iowa Code section 85.36(5), in the case of an employee who is paid on a 
yearly pay period basis, average gross weekly earnings is defined as the yearly

earnings divided by 52. Iowa Code section 85.36(5).  See also, Herron v. Meadows, File 
No. 1002230, 1993 WL 13021379, at *6 (Arb. December 30, 1993); Chown v. Dana 
Chown Trucking, File No. 5003605, 2004 WL 2601564, at *9 (Arb. November 4, 2004); 
Head v. Prestage Foods of Iowa, LLC, File No. 22003131.01, 2023 WL 8172358, at *9 
(Arb. November 20, 2023). Claimant’s yearly earnings of $41,600.00 divided by 52 is 
$800.00, which is adopted as claimant's gross average weekly wage. The rate tables 
published by the commissioner show that a single individual with one exemption and 
that average weekly wage is entitled to a compensation rate of $507.95.  
Claimant is seeking weekly benefits from May 9, 2022 through August 31, 2022. 
(Hearing Report, numbered paragraph 4) Because the claimant has sustained 
permanent disability related to the work injury, any temporary disability benefits would 
be classified as healing period benefits. (Hearing Report, numbered paragraph 3(b)).   
Healing period compensation describes temporary workers’ compensation 
weekly benefits that precede an allowance of permanent partial disability benefits.  
Ellingson v. Fleetguard, Inc., 599 N.W.2d 440, 446 (Iowa 1999).  Healing period benefits 
are payable to an injured worker who has suffered permanent partial disability until the 
first to occur of three events.  Iowa Code § 85.34(1) (2015).  These are:  (1) the worker 
has returned to work; (2) the worker medically is capable of returning to substantially 
similar employment; or (3) the worker has achieved maximum medical recovery.  Id.  
Maximum medical recovery is achieved when healing is complete, and the extent of 
permanent disability can be determined.  Armstrong Tire & Rubber Co. v. Kubli, 312 
N.W.2d 60, 65 (Iowa Ct. App. 1981).  Neither maintenance medical care nor an 
employee's continuing to have pain or other symptoms necessarily prolongs the healing 
period.  Id. 
Based on the above findings of fact, I conclude claimant failed to demonstrate by 
a preponderance of the evidence that he is entitled to any additional healing period 
benefits.   
We now turn to the issue of permanent disability.  The parties stipulated that 
claimant sustained disability limited to his left leg as the result of the work injury.  
(Hearing Report, numbered paragraph 5(b)) The parties agree that prior to hearing 
defendants paid claimant 11 weeks of permanent partial disability benefits at the rate of 
$509.75.  (Tr. pp. 5-6) Pursuant to claimant’s post-hearing brief, the only remaining 
issue regarding permanent partial disability benefits is any underpayment due to the 
rate dispute.  However, I conclude that $507.95 is the correct weekly workers’ 
compensation rate; therefore, I further conclude that there was no underpayment of 
permanent partial disability benefits.  Claimant has failed to demonstrate entitlement to 
any additional permanent partial disability benefits. 
Claimant was also seeking additional healing period benefits due to 
underpayment of the weekly rate.  However, I conclude that $507.95 is the correct 
weekly workers’ compensation rate; therefore, I further conclude that there was no 
underpayment of healing period benefits.  Claimant has failed to demonstrate 
entitlement to any additional healing period benefits.

Claimant is seeking reimbursement for the full amount of Dr. Bansal’s IME.  
Under Iowa law, an employer is only liable to reimburse an employee for the cost of 
an examination conducted pursuant to this subsection if the injury for which the 
employee is being examined is determined to be compensable.  Iowa Code § 
85.39(2) (2017)(emphasis added).  “An employer is not liable for the cost of such 
examination if the injury for which the employee is being examined is determined not to 
have a compensable injury.”  Iowa Code § 85.39(2); Tweeten v. Tweeten, 999 N.W.2d 
270, 282 (Iowa 2023).   
Dr. Bansal conducted an IME and rendered his opinions for two different body 
parts.  I conclude that only one of the two body parts was compensable as part of the 
May 9, 2022 work injury. Based on the above findings of fact, I conclude that only one 
half of the body parts he evaluated were for the claimant’s compensable left lower 
extremity injury.  Defendants voluntarily paid one half of the total amount of Dr. Bansal’s 
invoice.  (Def. Ex. E, p. 11) I conclude that claimant failed to demonstrate by a 
preponderance of the evidence that defendants should be liable for more than one-half 
of the IME invoice.  Claimant has failed to demonstrate entitlement to reimbursement for 
any additional expenses associated with Dr. Bansal’s IME. 
Claimant is seeking an assessment of costs.  Costs are to be assessed at the 
discretion of the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commissioner or the deputy hearing the 
case.  876 IAC 4.33.  I conclude that claimant was generally not successful in his case 
and therefore exercise my discretion to not assess any costs against the defendants.  
Each party shall bear their own costs.

THEREFORE, IT IS ORDERED: 
The correct weekly workers’ compensation rate in this case is five hundred seven

Claimant has failed to demonstrate entitlement to any additional weekly benefits. 
Claimant failed to demonstrate entitlement to reimbursement for any additional 
IME expense.
