STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A. The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Joann Rupe.  
 
The hearing for this alternate medical care claim was held on June 5, 2024.  
Claimant participated through her attorney John Lawyer. Defendants appeared through 
their attorney Bryan Brooks. The proceedings were digitally recorded. That recording 
constitutes the official record of this proceeding.  
 
Pursuant to the Commissioner’s July 21, 2023, Order, the undersigned has been 
delegated authority to issue a final agency decision in this alternate medical care 
proceeding. Therefore, this ruling is designated final agency action and any appeal of 
the decision would be to the Iowa District Court pursuant to Iowa Code section 17A.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
The undersigned having considered all the evidence in the record finds: 
 
Claimant sustained injuries to her neck, back, knee and leg while working for 
defendants on July 13, 2023.  (Petition; Answer).  Defendants accepted liability for the 
injuries and provided medical care.  (Answer; Hearing Testimony).  Sarah Brewer, D.O., 
at Davis County Hospital was designated as one of claimant’s authorized treating 
physicians. (Hearing Testimony).  On September 25, 2023, Dr. Brewer recommended 
claimant undergo an MRI of her lumbar spine.  (Id.).  However, as of the hearing date, 
that MRI still had not taken place. (Id.).  On April 25, 2024, claimant’s counsel sent 
defendants’ counsel an email.  (Ex. 1, p. 1).  That email indicates defendants’ third-party 
adjuster had previously authorized the MRI and attempted to schedule it at a facility in 
Dubuque, Iowa. (Id.).  Dubuque, however, is far from claimant’s home.  (Id.).  Claimant 
requested the MRI be scheduled at a facility near her residence. (Id.).  The adjuster 
apologized and agreed to schedule the test closer to claimant’s home. (Id.).  However, 
by April 25, 2024, the MRI had still not been scheduled. (Id.).  Claimant notified the 
defendants of her dissatisfaction with the care being offered. (Id.).   Claimant filed her 
alternate care petition on May 23, 2024. (See Petition).   
 
At the hearing, defendants’ counsel indicated that the recommended MRI had 
already been authorized by defendants and a pre-authorization form sent to Dr. 
Brewer’s office.  (Hearing Testimony).  However, Iowa Ortho, the facility that is going to 
perform the MRI, and Homelink, defendants’ MRI vendor, require a script from Dr. 
Brewer, as opposed to just a general order, before they will set a date for that MRI. (Id.).  
Defense counsel indicated their third-party adjuster had communicated this need to a 
pre-authorization specialist at Davis County Hospital, Ariadne Ramirez-Florez, several 
times, the last time occurring on May 28 or 29, 2024. (Id.).  Defendants’ counsel himself 
contacted the Davis County Hospital the morning of the hearing, requesting Dr. 
Brewer’s office fax a script to the carrier so the MRI can be scheduled. (Id.).  
Defendants still have not received the requested script from Dr. Brewer’s office. 
 
Claimant would like to proceed with the recommended MRI as soon as possible 
and has requested the agency order defendants to get it scheduled within the next (10) 
days.  
CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Under Iowa law, an employer who has accepted compensability for a workplace 
injury has a right to control the care provided to the injured employee.  Ramirez-Trujillo

Page 3 
 
v. Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 N.W.2d 759, 769 (Iowa 2016).  The relevant statute provides 
as follows: 
For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish 
reasonable services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has the 
right to choose the care. . . . The treatment must be offered promptly and 
be reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the 
employee. If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the care 
offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such dissatisfaction 
to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the employer and 
the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited to treat the 
injury. If the employer and employee cannot agree on such alternate care, 
the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable proofs of the 
necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
Iowa Code § 85.27(4).   
Defendants’ “obligation under the statute is confined to reasonable care for the 
diagnosis and treatment of work-related injuries.” Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 
N.W.2d 122, 124 (Iowa 1995) (emphasis in original). In other words, the “obligation 
under the statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.”  Id.  An 
application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because claimant is 
dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving. Mere dissatisfaction with the medical 
care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical care. Rather, 
the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not reasonably 
suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the claimant. See 
Iowa Code § 85.27(4). By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment and seeking 
alternate care, claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is 
unreasonable. See Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e); Long, 528 N.W.2d at 124.    
An employee’s desire for a different “reasonable” treatment plan does not make 
the employer-authorized care unreasonable.  See Long, 528 at 124.  A finding that the 
treatment requested by the claimant is reasonable does not result in an implicit finding 
that the authorized treatment is unreasonable. Id.  The employee must prove the care 
being offered by the employer is unreasonable to treat the work injury, not that another 
treatment plan is reasonable. Id.; See also Lynch Livestock, Inc. v. Bursell, 870 N.W.2d 
274 (Table) (Iowa Ct. App. 2015).  Determining whether care is reasonable under the 
statute is a question of fact.  Long, 528 N.W.2d at 123.  
 
Under Iowa Code section 85.27, claimant bears the burden of providing 
“reasonable proofs of the necessity” to order alternate care.  Defendants have already 
authorized the MRI recommended by Dr. Brewer. Because defendants have already 
authorized the care claimant seeks, the issue of authorization is moot and need not be 
ruled upon.  However, under the statute, a claimant is entitled to care that is both 
reasonable and prompt.  Defendants agreed to authorize the MRI scan several months 
ago, and claimant sill does not have an appointment date for the procedure.  That is not 
prompt.  While defendants cannot control the scheduling requirements of Iowa Ortho or

Homelink, they can assist claimant with the process and use their resources to make 
sure she obtains a date certain for the MRI scan to take place.  Therefore, within the 
next ten days, defendants must provide claimant with an appointment date for the MRI 
at Iowa Ortho or contact another provider of their choosing to perform the MRI promptly.   
 
THEREFORE, IT IS ORDERED: 
 
Claimant's petition for alternate medical care is denied in part and granted in part.   
 
Claimant’s request for authorization for the lumbar MRI is denied as moot.  
However, defendants are ordered to provide claimant an appointment date for the MRI 
at Iowa Ortho within the next ten (10) days or contact another provider of their choosing 
to perform the MRI promptly.
