STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Marguriette Irvin, claimant, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from Tyson Fresh Meats, Inc., employer and self-insured 
defendant.  The hearing was held on January 31, 2025. Pursuant to an order from the 
Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commissioner, this case was heard via videoconference 
using Zoom with all parties and the court reporter appearing remotely.   
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  Those 
stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration decision and 
no factual issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised or discussed in this 
decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.  
Marguriette Irvin was the only witness to testify at the trial.  The evidentiary 
record also includes Joint Exhibits 1-8, Claimant’s 3rd Amended Exhibits 1-17; and 
Defendants’ 2nd Amended Exhibits A-N.  All exhibits were received into the record.   
The parties submitted post-hearing briefs on March 17, 2025, at which time the 
case was fully submitted to the undersigned.  
ISSUES 
The parties identified the following disputed issues on the hearing report: 
 
1. Whether claimant sustained multiple cumulative injuries that arose out of and 
in the course of her employment with Tyson on April 6, 2023.  
 
2. Whether the case is untimely under Iowa Code section 85.26. 
 
3. Whether the claimant is entitled to temporary disability or healing period 
benefits because of the alleged injury.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
The undersigned, having considered all the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds as follows: 
At the time of the hearing the claimant, Marguriette Irvin (hereinafter “Irvin”), was 
61 years old and living in Waterloo. (Hearing Transcript, p. 17).  She graduated from 
East High School in 1982. (Id.).  During school, Irvin worked part-time for the Boys & 
Girls Club. (Id. at 19).  After graduation, she worked at a chicken plant for a summer. 
(Id. at 18).  
Tyson hired Irvin in May of 1990. (Tr., p. 18).  Irvin started on the ham line.  (Id. 
at 20).  She worked a knife job on the line for three and a half years before being 
transferred to a position as a membrane skinner. (Id.).  She worked as a membrane 
skinner for approximately 14 years. (Id.).  At the hearing, Irvin described both of these 
jobs as very physically demanding, requiring looking down, standing, twisting, as well as 
repetitive throwing, pushing and pulling meat on a conveyor belt at a fast pace and 
using a foot pedal. (Id. at 22-29).  In 2008, Irvin transferred to the box shop. (Id. at 20).  
She worked in the box shop for almost 16 years. (Id. at 21).  There were three positions 
in the box shop—box maker, tape operator, and utility. (Id. at 40).  Irvin was a utility 
worker. (Id. at 39-41).  At the hearing, she described the utility position as requiring her 
to take stacks of boxes off a pallet, line them up, push them down a hallway and put 
them into a chute. (Id. at 42-45).  Irvin testified she did this with thousands of boxes a 
day. (Id. at 45-46).   
Defendants submitted job analysis summaries for two different positions at 
Tysons—the Trayformer Operator and a Box Maker/Handler. (See Exhibits G and H).  
According to these summaries, a Trayformer Operator produces case seal boxes and/or 
piece boxes, by pushing skids of cardboard from a roller to a trayformer feed, then 
feeding the boxes into the machine, stacking up the completed boxes after they go 
through the machine, and then moving stacks of boxes to their assigned areas in the 
Box Shop. (Ex. G, p. 29).  A Trayformer Operator might also have to apply labels to the

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 
employment.  Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 150 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. 
Dial Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309, 311 (Iowa 1996).  The words “arising out of” refer to the 
cause or source of the injury.  The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 
circumstances of the injury.  2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124, 128 (Iowa 
1995).  An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists 
between the injury and the employment.  Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311.  The injury must 
be a rational consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely 
incidental to the employment.  Koehler Electric v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1, 3 (Iowa 2000); 
Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311.  An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it 
happens within a period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may 
be when performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties 
or doing an activity incidental to them.  Ciha, 552 N.W.2d at 150. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony.  The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question.  The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances.  The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410, 420 
(Iowa 2001); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995).  Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994).  Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected.  Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
When an expert's opinion is based upon an incomplete or incorrect history, it is 
not necessarily binding on the commissioner or the court. It is then to be weighed, 
together with other facts and circumstances, the ultimate conclusion being for the finder 
of the fact.  Musselman v. Central Telephone Company, 154 N.W.2d 128, 133 (Iowa

1967); Bodish v. Fischer, Inc., 257 Iowa 516, 522, 133 N.W.2d 867, 870 (1965).  The 
commissioner as trier of fact has the duty to determine the credibility of the witnesses 
and to weigh the evidence, together with the other disclosed facts and circumstances, 
and then to accept or reject the opinion.  Dunlavey, 526 N.W.2d at 853. 
While a claimant is not entitled to compensation for the results of a preexisting 
injury or disease, its mere existence at the time of a subsequent injury is not a defense. 
Rose v. John Deere Ottumwa Works, 247 Iowa 900, 908, 76 N.W.2d 756, 760-61 
(1956). If the claimant had a preexisting condition or disability that is materially 
aggravated, accelerated, worsened or lighted up so that it results in disability, claimant 
is entitled to recover. Nicks v. Davenport Produce Co., 254 Iowa 130, 135, 115 N.W.2d 
812, 815 (1962); Yeager v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co., 253 Iowa 369, 374-75, 112 
N.W.2d 299, 302 (1961). 
Based on the above findings of fact, I conclude that Irvin failed to meet her 
burden of proof to show she sustained cumulative injuries to her neck, back and left 
shoulder that arose out of and in the course of her employment with Tyson on April 6, 
2023.  Irvin relied upon the opinions of Dr. Manshadi to causally relate her alleged neck, 
back and left shoulder conditions to her employment at Tyson.  However, as explained 
above, those opinions are entitled to very light weight as they appear to be conclusory 
without an adequate or complete understanding of Irvin’s job duties at Tyson.  Irvin is 
alleging cumulative injuries to three separate body parts.  However, the medical 
evidence shows that she was diagnosed with congenital and degenerative conditions in 
her lumbar and cervical spine and did not receive any real diagnosis for her left 
shoulder until November 2024, over a year after she stopped working at Tyson.  Dr. 
Manshadi’s reports do not contain any explanation of how exactly Irvin’s work duties at 
Tyson caused or permanently aggravated all three of these body parts.  There is 
insufficient evidence that Irvin’s alleged conditions are causally related to her job at 
Tyson.  Claimant shall take nothing from these proceedings.  
Irvin is seeking reimbursement for several IMEs with Dr. Manshadi. (See Hearing 
Report).  A claimant’s right to reimbursement for an IME exam is controlled by Iowa 
Code section 85.39. This section permits an employee to be reimbursed for a 
subsequent examination by a physician of the employee’s choice where an employer-
retained physician has previously evaluated “permanent disability” and the employee 
believes that the initial evaluation is too low. Iowa Code section 85.39(2)(2019).  
However, this right to reimbursement is also dependent upon the claimant’s success at 
hearing. Iowa Code section 85.39(2), states, 
An employer is only liable to reimburse an employee for the cost of an 
examination conducted pursuant to this subsection if the injury for which the 
employee is being examined is determined to be compensable under this 
chapter or chapter 85A or 85B. An employer is not liable for the cost of such 
an examination if the injury for which the employee is being examined is 
determined not to be a compensable injury.  
Id.
