This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A. The 
expedited procedures of rule 876 IAC 4.48, the “alternate medical care” rule, are 
invoked by claimant, Hilda Muniz.  
This alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on October 20, 2025. The 
proceedings were recorded digitally and constitute the official record of the hearing.  By 
an order filed by the Workers’ Compensation Commissioner, this decision is designated 
final agency action. Any appeal would be by petition for judicial review under Iowa Code 
section 17A.15(1).  
The record in this case consists of Claimant’s Exhibit 1 and Defendants’ Exhibits 
A-B. 
ISSUE 
The issue presented for resolution in this case is whether claimant is entitled to 
alternate medical care consisting of authorization and scheduling of an evaluation with 
Baylor Scott & White-Hillcrest Neurology 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
Defendants accept liability for an injury occurring on May 21, 2024. 
On September 22, 2025, claimant was seen in the emergency department of 
Baylor Scott & White (BSW) Medical Center in Waco, Texas. Claimant was evaluated 
by Andrew Fehler, PA-C.  Claimant had complaints of worsening back pain.  Claimant

had intermittent spasms from a motor vehicle accident in 2023 and a work incident in 
2024.  (Exhibit 1, page 6) Claimant was referred to neurosurgery at BSW medical center 
(Ex. 1, pp. 3-4, 7) 
In a letter, written by claimant’s attorney, Alyson Brzezinski, FNP-C, checked a 
box marked “yes” to the sentence “You recommend Ms. Muniz be evaluated by Baylor 
Scott & White Medical Center-Hillcrest’s Neurosurgery Department for her May 21,

also indicated that if the emergency department felt a neurosurgery evaluation was 
necessary, then claimant should follow up as suggested (Ex. 1, p 2) Nurse Practitioner 
Brzezinski is an authorized health care provider for claimant. 
In an October 1, 2025, email, claimant’s attorney requested defendants authorize 
and schedule claimant for an evaluation with neurosurgery with BSW. (Ex. 1, p.1) 
On October 7, 2025, claimant was evaluated by Nurse Practitioner Brzezinski for 
lower back pain.  Claimant had a transforaminal epidural steroid injection (TFESI) on 
the right S1 level two weeks prior with “100%” relief of pain down the posterior leg.  Pain 
was now worse down the lateral aspect of the right leg. A medial branch block (MBB) 
was discussed as a treatment option.  (Ex. A) 
In an October 16, 2025, email, Susan Stewart, RN, indicated she was at the 
October 7, 2025, appointment with Nurse Practitioner Brzezinski.  Nurse Stewart is the 
nurse case manager for claimant’s claim.  Nurse Stewart indicated Nurse Practitioner 
Brzezinski discussed the possibility of sending claimant to an orthopedic spine specialist 
and not a neurosurgeon, if appropriate.  Nurse Practitioner Brzezinski did not want to 
send claimant to an orthopedic or neurosurgeon specialist until claimant completed 
conservative treatment.  (Ex. B) 
CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance, and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law. The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services. The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury. Section 85.27. Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening, October 16, 1975).  
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable. See Iowa 
Rule of Appellate Procedure 6.904(3)(e); Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 
(Iowa 1995). Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of 
fact. Id. The employer’s obligation turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not 
desirability. Id.; Harned v. Farmland Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983). In Pirelli-

Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 433 (Iowa 1997), the court approvingly 
quoted Bowles v. Los Lunas Schools, 109 N.M. 100, 781 P.2d 1178 (App. 1989):  
[T]he words “reasonable” and “adequate” appear to describe the same 
standard.  
[The New Mexico rule] requires the employer to provide a certain standard 
of care and excuses the employer from any obligation to provide other 
services only if that standard is met. We construe the terms "reasonable” 
and “adequate” as describing care that is both appropriate to the injury 
and sufficient to bring the worker to maximum recovery.  
In an October 1, 2025, letter, written by claimant’s attorney, Nurse Practitioner 
Brzezinski marked a box “yes” to the sentence that she recommended claimant be 
evaluated in the neurosurgery department at BSW.  When Nurse Practitioner Brzezinski 
examined claimant on October 7, 2025, she noted that a MBB was discussed as a 
treatment option.  There is nothing in the treatment section of the October 7, 2025, 
exam that Nurse Practitioner Brzezinski again recommended claimant be seen in the 
neurosurgery department at BSW.  Neither claimant nor Nurse Practitioner Brzezinski 
testified at hearing.  The only evidence in the record that further explains the exam of 
October 7, 2025, is the email from Nurse Stewart indicating Nurse Practitioner 
Brzezinski did not want to send claimant to an orthopedic or neurosurgeon specialist 
until claimant completed conservative treatment.  (Ex. B) 
I appreciate claimant’s position in this case.  Nurse Practitioner Brzezinski did 
sign a letter indicating she recommended claimant be evaluated in the neurosurgery 
department at BSW.  However, the most recent exam by Nurse Practitioner Brzezinski 
does not recommend a referral to neurosurgery at BSW.  The only other evidence in the 
record regarding the October 7, 2025 exam indicates Nurse Practitioner Brzezinski did 
not want to send claimant to an orthopedic or neurosurgeon specialist until claimant 
completed conservative treatment. 
Because the record suggests that, currently, Nurse Practitioner Brzezinski does 
not recommend sending claimant to an orthopedic or neurosurgeon specialist, and 
because defendants continue to authorize conservative care for claimant, claimant has 
failed to carry her burden of proof the care provided by defendants, at this time, is 
unreasonable. 
ORDER  
THEREFORE, IT IS ORDERED:  
That claimant’s petition for alternate medical care is denied.
