STATEMENT OF THE CASE   
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A. The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, William 
Black. Claimant appeared telephonically and through his attorney, Luke Guthrie. 
Defendants appeared through Attorney Sara Smalley. 
Claimant’s application for alternate medical care was filed on December 26, 
2025. Defendants filed an answer on January 2, 2026. The claim came on for hearing 
on January 7, 2026. The proceedings were digitally recorded. That recording constitutes 
the official record of this proceeding. Pursuant to the Interim Commissioner’s December 
12, 2025 Order, the undersigned has been delegated authority to issue a final agency 
decision in this alternate medical care proceeding. Therefore, this ruling is designated 
final agency action and any appeal of the decision would be to the Iowa District Court 
pursuant to Iowa Code section 17A. 
The record consists of claimant’s exhibits 1 through 3, consisting of 10 pages; 
defendants’ exhibits A through C, consisting of 5 pages (attached to defendants’ brief), 
and claimant’s sworn testimony. Both attorneys also filed short briefs, and presented 
arguments regarding their clients’ positions.

ISSUE 
The issue presented for resolution is whether the claimant is entitled to alternate 
medical care consisting of authorization for a left wrist evaluation with Clifford 
Christopher Novak, M.D. 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned having considered all of the testimony and evidence in the 
record finds: 
Claimant sustained an injury, arising out of and in the course of his employment,  
involving his left upper extremity on July 11, 2024. Defendants accepted liability for the 
injury and directed care. Defendants authorized Dr. Novak as the treating physician. 
Claimant had a prior right upper extremity injury, on February 29, 2024, for which he 
had received treatment and was awaiting surgery. (See Defendants’ Brief, p. 1) On 
August 29, 2024, he saw Erin Kennedy, M.D., with complaints related to his left wrist. 
(Defendant’s Exhibit A, p. 1) He told Dr. Kennedy it felt similar to the right wrist, which 
was being scheduled for surgery. After evaluation, Dr. Kennedy provided restrictions 
and ordered physical therapy. (Def. Ex. A, p. 2) She noted she might consider an MRI if 
claimant did not make progress with therapy. 
Claimant testified that he had some physical therapy for the left wrist, but nothing 
substantial because he was waiting on surgery for his right wrist. He eventually had an 
MRI of the left wrist, and saw a nurse practitioner at Dr. Kennedy’s office for follow up 
on January 29, 2025. (Def. Ex. C, p. 1) At that visit he expressed frustration at the entire 
process, but also noted that his left wrist was feeling a little better with respect to pain. 
He noted that perhaps being off work due to his restrictions had helped. He noted that 
his wrist still was not “like a regular wrist,” and he was barely lifting or doing anything 
with it due to his restrictions. At that time, he had tentatively scheduled surgery for his 
right wrist with Dr. Novak in February 2025, but was waiting on approval from the 
insurance adjustor. 
Claimant saw Dr. Novak for his left wrist on March 24, 2025. (Claimant’s Exhibit 
1, p. 1) At that time, Dr. Novak noted claimant’s right wrist surgery for TFC tear and 
ulnar dislocation of the ECU tendon had been scheduled for May. With respect to his 
left wrist, Dr. Novak reviewed the MRI, and indicated it showed a tear of the inner two-
thirds of the TFC along the ulna, with no evidence of a full-thickness communicating 
tear with PCL triquetral degenerative changes and no overt widening of the SL interval, 
but a possible intermediate grade sprain and possible proximal carpal row instability.  
After examination of the left wrist, Dr. Novak noted that claimant seemed to have 
a painful TFC tear. (Cl. Ex. 1, pp. 2-3) He stated options for the issue included 
“observation versus immobilization versus injections versus therapy versus surgery.” 
(Cl. Ex. 1, p. 3) He noted claimant had right wrist surgery scheduled for May, so they 
would try physical therapy for the left wrist for range of motion and strength modalities to 
see if that would alleviate his symptoms. Dr. Novak issued a return to work status on

Claimant’s attorney replied, including medical records from Dr. Kennedy from a

Claimant’s attorney asked if defendants would proceed with surgical intervention for the

REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Defendants first argued that claimant did not provide proper communication 
regarding the basis of his dissatisfaction with care. Iowa Code section 85.27(4) 
provides, in relevant part: 
For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish 
reasonable services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has 
the right to choose the care. . . .  The treatment must be offered promptly 
and be reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience 
to the employee. If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the 
care offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such 
dissatisfaction to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the 
employer and the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited 
to treat the injury. If the employer and employee cannot agree on such 
alternate care, the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable 
proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
Iowa Code § 85.27(4). 
In this case, claimant’s attorney and defense counsel exchanged several emails 
over December 8-9, 2025, in which claimant’s attorney made it clear claimant did not 
believe he was at MMI for the left wrist, and felt he needed additional treatment. (Cl. Ex. 
3) Claimant’s attorney stated he would file an alternate care petition if defendants were 
planning to ask for an impairment rating of both the left and right wrists. Defendants’ 
response was to deny any additional treatment for the left wrist, stating claimant had 
reached MMI. The emails from claimant’s attorney clearly explain that claimant was 
dissatisfied with the lack of care being offered for his left wrist, on the basis that he did 
not believe he had reached MMI for the left wrist and felt additional evaluation was 
needed. As such, I find claimant has met his burden to prove he communicated his 
dissatisfaction with the care (or lack of care) offered, and the basis for his 
dissatisfaction. 
Defendants further argue that claimant has reached MMI for the left wrist, per Dr. 
Novak’s December 1, 2025 records, which indicates no further medical intervention is 
needed. Defendants also argue that claimant has not attempted any conservative 
measures to treat the left wrist since his March 24, 2025 visit with Dr. Novak.

Defendants argue that claimant’s “delay” in seeking treatment until after he was placed 
at MMI raises questions as to whether the treatment is necessary, and that the 
treatment provided for the left wrist to date has been reasonable and appropriate. 
Defendants’ “obligation under the statute is confined to reasonable care for the 
diagnosis and treatment of work-related injuries.” Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 
N.W.2d 122, 124 (Iowa 1995) (emphasis in original). In other words, the “obligation 
under the statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.” Id. 
Similarly, an application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained 
because claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving. Mere 
dissatisfaction with the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for 
alternate medical care. Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered 
promptly, was not reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly 
inconvenient for the claimant. See Iowa Code § 85.27(4). Thus, by challenging the 
employer’s choice of treatment and seeking alternate care, claimant assumes the 
burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable. See Iowa R. App. P 14(f)(5); 
Long, 528 N.W.2d at 124.   
Claimant argues that a finding of MMI without a medical evaluation is not a valid 
medical conclusion, and should not support the termination of care. I agree. 
Additionally, based on Dr. Novak’s December 1, 2025 records, I cannot find that he 
actually placed claimant at MMI for the left wrist. The record of the visit does not 
mention the left wrist at all. Not only was there no examination of the left wrist, the 
record specifically lists the chief complaint as the right wrist, and the assessment only 
discusses the diagnosis for the right wrist. (Cl. Ex. 2) Additionally, the patient status 
report lists the diagnosis as “right wrist,” and while it states that claimant can return to 
work with full use of both hands, it does not specifically state that claimant is at MMI for 
both hands.  
There is no indication that defendants followed up with Dr. Novak for clarification 
after claimant’s attorney presented his concerns regarding the left wrist. There is also 
nothing in the record indicating that Dr. Novak has stated that all treatment options have 
been exhausted or that no additional care for the left wrist is warranted. The last 
medical record specifically related to claimant’s left wrist contained several potential 
treatment options, none of which have been completed. As such, I cannot find that 
claimant has reached MMI with respect to the left wrist. 
That being said, even if claimant had reached MMI with respect to the left wrist, 
MMI alone does not relieve defendants of the responsibility to provide continuing 
necessary care causally related to the original injury. This is an accepted claim, and 
claimant continues to have symptoms in his left wrist and desires treatment. Dr. Novak 
provided treatment recommendations in March of 2025 that have not been completed. 
Defendants attempt to blame the lack of treatment for the left wrist on claimant, but that 
argument is disingenuous at best. Claimant’s understanding was that the medical 
professionals wanted to complete treatment for his right wrist injury first, and then

change focus to his left wrist. As such, it makes sense that he would not actively seek 
additional treatment for the left wrist until the treatment for his right wrist had concluded. 
Ultimately, determining whether care is reasonable under the statute is a 
question of fact. Long, 528 N.W.2d at 123. “[W]hen evidence is presented to the 
commissioner that the employer-authorized medical care has not been effective and 
that such care is ‘inferior or less extensive’ than other available care requested by the 
employee, Long, 528 N.W.2d at 124, the commissioner is justified by section 85.27 to 
order the alternate care.” Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d at 437. Further, Iowa Code section 
85.27(4) requires that employer-controlled care must be “offered promptly.” Offering no 
care is the same as offering no care reasonably suited to treat the injury. Pirelli-
Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 433, 436 (Iowa 1997).  
In this case, defendants’ failure to authorize claimant to return to Dr. Novak is a 
failure to provide reasonable and necessary care for the left wrist injury. As such, 
claimant is entitled to alternate care. The petition is granted. 
ORDER 
THEREFORE IT IS ORDERED: 
The claimant's petition for alternate medical care is granted. 
Defendants shall immediately authorize and pay for claimant to return to Dr. 
Novak for evaluation and treatment of his left wrist injury.
