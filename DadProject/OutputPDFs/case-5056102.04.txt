STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A.  The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Sherrie Erlbacher.  
Claimant appeared through attorney, Emily Anderson.  Defendants appeared through 
their attorney, Ed Rose. 
 
The alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on April 4, 2024.  The 
proceedings were digitally recorded on the agency’s recording software, Quality 
Management Suite.  That recording constitutes the official record of this proceeding.  
Pursuant to the Commissioner’s Order, the undersigned has been delegated authority 
to issue a final agency decision in this alternate medical care proceeding.  Therefore, 
this ruling is designated final agency action, and any appeal of the decision would be to 
the Iowa District Court pursuant to Iowa Code section 17A. 
 
The record consists of Claimant’s Exhibit 1, which was received without 
objection.  The defendants do not dispute liability for claimant’s February 1, 2014, work 
injury.  In addition, administrative notice was taken of claimant’s previous alternate care 
filings.   
 
ISSUE 
 
The issue presented for resolution is whether the claimant is entitled to medicine 
to treat her migraines.  At hearing, claimant raised an additional issue of sanctions 
under 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.36.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
The claimant sustained an injury which arose out of and in the course of her 
employment on February 1, 2014.  As a result of this work injury, Ms. Erlbacher has had 
continued treatment for migraine headaches.  Her underlying workers’ compensation 
claim was settled in April 2019 (File No. 5056102).  The parties agree her medical was 
left “open” and she is entitled to ongoing care for her conditions sustained in the injury. 
Ms. Erlbacher has filed three previous alternate medical care petitions.  In 
November 2020, she filed an application seeking authorization for a pharmacy which 
would fill her prescriptions.  Claimant alleged that over $25,000.00 worth of 
prescriptions had not been paid at that time.  (File No. 5056102.01)  On December 8, 
2020, I granted this application, stating “Defendants shall promptly authorize claimant’s 
medications prescribed by her authorized physicians, …”  (Alternate Care Decision, File 
No. 5056102.01, December 8, 2020)   
On April 21, 2021, claimant filed a second application, again asking for a 
pharmacy to be authorized.  In this application, claimant alleged that the outstanding 
balance on her account was interfering with her ability to fill new prescriptions.  (File No. 
5056102.02)  Defendants agreed to a consent order.  “Defendants REM Iowa and New 
Hampshire Insurance Company shall authorize the payment of all outstanding balances 
with IWP and will continue to authorize IWP unless and until a new pharmacy is 
authorized.”  (Alternate Care Consent Order, File No. 5056102.02, April 15, 2021) 
On July 25, 2023, claimant filed a third application, again asking for a pharmacy 
to be authorized.  (File No. 5056102.03)  Just prior to hearing, claimant dismissed her 
application. 
This is claimant’s fourth application for alternate care.  In her petition, she 
requests approval “of medications as prescribed by the authorized treating physicians.”  
On October 30, 2023, claimant’s counsel wrote to defense counsel: 
1. The carrier authorized Quick RX in Harlan.  That pharmacy is closing its 
doors.  Sherry [sic] would like to go to Pexton Pharmacy in Harlan.  Can the 
carrier authorize? 
2. Dr. Whyte writes the prescription for 9 refills.  Can the comp carrier just 
authorize the number of refills prescribed by the doc?  She needs to pick it up 
today and unfortunately the pharmacy says comp has not authorized it.  We 
need to find away [sic] to avoid this every month. 
(Claimant’s Exhibit 1, page 4) 
Defense counsel responded promptly stating he would convey claimant’s 
concerns to the adjustor.  (Cl. Ex. 1, p. 3)  On November 10, 2023, and December 4, 
2023, claimant’s counsel followed up.  (Cl. Ex. 1, p. 3)  Claimant’s counsel again

REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Iowa Code Section 85.27 (2013). 
 
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See 
Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  Determining what care is 
reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.  Id.  The employer’s obligation turns 
on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.  Id.; Harned v. Farmland 
Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983).   
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995). 
An employer’s statutory right is to select the providers of care and the employer 
may consider cost and other pertinent factors when exercising its choice.  Long, at 124. 
An employer (typically) is not a licensed health care provider and does not possess 
medical expertise.  Accordingly, an employer does not have the right to control the 
methods the providers choose to evaluate, diagnose and treat the injured employee. An 
employer is not entitled to control a licensed health care provider’s exercise of 
professional judgment.  Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 866389 (Declaratory 
Ruling, May 18, 1988).  An employer’s failure to follow recommendations of an 
authorized physician in matters of treatment is commonly a failure to provide reasonable 
treatment.  Boggs v. Cargill, Inc., File No. 1050396 (Alt. Care Dec. January 31, 1994). 
This agency has the authority to order sanctions against a party who fails to 
comply with an order of the agency.

876 Iowa Administrative Code section 4.36.  One of the purposes of Rule 4.36 is to 
ensure that the workers’ compensation system is self-effectuating.  Injured workers 
should not have to retain an attorney to obtain benefits to which they are entitled. 
I find it is unreasonable for the defendants to cause any further delay to Ms. 
Erlbacher receiving her authorized medications.  No representative for the defendants 
(other than defense counsel) appeared to explain why timely, prompt authorization for 
this undisputed medicine has not been made.  It is apparent to me that defense counsel 
has attempted in good faith to resolve the problems in these delays to no avail.  
Defense counsel apparently does not have the authority to call Pexton Pharmacy to 
authorize the expense himself. 
I find that the claimant has proven that the delays in authorization of her 
prescriptions are unreasonable.  By order of this agency, the current pending 
prescriptions by Dr. Whyte are hereby AUTHORIZED.  To be clear, Pexton Pharmacy 
need not wait for an adjustor or company representative to call them to confirm that the 
prescriptions are authorized.  Defendants are ordered to authorize and pay for these 
prescriptions, and this should happen immediately. 
 
Given the procedural history of this claim, I am tempted to order sanctions under 
Rule 4.36.  At this time, I choose not to do so for a couple of reasons.  The primary 
reason is that the previous orders in this case are now somewhat remote, with the last 
order of this agency being approximately three years ago.  I find it is fair and just to 
provide the defendants with a final opportunity to correct this ongoing problem.  The 
defendants, however, are ordered to immediately authorize all causally connected 
medications prescribed by the authorized physician(s) including Dr. Whyte with Pexton 
Pharmacy.  In essence, the defendants are ordered to find a way to promptly authorize 
claimant’s headache medicine so that this type of delay never happens again.   
If claimant is required to file another application for alternate care, however, I will 
expect a representative of the defendants who has authority to authorize the needed 
medications to appear at the hearing to explain any further alleged delay.  If I find this 
order has been violated, Rule 4.36 sanctions shall be applied for reasonable attorney 
fees or other reasonable costs the claimant may incur in order to obtain her needed 
authorized medications.
