STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, John McCoy, has filed a petition for arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits against Menard, Inc., employer, and XL Insurance America, 
insurance carrier, both as defendants.  
In accordance with agency scheduling procedures and pursuant to the Order of 
the Commissioner in the matter of the Coronavirus/COVID-19 Impact on Hearings, the 
hearing was held on August 20, 2020, via Court Call. The case was considered fully 
submitted on September 10, 2020, upon the simultaneous filing of briefs.  
The record consists of Joint Exhibits 1-5, Claimant’s Exhibits 1-8, and 
Defendants’ Third Amended Exhibits A-F along with the testimony of the claimant and 
Jeff Thompson.  
ISSUES 
1. The extent of claimant’s permanent disability;  
2. Whether claimant is limited to a functional capacity evaluation (FCE) or 
industrial impairment under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v);  
3. Whether claimant is entitled to penalty benefits for delay of payment of 
permanent partial disability (PPD) at the appropriate weekly rate and/or 
underpayment of PPD;

STIPULATIONS 
The parties agree claimant sustained an injury which arose out of and in the 
course of his employment on July 13, 2018. The injury was the cause of a temporary 
and permanent disability but parties disagree as to the extent and the nature of the 
disability. The parties agree that at all times relevant hereto claimant was married and 
entitled to two exemptions. He was paid a gross weekly wage of $309.00 per week at 
the time of his injury. Thus, his weekly benefit rate is $223.54. 
At hearing defendants stipulated that claimant was entitled to reimbursement of 
the 85.39 examination and the medical bills itemized in Exhibit 7.  
Prior to the hearing the claimant was paid 59.887 weeks of PPD at the rate of 
$223.54 per week and the defendants are entitled to a credit of that amount against any 
award of PPD herein.  
The commencement date for PPD benefits is January 7, 2019.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The parties have stipulated claimant has sustained an injury to his low back and 
to his right upper extremity. Defendants argue that since claimant has returned to work, 
he is limited to a functional capacity impairment while claimant asserts that he is earning 
less today than he was at the time of the injury.  
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence. Iowa R. App. P. 6.14(6). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based. A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause. A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible. George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996). 
Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v) states that “If an employee who is eligible for 
compensation under this paragraph returns to work or is offered work for which the 
employee receives or would receive the same or greater salary, wages, or earnings 
than the employee received at the time of the injury, the employee shall be 
compensated based only upon the employee’s functional impairment resulting from the 
injury, and not in relation to the employee’s earning capacity.”  
The wage records show that claimant was earning $12.20 per hour prior to his 
injury and that he was paid the same wage post the injury. Claimant also testified, 
although it is not reflected in his wage records, that he was paid an additional $2.00 per 
hour this summer due to the increased risk from the pandemic. He testified that he often 
leaves early and the argument that claimant appears to be making is that because of 
the lower number of hours he works post the injury, he is earning less than he was prior 
to the injury.  
Per the wage records, claimant worked an average of fifteen plus hours per 
week. This steady work schedule dropped off slightly post injury. In 2020, there were 
some weeks in which claimant only worked 3.77 hours or 3.25 hours per week. Thus, 
his overall post-injury “earnings” do appear to have decreased from his pre-injury status. 
However, his hourly wage has not changed.

Because Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v) is a new law that went into effect July 1, 
2017, there is little guidance from the appellate court as to how the “salary, wages, or 
earnings” should be interpreted. “It is a well-known rule of statutory construction that the 
courts will construe disjunctive words as conjunctive, and vice versa, and will disregard 
technical rules of grammar and punctuation, when necessary to arrive at the intent of 
the legislative body.” 
In re Det. of Altman, 723 N.W.2d 181, 187 (Iowa 2006) the apparent intent of the 
legislature in this section is to limit the recovery of the claimant if the injured worker has 
returned and his earnings have not diminished. Given that his salary and wages have 
not been diminished, the spirit of the statutory provision seems to provide that if any one 
of the itemized factors are met, the injured workers’ disability should be measured by 
the functional impairment.  
According to Iowa Code section 85.34(2), functional impairment is “the extent of 
loss or percentage of permanent impairment can be determined by use of the guides to 
the evaluation of permanent impairment, published by the American medical 
association, as adopted by the workers’ compensation commissioner by rule pursuant 
to chapter 17A.”  
Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(x) (2017) further provides:  
[W]hen determining functional disability and not loss of earning capacity, 
the extent of loss or percentage of permanent impairment shall be 
determined solely by utilizing the guides to the evaluation of permanent 
impairment, published by the American Medical Association, as adopted 
by the workers’ compensation commissioner by rule pursuant to chapter 
17A.  
Dr. Folkers, claimant’s surgeon, assigned a 15% upper extremity impairment 
rating for the hand and wrist, including 12% for the right hand. Dr. Gainer, a hand 
specialist, assigned an 18% impairment to the right hand. Dr. Bansal’s ratings were 
15% for the whole person, including 18% of the right upper extremity. Dr. Smith 
assigned a 5% whole person impairment due to muscular guarding spasms and 
reduced range of motion for the back. Dr. Mooney also assigned a 5% impairment 
rating for the back.  
Claimant testified that he is doing most of the work he did prior to the injury but 
has reduced grip strength, pain, and limited ability to walk, stoop, and bend. He does 
not do chores at home and asks for help from his coworkers.  
Based on the foregoing, weighing all of the experts’ opinions, it is determined 
claimant has sustained a 14% functional impairment to the whole body.  
Claimant also seeks a penalty award for the delay in payment of PPD at the 
appropriate weekly rate.  Iowa Code section 86.13(4) requires that if a delay in 
commencement or termination of benefits occurs without reasonable or probable cause 
or excuse, the workers’ compensation commissioner shall award additional weekly

benefits in an amount not to exceed 50 percent of the amount of benefits that were 
unreasonably delayed or denied.  Iowa Code section 85.13(4)(b).  A reasonable or 
probable cause or excuse must satisfy the following requirements: 
(1) 
The excuse was preceded by a reasonable investigation and evaluation 
by the employer or insurance carrier into whether benefits were owed to the 
employee; 
(2) 
The results of the reasonable investigation and evaluation were the actual 
basis upon which the employer or insurance carrier contemporaneously relied to 
deny, delay payment of, or terminate benefits; 
(3) 
The employer or insurance carrier contemporaneously conveyed the basis 
of the denial, delay in payment, or termination of benefits to the employee at the 
time of the denial, delay or termination of benefits. 
(Iowa Code section 86.13(4)(c)) 
The threshold factor is that there was a delay or termination of benefits. Claimant 
argues that defendants should pay a penalty for both erroneously underpaying the 
weekly benefit rate as well as failure to pay sufficient PPD benefits.  
The indemnity payment record in Exhibit C shows that claimant was paid 
$160.69 weekly in 2018 and then, starting August 5, 2019, he was paid a weekly benefit 
rate of $223.54. The parties have stipulated that the appropriate weekly benefit rate was 
$223.54. Based on the payment record and the stipulation, claimant was underpaid for 
the benefits issued during the time period from July 31, 2018, through August 5, 2019. 
(Ex. C:17-18)  
It is the defendants’ burden to provide the reasonable or probable cause of the 
lack of proper payment. Defendants argue the payment of $160.69 was appropriate 
because those payments were for temporary disability, and temporary disability is either 
35% of the statewide average or spendable weekly earnings, whichever is less. (Iowa 
Code section 85.37(1).  
However, even the defendants acknowledge that 6 weeks and 3 days of PPD 
was paid at the rate of $160.69. This underpayment was then rectified by a September 
18, 2019, payment. There was an unreasonable delay of benefit payment with no 
excuses or justifications provided for that delay. While the underpayment was rectified 
after claimant’s counsel contacted defendants on July 24, 2019, this error was not fully 
corrected until September 18, 2019. The benefit rate did change a week later, on August 
13, 2019.  
As of July 24, 2019, defendants were aware of the underpayment and while the 
weekly benefit rate changed to the appropriate amount on August 13, 2019, the lump 
sum was not issued for another month. For the underpayment amount of 6 weeks and 3 
days of PPD, defendants shall pay a 25% penalty benefit for the unreasonable delay of 
benefits.

Defendants voluntarily paid 59.887 weeks of PPD prior to hearing. Claimant 
argues that failure to pay the 5% whole person impairment rating was an unreasonable 
denial of benefits. Drs. Mooney, Smith and Bansal all opined claimant sustained a 5% 
body as a whole impairment. Dr. Folkers and Dr. Mooney’s combined impairment rating 
was 14% of the whole body.   
Defendants’ argument was that it revised their initial assessment of claimant’s 
right upper extremity impairment after receiving Dr. Gainer’s rating, which was lower 
than that issue by Dr. Folker. They also argue that they agreed to pay out the 23 weeks 
of PPD for the 5% issued by Dr. Smith. The defendants have the right to pay benefits 
based upon a reasonable assessment and reducing the benefits based on a lower 
impairment rating was reasonable. However, this was not well communicated with the 
claimant and part of defendants’ responsibility is to timely and contemporaneously 
explain their positions to the injured worker. For failing to communicate with the 
claimant, defendants are assessed a 5% penalty against the total underpayment of PPD 
benefits.  
Claimant seeks an assessment of costs which includes the vocational report. 
Given that claimant was only awarded functional impairment rather than industrial, the 
vocational report is not awarded. The remainder of the costs are awarded. 
THEREFORE, it is ordered: 
That defendant employer and insurer are to pay unto claimant seventy-five (75) 
weeks of permanent partial disability benefits at the rate of two hundred twenty-three 
and 54/100 dollars ($223.54) per week from January 7, 2019.  
That defendants shall pay unto claimant penalty benefits in the amount of twenty-
five (25) percent of all benefits underpaid for six (6) weeks and three (3) days of PPD 
and five (5) percent of the overall PPD owed for the unreasonable delay of benefits due 
to the lack of timely and contemporaneous communication.  
That defendant employer and insurer shall pay accrued weekly benefits in a lump 
sum. 
That defendant employer and insurer shall pay interest on unpaid weekly benefits 
awarded herein as set forth in Iowa Code section 85.30.   
That defendant employer and insurer shall pay the costs of this matter pursuant 
to rule 876 IAC 4.33 but for the vocational report.
