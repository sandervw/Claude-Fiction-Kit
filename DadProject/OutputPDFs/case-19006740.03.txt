STATEMENT OF THE CASE   
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A. The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Theodore 
Moellers. Claimant appeared telephonically and through his attorney, Zeke McCartney. 
Defendant appeared through attorney Rebecca Duffy. 
Claimant filed the petition on May 22, 2025. The petition alleges an injury arising 
out of and in the course of employment to his right leg, knee, hip, and low back on 
August 7, 2019. Defendant filed an answer on June 3, 2025, and admitted liability for 
the right leg, right knee and hip, and low back to the extent the August 29, 2023 
arbitration decision found the low back compensable. Claimant seeks authorization for 
treatment for his right hip with the University of Wisconsin, where he has been receiving 
authorized treatment for his right knee and low back. He also notes the University of 
Wisconsin is closer to his residence and more convenient for claimant. Defendant seeks 
to have claimant seen at University of Iowa for a second opinion regarding treatment 
recommendations for his hip. 
The alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on June 4, 2025. The 
proceedings were digitally recorded. That recording constitutes the official record of this 
proceeding. Pursuant to the Commissioner’s July 21, 2023 Order, the undersigned has 
been delegated authority to issue a final agency decision in this alternate medical care 
proceeding. Therefore, this ruling is designated final agency action and any appeal of 
the decision would be to the Iowa District Court pursuant to Iowa Code section 17A. 
The record consists of claimant’s sworn testimony, claimant’s exhibit 1 (attached 
to the petition), consisting of ten pages, and defendant’s exhibit A, consisting of ten 
pages. The undersigned took administrative notice of the arbitration decision, filed on 
August 29, 2023 (File No. 19006740.01) Counsel offered oral arguments to support 
their positions, and defendant also submitted a short brief.

ISSUE 
The issue presented for resolution is whether the claimant is entitled to alternate 
medical care consisting of authorization for treatment of his right hip at the University of 
Wisconsin, located in Madison, Wisconsin. 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned having considered all of the evidence in the record finds: 
Claimant sustained a work-related injury to his right knee on August 7, 2019. 
(See Arbitration Decision dated August 29, 2023). That injury led to a sequalae injury to 
claimant’s lumbar spine, for which claimant was awarded alternate medical care, 
specifically a lumbar MRI as recommended by Geoffrey Baer, M.D., Ph.D., at University 
of Wisconsin Hospitals and Clinics (UWHC), along with any follow-up treatment 
recommended thereafter. (Arb. Dec., p. 14; Claimant’s Exhibit 1, p. 10) 
Defendant authorized medical treatment as ordered in the arbitration decision 
with Dr. Baer. Eventually, Dr. Baer referred claimant to the UWHC Spine Orthopedics 
Clinic, where he began seeing Carey Bernards, PA. On June 13, 2024, claimant 
discussed potential hip issues with PA Bernards. (Defendant’s Exhibit A, p. 1) PA 
Bernards recommended an MRI of the hip in September of 2024, which showed a labral 
tear. (Def. Ex. A, p. 2)  
On January 10, 2025, claimant saw David Hennessy, M.D., at UWHC for 
evaluation of his right hip. (Claimant’s Exhibit 1, pp. 4-6) Dr. Hennessy recommended a 
corticosteroid injection, and noted if the injection provided relief, it would be reasonable 
to proceed with a right total hip arthroplasty. (Cl. Ex. 1, p. 5) Claimant testified that he 
had the injection in his hip, and it did provide relief. 
Defense counsel wrote to claimant’s attorney on February 5, 2024, indicating that 
defendant was not aware of who made the referral to Dr. Hennessy, nor did anyone 
authorize care with Dr. Hennessy. (Def. Ex. A, p. 7) The letter also indicates defendant 
had scheduled an examination under Iowa Code section 85.39 with Jonathon Fields, 
M.D., in order to more fully investigate the right hip condition. Defendant states that the 
reason for the IME with Dr. Fields was due to the inability to communicate with the 
medical providers, including PA Bernards, regarding claimant’s condition. (Def. Brief, p. 
2; See also Def. Ex. A, p. 3) In oral argument, defense counsel noted that 
communication with providers at UWHC has been an ongoing issue, as was also 
reflected in the Arbitration Decision. 
Dr. Fields opined that claimant’s work injury on August 7, 2019 caused a material 
aggravation of a chronic right sided primary hip osteoarthritic condition. (Cl. Ex. 1, pp. 8-
9) He was asked what treatment he would recommend, and stated that in his medical 
opinion, claimant should have a second opinion from an orthopedist regarding his right 
hip condition. (Cl. Ex. 1, p. 9) Defendant advised claimant they would be sending him to 
the University of Iowa Hospitals and Clinics (UIHC) for treatment recommendations for 
his hip. (Cl. Ex. 1, p. 3; See also Def. Brf., p. 2) Claimant, through his attorney,

expressed his dissatisfaction with going to UIHC, and subsequently filed the instant 
petition for alternate medical care.  
Claimant testified that he is happy with the treatment he has received at UWHC 
and has no desire to receive treatment at UIHC. He also testified that it takes about 40 
to 45 minutes to travel to Madison, Wisconsin from his home in Mineral Point, 
Wisconsin, and it takes about two and one-half hours to travel to Iowa City from his 
home. He testified that he works in Dubuque, Iowa, which is about one hour from his 
home. He also cares for his disabled wife, who is not always able to drive. He testified it 
would be more convenient for him to continue receiving treatment in Madison as 
opposed to Iowa City. 
On cross examination, claimant acknowledged that in the past, he requested 
physical therapy in Madison, which he attended after working in Dubuque on some 
days. The trip was about 180 miles total, but he noted it only took him 45 minutes to 
return home after physical therapy. Defendant argues that it is not unreasonable, in this 
case, for claimant to travel 128 miles to UIHC from his home for the second opinion 
appointment, given his willingness to drive over 180 miles for physical therapy. 
Additionally, defendant argues the difficulty in communicating with care providers at 
UWHC impedes the ability to offer reasonable, prompt care to claimant for his work-
related injuries.  
Considering the entirety of the evidence, circumstances, and arguments of 
counsel, I find that while it is reasonable and appropriate for defendant to offer care 
outside the UWHC system, the care offered in Iowa City is unduly inconvenient for 
claimant given the distance from his home, and his responsibilities in caring for his 
disabled wife. Claimant lives much closer to Madison, Wisconsin and Dubuque, Iowa; 
both of which are cities with multiple medical facilities that claimant travels to frequently 
for work and other medical treatment. There is no evidence that a qualified orthopedic 
specialist cannot be found in a location closer to claimant’s residence that would provide 
the same level of care and treatment as UIHC. As such, I find that the treatment offered 
by defendant is not reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to 
the claimant, and alternate care should be granted in part. However, defendant has the 
right to choose the provider of care in this case. As such, claimant’s request for 
authorization to seek treatment at UWHC for his hip is denied at this time. 
REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law. The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services. The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury. Section 85.27. Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening October 16, 1975).

By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable. See Iowa 
R. App. P 6.904(3); Bell Bros. Heating v. Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d 193, 209 (Iowa 2010); 
Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995). Determining what care is 
reasonable under the statute is a question of fact. Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 
N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995). The employer’s obligation turns on the question of reasonable 
necessity, not desirability. Id.; Harned v. Farmland Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 
1983).   
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving. Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care. Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995). 
Defendant contends that it has the right to select the authorized medical provider, 
which is correct. Iowa Code section 85.27. However, the right to select the authorized 
medical provider is a qualified right. West Side Transport v. Cordell, 601 N.W.2d 691 
(Iowa 1999). In making its selection of the authorized medical provider, the employer 
must provide prompt care that is reasonably suited to treat the injury. Id. at 693. The 
care must also be “without undue inconvenience to the claimant.” Id. Distance of travel 
from the claimant’s residence is an appropriate factor to consider when determining if 
the care offered is unduly inconvenient to the claimant. Id. at 694. 
Generally, care should be provided within a reasonable distance from claimant’s 
residence. Trade Professionals, Inc. v. Shriver, 661 N.W.2d 119, 124 (Iowa 2003) (more 
than 100 miles is an undue inconvenience); Schrock v. Corkery Waste Disposal, Inc., 
File No. 1133725, (Alt. Care June 26, 1996) (120-mile round trip excessive); Schulte v. 
Vocational Services of Area Residential Care, File No. 1134342, (Alt. Care September 
6, 1996) (care more than 70 miles away unreasonable). A 50-mile radius is generally 
considered a reasonable distance to travel for treatment in workers’ compensation 
cases. Bitner v. Cedar Falls Construction Co., File No. 5013852 (Alt. Care September 
24, 2004). However, it depends on what is reasonable under the circumstances in a 
particular case. See Warner v. City of Hawarden, File No. 5039478 (Arb. June 27, 
2013).   
In Trade Professionals, Inc. v. Shriver, 661 N.W.2d 119, 124 (Iowa 2003), the 
Iowa Supreme Court indicated that an offer of care approximately 100 miles from 
claimant’s residence failed to meet the standard of offering care “without undue 
inconvenience” to the claimant. Defendant asserts that offering care even further than 
the care found to be inconvenient by the Iowa Supreme Court in Trade Professionals 
should be found to be reasonable and appropriate in this case since claimant may only 
need to travel that distance one time, and has been willing to travel longer distances in 
the past for physical therapy. Defendant also argues it is necessary due to the genuine 
difficulties in communicating with providers at UWHC. I disagree.

The comparison to claimant’s travels for physical therapy are misplaced. As 
claimant testified, and the evidence confirms, on days claimant had physical therapy 
after work, he first drove about 50 miles to work in Dubuque, and then after his shift 
drove about 93 miles from Dubuque to Madison, and then about 45 miles home after 
therapy. (Def. Ex. A, p. 8) Driving to Iowa City from his home will take him about two 
and a half hours each way. This is much different than breaking up a 188-mile drive 
throughout the day.  
As for the argument that he may only have to make the trip to Iowa City one time, 
it is unreasonable to ask claimant to travel 128 miles one-way to see an orthopedic 
specialist when he lives only 45 miles from Madison, Wisconsin, and about 93 miles 
from Dubuque, Iowa. There are certainly orthopedic specialists in either city that are 
qualified to make treatment recommendations, as evidenced by claimant’s January 10, 
2025 visit with Dr. Hennessy. However, taking into consideration the communication 
difficulties defendant has experienced with UWHC, it is not unreasonable that defendant 
would want to seek a second opinion from a medical provider outside that system. It is, 
however, unreasonable to ask him to travel to Iowa City when there are qualified 
medical providers outside of UWHC closer to his home. 
Considering the entirety of the evidence, circumstances, and arguments of 
counsel, I found that while it is reasonable and appropriate for defendant to offer care 
outside the UWHC system, the care offered in Iowa City is unduly inconvenient for 
claimant. There is no evidence that a qualified orthopedic specialist cannot be found in 
a location closer to claimant’s residence that would provide the same level of care and 
treatment as UIHC. As such, I found that the treatment offered by defendant is not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the claimant, and 
alternate care should be granted in part. Defendant retains the right to choose the 
orthopedic specialist who will evaluate and treat claimant’s work-related hip condition. 
This decision only requires that specialist be located closer to claimant’s residence. As 
such, claimant’s request for authorization to seek treatment for his hip at UWHC is 
denied. 
ORDER 
THEREFORE IT IS ORDERED: 
The claimant's petition for alternate medical care is granted in part and denied in 
part. 
Claimant’s request for authorization to seek treatment for his hip at UWHC is 
denied. Defendant retains the right to choose the orthopedic specialist who will evaluate 
and treat claimant’s work-related hip condition. However, defendant shall authorize an 
orthopedic specialist located closer to claimant’s residence than the currently authorized 
specialist in Iowa City.
