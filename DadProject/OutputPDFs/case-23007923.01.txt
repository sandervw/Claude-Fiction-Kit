STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
On January 13, 2025, the claimant filed a petition for alternate medical care 
pursuant to Iowa Code 85.27(4) and 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.48.  The claimant 
filed their petition using an agency form.  On this form, Paragraph 1 includes a blank for 
the employer’s address.  Paragraph 2 includes a blank for the insurer’s address.  The 
claimant completed both of the address blanks.  Claimant’s counsel completed the 
“Proof of Service” section of the form, in which she swore, under Iowa law and the 
penalty of perjury, that a copy of the form was sent to the address of the employer in 
Paragraph 1 on January 13, 2025.  The claimant filed proof of service, including tracking 
results from the United States Postal Service (“USPS”) indicating that the documents 
were delivered to the defendant-employer on January 15, 2025.   
The agency form includes an admonition to the defendants that the claimant filed 
a petition with the agency, and that “[y]ou may file an answer, but it is not necessary 
due to time constraints.  If no answer is filed, you will be required to provide a response 
at a hearing.  Failure to participate may impact your rights and responsibilities under 
Iowa Code section 85.27.”   
The agency scheduled a telephone alternate care hearing for January 22, 2025, 
at 10:30 a.m., and sent notice to the parties via USPS.  The notice of hearing included 
the following language, “[y]ou must be prepared to present your case at the time of 
hearing.  Failure to participate may impact your rights and responsibilities under Iowa 
workers’ compensation law.”  The hearing notice also informs the parties that the

ISSUE 
The issue under consideration is whether the defendant should be ordered to 
provide and pay for medical care with Dr. Shawn Spooner and the UnityPoint Sports 
Medicine Clinic, including speech therapy, physical therapy, and other treatment 
recommended by Dr. Spooner.   
FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
The claimant, Nipentha Brumbaugh, alleges that she sustained injuries to her 
brain, head, neck, and spine, following a fall while working for defendant-employer 
Casey’s Marketing Company (“Casey’s”) on July 4, 2023.  Following her fall, Ms. 
Brumbaugh treated with Todd Caze, II, Ph.D. for symptoms of a concussion  
(Testimony).  This treatment was arranged by Casey’s.  (Testimony).  Mr. Caze 
recommended physical therapy, which Ms. Brumbaugh completed.  (Testimony).  Ms. 
Brumbaugh testified that this physical therapy provided her with no relief.  (Testimony).   
 
Mr. Caze drafted a letter dated September 12, 2023, in which he noted he 
examined the claimant.  (Claimant’s Exhibit 1:1).  In this note, he opined that Ms. 
Brumbaugh was “…cleared to return to full duty work from their concussion with no 
restrictions.  They have reached MMI for their concussion.”  (CE 1:1).  Mr. Caze 
recommended no additional treatment or care.  (Testimony).   
 
Subsequent to her release from care, Ms. Brumbaugh continued to experience 
concussion symptoms.  (Testimony).  Thus, she sought care with Shawn Spooner, M.D.  
(Testimony; CE 2:2-3).  On July 8, 2024, Dr. Spooner examined Ms. Brumbaugh.  (CE 
2:2-6).  He began his record by noting the history of Ms. Brumbaugh’s injury, noting:

“…at work she slipped and fell striking her head on the ground.”  (CE 2:2).  Ms. 
Brumbaugh reported “acute alteration of consciousness, headache, vision changes,

continued to experience “subacute symptoms including headache, tremor, troubles with

no further treatment besides “some limited physical therapy…”  (CE 2:2).  Dr. Spooner

2:3).  Dr. Spooner recommended a referral to physical therapy, speech therapy, and 
neuro-optometry.  (Testimony; CE 2:3).   
 
On October 7, 2024, Ms. Brumbaugh returned to see Dr. Spooner for follow-up 
care regarding her post-concussive symptoms.  (CE 2:4-6).  Ms. Brumbaugh was to 
undergo “alpha stim” to manage her somatic symptoms.  (CE 2:4).  Dr. Spooner noted 
that Ms. Brumbaugh finished speech therapy with positive results, and that she had 
“good effect from prior auricular acupuncture.”  (CE 2:4).  Dr. Spooner opined that Ms. 
Brumbaugh’s examination was “…consistent with late sequelae of prior concussive 
injury.”  (CE 2:5).   
 
On November 14, 2024, claimant’s counsel wrote to a claims adjuster with the 
defendant-insurer requesting alternate care pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.27.  (CE 
3:8).  Claimant’s counsel outlined Ms. Brumbaugh’s “…significant ongoing symptoms…” 
for which she established care with Dr. Spooner.  (CE 3:7). Claimant’s counsel 
forwarded Dr. Spooner’s records to the claims adjuster.  (CE 3:7).  Claimant’s counsel 
also argued that Mr. Caze is not a medical doctor and thus not qualified as an 
authorized treating physician.  (CE 3:8).   
Ms. Brumbaugh testified that care provided by Dr. Spooner was effective in 
treating her symptoms.  (Testimony).   
Considering the foregoing, the claimant requested that the undersigned order the 
defendants to provide care with Dr. Spooner, as well as ordering the defendants to 
provide for associated physical therapy, speech therapy, and other additional treatment.   
CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Iowa Code 85.27(4) provides, in relevant part: 
For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish reasonable 
services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has the right to 
choose the care….  The treatment must be offered promptly and be 
reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the 
employee.  If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the care 
offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such dissatisfaction 
to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the employer and 
the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited to treat the 
injury.  If the employer and employee cannot agree on such alternate care,

the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable proofs of the 
necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
Iowa Code 85.27(4). See Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 433 (Iowa 
1997).   
“Iowa Code section 85.27(4) affords an employer who does not contest the 
compensability of a workplace injury a qualified statutory right to control the medical 
care provided to an injured employee.”  Ramirez-Trujillo v. Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 
N.W.2d 759, 769 (Iowa 2016) (citing R.R. Donnelly & Sons v. Barnett, 670 N.W.2d 190, 
195, 197 (Iowa 2003)).  “In enacting the right-to-choose provision in section 85.27(4), 
our legislature sought to balance the interests of injured employees against the 
competing interests of their employers.”  Ramirez, 878 N.W.2d at 770-71 (citing Bell 
Bros., 779 N.W.2d at 202, 207; IBP, Inc. v. Harker, 633 N.W.2d 322, 326-27 (Iowa 
2001)).   
The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except where the 
employer has denied liability for the injury.  Iowa Code section 85.27; Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening, October 16, 1975).  An employer’s right to select 
the provider of medical treatment to an injured worker does not include the right to 
determine how an injured worker should be diagnosed, evaluated, treated, or other 
matters of professional medical judgment.  Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 
866389 (Declaratory Ruling, May 19, 1988).  Reasonable care includes care necessary 
to diagnose the condition, and defendants are not entitled to interfere with the medical 
judgment of its own treating physician.  Pote v. Mickow Corp., File No. 694639 (Review-
Reopening Decision, June 17, 1986).   
The employer must furnish “reasonable medical services and supplies and 
reasonable and necessary appliances to treat an injured employee.”  Stone Container 
Corp. v. Castle, 657 N.W.2d 485, 490 (Iowa 2003)(emphasis in original).  Such 
employer-provided care “must be offered promptly and be reasonably suited to treat the 
injury without undue inconvenience to the employee.”  Iowa Code section 85.27(4).  
More specifically in Long, the court noted that “the employer’s obligation under the 
statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.”  Long v. Roberts 
Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122, 128 (Iowa 1995).   
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment - and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See e.g. 
Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e); Bell Bros. Heating and Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 
N.W.2d 193, 209 (Iowa 2010); Long, 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  More specifically in 
Long, the court noted that “the employer’s obligation under the statute turns on the 
question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.”  Long, 528 N.W.2d at 128.   
An injured employee dissatisfied with the employer-furnished care (or lack 
thereof) may share the employee’s discontent with the employer and if the parties 
cannot reach an agreement on alternate care, “the commissioner may, upon application

and reasonable proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and order the care.”  Id.  
“Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.”  Long, 528 
N.W.2d at 123; Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 436.  As the party seeking 
relief in the form of alternate care, the employee bears the burden of proving that the 
authorized care is unreasonable.  Id. at 124; Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d at 209; Pirelli-
Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 436.  Because “the employer’s obligation under the 
statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability,” an injured 
employee’s dissatisfaction with employer-provided care, standing alone, is not enough 
to find such care unreasonable.  Id.  However, if substantial evidence exists that the 
care authorized by the employer was ineffective, it may be found that the care was not 
reasonable.  Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 437.  
Casey’s initially referred the claimant for treatment with Mr. Caze.  It is unclear 
what qualifications Mr. Caze possesses as it relates to Ms. Brumbaugh’s alleged 
injuries.  Ultimately, Mr. Caze pronounced Ms. Brumbaugh to have reached maximum 
medical improvement.  He recommended no additional treatment.  As such, the 
defendants offered no additional treatment.  In this case, the failure to offer treatment is 
substantial evidence that the care authorized by the employer was ineffective.   
As Ms. Brumbaugh credibly testified, she continued to experience symptoms 
related to her July 4, 2023, work injury.  Since Casey’s refused to offer additional care, 
she sought treatment on her own volition with Dr. Spooner.  In this case, this is a 
reasonable reaction by Ms. Brumbaugh.   
Dr. Spooner made a number of treatment recommendations in his records.  
According to Ms. Brumbaugh, his treatment also provided her with relief.  This is in 
contrast to the treatment provided by Casey’s.   
I find that Casey’s providing no care for the claimant’s ongoing symptoms is not 
reasonable.  Therefore, it is appropriate to grant the claimant’s petition for alternate 
care.   
 
IT IS THEREFORE ORDERED: 
1. The claimant’s petition for alternate care is granted.  
  
2. Treatment shall be authorized by the defendants with Dr. Spooner and Dr. 
Spooner shall be considered an authorized treating physician.   
 
3. Care recommended by Dr. Spooner, including physical therapy, speech 
therapy, and vision therapy, shall also be authorized by the defendants. 
 
4. Failing to comply with this order may result in sanctions pursuant to 876 Iowa 
Administrative Code 4.36.
