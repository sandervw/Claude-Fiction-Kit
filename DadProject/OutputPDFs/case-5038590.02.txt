STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Melynda Reida, claimant, filed a petition for review-reopening against REM -
Mentor Network, as the employer and its workers’ compensation carrier, New 
Hampshire Insurance Company.  This case came before the undersigned for a review-
reopening hearing on December 5, 2023.   
Pursuant to an order from the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commissioner, this 
case was heard via videoconference using Zoom.  All participants appeared remotely 
for the hearing. 
The parties filed a hearing report prior to the commencement of the hearing.  All 
issues related to weekly benefits are noted as not in dispute.  No affirmative defenses 
are at issue.  All issues are resolved by a 2014 arbitration decision except two pending 
disputes noted below. 
The evidentiary record includes Claimant’s Exhibits 1 through 11 and 13 through 
24.  Defendants filed written objections to many of claimant’s exhibits.  All objections 
were overruled except the objection to Claimant’s Exhibit 12, which was sustained.  
Claimant’s Exhibit 12 is contained in claimant’s exhibit set as filed but is excluded from 
the evidentiary record. The evidentiary record also contains Defendants’ Exhibits A 
through C, which were received without objection.  
Neither party offered testimonial evidence, but counsel for all parties offered 
cogent argument on the pending issues.  The evidentiary record closed at the

ISSUES 
The parties submitted the following disputed issues for resolution: 
1. 
Whether claimant is entitled to an award of medical expenses contained in 
Claimant’s Exhibit 2 and specifically medical bills for dates of service at 
the University of Iowa Hospitals and Clinics on May 19, 2022 and 
December 15, 2022. 
2. 
Whether defendants should be ordered to pay penalty benefits for their 
alleged unreasonable delay or denial of medical expenses. 
On the hearing report and at the commencement of the review-reopening 
hearing, defendants stipulated that the claimed medical expenses are due and owing.  
Defendants clarified that both payments have been paid or are in processing by the 
insurance carrier to be paid.  Claimant agreed that an oral order requiring defendants to 
pay the outstanding medical expenses is appropriate and defendants consented to the 
entry of such an order.  The undersigned entered a verbal order at the time of hearing 
requiring defendants to pay the outstanding medical expenses submitted at the time of 
hearing.  The above agreement and verbal order resolved the dispute about the 
outstanding medical expenses. 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned, having considered all of the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
Melynda Reida sustained compensable work injuries on March 18, 2010 and 
March 17, 2011.  Those injuries were the subject of an arbitration decision filed on 
September 9, 2014.  All findings of facts, conclusions of law, and award of benefits in 
that arbitration decision speak for themselves and are the law of the case.  Neither party 
challenges those findings, conclusions, or orders. 
The only disputed issues in this contested case proceeding revolve around 
outstanding medical expenses and whether penalty benefits should be awarded for an 
alleged unreasonable delay or denial in payment of medical expenses.  I find that there 
remained two outstanding medical expenses, contained in Claimant’s Exhibit 2, at the 
time of this hearing.  Defendants conceded liability for those medical expenses and 
represented to this agency that those expenses were already paid or in the process of 
being paid.  Accordingly, no other factual findings are required to decide the issues in 
this case. 
CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Iowa Code section 85.27(4) provides, in relevant part:

For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish 
reasonable services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has 
the right to choose the care. . . .  The treatment must be offered promptly 
and be reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience 
to the employee.  If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the 
care offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such 
dissatisfaction to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the 
employer and the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited 
to treat the injury.  If the employer and employee cannot agree on such 
alternate care, the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable 
proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
In this case, defendants concede liability for the outstanding medical expenses 
contained in Claimant’s Exhibit 2.  Pursuant to the verbal order entered at the time of 
hearing, I conclude defendants are responsible for and are ordered to pay the 
outstanding medical expenses in Claimant’s Exhibit 2, if not already paid.  Defendants 
should pay these medical expenses within 30 days of the entry of the verbal order on 
December 5, 2023.  If they have not already done so, those bills should be paid 
immediately. 
Ms. Reida also asserts a claim for penalty benefits for defendants’ failure to 
timely pay the outstanding medical expenses.  Claimant’s counsel expressed claimant’s 
frustration and explained the stress the delay in payment of medical expenses causes 
claimant.  Certainly, the Iowa workers’ compensation system is established to function 
as a non-adversarial and self-effectuating system.  Baker v. Bridgestone/Firestone, 872 
N.W.2d 672, 677 (Iowa 2015); Morrison v. Century Eng’g, 434 N.W.2d 874, 877 (Iowa 
1989).  Defendants should timely pay all medical expenses they concede are causally 
related to the alleged injuries.  Iowa Code section 85.27(1). 
Claimant persuasively asserts that there needs to be a remedy to penalize 
defendants when they unreasonably delay or deny medical expenses.  Claimant also 
persuasively argues that it should not be necessary to file or prosecute repeated review-
reopening cases to get medical expenses paid.  However, as defendants argued at the 
time of hearing, neither the undersigned nor the agency drafted the law.  Neither the 
undersigned nor the agency can craft new equitable remedies.  Darrow v. Quaker Oats 
Co., 570 N.W.2d 649, 652 (Iowa 1997) (“because the Workers’ Compensation Act is 
purely a creature of statute, it is the legislature’s prerogative to fix the conditions under 
which the act’s benefits may be obtained.”).  Rather, this agency is a creature of statute 
that has limited statutory powers and not the broad, general jurisdiction of a district 
court.  Mortimer v. Fruehauf Corp., 502 N.W.2d 12, 14 (Iowa 1993) (“As a creature of 
statute, our workers’ compensation law—subject to constitutional limitations—may 
provide such provisions and limitations as the legislature deems necessary.”). 
Iowa Code section 86.13(4) provides that penalty benefits can be awarded for 
the unreasonable delay or denial of weekly benefits.  However, the Iowa Supreme Court 
has interpreted Iowa Code section 86.13 narrowly.  Schadendorf v. Snap-On Tools 
Corp., 757 N.W.2d 330, 338 (Iowa 2008) (concluding penalty benefits cannot be

awarded on delayed interest payments).  This agency has consistently interpreted Iowa 
Code section 86.13(4) over the past four decades and held that penalty benefits cannot 
be ordered for a delay or denial of medical expenses.  Huff v. ABF Feight System, Inc., 
File No. 5010335 (Appeal March 2008); Zahn v. Iowa State Men’s Reformatory, 4 Iowa 
Indus. Comm’r Reports 409 (1983).  Regardless of whether the undersigned believes 
this is good policy to encourage the self-effectuating nature of the system or fair to 
claimant, he must follow agency appellate precedent.  Therefore, as a matter of law, I 
conclude claimant cannot establish a claim for penalty benefits.  Klein v. Furnas Elec. 
Co., 384 N.W.2d 370, 375 (Iowa 1986). 
ORDER 
THEREFORE, IT IS ORDERED: 
Defendants shall pay the medical expenses contained in Claimant’s Exhibit 2 
pursuant to their agreement to do so at the time of hearing and pursuant to the verbal 
order entered at the time of hearing.
