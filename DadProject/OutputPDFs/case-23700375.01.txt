STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Ruth Thomas, claimant, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from FedEx Gound Packaging Systems, Inc. (“FedEx”), self-
insured employer and the Second Injury Fund of Iowa (“SIF”), as defendants.  Hearing 
was held via Zoom videoconference on June 25, 2024.

arbitration hearing.  On the hearing reports, the parties entered into various stipulations.  
All of those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.

Penalty 
 
Ruth seeks penalty benefits against the defendant-employer for unreasonable 
denial of permanent partial disability benefits (PPD) for the November 28, 2022 work 
injury.  As noted above, none of the indemnity benefits defendant-employer paid prior to 
hearing may be properly categorized as PPD benefits. Based on the above findings of 
fact, I conclude that defendant-employer failed to pay any PPD benefits prior to hearing.  
I further find that payment of PPD benefits should have commenced on March 16, 2024.  
I find that from the March 16, 2024 commencement date, to the date of the June 25, 
2024 arbitration hearing 101 days had accrued, which is the equivalent of 14.429 
weeks. Thus, I find that claimant demonstrated that as of the date of the hearing 
defendant-employer had delayed or denied payment of 101 days, which is the 
equivalent of 14.429 weeks, of accrued PPD benefits.  At the stipulated rate of $341.45 
this amounts to $4,926.78.  Therefore, I conclude that claimant has demonstrated that 
PPD benefits in the amount of approximately $4,926.78 were denied or delayed.   
In its post-hearing brief defendant-employer contends that penalty benefits are 
not appropriate because they began paying PPD benefits based on what they estimated 
Ruth’s impairment rating would be.  However, this argument is not persuasive.  I find 
that there is no evidence that claimant’s healing period had ended.  Further, I find that 
defendant-employer has not offered a reasonable basis for the denial of benefits.  I 
further find the record is void of any evidence that the defendant-employer 
contemporaneously conveyed the basis for their denial of benefits.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony.  The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question.  The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances.  The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v.

Left Knee 
 
We now turn to claimant’s entitlement to PPD benefits due to the left lower

Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is compensated either for a loss or loss

earning capacity under section 85.34(2)(v). The extent of scheduled member disability 
benefits to which an injured worker is entitled is determined by using the functional 
method. Functional disability is "limited to the loss of the physiological capacity of the 
body or body part.” Mortimer v. Fruehauf Corp., 502 N.W.2d 12, 15 (Iowa 1993); 
Sherman v. Pella Corp., 576 N.W.2d 312 (Iowa 1998). 
 
The parties stipulated that claimant’s injury is limited to a left leg scheduled 
member injury. (Hearing Report; Transcript) Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(p) provides

that an injury to the leg should be compensated on a scheduled member basis using a 
220-week schedule.  
Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(x) provides: 
In all cases of permanent partial disability described in paragraphs 
“a” through “u,” or paragraph “v” when determining functional disability and 
not loss of earning capacity, the extent of loss or percentage of permanent

As noted above, a leg injury is compensated using a 220-week schedule.  Iowa

Credit 
As noted above, the claimant and the defendant-employer disagreed on the 
amount of indemnity benefits claimant was paid.  However, the issue regarding the 
amount of benefits is no longer in dispute.  Based on the above findings of fact, I 
conclude that prior to hearing Ruth was paid $6,694.54 in indemnity benefits.  (Cl. Brief, 
pp. 8-9; Def. Brief, pp. 3)   
There is still a dispute as to whether the benefits should be classified as healing 
period (HP) or PPD benefits.  Defendant contends these benefits should be classified 
as PPD benefits.  Claimant contends these benefits should be classified as HP benefits.   
Healing period compensation describes temporary workers’ compensation 
weekly benefits that precede an allowance of permanent partial disability benefits.  
Ellingson v. Fleetguard, Inc., 599 N.W.2d 440, 446 (Iowa 1999).  Healing period benefits 
are payable to an injured worker who has suffered permanent partial disability until the 
first to occur of three events.  Iowa Code § 85.34(1) (2015).  These are:  (1) the worker 
has returned to work; (2) the worker medically is capable of returning to substantially 
similar employment; or (3) the worker has achieved maximum medical recovery.  Id.  
Maximum medical recovery is achieved when healing is complete, and the extent of 
permanent disability can be determined.  Armstrong Tire & Rubber Co. v. Kubli, 312 
N.W.2d 60, 65 (Iowa Ct. App. 1981).  Neither maintenance medical care nor an 
employee's continuing to have pain or other symptoms necessarily prolongs the healing 
period.  Id. 
Based on the above findings of fact, I conclude that during the timeframe in 
which defendant paid weekly benefits, August 15, 2023 through February 29, 2024, 
there is no evidence that Ruth returned to work, was medically capable of returning to 
substantially similar work, or that she had reached MMI.  As such, there is no evidence 
that claimant’s healing period had ended.  I conclude that the weekly benefits defendant 
paid prior to hearing are properly categorized as HP benefits.  Therefore, defendant-
employer is not entitled to a credit against any award of PPD benefits.

Penalty 
Claimant seeks penalty benefits for defendants’ unreasonable delay or denial of 
payment of permanent partial disability benefits.   
In Christensen v. Snap-on Tools Corp., 554 N.W.2d 254 (Iowa 1996) and 
Robbennolt v. Snap-on Tools Corp, 555 N.W.2d 229 (Iowa 1996) the supreme court 
said: 
Based on the plain language of section 86.13,2 we hold an employee 
is entitled to penalty benefits if there has been a delay in payment unless 
the employer proves a reasonable cause or excuse.  [Iowa Code § 86.13 
(2015).]  A reasonable cause or excuse exists if either (1) the delay was 
necessary for the insurer to investigate the claim or (2) the employer had a 
reasonable basis to contest the employee’s entitlement to benefits.  A 
“reasonable basis” for denial of the claim exists if the claim is “fairly 
debatable.”  Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 260. 
The supreme court has stated: 
(1) If the employer has a reason for the delay and conveys that 
reason to the employee contemporaneously with the beginning of the delay, 
no penalty will be imposed if the reason is of such character that a 
reasonable fact finder could conclude that it is a "reasonable or probable 
cause or excuse" under Iowa Code section 86.13 [2015].  In that case, we 
will defer to the decision of the commissioner.  See Christensen, 554 
N.W.2d at 260 (substantial evidence found to support commissioner’s 
finding of legitimate reason for delay pending receipt of medical report); 
Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 236. 
(2) If no reason is given for the delay or if the “reason” is not one that 
a reasonable fact finder could accept, we will hold that no such cause or 
excuse exists and remand to the commissioner for the sole purpose of 
assessing penalties under section 86.13.  See Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 
261. 
(3) Reasonable causes or excuses include (a) a delay for the 
employer to investigate the claim, Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 260; 
Kiesecker v. Webster City Custom Meats, Inc., 528 N.W.2d at 109, 111 
(Iowa 1995); or (b) the employer had a reasonable basis to contest the 
claimthe “fairly debatable” basis for delay.  See Christensen, 554 N.W.2d 
at 260 (holding two-month delay to obtain employer’s own medical report 
reasonable under the circumstances).  
(4) For the purpose of applying section 86.13, the benefits that are 
underpaid as well as late-paid benefits are subject to penalties, unless the

Claimant seeks reimbursement for the IME of Dr. Crites pursuant to Iowa Code

Iowa Code section 85.39 permits an employee to be reimbursed for subsequent 
examination by a physician of the employee's choice where an employer-retained 
physician has previously evaluated “permanent disability” and the employee believes 
that the initial evaluation is too low.   Iowa Code § 85.39(2) (2023).  The section also 
permits reimbursement for reasonably necessary transportation expenses incurred and 
for any wage loss occasioned by the employee attending the subsequent examination.  
Iowa Code § 85.39 (1),(2).   
The Iowa Supreme Court has provided a literal interpretation of the plain 
language of Iowa Code section 85.39, stating that section only allows the employee to 
obtain an independent medical evaluation at the employer’s expense if an evaluation of 
permanent disability has already been made by an employer retained physician.  Des 
Moines Area Reg’l Transit Auth. v. Young, 867 N.W.2d 839, 847 (Iowa 2015). Under 
the Young decision, an employee can only obtain an IME at the employer's expense if 
an evaluation of permanent disability has been made by an employer-retained 
physician. Id. 
 
In the present case, I find that there was not an evaluation of permanent disability 
prior to the rating from Dr. Crites.  Because claimant failed to meet the prerequisites of

SIF Claim 
 
We now turn to the claim against the Second Injury Fund of Iowa.  Iowa Code 
section 85.64 governs Second Injury Fund liability.  Before liability of the Fund is 
triggered, three requirements must be met.  First, the employee must have lost or lost 
the use of a hand, arm, foot, leg, or eye.  Second, the employee must sustain a loss or 
loss of use of another specified member or organ through a compensable injury.  Third, 
permanent disability must exist as to both the initial injury and the second injury.   
The Second Injury Fund Act exists to encourage the hiring of handicapped 
persons by making a current employer responsible only for the amount of disability 
related to an injury occurring while that employer employed the handicapped individual 
as if the individual had had no preexisting disability.  See Anderson v. Second Injury 
Fund, 262 N.W.2d 789, 792 (Iowa 1978); 15 Iowa Practice, Workers’ Compensation, 
Lawyer, Section 17:1, p. 211 (2014-2015). 
The Fund is responsible for the industrial disability present after the second injury 
that exceeds the disability attributable to the first and second injuries.  Section 85.64.  
Second Injury Fund of Iowa v. Braden, 459 N.W.2d 467, 471 (Iowa 1990); Second Injury 
Fund v. Neelans, 436 N.W.2d 355, 358 (Iowa 1989); Second Injury Fund v. Mich Coal 
Co., 274 N.W.2d 300, 304 (Iowa 1979). 
The parties stipulated that claimant sustained a loss of 50 percent of her left 
lower extremity as the result of the November 28, 2022 work injury.  (Hearing Report, 
File No. 23700375.01, numbered paragraph 10(d))  The parties stipulated that claimant 
sustained a first qualifying injury to her right lower extremity prior to 2022.  (Hearing 
Report, File No. 23700375.01, numbered paragraph 10(a))  The parties also stipulated 
that as the result of that injury claimant sustained 7 percent loss of her right lower 
extremity.  (Hearing Report, File No. 23700375.01, numbered paragraph 10(b)).  Based 
on the stipulations of the party, I conclude that claimant has demonstrated entitlement to 
benefits from the SIF.   
Industrial disability was defined in Diederich v. Tri-City Ry. Co. of Iowa, 258 N.W. 
899, 902 (Iowa 1935) as follows:  “It is therefore plain that the Legislature intended the 
term ‘disability’ to mean ‘industrial disability’ or loss of earning capacity and not a mere 
‘functional disability’ to be computed in terms of percentages of the total physical and 
mental ability of a normal man.” 
Functional impairment is an element to be considered in determining industrial 
disability which is the reduction of earning capacity, but consideration must also be 
given to the injured employee's age, education, qualifications, experience, motivation, 
loss of earnings, severity and situs of the injury, work restrictions, inability to engage in 
employment for which the employee is fitted and the employer's offer of work or failure 
to so offer.  McSpadden v. Big Ben Coal Co., 288 N.W.2d 181 (Iowa 1980); Olson v. 
Goodyear Service Stores, 255 Iowa 1112, 125 N.W.2d 251 (1963); Barton v. Nevada 
Poultry Co., 253 Iowa 285, 110 N.W.2d 660 (1961).
