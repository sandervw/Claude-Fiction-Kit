I.  
STATEMEN T OF TH E  C ASE 
Claimant Brandon Wagner seeks workers’ compensation benefits from the 
defendants, employer Intren, LLC (Intren) and insurance carrier Travelers Indemnity Co. 
of CT (Travelers). The undersigned presided over an arbitration hearing on August 21, 
2024. Wagner participated personally and through attorney Darin H. Luneckas. The 
defendants participated by and through attorney Brian J. Hindman. 
II.  
ISSU ES  
Under Iowa Administrative Code rule 876—4.19(3)(f), the parties jointly 
submitted a hearing report defining the claims, defenses, and issues submitted to the 
presiding deputy commissioner. The hearing report was approved and entered into the 
record via an order because it is a correct representation of the disputed issues and 
stipulations in this case. The parties identified the following disputed issues in the 
hearing report: 
1) Did Wagner sustain an injury arising out of and in the course of his 
employment with Intren on August 31, 2021? 
2) Is Wagner entitled to temporary partial disability (TPD) benefits from 
November 3, 2021, through December 2, 2021? 
3) Is Wagner entitled to temporary total disability (TTD) or healing period (HP) 
benefits from:

Wa g n e r ’ s  E a r n i n g s  B e f o r e  A u g u s t  3 1 ,  2 0 2 1  
Intren placed Wagner’s pay into different categories. (Hr’g Tr. pp. 64–68) The 
parties dispute the effect this categorization has on the calculation of Wagner’s gross 
earnings for purposes of determining his workers’ compensation rate. Specifically, at 
issue are the per diem and rig pay Intren paid Wagner in the weeks before August 31, 
2021. Wagner provided unrefuted testimony about both types of pay. His testimony was 
credible. 
Intren paid Wagner $11.00 per hour in rig pay. (Hr’g Tr. p. 65) Wagner testified 
that he did not use the money Intren categorized as rig pay on his paycheck on 
equipment or repairs for his rig. (Hr’g Tr. p. 65) His testimony is unrefuted in the 
evidentiary record. The weight of the evidence shows there was no link between rig 
maintenance and the amount Intren paid under the rig pay classification; rather, Intren 
paid rig pay on an hourly basis. This shows it is more likely than not the amount of rig 
pay Intren paid depended on an employee’s hours worked as opposed to maintenance 
expenses incurrent for the employee’s rig.  
Wagner testified that Intren paid him $80.00 per day as a per diem. (Hr’g Tr. pp. 
67–68) According to his unrefuted testimony at hearing, this pay was intended as 
reimbursement for things like food and hotel rooms. (Hr’g Tr. pp. 67–68) However, 
Wagner rarely incurred such expenses while employed by Intren and the company 
nonetheless paid him the per diem. (Hr’g Tr. p. 67–68) Thus, the weight of the evidence 
shows there was no rational nexus between the payment of Wagner’s per diem and the 
expenses he incurred that it was intended to cover.

pain so significant that it compelled him to leave his job in the middle of the workday to

and manage the shoulder pain he experienced before August 31, 2021.

C ON C LU SION S OF LAW 
In 2017, the Iowa legislature amended the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Act. 
See 2017 Iowa Acts, ch. 23. The 2017 amendments took effect on July 1, 2017. Id. at § 
24(1); see also Iowa Code § 3.7(1). Because the injury at issue in this case occurred 
after July 1, 2017, the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Act, as amended in 2017, applies. 
See Stiles v. Annett Holdings, Inc., Iowa Workers’ Comp. Comm’r No. 5064673, 2020 
WL 6037539 *2 (App., Oct. 2, 2020). 
A .  
C a u s a t i o n  
The Iowa Workers’ Compensation Act “covers any and all personal injuries” 
relating to an employee’s work. Hanson v. Dickinson, 188 Iowa 728, 176 N.W. 823, 824 
(1920). Under section 85.3(1), an employer covered by the Act must “provide, secure, 
and pay compensation according to the provisions of this chapter for any and all 
personal injuries sustained by an employee arising out of and in the course of the 
employment.” The legislature has codified a partial definition of “personal injury” in 
section 85.61(4) that is not applicable in the current case. Rather, the expanded 
definition of “personal injury” as articulated by the Iowa Supreme Court in its seminal 
Almquist v. Shenandoah Nurseries, Inc. governs: 
“an injury to the body, the impairment of health, or a disease, not excluded 
by the act, which comes about, not through the natural building up and 
tearing down of the human body, but because of a traumatic or other hurt 
or damage to the health or body of an employee.... The injury to the 
human body here contemplated must be something, whether an accident 
or not, that acts extraneously to the natural processes of nature, and 
thereby impairs the health, overcomes, injures, interrupts, or destroys 
some function of the body, or otherwise damages or injures a part or all of 
the body.”  
Dunlavey v. Econ. Fire & Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 850–51 (Iowa 1995) (quoting 
Almquist, 218 Iowa 724, 732, 254 N.W. 35, 39 (1934)) (citations omitted in Dunlavey). 
“In order for an injury to be compensable in Iowa, there must be ‘a connection 
between the injury and the work.’” Lakeside Casino v. Blue, 743 N.W.2d 169, 173 (Iowa 
2007) (quoting Meyer v. IBP, Inc., 710 N.W.2d 213, 221 (Iowa 2006)). To recover, a 
claimant must prove by a preponderance of the evidence that the injury for which the 
claimant is seeking workers’ compensation arose: 

WAGNER V. INTREN, LLC 
Page 24 
 
1) Out of the claimant’s employment; and 
 
2) In the course of the claimant’s employment. St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 
N.W.2d 646, 652 (Iowa 2000) (citing Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 
143, 150 (Iowa 1996)); see also Miedema v. Dial Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309, 311 
(Iowa 1996).  
“‘The two tests are separate and distinct.’” Blue, 743 N.W.2d at 174 (quoting 
Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311). Under Iowa law, “‘both must be satisfied in order for an 
injury to be deemed compensable.’” Id. (quoting Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311). In this 
case, the parties dispute only whether the injury arose out of employment. 
 “An injury ‘arises out of’ employment if there is a causal connection between the 
employment and the injury.” Gray, 604 N.W.2d at 652 (citing Bailey v. Batchelder, 576 
N.W.2d 334, 338 (Iowa 1998)). “‘In other words, the injury must not have coincidentally 
occurred while at work, but must in some way be caused by or related to the working 
environment or the conditions of [the] employment.’” Blue, 743 N.W.2d at 174. (quoting 
Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311).  
Taking the words themselves, one is first struck by the fact that in the 
“arising” phrase the function of the employment is passive while in the 
“caused by” phrase it is active. When one speaks of an event “arising out 
of employment,” the initiative, the moving force, is something other than 
the employment; the employment is thought of more as a condition out of 
which the event arises than as the force producing the event in affirmative 
fashion. 
Meyer v. IBP, Inc., 710 N.W.2d 213, 223 (Iowa 2006) (quoting 1 Arthur Larson & Lex K. 
Larson, Larson’s Workers’ Compensation Law (2005) § 3.06, at 3-7 to 3-8 (emphasis in 
original)). 
“[S]ubject to ‘limited exceptions,’” the Iowa Supreme Court has “‘abandoned’ or 
‘discarded’ the increased-risk test in favor of the actual-risk test” when determining 
whether an employee’s injury arises out of employment under the Iowa Workers’ 
Compensation Act. Bluml v. Dee Jay’s Inc., 920 N.W.2d 82, 85–86 (Iowa 2018) (quoting 
Blue, 743 N.W.2d at 177, n.7). The actual risk rule holds: 
If the nature of the employment exposes the employee to the risk of such 
an injury, the employee suffers an accidental injury arising out of and 
during the course of the employment. And it makes no difference that the 
risk was common to the general public on the day of injury. 
Blue, 743 N.W.2d at 174 (quoting Hanson v. Reichelt, 452 N.W.2d 164, 168 (Iowa 
1990)).  
An expert opinion on causation may not be considered in isolation; rather, it must 
be considered along with all other evidence bearing upon the causal connection 

WAGNER V. INTREN, LLC 
Page 25 
 
between the injury and the disability. See Schujter v. Algona Manor Care Ctr., 780 
N.W.2d 549, 560 (Iowa 2010); see also Nellis v. Quealy, 237 Iowa 507, 512, 21 N.W.2d 
584, 586 (Iowa 1946). Moreover, the Iowa Supreme Court has long held that an injured 
worker’s credible testimony regarding the onset of symptoms “must be given 
considerable weight on the issue of causal connection.” Hinrichs v. Davenport 
Locomotive Works, 203 Iowa 1395, 214 N.W. 585, 586 (Iowa 1927).  
An injured worker is not entitled to workers’ compensation “for the results of a 
pre-existing injury or disease,” but “the mere existence thereof at the time of a 
subsequent injury is not a defense.” Nicks v. Davenport Produce Co., 254 Iowa 130, 
135, 115 N.W.2d 812, 815 (Iowa 1962). “For workers' compensation purposes a cause 
is proximate if it is a cause; it need not be the only cause.” Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, 
Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 420 (Iowa 1994). Thus, if a personal health condition such as 
osteoporosis increases a worker’s susceptibility to injury and the injury arises out of and 
in the course of the workers’ employment, it is compensable under the Iowa Workers’ 
Compensation Act. See St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 (Iowa 2000); 
see also Ziegler v. U.S. Gypsum Co., 252 Iowa 613, 620, 106 N.W.2d 591, 595 (Iowa 
1960); Hanson v. Dickinson, 176 N.W. 823, 824–25 (Iowa 1920). “If the employment of 
claimant resulted in a personal injury aggravating her already impaired physical 
condition, she is entitled to compensation to the extent of that injury.” Nicks, 254 Iowa at 
135,115 N.W.2d at 815. 
As found above, Dr. Taylor’s opinion on causation is most persuasive. His report 
establishes the most complete understanding of Wagner’s injury history, how his past 
shoulder symptoms led him to incorrectly believe his symptoms stemmed from his 
shoulder as opposed to his cervical spine, how the pain was different and worse than 
what he had previously experienced, and the progression of his symptoms from what he 
initially thought was a mere “tweak” of his shoulder to intense pain that caused him to 
call Christensen and meet her at the ER. Wagner has met his burden of proof. The 
weight of the evidence establishes it is more likely than not that the performance of his 
job duties for Intren were a significant factor in causing the symptoms that necessitated 
care from August 31, 2025, forward.

B e n e f i t s  
Workers’ compensation is “a creature of statute.” Darrow v. Quaker Oats Co., 
570 N.W.2d 649, 652 (Iowa 1997). This means an injured employee’s “right to workers' 
compensation is purely statutory.” Downs v. A & H Const., Ltd.,  481 N.W.2d 520, 527 
(Iowa 1992).  And “it is the legislature’s prerogative to fix the conditions under which the 
act’s benefits may be obtained.” Darrow, 570 N.W.2d at 652. 
The Iowa Supreme Court has held: 
The legislature enacted the workers' compensation statute primarily for the 
benefit of the worker and the worker's dependents. Therefore, we apply 
the statute broadly and liberally in keeping with the humanitarian objective 
of the statute. We will not defeat the statute's beneficent purpose by 

WAGNER V. INTREN, LLC 
Page 26 
 
reading something into it that is not there, or by a narrow and strained 
construction. 
Gregory v. Second Inj. Fund of Iowa, 777 N.W.2d 395, 399 (Iowa 2010) (quoting 
Holstein Elec. v. Breyfogle, 756 N.W.2d 812, 815–16 (Iowa 2008) (citations omitted)).  
“Although the workers' compensation statute is to be liberally construed in favor 
of the worker, the statute is not to be expanded by reading something into it that is not 
there.” Downs v. A & H Const., Ltd., 481 N.W.2d 520, 527 (Iowa 1992) (citing Cedar 
Rapids Community School Dist. v. Cady, 278 N.W.2d 298 (Iowa 1979)). “To determine 
legislative intent, we look to the language chosen by the legislature and not what the 
legislature might have said.” Ramirez-Trujillo v. Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 N.W.2d 759, 
(Iowa 2016) (citing Schadendorf v. Snap-On Tools Corp., 757 N.W.2d 330, 337 (Iowa 
2008)). The “broad purpose of workers’ compensation” is “to award compensation 
(apart from medical benefits), not for the injury itself, but the disability produced by a 
physical injury.” Bell Bros. Heating & Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d 193, (Iowa 
2010) (citing 4 Arthur Larson & Lex K. Larson, Larson’s Workers’ Compensation Law 
§ 80.02, at 80–2 (2009)).  
An employer covered by the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Act must “provide, secure, 
and pay compensation according to the provisions of [Iowa Code chapter 85] for any 
and all personal injuries sustained by an employee arising out of and in the course of 
the employment, and in such cases, the employer shall be relieved from other liability 
for recovery of damages or other compensation for such personal injury.” Iowa Code 
§ 85.3(1).  
“Permanent benefits and temporary benefits are very different. Temporary 
benefits compensate the employee for lost wages until he or she is able to return to 
work, whereas permanent benefits compensate either a disability to a scheduled 
member or a loss in earning capacity (industrial disability).” Mannes v. Fleetguard, Inc., 
770 N.W.2d 826, 830 (Iowa 2009) (citing Iowa Code §§ 85.33, 85.34) An injured 
employee may receive such temporary benefits as follows: 
 Temporary total disability (TTD) benefits for time lost from work for an injury 
that does not result in a permanent disability. Iowa Code § 85.33(1).  
 Temporary partial disability (TPD) benefits when the employee is able to 
perform light-duty work within restrictions but cannot return to work 
substantially similar to the job performed at the time of the work injury. Id. at § 
85.33(2). 
 Healing period benefits for time lost from work for an injury that results in 
permanent partial disability (PPD). Id. at § 85.34(1). 
As found above, the August 31, 2021 work injury has caused Wagner to work 
reduced hours while under work restrictions, which negatively impacted his gross 
earnings. The work injury also caused Wagner to sustain a permanent impairment and

Page 27 
 
disability. Therefore, the dispute is whether he has met his burden of proof on 
entitlement to TPD benefits, HP benefits, and PPD benefits.

T P D  B e n e f i t s  
Under Iowa Code section 85.33(4), “The temporary partial benefit shall be sixty-
six and two-thirds percent of the difference between the employee’s weekly earnings at 
the time of injury, computed in compliance with section 85.36, and the employee’s 
actual gross weekly income from employment during the period of temporary partial 
disability.” The Iowa Supreme Court has considered how Iowa Code section 85.33 
applies when an injured employee returns to work with reduced hours following a work 
injury. Ellingson v. Fleetguard, Inc., 599 N.W.2d 440, 446 (Iowa 1999), overruled on 
other grounds by Waldinger Corp. v. Mettler, 817 N.W.2d 1 (Iowa 2012).  Employees 
who return to work for partial days are limited TPD benefits. Id. 
Wagner seeks TPD benefits from November 3, 2021, through December 2, 2021. 
The defendants dispute Wagner’s entitlement to TPD benefits during this time period 
independent of the causation determination in this case. The evidence in this case 
shows Wagner is entitled to TPD benefits for the following weeks due to reduced gross 
earnings caused by the work restrictions prescribed due to the work injury.  
From November 7, 2021, through November 12, 2021, Wagner worked just 26 
hours on light duty. He earned $2,281.68. His gross earnings for purposes of rate were 
$2,329.19. Taking $2,329.19 minus $2,281.68 equals $47.51 and two two-thirds of that 
amount is $31.67. 
The week of November 15, 2021, through November 19, 2021, Wagner worked  
28 hours and had gross earnings of $1,931.04. Subtracting that amount from $2,329.19 
equals $398.15. Two-thirds of $398.15 is $265.43. 
For these two weeks, Wagner has met his burden of proof on entitlement to TPD 
benefits. Wagner has failed to meet his burden of proof on entitlement to TPD benefits 
from November 3, 2021, through November 6, 2021, and from November 20, 2021, 
through December 2, 2021. Under Iowa Code section 85.33(4), he is entitled to TPD 
benefits in the amount of $297.10.

P e r m a n e n cy  B e n e f i t s  
Iowa Code section 85.34(2) governs permanent partial disabilities that result from 
work injuries. The statute provides: 
The compensation shall be based upon the extent of the disability and 
upon the basis of eighty percent per week of the employee’s average 

WAGNER V. INTREN, LLC 
Page 29 
 
spendable weekly earnings, but not more than a weekly benefit amount, 
rounded to the nearest dollar, equal to one hundred eighty-four percent of 
the statewide average weekly wage paid employees as determined by the 
department of workforce development under section 96.1A, subsection 35, 
and in effect at the time of the injury. 
With the 2017 amendments, the legislature changed how permanent disability is 
determined for workers’ compensation purposes. Before the 2017 amendments, the 
agency could use all evidence in the administrative record, as well as agency expertise, 
when determining the permanent disability of an injured worker. See, e.g., Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Under agency rules before 
the 2017 amendments, the Guides were considered a “useful tool in evaluating 
disability.” Seaman v. City of Des Moines, File Nos. 5053418, 5057973, 5057974 (App. 
Oct. 11, 2019) (quoting Bisenius v. Mercy Med. Ctr., File No. 5036055 (App. Apr. 1, 
2013)); see also Westling v. Hormel Foods Corp., 810 N.W.2d 247, 252 (Iowa 2012). 
However, in cases involving injuries on or after July 1, 2017, the Guides are more than 
a tool when determining functional impairment; they are dispositive. 
[W]hen determining functional disability and not loss of earning capacity, 
the extent of loss or percentage of permanent impairment shall be 
determined solely by utilizing the guides to the evaluation of permanent 
impairment, published by the American medical association, as adopted 
by the workers’ compensation commissioner by rule pursuant to chapter 
17A. Lay testimony or agency expertise shall not be utilized in determining 
loss or percentage of permanent impairment pursuant to paragraphs “a” 
through “u”, or paragraph “v” when determining functional disability and 
not loss of earning capacity. 
Iowa Code § 85.34(2)(x).  
Thus, with the 2017 amendments, the legislature effectively incorporated the 
Guides into the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Act. The Guides expressly provide, “A 
patient may decline surgical, pharmacologic, or therapeutic treatment of an impairment.” 
Guides, § 2.5g, p. 20. They do not expressly provide for a downward adjustment to a 
functional impairment rating when an individual declines surgery. See id. Because the 
Guides expressly allow for an individual to decline surgery but do not expressly direct a 
physician to reduce an impairment rating when an individual does precludes such a 
reduction under section 85.34(2)(x)’s requirement that “when determining functional 
disability and not loss of earning capacity, the extent of loss or percentage of permanent 
impairment shall be determined solely by utilizing the [Guides] . . . .” 
Wagner’s work injury is to the cervical spine, which makes it unscheduled under 
Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v).  In this case, Intren terminated Wagner’s employment by 
layoff on December 3, 2021, before he underwent surgery on his cervical spine with Dr. 
Smith. After the surgery, Intren did not offer Wagner employment in which he would 
have the same or higher earnings as what he had on the injury date. Therefore, 
Wagner’s entitlement to permanency benefits is based on his lost earning capacity 

WAGNER V. INTREN, LLC 
Page 30 
 
using the traditional industrial disability analysis. See Chavez v. MS Tech. LLC, 972 
N.W.2d 662, 666–67 (Iowa 2022). The factors considered when determining industrial 
disability are: functional disability, age, education, qualifications, work experience, 
inability to engage in similar employment, earnings before and after the injury, 
motivation to work, personal characteristics, and the employer’s inability to 
accommodate the functional limitations. Neal v. Annett Holdings, Inc., 814 N.W.2d 512, 
526 (Iowa 2012); IBP, Inc. v. Al-Gharib, 604 N.W.2d 621, 632–33 (Iowa 2000); Ehlinger 
v. State, 237 N.W.2d 784, 792 (Iowa 1976).  
At the time of hearing, Wagner was 49 years of age. There is no indication in the 
record that he intended to retire prior to the common age of 65 before the work injury on 
August 31, 2021, and subsequent treatment. He has a high school education and 
training as a welder; however, his grades and ADD make him a poor candidate for re-
education or re-training.  
Wagner’s work experience is entirely welding pipe with a focus on heavy pipeline 
work. The heavy pipeline work is the highest paying type, but Wagner has been unable 
to get recertified after his work injury because of his physical limitations. He is now 
limited to fab work that does not require certification, which has reduced his earnings by 
about 52 percent. 
Intren accommodated Wager’s temporary work restrictions for a period of time 
after the work injury and before his surgery. Intren terminated Wagner’s employment on 
December 3, 2021, and the reason is unclear based on the evidence. Nonetheless, 
given Wagner’s work restrictions from Dr. Taylor and his inability to physically pass the 
recertification test for pipeline work—the type he was performing for Intren—it is more 
likely than not Intren could not have accommodated him at his pre-injury earnings level. 
Wagner contends he is permanently and totally disabled. While consideration of 
the evidence establishes a significant industrial disability, they fall short of permanent 
total disability. The weight of the evidence shows Wagner has sustained a 75 percent 
industrial disability caused by the injury arising out of and in the course of employment 
with Intren on August 31, 2021. 
Wagner’s injury is an unscheduled injury, which means it is compensated out of 
500 weeks. 75 percent multiplied by 500 equals 375 weeks. The defendants shall pay 
Wagner 375 weeks of PPD benefits commencing on May 9, 2022, with interest.

Iowa Code section 85.36 states, “The basis of compensation shall be the weekly 
earnings of the injured employee at the time of the injury. Weekly earnings means gross 
salary, wages, or earnings of an employee to which such employee would have been 
entitled had the employee worked the customary hours for the full pay period in which 
the employee was injured, as regularly required by the employee’s employer for the 
work or employment for which the employee was employed, computed or determined as 
follows and then rounded to the nearest dollar.” 
Intren placed Wagner’s pay into different categories. (Hr’g Tr. pp. 64–68) The 
parties dispute the effect this categorization has on the calculation of Wagner’s gross 
earnings for purposes of determining his workers’ compensation rate. Specifically at 
issue are the per diem and rig pay Intren paid Wagner in the weeks before August 31, 
2021. To determine whether the pay at issue is considered gross earnings for purposes 
of determining rate, the agency’s rational nexus test must be applied. Huff v. CRST 
Expedited, Inc., Iowa Workers’ Comp. Comm’r No. 5063162, 2020 WL 3146576, *2–*3 
(App., Jun. 5, 2020) (quoting Phillips v. C & K Trans., Iowa Workers’ Comp. Comm’r No. 
844999, 1993 WL 13020848, *4 (App. May 26, 1993)). 
Intren paid Wagner $11.00 per hour in rig pay that equaled about $440.00 per 
week. (Hr’g Tr. p. 65) Wagner testified that he did not use the money Intren categorized 
as rig pay on his paycheck on equipment or repairs for his rig. (Hr’g Tr. p. 65) His 
testimony is unrefuted in the evidentiary record. The weight of the evidence shows there 
was no link between rig maintenance and the amount Intren paid under the rig pay 
classification; rather, Intren paid rig pay on an hourly basis. This shows it is more likely 
than not the amount of rig pay Intren paid depended on an employee’s hours worked as 
opposed to maintenance expenses incurrent for the employee’s rig. Put otherwise, 
“[t]here does not appear to be any rational nexus between” rig maintenance expenses 
incurred by Wagner and the amount of rig pay Intren paid him. Huff, 2020 WL 3146576, 
*2–*3 (quoting Phillips1993 WL 13020848, *4. The weight of the evidence in this case 
shows that pay Intren categorized as rig pay should be included as part of Wagner’s 
gross earnings for purposes of calculating his compensation rate under the agency’s 
traditional rational nexus test. 
Wagner testified that Intren paid him $80.00 per day as a per diem. (Hr’g Tr. pp. 
65–68) According to his unrefuted testimony at hearing, this pay was intended as 
reimbursement for things like food and hotel rooms. (Hr’g Tr. pp. 66–68) However, 
Wagner rarely incurred such expenses while employed by Intren and the company 
nonetheless paid him the per diem. (Hr’g Tr. p. 67–68) Thus, the weight of the evidence 
shows there was no rational nexus between the payment of Wagner’s per diem and the 
expenses he incurred that it was intended to cover. The per diem must therefore be 
considered as part of Wagner’s gross earnings for rate purposes. 
Wagner has met his burden of proof on gross earnings. The evidence 
establishes there was no rational nexus between the amount Intren paid him in rig pay

or per diem and his expenses under either category; he received the pay regardless of 
his expenditures. Both therefore qualify as gross earnings under section 85.36.

M e d i c a l  B e n e f i t s  
For all compensable injuries under Iowa Code chapter 85 or 85A, the employer 
must “furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, chiropractic, podiatric, 
physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance, and hospital services and supplies therefor 
and shall allow reasonably necessary transportation expenses incurred for such 
services.” Iowa Code § 85.27(1). Here, the defendants provided care to Wagner until 
they denied liability and refused care. After they did so, Wagner sought care on his own. 
The defendants’ denial of liability means they lost the right to choose the care 
received by Wagner for the work injury. Winnebago Indus., Inc. v. Haverly, 727 N.W.2d 
567, 575 (Iowa 2006) (citing Trade Prof’ls, Inc. v. Shriver, 661 N.W.2d 119, 124 (Iowa 
2003)). Wagner could therefore obtain reasonable care from any provider for the injury, 
at his expense, and seek reimbursement for such care through this contested case 
proceeding. See Trade Prof’ls, 661 N.W.2d at 121–25 (affirming on judicial review an 
agency decision ordering the payment of medical expenses for unauthorized care 
because the defendants denied liability for the alleged injury and therefore lost the right 
to control care).  
Here, Wagner has met his burden of proof on causation. The weight of the 
evidence shows that a significant causal factor in Wagner receiving the care detailed 
above was the August 31, 2021 work injury. The care was reasonable and necessary 
given Wagner’s symptoms. Therefore, Wagner has established under section 85.27 that 
he is entitled to payment of the medical expenses in Claimant’s Exhibits 6.

I M E  
Iowa Code section 85.39 governs examinations of injured employees to 
determine the extent of permanent impairment or disability caused by a work injury. Mid 
American Const. LLC v. Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d 838, 847–48 (Iowa 2024). Under subsection 
1, the employee must attend an examination by a physician of the employer’s choice or 
be disqualified from benefits for the period of refusal. An opinion on causation or the 
extent of permanent impairment or disability by a doctor retained by the employer 
triggers the injured employee’s right to an IME under subsection 2. 
“In subsection 2, the legislature leveled the playing field between the employer 
and the employee.” Id. 2 N.W.3d at 848. “The statutory process balances the competing 
interests of the employer and employee and permits the employee to obtain an 

WAGNER V. INTREN, LLC 
Page 33 
 
independent medical examination at the employer's expense.” Des Moines Area Reg'l 
Transit Auth. v. Young, 867 N.W.2d 839, 844 (Iowa 2015). Subsection 2 entitles the 
employee to receive an examination if the employee believes the evaluation made by 
the employer's physician was too low. Iowa Code § 85.39(2); Young, 867 N.W.2d at 
844. The employee's own examination “is known as an IME because the examination is 
independent of the examination done by the physician selected by the employer.” 
Young, 867 N.W.2d at 843–44. 
Subsection 2 further provides, “A determination of the reasonableness of a fee 
for an examination … shall be based on the typical fee charged by a medical provider to 
perform an impairment rating in the local area where the examination is conducted.” 
Under the Iowa Supreme Court’s holding in Sandlin, “The reasonableness of the fees 
will be analyzed based on what physicians in that area typically charge to perform the 
impairment rating examination—which may include ‘the costs of reviewing medical 
records, conducting a physical examination, opining on causation, assessing permanent 
impairment, assigning restrictions, and addressing further treatment recommendations.’” 
2 N.W.3d at 851–52 (quoting Turner v. NCI Bldg. Sys., Inc., Iowa Workers’ Comp. 
Comm'r No. 1652235.01, 2022 WL 1787301, at *26 (Feb. 24, 2022)).  
This analysis under the statute “effectively necessitates fact-finding by the 
commissioner on the fees typically charged in the local area where the examination is 
performed. Parties may offer evidence of the fees typically charged in that locality to 
support or challenge the fee charged in a particular case.” Id. at 849. The court 
concluded “‘many IMEs come before the agency, which allows the [commissioner] to 
use agency experience and expertise to conclude that [the physician's] charges are in 
line with those of an occupational medicine doctor with a practice in the ... [local] area 
performing an evaluation of permanent impairment in accordance with the [AMA] 
Guides.’” Id. (quoting Nguyen v. Hy-Vee, Inc., Iowa Workers’ Comp. Comm'r No. 
21003118.03, 2023 WL 7128682, at *32 (Arb. Oct. 20, 2023)). 
The introductory chapter of the Guides states the following with respect to the 
process it details for evaluating permanent impairment or disability: 
Generally, the physician evaluates all available information and provides 
as comprehensive a medical picture of the patient as possible, addressing 
the components listed in the Report of Medical Evaluation form discussed 
in Chapter 2. A complete impairment evaluation provides valuable 
information beyond an impairment percentage, and it includes a 
discussion about the person’s abilities and limitations, including the ability 
to perform common activities listed in Table 1-2. Combining the medical 
and nonmedical information, and including detailed information about 
essential work activities, if requested, is a basis for improved 
understanding of the degree to which the impairment may affect the 
individual’s work ability. 
Guides, § 1.12, p. 15.

Page 34 
 
Chapter 2, “Practical Application of the Guides,” provides the following with 
respect to the examiners’ roles and responsibilities: 
Full and complete reporting provides the best opportunity for physicians to 
explain health status and consequences to patients, other medical 
professionals, and other interested parties such as claims examiners and 
attorneys. Thorough documentation of medical findings and their impact 
will also ensure that reporting is fair and consistent and that individuals 
have the information needed to pursue any benefits to which they are 
entitled. 
Id., § 2.3, p. 18. Moreover, the Guides state, “A clear, accurate, and complete report is 
essential to support a rating of permanent impairment.” Id., § 2.6, p. 21. 
To that end, the Guides lay out a list of elements that “should be included in all 
impairment evaluation reports. Id. (emphasis in original). The first is a narrative history 
of medical condition(s) consisting of 
the onset and course of the condition, symptoms, findings on previous 
examination(s), treatments, and responses to treatment, including adverse 
effects. Include information that may be relevant to onset, such as an 
occupational exposure or injury. Historical information should refer to any 
relevant investigations. Include detailed list of prior evaluations in the 
clinical data section. 
Id., § 2.6a.1, p. 21. 
In addition, the Guides direct a physician to “[a]ssess current clinical status, 
including current symptoms, review of symptoms, physical examination, and a list of 
contemplated treatment, rehabilitation, and any anticipated reevaluation.” Id., § 2.6a.3, 
p. 21. They also direct inclusion of discussion of diagnostic study results and 
outstanding pertinent diagnostic studies, whether the injured employee is at MMI, and 
diagnoses and impairments. Id., §§ 2.6a.2–2.6a.7, pp. 21–22. Moreover, the Guides 
state a report should discuss impairment rating criteria, prognosis, residual function, and 
limitations; calculation of the impairment rating; and discussion of how the impairment 
rating was calculated. Id., §§ 2.6a.8, 2.6b, 2.6c. p. 22. 
The Guides also state that other elements may be included in an impairment 
evaluation that include “a work history with detailed, chronological description of work 
activities, specific type and duration of work performed, materials used in the workplace, 
any temporal associations with the medical condition and work, frequency, intensity and 
duration of exposure and activities, and any protective measures” as well as “discussion 
of causation and apportionment.” Id., §§ 2.6a.4, 2.6a.6, pp. 21–22. As discussed above, 
an Iowa Court of Appeals panel has opined that causation is effectively a zero percent 
impairment rating and therefore opinions on causation are reimbursable and the agency 
has followed this holding.

The Guides reflect what agency experience and expertise reinforces: The IME 
report is important in the determination of an employee’s entitlement to workers’ 
compensation benefits, especially in contested cases before the agency. The AMA has 
established criteria in the Guides so that doctors are providing the information injured 
workers, employers, insurance carriers, and tribunals such as the agency need to 
equitably determine an individual’s entitlement to workers’ compensation benefits based 
on the injury they sustained and the disability the injured has caused. Presumably, this 
is why the legislature mandated use of the Guides when determining permanent 
impairment. Consequently, this mandate cannot be ignored when determining what is 
reimbursable as a reasonable cost under section 85.39(2). The costs that stem from a 
physician following the Guides in accordance with the requirement in section 85.34(2)(x) 
when performing an IME under section 85.39(2) are therefore reasonable. 
A worker’s right to an IME under section 85.39 for a compensable work injury is 
only triggered when a doctor chosen by the employer opines on causation or 
permanency. See Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d at 851 (citing Kern v. Fenchel, Doster, & Buck, No. 
20-1206, 2021 WL 3890603, at *4 (Iowa Ct. App. Sept. 1, 2021) (for the proposition 
“there can be no disability determination arising out a disability evaluation without a 
determination there was causation”). The worker is entitled to an IME if the worker 
believes the opinion is too low. For purposes of IME reimbursement, an opinion of “no 
causation” such as that issued by Dr. Westpheling is the functional equivalent to an 
opinion of no permanent disability. See Kern, 2021 WL 3890603, at *4 (Iowa Ct. App. 
Sept. 1, 2021) (holding “the clear effect of [a] no-causation determination was a finding 
of no compensable permanent disability”). Wagner arranged for the IME by Dr. Taylor in 
response to the report on causation and permanency issued by Dr. Westpheling. 
Therefore, this statutory requirement is also met. 
As Dr. Westpheling observed, the stack of documents relating to Wagner’s injury 
and treatment history was about one foot tall—voluminous even by workers’ 
compensation standards. Dr. Taylor reviewed all of those records and obtained a history 
from Wagner in accordance with the Guides. Dr. Taylor’s report reflects an IME and 
report that substantially comply with Chapter 2 of the Guides. Further, Dr. Taylor 
performed his evaluation of Wagner’s permanent impairment to the spine in accordance 
with the DRE and ROM methods set forth in the Guides. Based on the undersigned’s 
experience, Dr. Taylor’s $6,160.00 fee is reasonable given what doctors in the Cedar 
Rapids area typically charge in cases that include such an extensive injury and 
treatment history.
