STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Sonya Louderback, claimant, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from the Second Injury Fund of Iowa as defendant.  Hearing was 
held via Zoom videoconference on January 10, 2024.     
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.  
Sonya Louderback was the only witness to testify live at trial.  The evidentiary 
record also includes joint exhibits 1-10, claimant’s exhibits 1-3, and defendant’s exhibits 
A-L.  All exhibits were received without objection.  The evidentiary record closed at the 
conclusion of the arbitration hearing.       
The parties submitted post-hearing briefs on February 9, 2024, at which time the 
case was fully submitted to the undersigned.     
ISSUES 
The parties submitted the following issues for resolution: 
1. The extent of claimant’s entitlement to benefits from the Second Injury Fund 
(“Fund”) of Iowa. 
2. The amount of credit the Fund is entitled to. 
3. The appropriate commencement date for benefits from the Fund.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 
Section 85.64 governs Second Injury Fund liability.  Before liability of the Fund is 
triggered, three requirements must be met.  First, the employee must have lost or lost 
the use of a hand, arm, foot, leg, or eye.  Second, the employee must sustain a loss or 
loss of use of another specified member or organ through a compensable injury.  Third, 
permanent disability must exist as to both the initial injury and the second injury.   
 
The Second Injury Fund Act exists to encourage the hiring of handicapped 
persons by making a current employer responsible only for the amount of disability 
related to an injury occurring while that employer employed the handicapped individual 
as if the individual had had no preexisting disability.  See Anderson v. Second Injury 
Fund, 262 N.W.2d 789 (Iowa 1978); 15 Iowa Practice, Workers’ Compensation, Lawyer, 
Section 17:1, p. 211 (2014-2015). 
 
The Fund is responsible for the industrial disability present after the second injury 
that exceeds the disability attributable to the first and second injuries.  Section 85.64.  
Second Injury Fund of Iowa v. Braden, 459 N.W.2d 467 (Iowa 1990); Second Injury 
Fund v. Neelans, 436 N.W.2d 355 (Iowa 1989); Second Injury Fund v. Mich Coal Co., 
274 N.W.2d 300 (Iowa 1979).

In the present case, the parties stipulated that claimant sustained a prior 
qualifying loss to her right arm on October 11, 2005 and a compensable loss to the left 
arm on November 16, 2018.  (Hearing Report, numbered paragraph 10) The Fund does 
not dispute that claimant is entitled to industrial loss as a result of the combined effect of 
her upper extremity injuries.  Rather, the Fund disputes that claimant has demonstrated 
that she sustained significant industrial loss.  (Def. Brief, p. 19)   
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony.  The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question.  The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances.  The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); 
Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995).  Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994).  Unrebutted expert medical 
testimony cannot be summarily rejected.  Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 
N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 1994). 
Since claimant has an impairment to the body as a whole, an industrial disability 
has been sustained.  Industrial disability was defined in Diederich v. Tri-City R. Co., 219 
Iowa 587, 258 N.W. 899 (1935) as follows:  "It is therefore plain that the legislature 
intended the term 'disability' to mean 'industrial disability' or loss of earning capacity and 
not a mere 'functional disability' to be computed in the terms of percentages of the total 
physical and mental ability of a normal man." 
Functional impairment is an element to be considered in determining industrial 
disability which is the reduction of earning capacity, but consideration must also be 
given to the injured employee's age, education, qualifications, experience, motivation, 
loss of earnings, severity and situs of the injury, work restrictions, inability to engage in 
employment for which the employee is fitted and the employer's offer of work or failure 
to so offer.  McSpadden v. Big Ben Coal Co., 288 N.W.2d 181 (Iowa 1980); Olson v. 
Goodyear Service Stores, 255 Iowa 1112, 125 N.W.2d 251 (1963); Barton v. Nevada 
Poultry Co., 253 Iowa 285, 110 N.W.2d 660 (1961).

Compensation for permanent partial disability shall begin at the termination of the 
healing period.  Compensation shall be paid in relation to 500 weeks as the disability 
bears to the body as a whole.  Section 85.34. 
Having considered claimant’s age, proximity to retirement, educational 
background, employment history, ability to return to work or retrain, permanent 
impairment rating, permanent restrictions, motivation, and all other factors of industrial 
disability identified by the Iowa Supreme Court, I found that claimant proved a 50 
percent loss of future earning capacity as a result of the combined effect of her upper 
extremity injuries. This is the equivalent of a 50 percent industrial disability.  Industrial 
disability benefits are paid as a percentage of 500 weeks.  Iowa Code section 
85.34(2)(v).  Therefore, I conclude claimant is entitled to an award of 250 weeks of 
permanent partial disability benefits.

The Iowa Court of Appeals has held that the Fund’s credit should be the 
amount actually paid to a claimant when that amount exceeds the 
employer’s statutory liability.  See, e.g., Second Injury Fund v. Greenman, 
725 N.W.2d 658, *5-6 (Iowa Ct. App. 2006); Carberry v. Second Injury 
Fund, File No. 5020052 (Rehearing Dec. Feb. 4, 2009) (stating the 
compensable value of the scheduled loss for purposes of granting the 
Fund credit is the amount of the settlement when the settlement is in 
excess of the employer’s statutory liability under Iowa Code §86.34).   
Further, this agency has held the Fund may be entitled to take additional 
credit for impairment ratings concerning unpled injuries involving the same 
scheduled member(s) as the scheduled member(s) pled by the claimant.  
See Hunt v. Second Injury Fund of Iowa, File No. 1650632.03 (App. 
3/1/23); Harrell v. Denver Findley & Sons, Inc. and Second Injury Fund of 
Iowa, File No. 5066742 (App. 10/6/20); Meader v. Second Injury Fund of

Cortese in Hunt, supra, ‘[t]his Agency has also provided that it would 
create an unfair double standard to allow a claimant to establish a 
qualifying first injury without an impairment rating, while simultaneously 
not allowing the Fund to assert a credit for a qualifying injury without an 
impairment rating.”  Id., at p. 5. 
(Fund brief, pp. 25-26) 
 
Additionally, the Fund also argues that the principles to avoid double recovery 
dictate allowing the Fund credit for prior injuries.  See Gregory v. Second Injury Fund of 
Iowa, 777 N.W.2d 395, 401 (Iowa 2010); Warren Properties v. Stewart, 864 N.W.2d 
307, 315-16 (Iowa 2015).  The Fund contends that if claimant’s past injuries are to be 
taken into consideration in her industrial disability claim, then it is only fair to allow the 
Fund credit for the past PPD benefits she was paid.   
In the present case, the claimant has sustained multiple injuries involving her 
right and left upper extremities.  Claimant admits that she has received PPD benefits for 
all the impairment ratings assigned by treating physicians throughout the years.  There 
is no dispute that she has been compensated for all of her permanent impairment 
ratings to her right and left upper extremities. 
I find the Fund’s argument and legal authority to be persuasive.  Claimant has 
been compensated for the impairment ratings that were assigned to her left and right 
upper extremities, the same scheduled members which are the basis of the claim 
against the Fund.  In the present case the first alleged date of injury plead by the 
claimant is October 11, 2005.  Although the claimant was assigned and paid 4 percent 
impairment of each upper extremity in connection with the October 11, 2015 injury, 
when she filed her petition against the Fund, she opted to only plead the right upper 
extremity.  Limiting the Fund’s credit to only the dates of injuries and scheduled 
members a claimant opted to plead against the Fund, would allow the claimant to 
control the amount of the Fund’s credit.  Permitting the claimant to essentially determine

the amount of the Fund’s credit appears fundamentally unfair and contrary to the

is entitled to 132.5 weeks of credit as set forth above.   
Finally, the commencement date for benefits from the Fund must be determined.  
The Fund’s obligation to pay benefits begins after the expiration of the employer’s 
liability.  See Iowa Code section 85.64(1); see also Warner v. The Scotts Miracle-Gro 
Company and Second Injury Fund of Iowa, File No. 5041291 (Arb. 10/4/13).  Prior to the 
hearing, with respect to the November 16, 2018 date of injury, Whirlpool paid claimant 
75 weeks of benefits which is the equivalent of Dr. Buckwalter’s 30 percent left upper 
extremity rating.  (Hearing Report, numbered paragraph 9)  Dr. Buckwalter’s last MMI 
opinion was issued on August 16, 2022 and he issued a 30 percent (75 weeks) left arm 
impairment rating.  (JE2, pp. 96-97) According to the November 16, 2018 payment log, 
Whirlpool reinitiated PPD benefits on August 16, 2022 in connection with Dr. 
Buckwalter’s rating.  (Def. Ex. K, p. 93)  Seventy-five weeks after August 16, 2022 is 
January 23, 2024.  Thus, the Fund’s benefit commencement date is January 23, 2024.     
Claimant is entitled to 250 weeks of permanent partial disability benefits, less the 
Fund’s credit of 132.5 weeks of Fund benefits (250 weeks minus 132.5 weeks) for a 
total of 117.5 weeks commencing on January 23, 2024.
