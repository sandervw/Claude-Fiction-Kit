STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Areli Brus, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ compensation 
benefits against Tyson Fresh Meats, Inc., a self-insured employer, for an alleged injury

submitted as of September 29, 2025. The record consists of Joint Exhibits 1-4,

rate injury to this nerve, you would have to state that you are rating using

state you cannot determine the maximum value of the nerve from the Fifth 
Edition.  However, in the Second Edition, Table 13, Unilateral Spinal

impairment for loss of sensation and/or for pain.  In the Sixth Edition,

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury actually occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 
employment. Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 150 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. 
Dial Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309, 311 (Iowa 1996). The words “arising out of” refer to the 
cause or source of the injury. The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 
circumstances of the injury. 2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124, 128 (Iowa 
1995).  An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists 
between the injury and the employment. Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. The injury must 
be a rational consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely 
incidental to the employment. Koehler Elec. v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1, 3 (Iowa 2000); 
Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it 
happens within a period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may 
be when performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties 
or doing an activity incidental to them. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d at 150. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
A possibility of causation is not sufficient; a probability is necessary. Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996) (citing Holmes v. Bruce 
Motor Freight, Inc., 215 N.W.2d 296, 297 (Iowa 1974)). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 

BRUS V. TYSON FRESH MEATS, INC. 
Page 11 
 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 
621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 
(Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
Because claimant has returned to work, earning as much or more than she did at 
the time of the injury, she falls under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v) which mandates 
that even nonscheduled injuries be evaluated by strict adherence to the 5th Edition 
AMA Guides.  
Under the Iowa Workers' Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is 
compensated either for a loss or loss of use of a scheduled member under Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(a)-(u) or as an unscheduled injury pursuant to the provisions of section 
85.34(2)(v). The extent of scheduled member disability benefits to which an injured 
worker is entitled is determined by using the functional method. Functional disability is 
"limited to the loss of the physiological capacity of the body or body part.” Mortimer v. 
Fruehauf Corp., 502 N.W.2d 12, 15 (Iowa 1993); Sherman v. Pella Corp., 576 N.W.2d 
312, 320 (Iowa 1998).   
Iowa Code section 85.34(x) states:  
x. In all cases of permanent partial disability described in paragraphs 
“a” through “u”, or paragraph “v” when determining functional disability and 
not loss of earning capacity, the extent of loss or percentage of permanent 
impairment shall be determined solely by utilizing the guides to the 
evaluation of permanent impairment, published by the American medical 
association, as adopted by the workers' compensation commissioner by 
rule pursuant to chapter 17A. Lay testimony or agency expertise shall not 
be utilized in determining loss or percentage of permanent impairment 
pursuant to paragraphs “a” through “u”, or paragraph “v” when determining 
functional disability and not loss of earning capacity.  
Iowa Code section 85.34 (x).  
This agency has adopted the Guides to the Evaluation of Permanent Impairment, 
Fifth Edition, published by the American Medical Association for determining the extent 
of loss or percentage of impairment for permanent partial disabilities.  See 876 IAC 2.4.

RATE OF WEEKLY BENEFITS.  
Claimant asserts any weekly wage calculation that is based on under 40 hours a 
week worked is not a representative week. 
The basis of compensation is the weekly earnings of the employee at the time of 
the injury.  Iowa Code § 85.36 (2015).  Weekly earnings are the gross salary, wages, or 
earnings to which an employee would have been entitled had the employee worked the 
customary hours for the full pay period in which the employee was injured as the 
employer regularly required for the work or employment.  (Id.)  The various subsections 
of section 85.36 set forth methods of computing weekly earnings depending upon the 
type of earnings and employment.  (Id.) 
If the employee is paid on a daily or hourly basis or by output, weekly earnings 
are computed by dividing by thirteen the earnings over the thirteen-week period 
immediately preceding the injury.  Iowa Code § 85.36(6) (2015).  Any week that does 
not fairly reflect the employee’s customary earnings is excluded, however.  (Id.) 
 As discussed in the Findings of Fact, almost one-quarter of the 13 weeks 
preceding the date of injury were less than 40 hours. Almost one-fourth of the 13 weeks 
contained weeks without overtime work. In some of the weeks, claimant used holiday or 
vacation pay to meet the 40 hour a week threshold, but in three cases she did not. It is 
found that the weeks where claimant did not work 40 hours were customary weeks and 
the wage rate of the defendant is adopted herein as a more accurate representation of 
the customary wages of the claimant. See Acosta v. Tyson Fresh Foods, Inc., File No. 
1663215.01 (Arb. Dec., Sept. 15, 2022) (including weeks below 40 hours when they fall 
within the range of customary weeks for the claimant) Based on defendant’s evidence, 
claimant’s gross weekly wages at the time of the August 9, 2021, injury were $911.42. It 
was stipulated claimant was single and entitled to three exemptions. Based on those 
foregoing numbers, claimant’s weekly benefit rate is $593.58.  
CONTINUED MEDICAL CARE  
For all injuries compensable under Iowa Code chapter 85 or 85A, the employer 
must “furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, chiropractic, podiatric, 
physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance, and hospital services and supplies therefor  
and shall allow reasonably necessary transportation expenses incurred for such 
services.” Iowa Code Ann. § 85.27(1). This responsibility is coupled with the right to 
choose care for the employee’s work injury. (Id.) at § 85.27(4). “[A]n employer who 
authorizes care is responsible for the cost of care up to the time when the employer 
notifies the employee it is no longer authorizing care.” Ramirez-Trujillo v. Quality Egg, 
L.L.C., 878 N.W.2d 759, 772 (Iowa 2016). 
 
The statute obligates the employer to monitor the care and determine if, and 
when, it no longer wishes to authorize care. (Id.) at 771. “An employer can easily 
reconsider whether it wishes to authorize further care when an authorized medical 
provider indicates an employee requires no further care for a workplace injury or when 

BRUS V. TYSON FRESH MEATS, INC. 
Page 13 
 
the employer authorizes a new provider to take over an employee’s care.” (Id.) at 772. 
“Section 85.27(4) balances this minimal burden with a significant corresponding 
benefit—a means of extinguishing the employer’s ongoing obligation to pay for medical 
expenses following its acknowledgment of compensability and exercise of the right to 
choose care.” (Id.) 
While claimant’s ratable impairment under the AMA Guides is 0 percent, the 
evidence supports a finding that she is limited in her function by pain. Both FCEs 
determined that claimant gave maximal effort. There is no suggestion that her pain 
complaints are not credible. Even defendant’s therapist administering the FCE found 
her subjective complaints to match her exertions during the testing. Dr. Martin also does 
not disagree that claimant has pain complaints; only that those complaints do not factor 
into his impairment assessment.  
Under Iowa law, the employer is required to provide care to an injured employee 
and is permitted to choose the care.  Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 
562 N.W.2d 433, 435 (Iowa 1997). 
[T]he employer is obliged to furnish reasonable services and supplies to 
treat an injured employee, and has the right to choose the care.  The 
treatment must be offered promptly and be reasonably suited to treat the 
injury without undue inconvenience to the employee.  If the employee has 
reason to be dissatisfied with the care offered, the employee should 
communicate the basis of such dissatisfaction to the employer, in writing if 
requested, following which the employer and the employee may agree to 
alternate care reasonably suited to treat the injury.  If the employer and 
employee cannot agree on such alternate care, the commissioner may, 
upon application and reasonable proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and 
order other care.  (Id.) 
 
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  Long v. 
Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122, 123 (Iowa 1995).  Determining what care is 
reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.  (Id.)  The employer’s obligation turns 
on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.  (Id.) at 124; Harned v. 
Farmland Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98, 101 (Iowa 1983).  In Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co., 
562 N.W.2d at 437, the court approvingly quoted Bowles v. Los Lunas Schools, 109 
N.M. 100, 106, 781 P.2d 1178, 1184 (N.M. Ct. App. 1989): 
[T]he words “reasonable” and “adequate” appear to describe the same 
standard. 
[The New Mexico rule] requires the employer to provide a certain standard 
of care and excuses the employer from any obligation to provide other 
services only if that standard is met.  We construe the terms "reasonable” 
and “adequate” as describing care that is both appropriate to the injury and 
sufficient to bring the worker to maximum recovery.

The commissioner is justified in ordering alternate care when employer-authorized care 
has not been effective, and evidence shows that such care is “inferior or less extensive” 
care than other available care requested by the employee.  Long, 528 N.W.2d at 124; 
Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 437. 
At the current time, defendant is proffering no care.  Claimant has expressed her 
disagreement with this position. No care is not reasonable in this circumstance. 
Claimant’s request for pain management is granted. Further, her past medication costs 
are reimbursable as they are reasonably necessary expenses.  
There was no explanation given to claimant why her pain management treatment 
was discontinued or her medications cut off. There was no evidence offered at hearing 
that Dr. Archer or anyone at Dunes Pain ordered the medication or treatment ended. 
Claimant testified both the medication and the injections relieved her pain to some

Claimant has satisfied the elements of an alternate care claim. She has

credible ongoing complaints of pain and dysfunction as a result of her work injury, she is 
entitled to ongoing treatment including, but not limited to, pain management. If the 
defendant continues to refuse care, she is free to choose her own care at the 
defendant’s expense.  
ASSESSMENT OF IME COSTS  
Iowa Code section 85.39 governs examinations of injured employees to 
determine the extent of permanent impairment or disability caused by a work injury. Mid 
American Const. LLC v. Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d 838, 847–48 (Iowa 2024). Under subsection 
1, the employee must attend an examination by a physician of the employer’s choice or 
be disqualified from benefits for the period of refusal. An opinion on causation or the 
extent of permanent impairment or disability by a doctor retained by the employer 
triggers the injured employee’s right to an IME under subsection 2. 
 
“In subsection 2, the legislature leveled the playing field between the employer 
and the employee.” Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d at 848. “The statutory process balances the 
competing interests of the employer and employee and permits the employee to obtain 
an independent medical examination at the employer’s expense.” Des Moines Area 
Reg. Transit Auth. v. Young, 867 N.W.2d 839, 844 (Iowa 2015). 
 
The evaluation made by the employer’s physician was too low. Iowa Code § 
85.39(2); Young, 867 N.W.2d at 844. The employee’s own examination “is known as an 
IME because the examination is independent of the examination done by the physician 
selected by the employer.” Young, 867 N.W.2d at 843–44. 
 
Subsection 2 further provides, “A determination of the reasonableness of a fee 
for an examination … shall be based on the typical fee charged by a medical provider to 
perform an impairment rating in the local area where the examination is conducted.” 

BRUS V. TYSON FRESH MEATS, INC. 
Page 15 
 
Under the Iowa Supreme Court’s holding in Sandlin, “The reasonableness of the fees 
will be analyzed based on what physicians in that area typically charge to perform the 
impairment rating examination—which may include ‘the costs of reviewing medical 
records, conducting a physical examination, opining on causation, assessing permanent 
impairment, assigning restrictions, and addressing further treatment recommendations.’” 
2 N.W.3d at 851–52 (quoting Turner v. NCI Bldg. Sys., Inc., Iowa Workers’ Comp. 
Comm’r No. 1652235.01, 2022 WL 1787301, at *26 (Feb. 24, 2022)). This analysis 
under the statute “effectively necessitates fact-finding by the commissioner on the fees 
typically charged in the local area where the examination is performed. Parties may 
offer evidence of the fees typically charged in that locality to support or challenge the 
fee charged in a particular case.” (Id.) at 849. The court concluded “‘many IMEs come 
before the agency, which allows the [commissioner] to use agency experience and 
expertise to conclude that [the physician’s] charges are in line with those of an 
occupational medicine doctor with a practice in the ... [local] area performing an 
evaluation of permanent impairment in accordance with the [AMA] Guides.’” (Id.) 
(quoting Nguyen v. Hy-Vee, Iowa Workers’ Comp. Comm’r, No. 21003118.03, 2023 WL 
7128682, at *32 (Oct. 20, 2023)). 
Claimant seeks reimbursement of Dr. Bansal’s IME and the FCE pursuant to 
§85.39 and/or Adm. Rule 876-4.33(86).  
“All costs incurred in the hearing before the commissioner shall be taxed in the 
discretion of the commissioner.” Iowa Code § 10A.3280. “Fee-shifting statutes using ‘all 
costs’ language have been construed ‘to limit reimbursement for litigation expenses to 
those allowed as taxable court costs.’” Des Moines Area Reg. Transit Auth. v. Young, 
867 N.W.2d 839, 846 (Iowa 2015) (quoting City of Riverdale v. Diercks, 806 N.W.2d 
643, 660 (Iowa 2011)). Statutes and administrative rules providing for recovery of costs 
are strictly construed. (Id.) (quoting Hughes v. Burlington N. R.R., 545 N.W.2d 318, 321 
(Iowa 1996)).

2024)
