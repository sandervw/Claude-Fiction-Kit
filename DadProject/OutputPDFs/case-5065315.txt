Claimant Matthew Carfi filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from defendants Brand Energy Services, LLC, employer, and 
Illinois National Insurance Company, insurance carrier.  This matter was heard in Cedar 
Rapids, Iowa, on May 15, 2018, by Deputy Workers’ Compensation Commissioner 
Erica J. Fitch. 
On July 9, 2019, pursuant to Iowa Code section 17A.15(2), the Iowa Workers’ 
Compensation Commissioner delegated authority to the undersigned to enter a 
proposed in this matter due to the unavailability of Deputy Commissioner Fitch.   
I issued an arbitration decision on July 23, 2019.  In the decision, I concluded it 
was appropriate to issue my proposed decision without a rehearing pursuant to Iowa 
Code section 17A.15(2) because neither party argued demeanor was the operative 
decision-making factor in this case. 
Claimant appealed the arbitration decision.  Among other issues, he asserted the 
decision was in violation of Iowa Code section 17A.15(2) because his demeanor and 
credibility were a substantial factor in his case. 
On April 23, 2020, the Commissioner ordered claimant to show cause as to why 
portions of the hearing involving demeanor should be reheard.  After responsive 
pleadings by the parties, the Commissioner issued an Order for Rehearing on May 22, 
2020, that remanded the case back to me to rehear the portions of the case involving 
demeanor.  
The rehearing was conducted on August 5, 2020, by the undersigned via 
CourtCall.  The only evidence introduced at rehearing was claimant’s testimony.  The

ISSUE 
Whether claimant’s testimony in the August 5, 2020 rehearing regarding 
demeanor impacted or changed the findings of fact or conclusions of law contained in 
the proposed July 23, 2019 arbitration decision. 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
On rehearing, claimant’s testimony on direct examination focused primarily on 
the gradual onset of his symptoms and what he reported to his employer.  This 
testimony was generally consistent with his earlier testimony; claimant did not appear to 
embellish or exaggerate his earlier testimony. 
During cross-examination on rehearing, defendants’ counsel asked several 
questions regarding discrepancies between claimant’s earlier testimony and the medical 
records.  Many of these questions highlighted the contradictions I identified in the 
arbitration decision.  Throughout the course of these questions, however, claimant 
remained calm and respectful.  He was not animated or argumentative.  Though he may 
not have been as concise or straightforward as he could have been on cross-
examination, I was not given the impression that claimant was attempting to conceal 
information. 
Ultimately, therefore, after listening to and observing claimant on rehearing, I find 
nothing about claimant’s demeanor was alarming or concerning.  In other words, 
claimant’s demeanor does not reflect particularly negatively or positively on his case; it 
is essentially a neutral factor. 
Though I did not find claimant’s demeanor to be concerning, this finding does not 
change or impact any of the findings contained in the arbitration decision.  Nothing 
about claimant’s demeanor “explained away” the inconsistencies I identified in my 
arbitration decision.  For example, in the arbitration decision, I found as follows: 
The absence of any discussion by claimant’s treating physicians about his 
work duties as a possible source of or contributing factor to his condition is 
troubling, as are the contradictions in the record regarding claimant’s 
descriptions of the timing and onset of his symptoms.  Given Dr. Bansal’s 
failure to address these issues in his report, I do not find his opinions 
convincing.   
Without a credible expert opinion or corroborating references to his work 
duties in the medical records, I find insufficient evidence that claimant’s 
spine condition was aggravated by his work duties. 
(Arbitration Decision, page 6)  Again, while claimant’s demeanor was not a negative 
factor in his case, the concerns I identified remain.

Thus, because claimant’s testimony on rehearing regarding demeanor does not 
impact or change my findings in the arbitration decision, the findings of fact in the 
arbitration decision are adopted in their entirety. 
CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
I concluded in the arbitration decision that claimant failed to carry his burden to 
prove he sustained a cumulative injury that arose out of and in the course of his 
employment.  As I explained in the arbitration decision: 
In this case, claimant offered the report of Dr. Bansal, who opined 
claimant’s work for defendant-employer was a substantial contributing 
factor in permanently aggravating claimant’s degenerative spine condition.  
As explained above, however, I did not find Dr. Bansal’s opinion 
convincing.  Dr. Bansal failed to explain the absence of any discussion 
regarding claimant’s work duties in his treatment records and the 
contradictions in claimant’s descriptions of the timing and onset of his 
symptoms.  Without a credible expert opinion, I found insufficient evidence 
that claimant’s work activities aggravated his pre-existing spine 
condition. . . . 
Claimant is critical of the opinions offered by Dr. Foster and Dr. Bansal.  
Claimant asserts Dr. Chen failed to address whether claimant’s work 
duties for defendant-employer were a substantial contributing factor in 
aggravating his pre-existing spine condition.  While claimant is correct that 
Dr. Chen did not specifically address whether claimant’s employment was 
“a substantial contributing factor” to the aggravation, it is claimant—not 
defendants—who ultimately bears the burden of proof, and I did not find 
claimant’s expert persuasive.   
Furthermore, Dr. Chen’s opinion that there was no evidence in claimant’s 
history, physical examination, or studies to establish that his condition was 
the result of cumulative trauma from his employment is more consistent 
with claimant’s treatment records—which, as discussed in detail above, 
are without any significant mention of his work duties as a possible source 
of or contributing factor to his symptoms.  Thus, claimant’s criticisms of 
defendants’ experts do not impact my determination that claimant failed to 
satisfy his burden to prove he sustained a cumulative injury that arose out 
of and in the course of his employment.   
(Arb. Dec., p. 8) 
Ultimately, nothing about claimant’s testimony on rehearing regarding demeanor 
impacted or changed any of the above-stated analysis or my conclusion that claimant 
failed to prove he sustained a work-related cumulative injury.

With the additional conclusions stated above, the conclusions of law in the

Both at the outset and conclusion of the rehearing, claimant’s counsel asked me 
to address the potential cost of the transcript of the August 5, 2020 proceeding.  I 
interpret claimant’s counsel’s requests as a motion to fix the reasonable costs of the 
hearing transcript.  
Iowa Code section 86.19 provides in part: 
The workers' compensation commissioner, or a deputy commissioner, 
may appoint or may direct a party to furnish at the party's initial expense a 
certified shorthand reporter to be present and report, or to furnish 
mechanical means to record, and if necessary, transcribe proceedings of 
any contested case under this chapter, chapters 85 and 85A and fix the 
reasonable amount of compensation for such service. The charges shall 
be taxed as costs and the party initially paying the expense of the 
presence or transcription shall be reimbursed. The reporter shall faithfully 
and accurately report the proceedings. 
Iowa Code § 86.19(1).  This section allows a deputy commissioner to fix the reasonable 
cost of the services of a court reporter. 
Relying on Iowa Code section 86.19, claimant’s motion is granted.  I find, based 
upon agency expertise and experience, that a reasonable fee for a copy of a transcript 
is $1.75 per page.

The arbitration decision on July 23, 2019 is adopted in its entirety with the 
above-stated additional findings of fact and conclusions of law. 
The maximum that may be charged for a copy of a transcript in this case is one 
and 75/100 dollars ($1.75) per page.
