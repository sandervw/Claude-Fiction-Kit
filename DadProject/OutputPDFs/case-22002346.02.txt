STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Brittany Meyer, claimant, filed a petition for arbitration against RR Donnelley & 
Sons Company, as the employer, and AUI Insurance Company, as the insurance 
carrier.  This case came before the undersigned for an arbitration hearing on July 10, 
2023.  Pursuant to the hearing assignment order, this hearing was held via a Zoom 
conference call with all participants appearing remotely. 
The parties filed a hearing report prior to the commencement of the hearing.  On 
the hearing report, the parties entered into numerous stipulations.  Those stipulations 
were accepted and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be 
made or discussed.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations. 
The evidentiary record includes Joint Exhibits 1 through 7, Claimant’s Exhibits 1 
through 10, and Defendants’ Exhibits A though J.  All exhibits were received without 
objection.   
Claimant testified on her own behalf and called Jacob Sanders to testify.  
Defendants called Matthew Mason and Craig Wolthoff to testify.  The evidentiary record 
closed at the conclusion of the evidentiary hearing on July 10, 2023.   
However, counsel for the parties requested an opportunity to file post-hearing 
briefs.  This request was granted and the parties filed briefs on August 24 and 25, 2023.  
The case was considered fully submitted to the undersigned on August 25, 2023.

ISSUES 
The parties completed a hearing report prior to the commencement of the 
hearing and submitted the following disputed issues for resolution: 
1. 
Whether claimant sustained an injury that arose out of and in the course of 
her employment on January 22, 2022. 
2. 
Whether claimant is entitled to an award of temporary disability, or healing

Whether the alleged work injury caused permanent disability and, if so, the

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The initial disputed issue submitted by the parties is whether Ms. Meyer proved 
she sustained an injury that arose out of and in the course of her employment on 
January 22, 2022.   
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury actually occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 
employment.  Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. Dial 
Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309 (Iowa 1996).  The words “arising out of” refer to the cause or 
source of the injury.  The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 
circumstances of the injury.  2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124 (Iowa 1995).  
An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists between the 
injury and the employment.  Miedema, 551 N.W.2d 309.  The injury must be a rational 
consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely incidental to 
the employment.  Koehler Elec. v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1 (Iowa 2000); Miedema, 551 
N.W.2d 309.  An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it happens within a 
period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may be when 
performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties or doing 
an activity incidental to them.  Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143. 
 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based. A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause. A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible. George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996). 
 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.

Page 8 
 
 
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); 
Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995). Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted expert medical 
testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 
N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 1994). 
 
A personal injury contemplated by the workers’ compensation law means an 
injury, the impairment of health or a disease resulting from an injury which comes about, 
not through the natural building up and tearing down of the human body, but because of 
trauma.  The injury must be something that acts extraneously to the natural processes 
of nature and thereby impairs the health, interrupts or otherwise destroys or damages a 
part or all of the body.  Although many injuries have a traumatic onset, there is no 
requirement for a special incident or an unusual occurrence.  Injuries which result from 
cumulative trauma are compensable.  Increased disability from a prior injury, even if 
brought about by further work, does not constitute a new injury, however.  St. Luke’s 
Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); Ellingson v. Fleetguard, Inc., 599 N.W.2d 
440 (Iowa 1999); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 
1995); McKeever Custom Cabinets v. Smith, 379 N.W.2d 368 (Iowa 1985).  An 
occupational disease covered by chapter 85A is specifically excluded from the definition 
of personal injury.  Iowa Code section 85.61(4)(b); Iowa Code section 85A.8; Iowa Code 
section 85A.14. 
Ultimately, I found claimant’s testimony to be credible and also found the medical 
opinions of Dr. Manshadi to be the most convincing and credible in this evidentiary 
record.  Having made those findings, I also found that claimant proved by a 
preponderance of the evidence that her left cubital tunnel syndrome and left ulnar nerve 
entrapment at the wrist (but not the ganglion cyst in her left wrist) arose out of and in the 
course of her employment on January 22, 2022.  Therefore, I conclude Ms. Meyer 
proved a compensable work injury. 
Claimant seeks an award of temporary disability benefits, or healing period.  
Section 85.34(1) provides that healing period benefits are payable to an injured worker 
who has suffered permanent partial disability until (1) the worker has returned to work; 
(2) the worker is medically capable of returning to substantially similar employment; or 
(3) the worker has achieved maximum medical recovery.  The healing period can be 
considered the period during which there is a reasonable expectation of improvement of 
the disabling condition.  See Armstrong Tire & Rubber Co. v. Kubli, 312 N.W.2d 60 
(Iowa App. 1981).  Healing period benefits can be interrupted or intermittent.  Teel v. 
McCord, 394 N.W.2d 405 (Iowa 1986).

Ms. Meyer proved she was off work under medical restrictions caused by her 
work injury from January 31, 2022 through February 15, 2022 and from July 20, 2022 
through October 12, 2022.  I conclude claimant proved entitlement to healing period 
benefits during these periods. 
Defendants responded to the request for healing period benefits, asserting a 
claim for credit for payment of short-term disability benefits.  Iowa Code section 85.38(2) 
permits the employer a credit for payment of disability benefit.  However, Iowa Code 
section 85.38(2) establishes certain conditions that must be established to receive the 
credit.  Among those pre-requisites, or conditions, is the employer paid for the short-
term disability plan in whole or in part.  Section 85.38(2) does not permit a credit if the 
short-term disability benefits “would have been payable” even though there was a work-
related injury.  
I found that defendants did not prove the amount or dates such short-term 
disability benefits were paid.  Nor did defendants prove that the employer paid for the 
short-term disability benefits in whole or in part.  Finally, defendants failed to establish 
the terms of the short-term disability policy and establish that the benefits paid were not 
payable in addition to any worker’s compensation benefits.  Defendants failed to carry 
their burden of proof to establish any entitlement to a credit for payment of short-term 
disability benefits.  Iowa Code section 85.38(2). 
Ms. Meyer also seeks an award of permanent partial disability benefits for her 
injury.  The parties stipulate that the injury is confined to the left arm and results in a 
scheduled member injury.  (Hearing Report) 
Injuries to the arm are compensated on a 250-week schedule.  Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(m).  Injuries that result in less than a total loss of a scheduled member 
are awarded on a proportional basis.  Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(w)  Seven percent of 
the left arm, multiplied by 250 weeks, totals 17.5 weeks.  Therefore, I conclude claimant 
has proven entitlement to an award of 17.5 weeks of permanent partial disability 
benefits.  Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(m), (w).  The permanent partial disability benefits 
should commence on March 31, 2023 pursuant to the stipulation of the parties.  
(Hearing Report).

denial or delay in payment of benefits.  Ms. Meyer asserts that defendants 
unreasonably delayed and/or denied her weekly benefits in this case and that 
defendants should be ordered to pay penalty benefits pursuant to Iowa Code section 
10A.315(4).

In Christensen v. Snap-on Tools Corp., 554 N.W.2d 254 (Iowa 1996), and

employee is entitled to penalty benefits if there has been a delay in

reasonable cause or excuse exists if either (1) the delay was necessary 
for the insurer to investigate the claim or (2) the employer had a 
reasonable basis to contest the employee’s entitlement to benefits.  A 
“reasonable basis” for denial of the claim exists if the claim is “fairly

Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 260.

will defer to the decision of the commissioner.  See Christensen, 554

(7) An employer’s bare assertion that a claim is “fairly debatable” does 
not make it so.  A fair reading of Christensen and Robbennolt, makes it 
clear that the employer must assert facts upon which the commissioner 
could reasonably find that the claim was “fairly debatable.”  See 
Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 260. 
Meyers v. Holiday Express Corp., 557 N.W.2d 502 (Iowa 1996).   
Weekly compensation payments are due at the end of the compensation week.  
Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d 229, 235. 
Penalty is not imposed for delayed interest payments.  Davidson v. Bruce, 594 
N.W.2d 833, 840 (Iowa App. 1999).  Schadendorf v. Snap-On Tools Corp., 757 N.W.2d 
330, 338 (Iowa 2008).   
When an employee’s claim for benefits is fairly debatable based on a good faith 
dispute over the employee’s factual or legal entitlement to benefits, an award of penalty 
benefits is not appropriate under the statute.  Whether the issue was fairly debatable 
turns on whether there was a disputed factual dispute that, if resolved in favor of the 
employer, would have supported the employer's denial of compensability.  Gilbert v. 
USF Holland, Inc., 637 N.W.2d 194 (Iowa 2001). 
In this case, I found that defendants did not offer a reasonable excuse for the 
delay in payment of benefits between January 31, 2022 through February 15, 2022 and 
from July 20, 2022 through October 12, 2022.  Iowa Code section 10A.315(4)(b)(2).  
Defendants did not contemporaneously convey their bases for delay of benefits.  Iowa 
Code section 10A.315(4)(c)(3).  Defendants bore the burden to establish a reasonable 
basis, or excuse, and to prove the contemporaneous conveyance of those bases to the 
claimant.  Defendants failed to carry their burden of proof on the penalty issues, and a 
penalty award is appropriate.  Iowa Code section 10A.315. 
The purpose of Iowa Code section 10A.315 is both punishment for unreasonable 
conduct but also deterrence for future cases.  Id. at 237.  In this regard, the Commission 
is given discretion to determine the amount of the penalty imposed with a maximum 
penalty of 50 percent of the amount of the delayed, or denied, benefits.  Christensen v. 
Snap-On Tools Corp., 554 N.W.2d 254, 261 (Iowa 1996).   
In exercising its discretion, the agency must consider factors such as the length 
of the delays, the number of delays, the information available to the employer regarding 
the employee’s injury and wages, and the employer’s past record of penalties.  Meyers 
v. Holiday Express Corp., 557 N.W.2d 502, 505 (Iowa 1996).  Neither party put 
evidence on, or offered argument about, the employer’s past record of penalties. 
Having considered the relevant factors and the purposes of the penalty statute, I 
conclude that a section 10A.315 penalty in the amount of $2,000.00 is appropriate in 
this case.  This represents an amount less than 50 percent of the healing period

payment of benefits under these facts and should serve as a deterrent against future

Ms. Meyer introduced and requests an award of past medical expenses.  Having 
found that claimant failed to prove the ganglion cyst in her left wrist arose out of her 
employment, I found that the medical expenses specifically related to that condition are 
not causally connected to her work injury.  Specifically, I found that the medical 
expenses incurred with Dr. Timpe on May 18, 2022 as well as the outpatient expenses 
incurred on May 24, 2022 and the ultrasound guidance and aspiration charges incurred 
on May 24, 2022 do not arise out of claimant’s employment and are not owed by 
defendants.  Dr. Manshadi’s independent medical evaluation fees are dealt with 
separately below.  All other medical expenses listed in Claimant’s Exhibit 4, pages 15-
16, are causally related to the work injury.  Defendants are obligated to pay for the 
remainder of the expenses contained in Claimant’s Exhibit 4, pages 15-16, and must 
pay those expenses directly to claimant’s medical providers or reimburse any third-party 
payor for those expenses.  Iowa Code section 85.27; Krohn v. State, 420 N.W.2d 463 
(Iowa 1988). 
Claimant also asserted a claim for medical mileage.  Claimant’s Exhibit 8, page 
20 contains a summary of the claimed mileage.  Per the findings and conclusions noted 
above, claimant is not entitled to reimbursement for medical mileage incurred for 
treatment of the ganglion cyst on either May 18, 2022 or May 24, 2022.  Subtracting 
those miles and requested reimbursements from Claimant’s itemized mileage claim, I 
conclude claimant has established entitlement to medical mileage reimbursement 
totaling $906.60 through the date of hearing. 
Claimant seeks reimbursement of Dr. Manshadi’s independent medical 
evaluation fee pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.39(2).  Iowa Code section 85.39(2) 
imposes certain pre-requisites that must be met before claimant qualifies for 
reimbursement of her independent medical evaluation.  Among those pre-requisites is 
that “an evaluation of permanent disability has been made by a physician retained by 
the employer.”  Iowa Code section 85.39(2).  In this case, defendants did not obtain an 
evaluation of permanent disability from a physician of their choosing prior to Dr. 
Manshadi’s evaluation.  But see Kern v. Fenchel, Doster & Buck, P.L.C., 966 N.W.2d 
326 (Iowa App. 2021) (Table of Unpublished Decisions) (2021 WL 3890603). I conclude 
that claimant has not met the pre-requisites to qualify for reimbursement of Dr. 
Manshadi’s evaluation fees pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.39(2). 
Claimant asserts that she should be reimbursed for lost wages to attend the 
defendants’ independent medical evaluation with Dr. Paulson.  Indeed, Iowa Code 
section 85.39(1) provides, “If an employee is required to leave work for which the 
employee is being paid wages to attend the requested examination, the employee shall 
be compensated at the employee’s regular rate for the time the employee is required to 
leave work.” In this instance, claimant was not scheduled for work and was not “required

to leave work” to attend the defendants’ evaluation with Dr. Paulson.  Nor was claimant 
being paid wages at the time of the requested evaluation.  Nor did claimant establish 
her “regular rate” of pay at the time of the requested evaluation.  I conclude claimant 
failed to establish entitlement to payment, or reimbursement, of wages to attend the 
evaluation with Dr. Paulson.  Iowa Code section 85.39(1). 
Finally, claimant seeks assessment of costs.  Assessment of costs is a 
discretionary function of the agency.  Iowa Code section10A.328.  Claimant prevails and 
receives an award of permanent disability in this case.  Exercising this agency’s 
discretion, I conclude it is appropriate to assess claimant’s costs in some amount. 
Claimant seeks assessment of her filing fee ($103.00).  This is a reasonable cost 
and is permitted pursuant to 876 IAC 4.33(7).  Claimant also seeks assessment of the 
transcription cost of claimant’s deposition.  This case is limited to a scheduled member 
injury to the left arm.  I did not find the deposition transcript to be helpful.  Yet, 
defendants put the deposition transcript into evidence.  Therefore, I conclude it is 
reasonable to assess claimant’s costs ($157.50) relative to transcription of that 
deposition. 
Claimant’s final cost request is the independent medical evaluation fees from Dr. 
Manshadi.  Claimant seeks assessment of the entirety of the expense.  However, only 
that portion of the charges that constitute preparation of the report, which is introduced 
in lieu of Dr. Manshadi’s trial testimony, is permissible.  Des Moines Area Regional 
Transit Authority v. Young, 867 N.W.2d 839 (Iowa 2015).  
In this instance, Dr. Manshadi’s invoice lumps in his charges for drafting his 
report as well as reviewing medical records.  Again, only those charges incurred for 
drafting the report are permissible as costs.  Claimant has not done a good job of 
having Dr. Manshadi break down or itemize his charges.  I conclude that only $1,000.00 
of Dr. Manshadi’s charges should be assessed as costs related to drafting his report.
