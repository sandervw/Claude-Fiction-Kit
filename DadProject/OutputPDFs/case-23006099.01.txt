STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
The claimant, Robin Swick, filed a petition for arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from employer Hy-Vee, Inc. (“Hy-Vee”) and its insurer, Union 
Insurance Company of Providence.  Marvin Miller appeared on behalf of the claimant.  
Lindsey Mills appeared on behalf of the defendants.  Also present was a member of 
defendants’ counsel’s office, Austin Lower, and Hy-Vee’s representative, Jamie 
Renken. 
 
The matter came on for hearing on May 21, 2025, before Deputy Workers’ 
Compensation Commissioner Andrew M. Phillips.  Pursuant to an order of the Iowa 
Workers’ Compensation Commissioner, the hearing occurred electronically via Zoom.  
The hearing proceeded without difficulty.  
 
The record in this case consists of Joint Exhibits 1-7, and Defendants’ Exhibit A-
J.  During the hearing, the claimant attempted to offer several exhibits.  Claimant’s 
counsel was informed of the requirements of 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.19(3)(d) 
as well as the Hearing Assignment Order to file proposed exhibits at least 14 days in 
advance of the hearing date.  Thus, the exhibits offered by claimant were not included in 
the record.   
 
The claimant testified on her own behalf.  The claimant’s husband, Kelly Swick, 
also testified on behalf of the claimant.  Janis Lavorato was appointed the official 
reporter and custodian of the notes of the proceeding.  The matter was considered fully 
submitted following briefing by the parties on June 20, 2025.

STIPULATIONS 
 
Through the hearing report, as reviewed at the commencement of the hearing, 
the parties stipulated and/or established the following: 
1. There was an employer-employee relationship at the time of the alleged injury. 
  
2. That the claimant sustained an injury which arose out of, and in the course of, 
her employment on September 8, 2022. 
  
3. That the claimant’s gross earnings were seven hundred ninety-one and 00/100 
dollars ($791.00) per week, that the claimant was married and entitled to two 
exemptions, providing a weekly rate of compensation of five hundred thirty-two 
and 59/100 dollars ($532.59).   
 
4. That, prior to the hearing, the claimant was paid six thousand eight hundred 
twenty-nine and 70/100 dollars ($6,829.70) in weekly benefits. 
 
5. That the claimant was paid sick pay or disability income in the amount of six 
thousand sixteen and 92/100 dollars ($6,016.92), and that the defendants are 
entitled to a credit pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.38(2).  
 
The parties indicated that entitlement to temporary disability and/or healing period 
benefits and entitlement to permanent partial disability benefits were not in dispute.  
Medical benefits were also no longer in dispute. 
The parties are now bound by their stipulations.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa Rule of Appellate 
Procedure 6.904(3).   
Affirmative Defenses 
 
 
The hearing report indicates that the defendants asserted a number of affirmative 
defenses.  However, the defendants made no mention of these affirmative defenses in 
their posthearing brief.  I also find no evidence in the record that would be adequate to 
support their claims.  Therefore, I find that the defendants’ affirmative defenses fail. 
Temporary Disability 
There is a dispute as to whether or not the claimant’s September 8, 2022, work 
injury was a cause of temporary disability.  However, entitlement to temporary disability 
benefits was not in dispute. 
As a general rule, “temporary total disability compensation benefits and healing-
period compensation benefits refer to the same condition.”  Clark v. Vicorp Rest., Inc., 
696 N.W.2d 596 604 (Iowa 2005).  The purpose of temporary total disability benefits 
and healing period benefits is to “partially reimburse the employee for the loss of 
earnings” during a period of recovery from the condition.  Id.  The appropriate type of 
benefits depends on whether or not the employee has a permanent disability.  Dunlap v. 
Action Warehouse, 824 N.W.2d 545, 556 (Iowa Ct. App. 2012).   
When an injured worker has been unable to work during a period of recuperation 
from an injury that did not produce permanent disability, the worker is entitled to 
temporary total disability benefits during the time the worker is disabled by the injury.   
Iowa Code 85.33(1) provides 
...the employer shall pay to an employee for injury producing temporary 
total disability weekly compensation benefits, as provided in section 85.32, 
until the employee has returned to work or is medically capable of returning 
to employment substantially similar to the first employment in which the 
employee was engaged at the time of injury, whichever occurs first.   
Temporary total disability benefits cease when the employee returns to work, or 
is medically capable of returning to substantially similar employment. 
Healing period compensation describes temporary workers’ compensation 
weekly benefits that precede an allowance of permanent partial disability benefits.

Ellingson v. Fleetguard, Inc., 599 N.W.2d 440, 446 (Iowa 1999).  Healing period benefits 
are payable to an injured worker who has suffered permanent partial disability until the 
first to occur of three events.  Iowa Code § 85.34(1) (2015).  These are:  (1) the worker 
has returned to work; (2) the worker medically is capable of returning to substantially 
similar employment; or (3) the worker has achieved maximum medical recovery.  Id.  
Maximum medical recovery is achieved when healing is complete, and the extent of 
permanent disability can be determined.  Armstrong Tire & Rubber Co. v. Kubli, 312 
N.W.2d 60, 65 (Iowa Ct. App. 1981).  Neither maintenance medical care nor an 
employee continuing to have pain or other symptoms necessarily prolongs the healing 
period.  Id. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is 
probable, rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 
148, 153 (Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. 
App. 1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1996).    
A possibility of causation is not sufficient; a probability is necessary. Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996) (citing Holmes v. Bruce 
Motor Freight, Inc., 215 N.W.2d 296, 297 (Iowa 1974)). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 
621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 
(Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994).

Reimbursement of No-Show Fee for IME 
 
The defendants seek reimbursement for an IME no-show fee due to Ms. Swick’s 
failure to attend the March 25, 2025, IME with Dr. Martin.  The defendants argue that 
Ms. Swick was provided with adequate notice of the IME, yet failed to attend due to her 
counsel failing to advise her of the IME appointment.   The claimant offers no argument 
as to this point outside of a poor excuse that claimant’s counsel failed to monitor their 
email. 
The undersigned could not find explicit justification in statute or the rules to 
assess a no-show fee.  The defendants provided no legal justification for their 
argument.  The closest corollary would be to assess this no-show fee as a cost against 
the claimant.  Costs are to be assessed at the discretion of the deputy commissioner 
hearing the case.  See 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.33; Iowa Code section 
10A.328.  876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.33(86) provides:  
[c]osts taxed by the workers’ compensation commissioner or a deputy 
commissioner shall be (1) attendance of a certified shorthand reporter or 
presence of mechanical means at hearings and evidential depositions, (2) 
transcription costs when appropriate, (3) costs of service of the original 
notice and subpoenas, (4) witness fees and expenses as provided by Iowa 
Code sections 622.69 and 622.72, (5) the costs of doctors’ and 
practitioners’ deposition testimony, provided that said costs do not exceed 
the amounts provided by Iowa Code sections 622.69 and 622.72, (6) the 
reasonable costs of obtaining no more than two doctors’ or practitioners’ 
reports, (7) filing fees when appropriate, including convenience fees 
incurred by using the WCES payment gateway, and (8) costs of persons 
reviewing health service disputes.

Page 14 
 
Pursuant to the holding in Des Moines Area Regional Transit Authority v. Young, 867 
N.W.2d 839 (Iowa 2015), only the report of an IME physician, and not the examination 
itself, can be taxed as a cost according to 876 IAC 4.33(6).  The Iowa Supreme Court 
reasoned, “a physician’s report becomes a cost incurred in a hearing because it is used 
as evidence in lieu of the doctor’s testimony,” while “[t]he underlying medical expenses 
associated with the examination do not become costs of a report needed for a hearing, 
just as they do not become costs of the testimony or deposition.”  Id.  (noting 
additionally that “[i]n the context of the assessment of costs, the expenses of the 
underlying medical treatment and examination are not part of the costs of the report or 
deposition”).  The commissioner has found this rationale applicable to expenses 
incurred by vocational experts.  See Kirkendall v. Cargill Meat Solutions Corp., File No. 
5055494 (App. December 17, 2018); Voshell v. Compass Group, USA, Inc., File No. 
5056857 (App. September 27, 2019).   
 
The costs for the no-show fee do not fall under any of the eight categories 
contemplated by 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.33.  The no-show fee is not the 
equivalent to a witness fee or the costs of a doctor’s deposition testimony.   
 
Another possible avenue is a sanction pursuant to 876 Iowa Administrative Code 
4.36, which states: 
If any party to a contested case or an attorney representing such party shall 
fail to comply with these rules or any order of a deputy workers’ 
compensation commissioner or the workers’ compensation commissioner, 
the deputy workers’ compensation commissioner or the workers’ 
compensation commissioner may impose sanctions which may include 
dismissing the action without prejudice, excluding or limiting evidence, 
assessing costs or expenses, and closing the record in whole or in part to 
further activity by the party. 
See 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.36.  In failing to attend the scheduled IME, the 
claimant failed to comply with Iowa Code section 85.39(1).  This is not a failure to abide 
by the “rules” referenced by 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.36.  The scenario where 
this rule may apply would involve the defendants seeking (and being granted) an order 
to compel the claimant to attend the IME pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.39(1).  If the 
claimant failed to attend the IME following the issuance of an order to compel the 
claimant to attend the IME, then sanction pursuant to 876 Iowa Administrative Code 
4.36 may be appropriate.  However, the defendants neither sought, nor obtained, an 
order to compel the claimant to attend the IME.   
 
Iowa Code section 85.39(1) provides that when an employee refuses to submit to 
an IME pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.39(1), the employee forfeits their right to any 
compensation for the period of the refusal.  See Iowa Code section 85.39(1).  This is the 
consequence or sanction of the failure to comply with the statute.  There is no other 
avenue for recovery of the no-show fee for the defendants in this case.
