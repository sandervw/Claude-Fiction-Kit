This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A. The 
expedited procedures of rule 876 IAC 4.48, the “alternate medical care” rule, are 
invoked by claimant, Nicole Lyons.  
This alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on March 14, 2024. The 
proceedings were recorded digitally and constitute the official record of the hearing.  By 
an order filed by the Workers’ Compensation Commissioner, this decision is designated 
final agency action.  Any appeal would be by petition for judicial review under Iowa 
Code section 17A.15(1).  
The record in this case consists of Claimant’s Exhibits 1-3 and Defendants’ 
Exhibit A. 
ISSUE 
The issue presented for resolution in this case is whether claimant is entitled to 
alternate medical care consisting of authorization for surgery recommended by Keith 
Lodhia, M.D. 
FINDINGS OF FACTS 
Defendants accept liability for an injury occurring on March 3, 2023. 
On June 27, 2023, claimant was evaluated by Keith Lodhia, M.D.  Dr. Lodhia is a 
neurosurgeon specializing in spinal conditions.  Dr. Lodhia is authorized to treat 
claimant.  Claimant had right-sided leg pain with a right central to paracentral disc

In an October 30, 2023, report, Trevor Schmitz, M.D., gave his opinion of

symptoms.  (Exhibit A, pp. 3-5) 
On November 9, 2023, claimant began EMG testing for her right lower extremity.  
Testing was terminated due to claimant’s significant pain.  (Ex. A, pp. 1-2) 
In a February 23, 2024, letter, claimant’s attorney indicated Dr. Lodhia 
recommended spinal surgery for claimant, and defendants had failed to authorize the 
recommended surgery.  Claimant gave notice of her dissatisfaction for defendants’ 
failure to authorize surgery.  (Ex. 3) 
CONCLUSION OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3).   
Iowa Code section 85.27(4) provides, in relevant part:   
    For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish 
reasonable services and supplies to treat an injured employee and has the 
right to choose the care.  The treatment must be offered promptly and be 
reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the 
employee.  If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the care 
offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such 
dissatisfaction to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the 
employer and the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited 
to treat the injury.  If the employer and employee cannot agree on such 
alternate care, the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable 
proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and order other care.   
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving. Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care. Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant. Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).

Under the statute on alternate medical care, the employer is permitted to choose 
the care.  Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 433 (Iowa 1997).  The 
question of reasonable care is a question of fact.   
        The employer is not entitled to interpose its judgment in contravention of the 
recommendation of the authorized treating physician.  The employer’s failure to follow 
recommendations of an authorized physician is a failure to provide reasonable 
treatment pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.27.  Boggs v. Cargill, Inc., File No. 1050396 
(Alt Care Dec. January 31, 1994). 
Dr. Lodhia is an authorized treating physician.  On June 27, 2023, Dr. Lodhia 
recommended claimant have a lumbar discectomy.  Nearly three months after that 
recommendation, and at defendants’ request, claimant underwent an IME with Dr. 
Schmitz on September 8, 2023.  Nearly two months after that exam, a report was 
issued from Dr. Schmitz on October 30, 2023.  In that report, Dr. Schmitz recommended 
claimant undergo an EMG.  On November 9, 2023, claimant underwent that EMG.  That 
EMG was terminated because of the pain in testing. 
Defendants’ counsel suggests claimant undergo a second EMG, and then, 
perhaps, the surgery recommended by Dr. Lodhia over eight months ago will be 
authorized.  No expert has opined claimant should undergo a second EMG. 
The record indicates that after Dr. Lodhia recommended surgery, claimant has 
complied with every request made by defendants for an IME and further testing.  It has 
been approximately 260 days since the authorized provider has recommended surgery 
for claimant.  Defendants have not authorized the surgery.  The record does not show 
defendants have authorized care for claimant during this period.  
Obviously, from the record as detailed above, defendants have not promptly 
offered care to claimant.  The approximate 260-day delay in authorizing the care 
recommended by Dr. Lodhia is found unreasonable.  Claimant has carried her burden of 
proof she is entitled to the requested care. 
ORDER 
THEREFORE, IT IS ORDERED claimant’s petition for alternate medical is 
granted.  Defendants shall authorize the surgery recommended by Dr. Lodhia.
