STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Melissa Wooster, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers' 
compensation benefits from SBL Nutrition (SBL) as defendant, as a result of an injury 
she sustained on April 26, 2024, that arose out of and in the course of employment. 
On August 6, 2024, claimant filed a motion for entry of default against defendant 
for failure to timely answer the petition.  On August 7, 2024, an entry of default was 
entered against the defendant, and a hearing was scheduled for October 2, 2024, by 
telephone.  The hearing took place as scheduled.  The proceedings were recorded 
digitally, which constitutes the official record of this proceeding.  The record consists of 
Claimant’s Exhibits 1 through 9 and the testimony of claimant. 
ISSUES 
1. The extent of claimant’s entitlement to temporary disability benefits. 
 
2. Whether claimant’s injury resulted in a permanent disability; and if so, 
 
3. The extent of claimant's entitlement to permanent partial disability benefits. 
 
4. Rate. 
 
5. Whether there is a causal connection between the injury and the claimed 
medical expenses. 
 
6. Whether claimant is entitled to reimbursement for an independent medical 
evaluation (IME) under Iowa Code section 85.39. 
 
7. Whether the defendant is liable for a penalty under Iowa Code section 
10A.315.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The first issue to be determined is if claimant is entitled to temporary benefits. 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e). 
Healing period compensation describes temporary workers’ compensation 
weekly benefits that precede an allowance of permanent partial disability benefits.  
Ellingson v. Fleetguard, Inc., 599 N.W.2d 440, 446 (Iowa 1999).  Healing period benefits 
are payable to an injured worker who has suffered permanent partial disability until the 
first to occur of three events.  Iowa Code § 85.34(1) (2015).  These are:  (1) the worker 
has returned to work; (2) the worker medically is capable of returning to substantially 
similar employment; or (3) the worker has achieved maximum medical recovery.  Id.  
Maximum medical recovery is achieved when healing is complete, and the extent of 
permanent disability can be determined.  Armstrong Tire & Rubber Co. v. Kubli, 312 
N.W.2d 60, 65 (Iowa Ct. App. 1981).  Neither maintenance medical care nor an

Page 5 
 
employee's continuing to have pain or other symptoms necessarily prolongs the healing 
period.  Id. 
Claimant was terminated from her employment with SBL on May 22, 2024.  Dr. 
Bansal opined claimant was at MMI as of August 29, 2024.  Claimant has carried her 
burden of proof she is entitled to healing period benefits from May 23, 2024, through 
August 29, 2024. 
The next issue to be determined is whether the work injury resulted in a 
permanent disability. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
Claimant injured her head on April 26, 2024.  Since that time claimant has had 
problems including, but not limited to, headaches, photophobia, vertigo, fatigue, blurred 
vision, and cognitive issues.  As of October 2024, approximately eight months after the 
date of injury, claimant was still experiencing the same symptoms.  Dr. Bansal opined 
that claimant has a permanent impairment as a result of her April 26, 2024, injury.  
Based on this record, claimant has carried her burden of proof she sustained a 
permanent disability from the April of 2024 work injury. 
The next issue to be determined is the extent of claimant’s entitlement to 
permanent partial disability benefits. 
Claimant was 38 years old at the time of hearing.  Claimant graduated from high 
school.  Claimant has a BS in business.  At the time of hearing, claimant was working in 
a master’s program.  Claimant has worked as a housekeeper and done laundry.  She 
has worked as a cashier and done accounting.  Claimant has also done clerical work.   
Dr. Bansal found that claimant had a 14 percent permanent impairment to the 
body as a whole.  He limited claimant, in part, to avoiding tasks involving prolonged 
concentration and multitasking.  He also recommended claimant avoid working at 
heights or operating heavy machinery.  Claimant was terminated from her job with SBL.  
Claimant’s unrebutted testimony, based on the restrictions from Dr. Bansal and her own 
symptoms, is that claimant does not believe she could perform any of the jobs she has 
held in the past.  Based on this record, it is found that claimant has a 75 percent loss of 
earning capacity or industrial disability. 
The next issue to be determined is rate.  Based on Iowa Code section 85.36(7), 
claimant’s rate in this case is calculated by adding the six weeks claimant worked at

SBL.  This results in an average weekly wage of $132.48 per week ($794.88 divided by

Claimant has an average weekly wage of $132.48 per week.  Claimant is single

The next issue to be determined is whether there is a causal connection between 
the injury and the claimed medical expenses. 
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance, and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Iowa Code section 85.27.  Holbert 
v. Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening October 1975). 
Exhibit 2 is a breakdown of claimant’s medical bills.  Claimant testified that the 
medical charges detailed in Exhibit 2, page 1, are related to her April of 2024 work 
injury.  There is no evidence that the charges at issue are not reasonable and 
necessary.  Claimant has carried her burden of proof that defendant is liable for 
claimant’s medical bills.  Defendant shall pay claimant’s out-of-pocket charges as 
detailed in Exhibit 2, page 1.  Defendant shall also reimburse Medicare for charges as 
shown in Exhibit 2, page 1.  Defendant shall also pay claimant’s medical mileage as 
shown in Exhibit 7. 
The next issue to be determined is whether claimant is entitled to reimbursement 
for an IME.   
Iowa Code section 85.39 permits an employee to be reimbursed for subsequent 
examination by a physician of the employee's choice where an employer-retained 
physician has previously evaluated “permanent disability” and the employee believes 
that the initial evaluation is too low.   Iowa Code § 85.39(2) (2023).  The section also 
permits reimbursement for reasonably necessary transportation expenses incurred and 
for any wage loss occasioned by the employee attending the subsequent examination.  
Iowa Code § 85.39 (1), (2).   
The Iowa Supreme Court has provided a literal interpretation of the plain 
language of Iowa Code section 85.39, stating that section only allows the employee to 
obtain an independent medical evaluation at the employer’s expense if an evaluation of 
permanent disability has already been made by an employer retained physician.  Des 
Moines Area Reg’l Transit Auth. v. Young, 867 N.W.2d 839, 847 (Iowa 2015). Under

the Young decision, an employee can only obtain an IME at the employer's expense if 
an evaluation of permanent disability has been made by an employer-retained 
physician. Id. 
Defendant failed to file an answer, a pleading or appear at hearing.  The record 
indicates defendant failed to have workers’ compensation insurance.  Defendant failed 
to provide any medical care to claimant.  Based on this failure of defendant to assume 
any responsibility for claimant’s April of 2024 work injury, at my discretion I award the 
costs of Dr. Bansal’s IME to claimant. 
The final issue to be determined is if defendant is liable for a penalty under Iowa 
Code section 10A.315(4). 
In Christensen v. Snap-on Tools Corp., 554 N.W.2d 254 (Iowa 1996) and 
Robbennolt v. Snap-on Tools Corp, 555 N.W.2d 229 (Iowa 1996) the supreme court 
said: 
Based on the plain language of section 86.13,1 we hold an employee 
is entitled to penalty benefits if there has been a delay in payment unless 
the employer proves a reasonable cause or excuse.  [Iowa Code § 86.13 
(2015).]  A reasonable cause or excuse exists if either (1) the delay was 
necessary for the insurer to investigate the claim or (2) the employer had a 
reasonable basis to contest the employee’s entitlement to benefits.  A 
“reasonable basis” for denial of the claim exists if the claim is “fairly 
debatable.”  Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 260. 
The supreme court has stated: 
(1) If the employer has a reason for the delay and conveys that 
reason to the employee contemporaneously with the beginning of the delay, 
no penalty will be imposed if the reason is of such character that a 
reasonable fact finder could conclude that it is a "reasonable or probable 
cause or excuse" under Iowa Code section 86.13 [2015].  In that case, we 
will defer to the decision of the commissioner.  See Christensen, 554 
N.W.2d at 260 (substantial evidence found to support commissioner’s 
finding of legitimate reason for delay pending receipt of medical report); 
Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 236. 
(2) If no reason is given for the delay or if the “reason” is not one that 
a reasonable fact finder could accept, we will hold that no such cause or 
excuse exists and remand to the commissioner for the sole purpose of 
assessing penalties under section 86.13.  See Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 
261.

(3) Reasonable causes or excuses include (a) a delay for the 
employer to investigate the claim, Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 260; 
Kiesecker v. Webster City Custom Meats, Inc., 528 N.W.2d at 109, 111 
(Iowa 1995); or (b) the employer had a reasonable basis to contest the 
claimthe “fairly debatable” basis for delay.  See Christensen, 554 N.W.2d 
at 260 (holding two-month delay to obtain employer’s own medical report 
reasonable under the circumstances).  
(4) For the purpose of applying section 86.13, the benefits that are 
underpaid as well as late-paid benefits are subject to penalties, unless the 
employer establishes reasonable and probable cause or excuse. [Iowa 
Code § 86.13 (2015);] Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 237 (underpayment 
resulting from application of wrong wage base; in absence of excuse, 
commissioner required to apply penalty). 
If we were to construe [Iowa Code § 86.13 (2015)] to 
permit the avoidance of penalty if any amount of 
compensation benefits are paid, the purpose of the penalty 
statute would be frustrated.  For these reasons, we conclude 
section 86.13 is applicable when payment of compensation is 
not timely . . . or when the full amount of compensation is not 
paid.  Id. 
(5) For purposes of determining whether there has been a delay, 
payments are “made” when (a) the check addressed to a claimant is mailed 
(Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 236; Kiesecker, 528 N.W.2d at 112), or (b) the 
check is delivered personally to the claimant by the employer or its workers’ 
compensation insurer.  Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 235.   
(6) In determining the amount of the penalty, the commissioner is to 
consider factors such as the length of the delay, the number of delays, the 
information available to the employer regarding the employee’s injury and 
wages, and the employer’s past record of penalties.  Robbennolt, 555 
N.W.2d at 238. 
(7) An employer’s bare assertion that a claim is “fairly debatable” 
does not make it so.  A fair reading of Christensen and Robbennolt, makes 
it clear that the employer must assert facts upon which the commissioner 
could reasonably find that the claim was “fairly debatable.”  See 
Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 260. 
Meyers v. Holiday Express Corp., 557 N.W.2d 502, 504-505 (Iowa 1996).   
Weekly compensation payments are due at the end of the compensation week.  
Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 235.

Penalty is not imposed for delayed interest payments.  Davidson v. Bruce, 594

When an employee’s claim for benefits is fairly debatable based on a good faith 
dispute over the employee’s factual or legal entitlement to benefits, an award of penalty 
benefits is not appropriate under the statute.  Gilbert v. USF Holland, Inc., 637 N.W.2d 
194, 199 (Iowa 2001).  Whether the issue was fairly debatable turns on whether there 
was a disputed factual dispute that, if resolved in favor of the employer, would have 
supported the employer's denial of compensability.  Id. 
Claimant is due healing period benefits from May 23, 2024, through August 29, 
2024.  Defendant gave no reason why those benefits were not paid.  A penalty of 50 
percent is appropriate.  The period of time between May 23, 2024, and August 29, 
2024, is approximately 14 weeks.  A penalty of $853.30 is appropriate (14 weeks x 
$121.90 x 50 percent). 
Claimant was due permanent partial disability benefits beginning on August 29, 
2024.  The period of time between August 29, 2024, to the date of hearing is 
approximately six weeks.  A penalty of $1,155.00 is appropriate (6 weeks x $385.00 x 
50 percent). 
ORDER 
THEREFORE IT IS ORDERED: 
That defendant shall pay claimant healing period benefits from May 23, 2024, 
through August 29, 2024, at the rate of one hundred twenty-one and 90/100 dollars 
($121.90) per week. 
That defendant shall pay 350 weeks of permanent partial disability benefits at the 
rate of three hundred eighty-five and 00/100 dollars ($385.00) per week commencing on 
August 30, 2024.  
Defendant shall pay accrued weekly benefits in a lump sum together with interest 
at an annual rate equal to the one-year treasury constant maturity published by the 
federal reserve in the most recent H15 report settled as of the date of injury, plus two 
percent.  
That defendant shall pay a penalty of eight hundred fifty-three and 30/100 dollars 
($853.30) for the nonpayment of healing period benefits.  
That defendant shall pay a penalty of one thousand one hundred fifty-five and 
00/100 dollars ($1,155.00) for the nonpayment of permanent partial disability benefits.  
That defendant shall reimburse claimant the costs associated with Dr. Bansal’s 
IME.
