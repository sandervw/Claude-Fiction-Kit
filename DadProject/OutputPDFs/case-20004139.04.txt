STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Shawn Stephenson, claimant, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from DB&J Enterprise, Inc., employer and SFM Insurance, 
insurance carrier, as defendants.  Hearing was held via Zoom videoconference on June 
18, 2024.     
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised 
or discussed in this decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.  
Shawn Stephenson and Mike Ruch were the only witnesses to testify live at trial.  
The evidentiary record also includes joint exhibits 1-17, claimant’s exhibits 1-7, and 
defendant’s exhibits A-M.  All exhibits were received without objection.  The evidentiary 
record closed at the conclusion of the arbitration hearing.       
At the outset of the hearing the undersigned expressed concern that the joint 
exhibits which consisted of 477 pages, claimant’s exhibits consisted of 80 pages, and 
defendants’ exhibits consisted of 180 pages; each set of proposed exhibits did not 
comply with our agency hearing guidelines which can be found at:  
https://dial.iowa.gov/hearings/workers-comp/exhibits.  It also appeared the joint exhibits 
did not comply with Iowa Code section 17A.14(1) which states that irrelevant, 
immaterial, or unduly repetitious evidence should be excluded from evidence in 
contested cases.  (Transcript, pages 12-16) Unfortunately, reviewing the evidence and 
post-hearing briefs in this case confirmed my concerns that numerous exhibits in this 
case were irrelevant, immaterial, or unduly repetitious.  For example, joint exhibit 2, 
page 8 is a physical therapy sheet not for Shawn Stephenson, but for a patient by a 
different name who has nothing to do with this case.  Therefore, JE 2, page 8 is stricken

STIPULATIONS 
The parties submitted the following stipulations: 
1. The existence of an employer-employee relationship at the time of the alleged 
injury. 
2. Claimant sustained an injury, which arose out of and in the course of 
employment, on August 14, 2019. 
3. Claimant sustained temporary disability and permanent disability as the result 
of the work injury. 
4. The parties’ stipulations result in a weekly workers’ compensation rate of 
$1,156.09. 
5. Defendants would pay or reimburse claimant for an independent medical 
examination. 
ISSUES 
The parties submitted the following issues for resolution: 
1. The nature and extent of permanent partial disability that claimant sustained 
as the result of the stipulated August 14, 2019 work injury. 
2. Whether claimant has reached maximum medical improvement from the 
August 14, 2019 injury.  If so, the appropriate commencement date for any 
permanent partial disability benefits. 
3. Whether claimant refused to submit to an Iowa Code section 85.39 exam, and 
if so, did the refusal result in a forfeiture of compensation. 
4. Whether defendants are responsible for any past medical expenses. 
5. Whether defendants are responsible for additional medical treatment. 
6. Assessment of costs.

The first issue to be addressed is permanent impairment.   
Following the work injury Shawn received treatment involving his knees and his

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony.  The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question.  The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances.  The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); 
Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995).  Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994).  Unrebutted expert medical 
testimony cannot be summarily rejected.  Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 
N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 1994).    
Based on the above findings of fact, I conclude that claimant demonstrated that 
he sustained permanent injury to his right shoulder, neck, left shoulder, and left lower 
extremity.  Thus, claimant has demonstrated that as the result of the single August 14, 
2019 work injury he sustained permanent injury to scheduled members and to portions 
of his body not included in the schedule.   
The Supreme Court recently reiterated that “under our case law, ‘when there is 
injury to some scheduled member and also to parts of the body not included in the 
schedule, the resulting disability is compensated on the basis of an unscheduled 
injury.’” Bridgestone Americas, Inc. v. Anderson, 4 N.W.3d 676, 685 (Iowa 2024)(citing 
Mortimer v. Fruehauf Corp., 502 N.W.2d 12, 16 (Iowa 1993)).  I conclude that claimant 
should be compensated on the basis of an unscheduled injury. 
Because claimant has not had the recommended surgery for his left elbow the 
issue of permanent impairment to his left upper extremity is not ripe for determination. 
Under Iowa law,  
Compensation for permanent partial disability shall begin when it is 
medically indicated that maximum medical improvement from the injury

 As the result of the single August 14, 2019 work injury, claimant sustained injury

Refusal to attend IME 
Defendants assert claimant refused to attend an 85.39 examination.  Based on 
the above findings of fact, I conclude that as of May 19, 2023, claimant refused to 
submit to an IME with Dr. Chen.  At the June 18, 2024 hearing claimant stated he is 
willing to attend an IME with Dr. Chen.  I concluded claimant’s refusal to attend the 
IME ended on June 18, 2024.  Thus, I conclude that from May 19, 2023 through June 
17, 2024 claimant refused to submit to a section 85.39 examination.   
Iowa Code section 85.39(1) states: 
After an injury, the employee, if requested by the employer, shall submit 
for examination at some reasonable time and place and as often as 
reasonably requested, to a physician or physicians authorized to practice 
under the laws of this state or another state, without cost to the employee; 
but if the employee requests, the employee, at the employee's own cost, is 
entitled to have a physician or physicians of the employee's own selection 
present to participate in the examination. If an employee is required to 
leave work for which the employee is being paid wages to attend the 
requested examination, the employee shall be compensated at the 
employee's regular rate for the time the employee is required to leave 
work, and the employee shall be furnished transportation to and from the 
place of examination, or the employer may elect to pay the employee the 
reasonable cost of the transportation. The refusal of the employee to 
submit to the examination shall forfeit the employee's right to any 
compensation for the period of refusal. Compensation shall not be payable 
for the period of refusal. 
The Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commissioner has addressed this issue.  He 
stated,  
The plain language of Iowa Code section 85.39(1) provides, “The refusal 
of the employee to submit to the examination [requested by the employer] 
shall forfeit the employee's right to any compensation for the period of the 
refusal. Compensation shall not be payable for the period of refusal.” 
There is not a “reasonableness” test in the statute that requires 
defendants to prove their request for an examination was reasonable. 
There is no exception within the statute that exempts claimant from 
attending the examination to avoid forfeiture of benefits and requires 
payment of weekly benefits. 
Poe v. Capital Express, Inc., File No. 2200411.01, 2024 WL 2035238, at *8 (Apr. 30, 
2024) 
Defendants assert that, under the plain language of Iowa Code section 85.39, 
claimant's refusal to submit to an examination requested by defendants results in a 
forfeiture of any compensation benefits occurring after May 19, 2023. Claimant 

STEPHENSON V. DB&J ENTERPRISE, INC. 
Page 19 
contends that the May 19, 2023 IME was redundant and not reasonable because 
defendants already had a prior IME and denied the claim.  (Cl. Brief, p. 18).  However, 
based on the plain language of section 85.39 and agency precedent, I conclude Iowa 
Code section 85.39(1) required claimant to attend the examination defendants 
scheduled for May 19, 2023.  Claimant never agreed to attend an examination 
thereafter and prior to the arbitration hearing.  Claimant’s refusal to attend that 
examination resulted in a forfeiture of any compensation after May 19, 2023 through the

Credits 
 
Defendants assert a credit on several bases.  (Def. Ex. B, p. 1a)  Defendants 
assert a credit for permanency benefits paid.  Claimant does not dispute that 
defendants are entitled to a credit for the 26.04 weeks of permanent partial disability 
benefits.  (Def. Ex. B; Cl. Post-hearing brief, p. 15) Thus, I conclude that defendants are 
entitled to a credit for the 26.04 weeks of permanent partial disability benefits.  At the 
stipulated weekly rate of $1,156.09, 26.04 weeks amounts to $30,104.58. 
Defendants also assert a credit for overpayment of TTD benefits. Based on the 
above findings of fact, I conclude defendants overpaid temporary disability benefits in 
the amount of $6,632.75 (Def. Ex. B, p. 1a; Cl. Ex. 2, p. 10)  
Claimant does not dispute that these benefits were paid or that there was an 
overpayment of weekly benefits.  Rather, claimant asserts that credit for overpayment of 
temporary benefits is to be applied only against a future claim for benefits.  In support of 
his position claimant cites a number of cases that address Iowa Code section 85.34(5) 
which predate 2017.  However, these cases are no longer applicable because in 2017 
the Iowa legislature amended Iowa Code section 85.34(5). The amendment allows for a 
credit for overpayment of temporary benefits to be applied against a current or future 
claim for benefits. 
 
Since the 2017 amendment, Iowa Code section 85.34(5) reads,  
Recovery of employee overpayment. If an employee is paid any weekly 
benefits in excess of that required by this chapter and chapters 85A, 85B, 
and 86, the excess paid by the employer shall be credited against the 
liability of the employer for any future weekly benefits due pursuant to 
subsection 2, for any current or subsequent injury to the same 
employee. 
(Iowa Code section 85.34(5)(2019)(emphasis added). 
 
The statute clearly states that excess paid by the employer shall be credited 
against liability of the employer for the current injury. Thus, I conclude defendants are 
entitled to claim a credit for overpayment of weekly benefits in the amount of $6,632.75.   
As noted above defendants also assert a credit for payments made and for pre-
existing disability for an injury claimant sustained with a different employer. Defendants

assert they are only liable for the portion of claimant’s disability that arose out of and in 
the course of claimant’s employment with DB&J that relates to the injury and not the 
pre-existing left upper extremity disability caused by a work injury with a different 
employer. 
Iowa Code section 85.34(7) states:  
Successive disabilities.  An employer is liable for compensating only that 
portion of an employee’s disability that arises out of and in the course of 
the employee’s employment with the employer and relates to the injury 
that serves as a basis for the employee’s claim for compensation under 
this chapter… an employer is not liable for compensating an employee’s 
pre-existing disability that arose out of and it the course of employment 
with a different employer or from causes unrelated to employment. 
Defendants argue that claimant sustained an injury to his left upper extremity 
arising from an injury at Bon-Ton Stores, Inc.  (Def. Ex. D, p. 85)   
Iowa Code section 85.34(7) states, in relevant part:  
An employer is liable for compensating only that portion of an employee's 
disability that arises out of and in the course of the employee's 
employment with the employer and that relates to the injury that serves as 
the basis for the employee's claim for compensation under chapter 10A, 
subchapter III, this chapter, or chapter 85A or 85B. An employer is not 
liable for compensating an employee's preexisting disability that arose out 
of and in the course of employment from a prior injury with the employer, 
to the extent that the employee's preexisting disability has already been 
compensated under chapter 10A, subchapter III, this chapter, or chapter 
85A or 85B.  
In interpreting Iowa Code section 85.34(7), the Iowa Supreme Court held:  
The statute does not establish a credit system where an employer gets to 
avoid paying for a new disability. Rather, the statutory text limits an 
employer’s liability for preexisting disabilities: the employer “is not liable for 
compensating an employee’s preexisting disability” but only “to the extent 
that the employee’s preexisting disability has already been compensated

Claimant settled his prior workers’ compensation claim on a compromise 
settlement basis under Iowa Code section 85.35(3). (Def. Ex. D, pp. 91-93) It is unclear 
whether amounts paid under a compromise settlement, or permanent impairments 
associated with those compromise settlements, can be used to apportion defendants’ 
liability under Iowa Code section 85.34(7). The plain language of Iowa Code section 
85.35(10) suggests they cannot. Defendants offer no precedent indicating a 
compromise settlement is applicable to apportionment under Iowa Code section 
85.34(7).  
Defendants bear the burden of proof to prove apportionment applies in this case. 
Defendants offered no evidence regarding apportionment of any future rating. They 
offer no evidence the prior permanent impairment rating should be apportioned. Given 
this record, defendants have failed to carry their burden of proof apportionment applies.  
In conclusion, defendants have failed to carry their burden of proof 
apportionment under Iowa code section 85.34(7) applies in this case.  Defendants have 
not demonstrated entitlement to payments made and for pre-existing disability for an 
injury claimant sustained with a different employer.
