STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A. The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Laritha Sweatte.   
The alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on April 4, 2024. Claimant 
appeared personally and through her attorney Greg Egbers.  Defendant appeared 
through its attorney Jessica Voelker.  The proceedings were digitally recorded. That 
recording constitutes the official record of this proceeding.  
Pursuant to the Commissioner’s July 21, 2023, Order, the undersigned has been 
delegated authority to issue a final agency decision in this alternate medical care 
proceeding. Therefore, this ruling is designated final agency action and any appeal of 
the decision would be to the Iowa District Court pursuant to Iowa Code section 17A. 
The hearing record consists of: 
 Claimant’s Exhibits 1-3 
 Claimant’s Brief 
 Defendant’s Exhibit A 
Claimant was the only witness to provide testimony.  Counsel for both parties 
provided argument.  The record closed at the end of the alternate medical care 
telephonic hearing.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned having considered all the evidence in the record finds: 
On June 15, 2022, claimant sustained a work-related injury to her right shoulder. 
(See Petition; Hearing Testimony).  Defendant admitted liability for the injury and 
authorized treatment for the injury.  (Answer; Hearing Testimony).  At some point, 
claimant’s care was transferred to Mark Morrey, M.D., an orthopedic surgeon at Mayo 
Clinic in Rochester, MN.  (Hearing Testimony; Ex. 1; Ex. A, p. 9).   
On August 22, 2023, Dr. Morrey performed a revision reverse total shoulder 
arthroplasty on claimant’s right shoulder.  (Ex. 1, p. 1).  Following surgery, claimant 
attended physical therapy at Athletico Physical Therapy (hereinafter “Athletico”) in 
Urbandale, Iowa.  (Hearing Testimony; Ex. A, pp. 3-8).  It is not entirely clear when

therapy appointments and cancelled 14 appointments at Athletico.  (Id.).  Claimant

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Under Iowa law, an employer who has accepted compensability for a workplace 
injury has a right to control the care provided to the injured employee.  Ramirez-Trujillo 
v. Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 N.W.2d 759, 769 (Iowa 2016).  The relevant statute provides 
as follows: 
For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish 
reasonable services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has the 
right to choose the care. . . . The treatment must be offered promptly and 
be reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the 
employee. If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the care 
offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such dissatisfaction

to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the employer and 
the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited to treat the 
injury. If the employer and employee cannot agree on such alternate care, 
the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable proofs of the 
necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
Iowa Code § 85.27(4).   
Defendant’s “obligation under the statute is confined to reasonable care for the 
diagnosis and treatment of work-related injuries.” Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 
N.W.2d 122, 124 (Iowa 1995) (emphasis in original).  In other words, the “obligation 
under the statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.”  (Id.). 
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  See Iowa Code § 85.27(4). By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment 
and seeking alternate care, claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care 
is unreasonable.  See Iowa R. App. P 6.904(3)(e); Long, 528 N.W.2d at 124.  
Ultimately, determining whether care is reasonable under the statute is a question of 
fact.  Long, 528 N.W.2d at 123. 
Under Iowa Code section 85.27, claimant bears the burden of providing 
“reasonable proofs of the necessity” to order alternate care.  Defendant has already 
authorized a transfer of her physical therapy from Athletico to Kinetic Edge.  Claimant 
alleges this is insufficient.  She argues that Dr. Morrey, whom both parties agree is the 
authorized treating physician, does not trust she will receive adequate physical therapy 
in Iowa, and indicated she needs to undergo therapy at Mayo Clinic instead.  Claimant, 
however, has not produced sufficient evidence to support this assertion.  Claimant 
testified about a phone conference she had with Dr. Morrey where they discussed his 
recommendations, but she has not produced any letters or reports from Dr. Morrey 
documenting these alleged recommendations.  She also did not produce any reports 
from the physical therapist she met with at Mayo on March 12, 2024.  The only medical 
record claimant submitted was a March 11, 2024, treatment note from Dr. Dugdale 
which stated claimant was “hoping to get plugged into the Mayo therapy team.”  (Ex. 1, 
p. 1).  That is not a prescription from Dr. Morrey or his resident to attend physical 
therapy at Mayo Clinic.  The only document from Dr. Morrey in the hearing record is his 
March 29, 2024 letter, and that states, “[I]f patient wants PT in Rochester she can have 
it here, but there is no surgical reason she could not have PT locally.”  (Ex. A, p. 9).  
Claimant has not demonstrated that Dr. Morrey actually ordered her to attend physical 
therapy at Mayo Clinic, nor has she shown the necessity of a continuous six-week to 
three-month hotel stay in Minnesota, when there are physical therapy providers near 
her home in Iowa, that can implement Dr. Morrey’s recommended therapy plan.   
Defendant’s plan to authorize additional physical therapy at Kinetic Edge near 
claimant’s home in Iowa, utilizing Dr. Morrey’s proposed physical therapy plan, is

reasonable.  In alternate care actions, claimants bear the burden of proving that the 
care provided by defendants is unreasonable.  See Iowa R. App. P 6.904(3)(e); Long, 
528 N.W.2d at 124.  Claimant did not meet that burden.   
THEREFORE IT IS ORDERED: 
Claimant's petition for alternate medical care is denied.
