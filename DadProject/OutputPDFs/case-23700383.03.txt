STATEMENT OF PROCEEDINGS 
The above captioned matter came before the undersigned on March 6, 2024, 
and was heard via internet telephone conference upon a petition for alternate medical 
care filed pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.27 on February 22, 2024, by Shawn Durbin, 
claimant, against John Deere Des Moines Works, employer, and AIU Insurance Co., 
insurer, both as defendants. An answer was filed on February 26, 2024, accepting 
responsibility for the right upper extremity, the body part for which claimant sought 
treatment.  
The petition invokes summary procedures set forth 876 IAC 4.48. 
The claimant participated personally, and defendants appeared through their 
attorney.   
FINDINGS OF FACT 
The claimant sustained an injury on February 20, 2023, which arose out of and in 
the course of his employment. As the result of this injury, he suffered symptoms of pain 
and dysfunction in the bilateral upper extremities. Claimant was hired by defendant 
employer approximately six years ago. He developed pain in his bilateral extremities 
due to his work-related tasks. He was eventually referred to Dr. Michael Gainer who he 
began seeing on June 22, 2023.

Per claimant’s testimony, Dr. Gainer offered an epidural which claimant refused. 
Eventually, claimant’s condition on the left side worsened to the point that Dr. Gainer 
recommended a left cubital tunnel release, which claimant underwent in November 
2023. After surgery, claimant was referred to physical therapy. Claimant was discharged 
from physical therapy on January 29, 2024, having achieved his goals of being able to 
do his work-related tasks with no pain or discomfort in the elbow. (Defendants’ Exhibit 
A:1) Claimant still had stiffness and tightness in his hands with moderate pain and 
discomfort in the hands and wrists. Id. The therapist recommended further evaluation of 
the hands and wrists by a doctor.  
Claimant was seen on February 7, 2024, by Dr. Gainer. (Ex. B:1) At the visit, 
claimant had increased numbness in the right hand and not much improvement in 
numbness in the left ring and small fingers. Id. Claimant testified at hearing that he still 
had pain in the left upper extremity. The medical note indicates claimant had a 71-
pound grip strength in the left hand. (Ex. B:5) Dr. Gainer discontinued therapy and 
returned claimant to work without restrictions for the left arm. Id. Because claimant was 
having signs and symptoms of carpal tunnel syndrome, a new EMG on the right side 
was ordered. Id.  
The nerve conduction study of February 13, 2024, showed claimant had right 
median sensory neuropathy, localizing at the level of the wrist. (Claimant’s Exhibit 3) Dr. 
Gainer saw claimant on February 14, 2024, and because of the mild right carpal tunnel 
diagnosis, Dr. Gainer did not believe that claimant was a surgical candidate. (Ex. 3) Dr. 
Gainer offered a steroid injection which claimant declined.   
Claimant testified at hearing that he did not believe the injection would be helpful. 
Claimant was also prescribed medication which he said was never called in or approved 
at the pharmacy. He appeared disinterested in pursuing that route, testifying that he 
would take the medication only if it would be helpful and if he was forced to do so.  
The inference was that claimant believes his right-sided carpal tunnel syndrome 
should be treated the same as his left cubital tunnel symptoms. Once he turned down 
palliative or conservative care, surgery should be proffered.  
Dr. Gainer appears to disagree and offered only conservative care in the form of 
steroid injections and thus, upon the denial of that care, Dr. Gainer released claimant to 
work full duty without further treatment recommendations. (Ex. 2)  According to 
claimant, after the clinical examination on that date, claimant wanted to seek out a 
second opinion, but Dr. Gainer was dismissive.  
Claimant testified that he has returned to work and the work he is doing, which 
requires the use of force with his hands and repetitive motion, is aggravating his carpal 
tunnel in the right upper extremity.  
CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
As claimant is seeking relief in this case, claimant bears the burden of proof to 
show by a preponderance of the evidence that the offered medical treatment is not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the employee.

See Lawyer and Higgs, Iowa Practice, Workers’ Compensation, §15-4 and cases cited 
therein. 
Under Iowa law, the employer is required to provide care to an injured employee 
and is permitted to choose the care.  Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 
N.W.2d 433 (Iowa 1997).  Iowa Code section 85.27 provides, in relevant part: 
For purposes of this section, this employer is obliged to furnish 
reasonable services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has 
the right to choose the care.  The treatment must be offered promptly and 
be reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the 
employee.  If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the care 
offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such 
dissatisfaction to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the 
employer and the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited 
to treat the injury.  If the employer and employee cannot agree on such 
alternate care, the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable 
proofs of the necessity therefore, allow and order other care. 
The question of reasonable care is a question of fact.  An application for alternate 
medical care is not granted simply because the employee is dissatisfied with the care 
the employer has chosen.  Mere dissatisfaction with the care is not sufficient grounds to 
grant an application for alternate medical care.  The employee has the burden of 
proving that the care chosen by the employer is unreasonable.  Unreasonableness can 
be established by showing that the care was not offered promptly, was not reasonably 
suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the claimant.  
West Side Transport v. Cordell, 601 N.W.2d 691 (Iowa 1999);  Long v. Roberts Dairy 
Company, 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  Unreasonableness can be established by 
showing that the care authorized by the employer has not been effective and is “inferior 
or less extensive” than other available care requested by the employee.  Pirelli-
Armstrong, at 437. 
At the present time, claimant is without care. He does not have an appointment 
scheduled with Dr. Gainer, no physical therapy is being proffered, and he is not being 
treated by the in-house nursing staff. There appears to be no dispute that claimant has 
mild carpal tunnel syndrome on the right which requires treatment of some kind. Dr. 
Gainer believed the condition warranted at least a steroid injection.  
However, claimant’s request in the petition is for a second opinion on whether 
surgery is warranted for the right carpal tunnel symptoms. There is no right to a second 
opinion written into the statute. Instead, the medical request needs to be evaluated 
under whether it is reasonably suited to treat the injury or that the alternative care that is 
being offered unduly inconveniences the claimant.  
The alternative care that was offered, but turned down by the claimant, is in the 
form of a steroid injection. Claimant provided no reason for his refusal other than he did 
not believe it would do any good. He did admit that he has not had an injection in the

past; thus the basis of his opinion, at least on the record, is one of conjecture. 
Conjecture cannot provide the basis for an argument that the care is unduly 
inconvenient. Claimant’s request for surgery is also unsupported by medical evidence. 
There is no expert in evidence that supports claimant’s need for surgery. It is a desire of 
the claimant to bypass conservative treatment and move forward with the more 
aggressive treatment of surgery.  
The inference to be drawn from claimant’s testimony is that he believes surgery 
is inevitable and so why should he have to be forced to go through a period of worthless 
conservative care when surgery will be the end result. However, in order to grant 
claimant’s request for a second opinion, there needs to be some evidence that denying 
a second opinion is unreasonable. For example, in Barker v. MRC Global, File No. 
5061174 (December 23, 2016), the claimant presented a letter from a treating physician 
that a second opinion was more than reasonable. Similarly, in Shinn v. Clow Valve 
Company,  File No. 21700759.01 (August 12, 2021), the treating physician wrote in his 
medical notes that he did not feel comfortable recommending surgical intervention at 
the time but would like to get a second opinion.  
There is no such evidence in this case.  
Claimant also seemed to be arguing, although it was not pled specifically, a 
breakdown of trust to the extent that he can longer return to Dr. Gainer. Alternate 
medical care can be awarded when there is a breakdown in the physician-patient 
relationship. Seibert v. State of Iowa, File No. 938579 (September 14, 1994); Neuaone 
v. John Morrell & Co., File No. 1022976 (January 27, 1994); Williams v. High Rise 
Const., File No. 1025415 (February 23, 1993); Wallech v. FDL Foods, File No. 1020245 
(September 3, 1992) (aff’d Dist. Ct. June 21, 1993). Typically, this involves transfer of 
care from one physician to another. The claimant described Dr. Gainer as dismissive. In 
correspondence between the lawyers, claimant’s counsel passed along that claimant 
felt that Dr. Gainer was mocking claimant’s request for a second opinion. The medical 
records are silent as to Dr. Gainer’s opinion or feelings about claimant’s request for a 
second opinion, but there is no evidence that Dr. Gainer ignored claimant’s complaints 
of pain and discomfort.  Instead, Dr. Gainer promptly ordered tests such as the EMG on 
the right side and offered treatment in the form of injections. The EMG test was ordered 
on February 7, 2024, took place on February 13, 2024, and claimant was seen by Dr. 
Gainer to review those results on February 14, 2024. This timeline does not suggest 
that Dr. Gainer is dismissive of claimant’s reports of physical discomfort and 
dysfunction.  
That said, claimant should be offered some kind of treatment which the 
defendants still have the right to direct.  
THEREFORE IT IS ORDERED:  
Claimant’s petition for alternate care is DENIED in part and GRANTED in part. 
Claimant is entitled to more care for his ongoing carpal tunnel symptoms, but the 
specific request for a second opinion is denied.
