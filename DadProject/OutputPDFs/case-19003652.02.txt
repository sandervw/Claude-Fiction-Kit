STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Robert Gustin, has filed a petition for arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits against Central States Roofing, employer, and Arch Insurance 
Company, insurer.  
In accordance with agency scheduling procedures and pursuant to the Order of 
the Commissioner the hearing was held on May 18, 2023, via Zoom. The case was 
considered fully submitted on June 15, 2023, upon the simultaneous filing of briefs.  
The record consists of Joint Exhibits 1-11, Claimant’s Exhibits 1-5, Defendants’ 
Exhibits A-C, along with the testimony of the claimant. 
ISSUES 
1. Whether claimant sustained a separate and distinct low back injury arising 
from the work injury date of September 23, 2019; 
 
2. The extent of claimant’s permanent disability;  
 
3. Whether claimant has returned to work for the employer receiving the same 
or greater salary, wages, or earnings than he received at the time of the 
injury; 
 
4. The appropriate number of exemptions; and

STIPULATIONS 
 
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised 
or discussed in this decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations. 
The parties stipulated claimant sustained an injury to his left leg arising out of 
and in the course of his employment on September 23, 2019. They further agreed the 
injury was the cause of temporary disability entitlement which is no longer in dispute. 
The September 23, 2019, injury resulted in a permanent disability, industrial in nature, 
and the permanent benefits commenced on April 8, 2022.  
While the rate is in dispute, the parties agree claimant was married at all relevant 
times.  
In claimant’s brief, it was acknowledged defendants have paid the IME of Dr. 
Bansal.  
All affirmative defenses have been waived.   
FINDINGS OF FACT 
Claimant, Robert Gustin, was a 57-year-old person at the time of the hearing. At 
all relevant times hereto, he was married with no other dependents. Defendants 
stipulated claimant was married and there is no contrary evidence. Thus it is determined 
claimant’s exemptions are two.  
Claimant’s educational background includes a high school diploma achieved in 
1985. His work history is primarily manual labor including delivering water; installing 
fiber lines; working on a hog farm, packing plant, and salvage yard; and roofing. He 
began working for defendant employer in 2007 and has worked continuously in that 
position until the date of his injury on September 23, 2019. Since his work injury, 
claimant has worked for the defendant as a loader and unloader due to his permanent 
restrictions.  
On September 23, 2019, claimant stepped over a wall at work and his ankle gave 
out. During his recovery process, claimant developed CRPS of the left lower extremity.   
On October 15, 2019, Mr. Gustin was evaluated by Emily O’Leary, DPT, at 
McFarland Clinic – Physical Therapy. (Joint Exhibit 1) His complaint was pain in the 
joint involving left ankle and foot. (Id.) He had swelling inferior to the lateral malleolus, 
reduced range of motion in the left ankle and acute tenderness to light palpation of the 
left lateral malleolus, ATFL, CFL, as well as the 4th and 5th metatarsals proximally and 
the distal fibula. (JE 1:2) He was using crutches and a CAM boot in addition to a knee 
scooter to help ambulate longer distances and complained of difficulty with standing, sit 
to stand transfers, ambulation, ascending and descending stairs, squatting and bending, 
and lifting. (JE 1:1-2)

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Claimant alleges he has sustained a permanent disability to his left lower 
extremity and his low back arising out of the September 23, 2019, work injury. 
Defendants have stipulated that claimant sustained both temporary and permanent 
disability as a result of the left lower extremity work injury as well as the development of 
CRPS following the injury, but deny claimant has sustained a back injury.  
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury actually occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 

GUSTIN V. CENTRAL STATES ROOFING 
Page 14 
 
employment.  Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. Dial 
Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309 (Iowa 1996).  The words “arising out of” refer to the cause or 
source of the injury.  The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 
circumstances of the injury.  2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124 (Iowa 1995).  
An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists between the 
injury and the employment.  Miedema, 551 N.W.2d 309.  The injury must be a rational 
consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely incidental to 
the employment.  Koehler Electric v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1 (Iowa 2000); Miedema, 551 
N.W.2d 309.  An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it happens within a 
period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may be when 
performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties or doing 
an activity incidental to them.  Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony.  The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question.  The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances.  The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); 
Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995).  Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994).  Unrebutted expert medical 
testimony cannot be summarily rejected.  Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 
N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 1994). 
A personal injury contemplated by the workers’ compensation law means an 
injury, the impairment of health or a disease resulting from an injury which comes about, 
not through the natural building up and tearing down of the human body, but because of 
trauma.  The injury must be something that acts extraneously to the natural processes 
of nature and thereby impairs the health, interrupts or otherwise destroys or damages a 
part or all of the body.  Although many injuries have a traumatic onset, there is no 
requirement for a special incident or an unusual occurrence.  Injuries which result from 
cumulative trauma are compensable.  Increased disability from a prior injury, even if 
brought about by further work, does not constitute a new injury, however.  St. Luke’s 
Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); Ellingson v. Fleetguard, Inc., 599 N.W.2d 
440 (Iowa 1999); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 

GUSTIN V. CENTRAL STATES ROOFING 
Page 15 
 
1995); McKeever Custom Cabinets v. Smith, 379 N.W.2d 368 (Iowa 1985).  An 
occupational disease covered by chapter 85A is specifically excluded from the definition 
of personal injury.  Iowa Code section 85.61(4) (b); Iowa Code section 85A.8; Iowa 
Code section 85A.14. 
When the injury develops gradually over time, the cumulative injury rule applies.  
The date of injury for cumulative injury purposes is the date on which the disability 
manifests.  Manifestation is best characterized as that date on which both the fact of 
injury and the causal relationship of the injury to the claimant’s employment would be 
plainly apparent to a reasonable person.  The date of manifestation inherently is a fact-
based determination.  The fact-finder is entitled to substantial latitude in making this 
determination and may consider a variety of factors, none of which is necessarily 
dispositive in establishing a manifestation date.  Among others, the factors may include 
missing work when the condition prevents performing the job, or receiving significant 
medical care for the condition.  For time limitation purposes, the discovery rule then 
becomes pertinent so the statute of limitations does not begin to run until the employee, 
as a reasonable person, knows or should know, that the cumulative injury condition is 
serious enough to have a permanent, adverse impact on his or her employment.  
Herrera v. IBP, Inc., 633 N.W.2d 284 (Iowa 2001); Oscar Mayer Foods Corp. v. Tasler, 
483 N.W.2d 824 (Iowa 1992); McKeever Custom Cabinets v. Smith, 379 N.W.2d 368 
(Iowa 1985). 
The only physician to diagnose claimant with a back injury was Dr. Bansal 
following the IME of March 23, 2023. The basis of his opinion is that claimant developed 
sacroiliitis as a result of claimant’s altered gait. Dr. Bansal stated that “it is logical that 
the back pain manifested months after his left ankle injury, as this is a cumulative 
process.” (JE 10:16) The physical therapy records confirm that claimant had an altered 
gait during treatment in 2020 and 2021, consistent with his presentation to Dr. Bansal. 
There was also a mention of back pain during the independent medical examination by 
Dr. Mooney in 2021. Other than that singular mention, however, the records do not 
contain any complaints of back pain to Dr. Cain or Dr. Thuku. It is true that Dr. Cain is a 
foot specialist and thus a complaint to Dr. Cain about back pain may not have been 
something he recorded, but Dr. Thuku is a pain specialist who was treating claimant for 
his CRPS in addition to the ankle specific complaints. Claimant specifically denied any 
back injury when Dr. Thuku questioned him about it. The dorsal root ganglion stimulator 
was installed in April 2021 by Dr. Thuku. The stimulator was placed around the L5-S1 
disc space levels but Dr. Thuku recorded no pain complaints in the region of the 
stimulator or the back in general.  
On July 20, 2021, claimant was advised by his treating physical therapist that he 
should avoid twisting and bending due to the wires connected to the stimulator. There 
was no mention of any back pain or restriction of motion due to back pain.  
Dr. Bansal opines that delayed back pain is a logical result of the left ankle injury 
due to the cumulative effects of an antalgic gait; presumably this is to explain away the 
lack of prior mentions of back pain. However, the claimant’s testimony is somewhat 
contrary to the underpinnings of Dr. Bansal’s opinions. Claimant maintains that his back

pain started a few months after his accident and that he relayed this symptom to Dr.

Moving to the issue of rate, the parties dispute the average weekly wage.  
Claimant’s accident occurred on September 23, 2019. Claimant was working an 
average of 49.36 - 50.25 hours per week. (Defendants’ Exhibit B:49 v. CE 5:1) Claimant 
argues that the week ending 7/5/2019 was not representative as he only worked 33.25 
hours with no overtime. Defendants acknowledge this week was lower but believe that it 
is still representative. However, the claimant’s alternative was to include the week prior 
ending on June 28, 2019, wherein claimant worked 64.50 hours which is far in excess of 
the average hours worked. (CE 5:1)  Claimant also was paid a $600 bonus for the week 
ending June 28, 2019. Bonuses are irregularly paid. Claimant testified that they would 
be paid when the defendant employer wanted to pay them with no set schedule and 
according to defendant employer’s discretion. Irregular bonuses are not to be counted.  
Neither the week ending July 7, 2019 with 33.25 hours worked, nor the week 
ending June 28, 2019, are representative weeks. The average weekly wages are most 
accurately represented by using the weeks in claimant’s exhibits excluding week 11 and 
the week ending on September 20, 2019, with 51.25 hours worked. That results in 13 
total weeks for an average weekly wage of $825.78 (50.33 hours per week x $25.75 = 
$1,295.9975 at a M2)  
The extent of claimant’s injury is based on his left ankle injury and the resulting 
CRPS. CRPS is a whole body injury but defendants argue that Iowa Code 85.34(2)(v) 
should be applied.  
Under Section 85.34(2)(v), if an employee is receiving the same or greater 
salary, wages, or earnings than the employee received at the time of the injury he is to 
be compensated based on a functional rating of his loss.  
 
If an employee who is eligible for compensation under this paragraph 
returns to work or is offered work for which the employee receives or 
would receive the same or greater salary, wages, or earnings than the 

GUSTIN V. CENTRAL STATES ROOFING 
Page 17 
 
employee received at the time of the injury, the employee shall be 
compensated based only upon the employee’s functional impairment 
resulting from the injury, and not in relation to the employee’s earning 
capacity.  
 
Iowa Code Section 85.34(2)(v).  
 
In the 13 weeks prior to his accident, it has been determined claimant’s gross 
weekly wages were $1,296.  He was paid $25.74 per hour with an overtime rate of 
$38.625 per hour and double overtime rate of $51.50 per hour. At the time of the 
hearing, he was earning over $30.00 per hour. (DE A) He returned to work again on or 
around June 23, 2021, and he has continued to work for the employer since and 
remained employed at the time of the trial. (DE C)  
 
Increases in hourly rate are not the sole determinant of whether an injured 
worker receives or would receive the same or greater salary, wages, or earnings than 
the employee received at the time of the injury. McCoy v. Menard, Inc., File No. 
1651840.01 (App. Dec. April 09, 2021). In McCoy the commissioner rejected the 
argument that a higher hourly wage would trigger the application of Section 85.34(2)(v). 
Instead, the hourly wage must be considered in tandem with the actual hours worked or 
that are available to the claimant.  
 
The parties agree that the summer months are busier than the winter months and 
that the employer is not offering claimant fewer hours and there is no medical restriction 
that limits claimant to fewer hours post incident. However, claimant testified that he no 
longer works weekends due to lack of demand from his employer.  
In Claimant’s Exhibit 4, claimant worked an average of 40+ hours per week from 
January 15, 2021, through March 12, 2021, excluding weeks where he took vacation. 
(CE 4) In 2022, the pay stubs from January 2022 to April 29, 2022, excluding vacation, 
showed he worked an average of 28 hours per week, close to the hours he worked in 
2023 during a similar timeframe. However those pay records show only the claimant’s 
post injury hours. There are only a few payroll dates in evidence for periods of time 
preceding the injury. In the spring of 2019, claimant worked an average of 36 hours for 
the three weeks of March excluding vacation time. (CE 4:1) His hourly wage is higher, 
but his hours are reduced from an average of around 36 hours per week to 28 hours per 
week in non-peak periods. See Lewis v. Hy-Vee, Inc., File No. 19700629.01 (Arb. Dec. 
Mar. 04, 2021) (finding a ten-hour reduction per week negates the application of Section 
85.34(2)(v)) Even with the higher hourly wage, 28 hours at $30.00 per hour is less than 
36 hours at $25.74 per hour.  
Therefore, looking at the claimant’s wages holistically, claimant is earning less 
today than prior to the injury.  
Based on the foregoing, claimant’s injury is unscheduled.

Since claimant has an impairment to the body as a whole, an industrial disability 
has been sustained.  Industrial disability was defined in Diederich v. Tri-City R. Co., 219 
Iowa 587, 258 N.W. 899 (1935) as follows:  "It is therefore plain that the legislature 
intended the term 'disability' to mean 'industrial disability' or loss of earning capacity and 
not a mere 'functional disability' to be computed in the terms of percentages of the total 
physical and mental ability of a normal man." 
Functional impairment is an element to be considered in determining industrial 
disability which is the reduction of earning capacity, but consideration must also be 
given to the injured employee's age, education, qualifications, experience, motivation, 
loss of earnings, severity and situs of the injury, work restrictions, inability to engage in 
employment for which the employee is fitted and the employer's offer of work or failure 
to so offer.  McSpadden v. Big Ben Coal Co., 288 N.W.2d 181 (Iowa 1980); Olson v. 
Goodyear Service Stores, 255 Iowa 1112, 125 N.W.2d 251 (1963); Barton v. Nevada 
Poultry Co., 253 Iowa 285, 110 N.W.2d 660 (1961). 
Compensation for permanent partial disability shall begin at the termination of the 
healing period.  Compensation shall be paid in relation to 500 weeks as the disability 
bears to the body as a whole.  Section 85.34. 
The whole-body rating for claimant is 9 percent by Dr. Mooney in 2021 and 10 
percent by Dr. Bansal in 2023 (excluding the 5 percent impairment rating given for the 
back). Claimant is no longer a roofer and has been placed in the upper medium 
category of physical demand which allows him to carry, lift, push or pull materials up to 
40 pounds on an occasional basis. He is limited to standing and walking combined up to 
67 percent of the day, cannot crouch, and is limited in using stairs and forward bend 
standing positions. He has a high school education, and his relevant work history is 
primarily as a laborer. He has some loss of wages due to decreased hours worked from 
the lack of overtime and weekend work, but has been able to work fifty hours a week as 
a loader and unloader for defendant employer.  
Based on the foregoing, it is determined claimant has sustained a 25 percent 
industrial loss primarily based on the 2023 FCE which placed claimant in the upper 
medium category of physical demand work. The heavy category of work is no longer an 
option for claimant.
