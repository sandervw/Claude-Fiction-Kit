STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Merchant Johnson, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from Duro Improvements Corp., defendant employer, and 
unknown insurer, as a result of an injury that arose out of and in the course of 
employment on March 11, 2024. 
On March 17, 2025, claimant file an entry of default against the defendant for 
failure to timely file an answer to the petition. On April 24, 2025, an entry of default was 
entered against defendant. Claimant moved to have the case submitted on the record 
on September 10, 2025. That motion was granted.. The record consists of Joint Exhibits 
1-6 and Claimant’s Exhibits 1-6.   
ISSUES 
1. Whether claimant's injury resulted in a permanent disability. 
 
2. The extent of claimant's entitlement to permanent partial disability benefits. 
 
3. Whether there is a causal connection between the injury and the claimed 
medical expenses. 
 
4. Whether claimant is entitled to reimbursement for an independent medical 
evaluation (IME) under Iowa Code section 85.39. 
 
5. Whether the defendant is liable for a penalty. 
Prior to the submission of this case, claimant also made a claim for benefits as 
against the Second Injury Fund of Iowa (Fund). Claimant settled his case as against the 
Fund before this matter was submitted on the record.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
On March 11, 2024, claimant was moving a large appliance from a warehouse to 
a truck by himself.  While claimant was maneuvering a refrigerator, he fell backwards. 
The refrigerator fell to the floor with claimant's right leg twisted under the refrigerator. 
(Claimant’s Exhibit 1, page 6; Joint Exhibit 4, page 37) 
On March 13, 2024, claimant was evaluated by Kurt Chamberlain, M.D., at the 
St. Luke's Hospital Emergency Department.  Claimant was trying to load a 400-pound 
refrigerator onto a dolly, when the dolly and the refrigerator fell backwards with the 
refrigerator landing on claimant’s right lower extremity. (JE 4, p. 37) 
Claimant had x-rays at St. Luke's. Claimant was assessed as having a closed 
displaced comminuted fracture of the shaft of the right fibula. (JE 4, pp. 39, 41)  
Claimant was given pain medication and discharged from the emergency department. 
(JE 4, p. 39)  
On March 25, 2024, claimant was seen by Qadnana Anwar, M.D. Claimant was 
advised to be nonweightbearing, but had walked into the clinic without crutches. 
Claimant had been able to walk. Claimant had worsening pain. Oxycodone that had 
been prescribed for pain was causing a rash. Claimant was given hydroxyzine for his 
rash. Claimant was given a referral to an orthopedic clinic. (JE 1, pp. 13-14) 
On March 27, 2024, claimant was evaluated by Gregory Hill, M.D., orthopedic 
specialist. Claimant had been able to walk with pain. Claimant had x-rays. He was 
assessed as having a right proximal minimally displaced fibular fracture. Claimant was 
advised to use crutches and told to use a compression sleeve or ACE wrap for support. 
He was recommended to return in four weeks for x-rays. (JE 2, pp. 22-24) 
In an April 24, 2025, report, Sunil Bansal, M.D., gave his opinions of claimant’s 
condition following an IME.  Claimant had pain in the right leg after walking for 15 to 20 
minutes. (Ex. 1, p. 7)  
Claimant was assessed as having a displaced comminuted fracture of the right 
proximal fibular shaft. (Ex. 1, p. 6)  Claimant's injury at Duro with the refrigerator was 
found to cause the fracture of his right proximal fibula. (Ex. 1, p. 7) 
Dr. Bansal found that claimant had a 12 percent permanent impairment of the 
right lower extremity. He also found claimant had a two percent permanent impairment 
to the right hand and a one percent permanent impairment to the left hand.  He 
restricted claimant, in part, from frequent kneeling and squatting, no prolonged walking 
more than 30 minutes, and to avoid multiple stairs. (Ex. 1, p. 8) Dr. Bansal found 
claimant at maximum medical improvement (MMI) as of April 16, 2025. (Ex. 1, p. 9)

CONCLUSION OF LAW 
The first issue to be determined is whether claimant's injury resulted in a 
permanent disability. 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
A possibility of causation is not sufficient; a probability is necessary. Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996) (citing Holmes v. Bruce 
Motor Freight, Inc., 215 N.W.2d 296, 297 (Iowa 1974)). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 
621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 
(Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
Claimant had a work-related injury that caused a fracture of the right fibula. 
Claimant’s injury occurred in March 2024. At the time of his IME with Dr. Bansal, 
claimant still had problems with prolonged walking. Dr. Bansal's unrebutted opinion is 
that claimant's March 11, 2024, work injury resulted in a permanent disability. Claimant 
has carried his burden of proof his March 11, 2024, injury resulted in a permanent 
disability.  
The next issue to be determined is the extent of claimant's entitlement to 
permanent partial disability benefits.  
Under the Iowa Workers' Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is 
compensated either for a loss or loss of use of a scheduled member under Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(a)-(u) or as an unscheduled injury pursuant to the provisions of section

85.34(2)(v). The extent of scheduled member disability benefits to which an injured 
worker is entitled is determined by using the functional method. Functional disability is 
"limited to the loss of the physiological capacity of the body or body part.” Mortimer v. 
Fruehauf Corp., 502 N.W.2d 12, 15 (Iowa 1993); Sherman v. Pella Corp., 576 N.W.2d 
312, 320 (Iowa 1998).   
Iowa Code section 85.34(x) states:  
x. In all cases of permanent partial disability described in paragraphs “a” 
through “u”, or paragraph “v” when determining functional disability and 
not loss of earning capacity, the extent of loss or percentage of permanent 
impairment shall be determined solely by utilizing the guides to the 
evaluation of permanent impairment, published by the American medical 
association, as adopted by the workers' compensation commissioner by 
rule pursuant to chapter 17A. Lay testimony or agency expertise shall not 
be utilized in determining loss or percentage of permanent impairment 
pursuant to paragraphs “a” through “u”, or paragraph “v” when determining 
functional disability and not loss of earning capacity.  
Iowa Code section 85.34 (x).  
This agency has adopted the Guides to the Evaluation of Permanent Impairment, 
Fifth Edition, published by the American Medical Association for determining the extent 
of loss or percentage of impairment for permanent partial disabilities.  See 876 IAC 2.4.  
Dr. Bansal's unrebutted opinion is claimant's March 2024 work injury resulted in a 
12 percent permanent partial disability to the right lower extremity.  He also found 
claimant had a two percent permanent impairment to the right hand and a one percent 
permanent impairment to the left hand.  Claimant is entitled to 32.1 weeks of permanent 
partial disability benefits [(1 percent + 2 percent x 190 weeks) + (12 percent x 220 
weeks)].  Guides at page 604 
Dr. Bansal placed claimant at MMI as of April 16, 2025. Permanent partial 
disability benefits shall commence as of that date.  
The next issue to be determined is rate.  
The basis of compensation is the weekly earnings of the employee at the time of 
the injury.  Iowa Code § 85.36 (2015).  Weekly earnings are the gross salary, wages, or 
earnings to which an employee would have been entitled had the employee worked the 
customary hours for the full pay period in which the employee was injured as the 
employer regularly required for the work or employment.  Id.  The various subsections of 
section 85.36 set forth methods of computing weekly earnings depending upon the type 
of earnings and employment.  Id. 
If the employee is paid on a daily or hourly basis or by output, weekly earnings 
are computed by dividing by thirteen the earnings over the thirteen-week period

immediately preceding the injury.  Iowa Code § 85.36(6) (2015).  Any week that does 
not fairly reflect the employee’s customary earnings is excluded, however.  Id. 
The representative weeks of claimant's wages with Duro indicate claimant 
earned $5,088.00. (Ex. 6)  Claimant's average weekly wage is $1,017.60 ($5,088.00 
divided by 5).  Claimant was married with three exemptions. Claimant’s rate is $695.68 
per week. (Ex. 4)  
The next issue be determined is whether there is a causal connection between 
the injury and the claimed medical expenses.  
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance, and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Iowa Code section 85.27.  Holbert 
v. Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening October 1975). 
Claimant seeks payment of medical expenses detailed in Joint Exhibit 6. Records 
indicate the medical bill shown in Joint Exhibit 6 relates to the care and treatment of 
claimant for his work-related injury. There is no evidence the charges are not causally 
connected to claimant’s work-related injury. There is no evidence the cost associated 
with Joint Exhibit 6 bill is not fair and reasonable.  Defendant is liable for charges as 
detailed in Joint Exhibit 6. 
The next issue to be determined is whether claimant is entitled to reimbursement 
for an IME from Dr. Bansal under Iowa Code section 85.39.  
An employer is only liable to reimburse an employee for the cost of an 
examination conducted pursuant to this subsection if the injury for which the employee 
is being examined is determined to be compensable.  Iowa Code § 85.39(2) (2017).  
“An employer is not liable for the cost of such examination if the injury for which the 
employee is being examined is determined not to have a compensable injury. A 
determination of the reasonableness of a fee for an examination made pursuant to this 
subsection, shall be based on the typical fee charged by a medical provider to perform 
an impairment rating in the local area where the examination is conducted.”  Iowa Code 
§ 85.39(2); Tweeten v. Tweeten, 999 N.W.2d 270, 282 (Iowa 2023).   
Iowa Code section 85.39(2), as amended, allows an employee with a 
compensable injury to be reimbursed the reasonable cost of an examination. See Iowa 
Code § 85.39(2)(2017); Mid American Construction LLC v. Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d 838, 849 
(Iowa 2024). Under the statute, an examination may include reviewing medical records, 
conducting a physical exam, opining on causation, assessing permanent impairment, 
assigning restrictions, and recommending further care. Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d at 851.

The burden is on the claimant to show the reasonableness of his or her chosen 
physician’s IME fee. Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d at 852; see also Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e) 
(stating “Ordinarily, the burden of proof on an issue is upon the party who would suffer 
loss if the issue were not established.”); Pexa v. Auto Owners Ins. Co., 686 N.W.2d 150, 
156 (Iowa 2004)(stating “the plaintiff has the burden to prove the reasonable value of 
the [medical] service rendered”).  The reasonableness of an IME fee is a question of 
fact to be decided by the commissioner. See Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 
154 (Iowa 1996). The reasonableness of an IME fee must be based upon the typical fee 
charged by a medical provider performing an impairment rating in the local area where 
the IME is conducted. Iowa Code § 85.39 (2); Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d at 852.   
In Des Moines Area Reg’l Transit Auth. v. Young, 867 N.W.2d 839, 847 (Iowa 
2015), the Supreme Court noted that in cases where Iowa Code section 85.39 IME 
reimbursement is not triggered, a claimant can still be reimbursed expenses associated 
with the preparation of a written report as a cost under administrative rule 876 IAC 4.33 
because the report is being used as evidence in lieu of the doctor’s testimony.  Id. at 
846-47.  However, only the cost of the report can be assessed, not the underlying exam 
or treatment. Id. 
Dr. Bansal charged $2,958.00 for claimant’s IME. He implied his charges are 
reasonable and typical for charges made in his local area. Claimant is entitled to 
reimbursement for Dr. Bansal's IME. 
The next issue to be determined is whether defendant is liable for a penalty.  
In Christensen v. Snap-on Tools Corp., 554 N.W.2d 254 (Iowa 1996) and 
Robbennolt v. Snap-on Tools Corp, 555 N.W.2d 229 (Iowa 1996) the supreme court 
said: 
Based on the plain language of section 86.13,1 we hold an employee 
is entitled to penalty benefits if there has been a delay in payment unless 
the employer proves a reasonable cause or excuse.  [Iowa Code § 86.13 
(2015).]  A reasonable cause or excuse exists if either (1) the delay was 
necessary for the insurer to investigate the claim or (2) the employer had a 
reasonable basis to contest the employee’s entitlement to benefits.  A 
“reasonable basis” for denial of the claim exists if the claim is “fairly 
debatable.”  Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 260. 
The supreme court has stated: 
(1) If the employer has a reason for the delay and conveys that 
reason to the employee contemporaneously with the beginning of the delay, 
no penalty will be imposed if the reason is of such character that a 
reasonable fact finder could conclude that it is a "reasonable or probable

cause or excuse" under Iowa Code section 86.13 [2015].  In that case, we 
will defer to the decision of the commissioner.  See Christensen, 554 
N.W.2d at 260 (substantial evidence found to support commissioner’s 
finding of legitimate reason for delay pending receipt of medical report); 
Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 236. 
(2) If no reason is given for the delay or if the “reason” is not one that 
a reasonable fact finder could accept, we will hold that no such cause or 
excuse exists and remand to the commissioner for the sole purpose of 
assessing penalties under section 86.13.  See Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 
261. 
(3) Reasonable causes or excuses include (a) a delay for the 
employer to investigate the claim, Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 260; 
Kiesecker v. Webster City Custom Meats, Inc., 528 N.W.2d at 109, 111 
(Iowa 1995); or (b) the employer had a reasonable basis to contest the 
claimthe “fairly debatable” basis for delay.  See Christensen, 554 N.W.2d 
at 260 (holding two-month delay to obtain employer’s own medical report 
reasonable under the circumstances).  
(4) For the purpose of applying section 86.13, the benefits that are 
underpaid as well as late-paid benefits are subject to penalties, unless the 
employer establishes reasonable and probable cause or excuse. [Iowa 
Code § 86.13 (2015);] Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 237 (underpayment 
resulting from application of wrong wage base; in absence of excuse, 
commissioner required to apply penalty). 
If we were to construe [Iowa Code § 86.13 (2015)] to 
permit the avoidance of penalty if any amount of 
compensation benefits are paid, the purpose of the penalty 
statute would be frustrated.  For these reasons, we conclude 
section 86.13 is applicable when payment of compensation is 
not timely . . . or when the full amount of compensation is not 
paid.  Id. 
(5) For purposes of determining whether there has been a delay, 
payments are “made” when (a) the check addressed to a claimant is mailed 
(Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 236; Kiesecker, 528 N.W.2d at 112), or (b) the 
check is delivered personally to the claimant by the employer or its workers’ 
compensation insurer.  Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 235.   
(6) In determining the amount of the penalty, the commissioner is to 
consider factors such as the length of the delay, the number of delays, the 
information available to the employer regarding the employee’s injury and 
wages, and the employer’s past record of penalties.  Robbennolt, 555 
N.W.2d at 238.

(7) An employer’s bare assertion that a claim is “fairly debatable” 
does not make it so.  A fair reading of Christensen and Robbennolt, makes 
it clear that the employer must assert facts upon which the commissioner 
could reasonably find that the claim was “fairly debatable.”  See 
Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 260. 
Meyers v. Holiday Express Corp., 557 N.W.2d 502, 504-505 (Iowa 1996).   
Weekly compensation payments are due at the end of the compensation week.  
Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 235. 
Penalty is not imposed for delayed interest payments.  Davidson v. Bruce, 594 
N.W.2d 833, 840 (Iowa Ct. App. 1999); Schadendorf v. Snap-On Tools Corp., 757 
N.W.2d 330, 338 (Iowa 2008).   
When an employee’s claim for benefits is fairly debatable based on a good faith 
dispute over the employee’s factual or legal entitlement to benefits, an award of penalty 
benefits is not appropriate under the statute.  Gilbert v. USF Holland, Inc., 637 N.W.2d 
194, 199 (Iowa 2001).  Whether the issue was fairly debatable turns on whether there 
was a disputed factual dispute that, if resolved in favor of the employer, would have 
supported the employer's denial of compensability.  Id. 
Claimant is due 32.1 weeks of permanent partial disability benefits. Defendant 
gave no good reason why claimant’s permanent partial disability benefits have not been 
paid. A penalty of 50 percent is appropriate. Defendant is liable for a penalty of 
$11,165.67 (32.1 weeks x $695.68 x 50 percent).
