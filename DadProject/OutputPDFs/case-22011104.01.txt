STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Patrick Conrad, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers' 
compensation benefits from International Paper Company (International Paper), 
employer, and Old Republic Insurance Company, insurer, both as defendants.  This 
matter was heard on February 1, 2024, with a final submission date of February 15, 
2024. 
The record in this case consists of Joint Exhibits 1-2, Claimant’s Exhibit 1, 
Defendants’ Exhibits A-C, and the testimony of claimant. 
STIPULATIONS 
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised 
or discussed in this decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations. 
ISSUES 
1. The extent of claimant’s entitlement to permanent partial disability benefits. 
 
2. Whether claimant is entitled to reimbursement for an independent medical 
evaluation (IME) under Iowa Code section 85.39.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
Claimant was 63 years old at the time of hearing.  (Hearing Transcript, page 9)  
Claimant began work with International Paper in August of 2006.  He worked for 
International Paper as a clamp truck driver.  (Tr., p. 10) 
In approximately August of 2022, claimant was assigned a different job in the 
“corrugator utility” position.  (Claimant’s Exhibit 1, page 2)  The job required claimant to 
push 7,500-pound rolls of paper onto a machine.  Claimant developed pain in the right 
shoulder as a result of this activity.  (Ex. 1, p. 2)   
Claimant went on vacation and his right shoulder symptoms improved.  His 
symptoms returned after he returned to work.  (Joint Exhibit 1, page 1) 
Claimant was referred to UnityPoint Health by his employer.  On September 19, 
2022, claimant was evaluated by Brooke Shivers, ARNP.  On exam claimant was found 
to have grinding and popping in the right shoulder.  Claimant was assessed as having 
right shoulder pain.  He was prescribed physical therapy and told to use ice and 
ibuprofen.  Claimant was limited to lifting, pushing or pulling up to 15 pounds with no 
overhead lifting.  (JE 1, pp. 1-5) 
The record indicates claimant was evaluated several times by Blake Eyberg, 
M.D.  Claimant was given a cortisone injection in the shoulder by Dr. Eyberg.  (Ex. 1, p. 
3) 
On April 12, 2023, claimant returned to Dr. Eyberg.  Claimant indicated the 
injection helped him for a short time.  Claimant was assessed as having tendinitis of the 
right rotator cuff.  Surgery was discussed as a treatment option.  (JE 2) 
Claimant testified Dr. Eyberg told him he could have surgery.  He said Dr. Eyberg 
told him surgery may not improve his symptoms.  Claimant said he chose not to have 
surgery as he could not afford to take off time from work.  (Tr., pp. 11-12) 
In a November 10, 2023, report, Mark Taylor, M.D., gave his opinion of 
claimant’s condition following an IME.  Dr. Taylor assessed claimant as having a right 
shoulder tendinitis, AC arthrosis and impingement syndrome.  Dr. Taylor found claimant 
had a 7 percent permanent impairment to the right upper extremity.  (Ex. 1, pp. 6-7) 
In a December 15, 2023, report (which appears to be incorrectly dated 
“December 15, 2022”), Jonathan Fields, M.D., gave his opinion of claimant’s condition 
following an IME.  He assessed claimant as having tendinitis of the right rotator cuff with 
AC joint arthritis.  He found claimant had a 5 percent permanent impairment to the right 
upper extremity.  (Defendants’ Exhibit C, pages 23-24) 
Claimant testified Dr. Fields examined him for approximately 45 minutes.  (Tr., p. 
13)  He testified Dr. Taylor examined him for approximately 1-1/2 hours.  (Tr., p.14)

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The first issue to be determined is the extent of claimant’s entitlement to 
permanent partial disability benefits.  
 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3). 
 
In 2017, the Iowa Legislature amended Iowa Code section 85.34. Before the 
2017 changes, shoulder injuries were considered proximal to the arm and compensated 
as a body as a whole injury, under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(u). Prior to the 2017 
changes to Iowa Code section 85.34, a shoulder injury was compensated as an 
unscheduled injury, and based on industrial disability. See Alm v. Morris Barick Cattle 
Co., 240 Iowa 1174, 38 N.W.2d 161(1949).     
   
One of the changes made to Iowa Code section 85.34 in 2017, dealt with the 
shoulder. The legislative changes added the shoulder to the list of scheduled members. 
Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(n) states:  “[f]or the loss of a shoulder, weekly 
compensation during four hundred weeks.” Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(n)(2018). The 
parties stipulated, in the hearing report, claimant’s injury was a scheduled member 
under section 85.34(2)(n). 
 
Two experts have given opinions of claimant’s permanent impairment.  Both Dr. 
Fields and Dr. Taylor saw claimant only once for an IME.  Both Dr. Fields and Dr. Taylor 
gave detailed reports.  Both Dr. Taylor and Dr. Fields relied on the AMA Guides to the 
Evaluation of Permanent Impairment, Fifth Edition, to reach their conclusions regarding 
claimant’s permanent impairment.  Dr. Fields’ and Dr. Taylor’s opinion regarding 
permanent impairment are relatively close. 
 
Claimant has a partial tear in the right rotator cuff.  (JE 2, p. 10)  Claimant 
credibly testified he has constant aching in his right shoulder.  Claimant credibly testified 
that he has lost strength in his right arm and relied mostly on his left arm for his work 
duties.  Claimant credibly testified Dr. Taylor spent twice as long evaluating him than did 
Dr. Fields.  I respect the opinions of both Dr. Taylor and Dr. Fields.  However, based on 
the record as detailed above, I find that Dr. Taylor’s permanent impairment rating of 7 
percent to the upper extremity is more convincing than Dr. Fields’ permanent

impairment rating.  Based on this, claimant is due 28 weeks of permanent partial 
disability benefits (400 weeks x 7 percent). 
 
Both Dr. Fields and Dr. Taylor opined that claimant reached maximum medical 
improvement (MMI) as of April 12, 2023.  (Ex. 1, p. 7; Ex. C, p. 24)  Based on this, the 
commencement date for benefits in this case is April 12, 2023. 
 
The next issue to be determined is whether claimant is due reimbursement for 
Dr. Taylor’s IME. 
 
Section 85.39 permits an employee to be reimbursed for subsequent 
examination by a physician of the employee's choice where an employer-retained 
physician has previously evaluated “permanent disability” and the employee believes 
that the initial evaluation is too low.  The section also permits reimbursement for 
reasonably necessary transportation expenses incurred and for any wage loss 
occasioned by the employee attending the subsequent examination. 
 
Defendants are responsible only for reasonable fees associated with claimant's 
independent medical examination.  Claimant has the burden of proving the 
reasonableness of the expenses incurred for the examination.  See Schintgen v. 
Economy Fire & Casualty Co., File No. 855298 (App. April 26, 1991).  Claimant need 
not ultimately prove the injury arose out of and in the course of employment to qualify 
for reimbursement under section 85.39.  See Dodd v. Fleetguard, Inc., 759 N.W.2d 133, 
140 (Iowa App. 2008). 
Regarding the IME, the Iowa Supreme Court provided a literal interpretation of 
the plain language of Iowa Code section 85.39, stating that section 85.39 only allows 
the employee to obtain an independent medical evaluation at the employer’s expense if 
dissatisfied with the evaluation arranged by the employer.  Des Moines Area Reg’l 
Transit Auth. v. Young, 867 N.W.2d 839, 847 (Iowa 2015). 
Under the Young decision, an employee can only obtain an IME at the 
employer’s expense if an evaluation of permanent disability has been made by an 
employer-retained physician. 
Iowa Code section 85.39 limits an injured worker to one IME.  Larson Mfg. Co., 
Inc. v. Thorson, 763 N.W.2d 842 (Iowa 2009). 
The Supreme Court, in Young noted that in cases where Iowa Code section 
85.39 is not triggered to allow for reimbursement of an independent medical 
examination (IME), a claimant can still be reimbursed at hearing the costs associated 
with the preparation of the written report as a cost under rule 876 IAC 4.33. Young at 
846-847. 
The employee-retained expert, Dr. Taylor, issued his opinion on permanent 
impairment before the employer-retained expert issued his opinion.  As a result, 
claimant cannot be reimbursed the full cost of the IME under Iowa Code section 85.39.

ORDER 
THEREFORE, IT IS ORDERED: 
That defendants shall pay claimant 28 weeks of permanent partial disability 
benefits at the rate of five hundred eighty-seven and 06/100 dollars ($587.06) per week 
commencing on April 12, 2023. 
 
Defendants shall pay accrued weekly benefits in a lump sum together with 
interest at an annual rate equal to the one-year treasury constant maturity published by 
the federal reserve in the most recent H15 report settled as of the date of injury, plus 
two percent.  
 
That defendants shall be given credit for benefits previously paid. 
 
That defendants shall pay claimant two thousand five hundred twenty and 00/100 
dollars ($2,520.00) for the cost of Dr. Taylor’s IME report. 
 
That defendants shall pay costs. 
 
That defendants shall file subsequent reports of injury as required by Rule 876 
IAC 3.1(2).
