STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Brandon Wilson, seeks workers’ compensation benefits from 
defendant employer, Ankeny Glass, Inc., and its workers’ compensation insurance 
carrier, Grinnell Select Insurance Company.  Hearing occurred on October 24, 2024.  
The hearing occurred via Zoom videoconference. 
Mr. Wilson testified on his own behalf and called no additional witnesses.  
Defendants called Eric Young and Brad Williams to testify.  The parties offered Joint 
Exhibits 1 through 10.  Claimant offered Exhibits 1 through 7.  Defendants offered 
Exhibits A through J.  All exhibits were received into evidence without objection.   
Prior to the commencement of the hearing, the parties submitted a hearing 
report.  On that hearing report, the parties entered into several stipulations.  The parties’ 
stipulations are accepted and relied upon in this decision.  No findings of fact or 
conclusions of law will be made with respect to the parties’ stipulations.  The parties are 
now bound by their stipulations. 
ISSUES 
The parties submitted the following issues for determination: 
1. Whether claimant sustained an injury that arose out of and in the course of 
his employment on November 16, 2022. 
2. Whether claimant’s current condition is causally related to the alleged 
November 16, 2022 work injury.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
The undersigned, having considered all the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
Claimant, Brandon Wilson, is a 38-year-old man.  He graduated from high school 
in 2004 and has no educational pursuits beyond high school.  Mr. Wilson commenced 
employment with the employer, Ankeny Glass, Inc., in August 2021.   
Mr. Wilson alleges he sustained a low back injury while performing work duties at 
Ankeny Glass on November 16, 2022.  On that date, Mr. Wilson worked as a delivery 
driver for the employer, delivering cabinets.  Claimant’s job required him to lift and 
manipulate cabinets weighing 50 to 75 pounds. 
Claimant had experienced low back pain prior to the alleged work injury.  He 
obtained chiropractic treatment before the alleged date of injury.  In 2008, he sustained 
a low back injury when he slipped and fell onto his back on a step.  In 2019, claimant fell 
off a scooter and sustained an injury to his low back.  He obtained chiropractic 
treatment in August 2020 and continued to receive chiropractic care through June 2021.  
However, he last obtained chiropractic care for his low back approximately 17 months 
before the alleged injury in this case. 
Mr. Wilson testified at hearing that he went deer hunting on the morning of the 
alleged work injury and reported to work at noon.1  At approximately 2:00 p.m. on 
November 16, 2022, Mr. Wilson was loading a truck with heavy cabinets for delivery to 
a customer.  Mr. Wilson testified that he was “downstacking” cabinets to load them into 
a box truck.  When he picked up a cabinet, he flexed backward and instantly felt a pain 
and burn in his lower back.  (Transcript, page 24)   
Claimant testified that he immediately set the cabinet down and went to the 
employer’s office.  He reported the injury to Eric Young, his supervisor, and explained

Having found that claimant proved a work injury occurred, I must address his

symptoms, range of motion, and physical capabilities at the time of maximum medical

MMI, Dr. Bansal does not acknowledge or explain the differences between his findings

(Joint Ex. 9, p. 137)  Given the findings of the therapist on the same date, the fact that 
claimant returned to his pre-injury job and worked without restriction for several months

and convincing than the work restrictions offered by Dr. Bansal that would preclude 
claimant from performing the cabinet delivery position he clearly performed without 
further injury or treatment after MMI. 
Finally, when I consider the competing permanent impairment ratings, I again 
find the opinions of Dr. Schmitz to be more convincing.  In a report dated June 9, 2023, 
Dr. Schmitz opined, “there is not any impairment with regard to this injury.”  
(Defendants’ Ex. E, p. 12)  Dr. Bansal opined that Mr. Wilson sustained a five percent

examination findings and opinions to be more convincing and consistent with 
simultaneous therapy records, I accept Dr. Schmitz’s permanent impairment rating to be 
more convincing as well.  I find claimant failed to prove this injury caused permanent 
impairment or the need for permanent restrictions.  I find he was capable of returning to 
his prior level of work function and I accept the findings of the physical therapist on May 
12, 2023 that Mr. Wilson “appears to have returned to his prior level of function.”  (Joint 
Ex. 8, p. 130)  I find claimant failed to prove he sustained permanent disability as a 
result of the November 16, 2022 work injury.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The initial disputed issue in this case is whether claimant sustained an injury to 
his low back that arose out of and in the course of his employment with Ankeny Glass 
on November 16, 2022.  The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of 
the evidence that the alleged injury actually occurred and that it both arose out of and in 
the course of the employment. Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 150 (Iowa 
1996); Miedema v. Dial Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309, 311 (Iowa 1996). The words “arising 
out of” refer to the cause or source of the injury. The words “in the course of” refer to the 
time, place, and circumstances of the injury. 2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124, 
128 (Iowa 1995).  An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship 
exists between the injury and the employment. Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. The injury 
must be a rational consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not 
merely incidental to the employment. Koehler Elec. v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1, 3 (Iowa 
2000); Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. An injury occurs “in the course of” employment 
when it happens within a period of employment at a place where the employee 
reasonably may be when performing employment duties and while the employee is 
fulfilling those duties or doing an activity incidental to them. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d at 150. 
Having found that claimant proved he sustained a low back injury on November 
16, 2022 as a result of his work duties at Ankeny Glass, I conclude that claimant proved 
he sustained an injury that both arose out of and in the course of his employment on 
November 16, 2022. 
Defendants also dispute whether claimant proved the work injury caused 
permanent disability.  The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of 
the evidence that the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is 
based.  A cause is proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it 
need not be the only cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal 
connection is probable rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 
569 N.W.2d 148, 153 (Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 
156 (Iowa Ct. App. 1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa 
Ct. App. 1996). 
A possibility of causation is not sufficient; a probability is necessary. Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996) (citing Holmes v. Bruce 
Motor Freight, Inc., 215 N.W.2d 296, 297 (Iowa 1974)). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 
621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652

(Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
While a claimant is not entitled to compensation for the results of a preexisting 
injury or disease, its mere existence at the time of a subsequent injury is not a defense.  
Rose v. John Deere Ottumwa Works, 247 Iowa 900, 908, 76 N.W.2d 756, 760-61 
(1956).  If the claimant had a preexisting condition or disability that is materially 
aggravated, accelerated, worsened or lighted up so that it results in disability, claimant 
is entitled to recover.  Nicks v. Davenport Produce Co., 254 Iowa 130, 135, 115 N.W.2d 
812, 815 (1962); Yeager v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co., 253 Iowa 369, 374-75, 112 
N.W.2d 299, 302 (1961). 
Having accepted the medical opinions of Dr. Schmitz and the treating physical 
therapist over those offered by Dr. Bansal, I found that claimant failed to prove the 
November 16, 2022 low back injury caused the need for permanent work restrictions or 
that it caused permanent functional impairment.  Ultimately, I found that claimant failed 
to prove the injury caused permanent disability.  I conclude claimant failed to carry his 
burden of proof to establish an award for permanent disability benefits. 
Mr. Wilson further sought an award of penalty benefits for defendants’ denial of 
his claim for permanent disability benefits.  Pursuant to Iowa Code section 86.13(4) 
(2023)3, claimant must prove a delay in payment or denial of weekly benefits for penalty 
benefits to be imposed against an employer.  Having concluded that claimant failed to 
prove entitlement to an award of any permanent disability benefits, I conclude claimant 
cannot establish a delay or denial in payment of weekly benefits.  Moreover, having 
found that claimant failed to prove permanent disability; I conclude defendants clearly 
had a reasonable basis for denial of such claim.  Iowa Code section 86.13(4); Gilbert v. 
USF Holland, Inc., 637 N.W.2d 194, 199 (Iowa 2001).  Penalty benefits are 
inappropriate and denied in this case.  
Claimant next asserts a claim for reimbursement of his independent medical 
evaluation pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.39.  Iowa Code section 85.39 permits an 
employee to be reimbursed for subsequent examination by a physician of the 
employee's choice where an employer-retained physician has previously evaluated 
“permanent disability” and the employee believes that the initial evaluation is too low.   
Iowa Code § 85.39(2) (2023).  The section also permits reimbursement for reasonably 
necessary transportation expenses incurred and for any wage loss occasioned by the 
employee attending the subsequent examination.  Iowa Code § 85.39 (1), (2).

The Iowa Supreme Court has provided a literal interpretation of the plain 
language of Iowa Code section 85.39, stating that section only allows the employee to 
obtain an independent medical evaluation at the employer’s expense if an evaluation of 
permanent disability has already been made by an employer retained physician.  Des 
Moines Area Reg’l Transit Auth. v. Young, 867 N.W.2d 839, 847 (Iowa 2015). Under 
the Young decision, an employee can only obtain an IME at the employer's expense if 
an evaluation of permanent disability has been made by an employer-retained 
physician. Id. 
An employer is only liable to reimburse an employee for the cost of an 
examination conducted pursuant to this subsection if the injury for which the employee 
is being examined is determined to be compensable.  Iowa Code § 85.39(2) (2017).  
“An employer is not liable for the cost of such examination if the injury for which the 
employee is being examined is determined not to have a compensable injury. A 
determination of the reasonableness of a fee for an examination made pursuant to this 
subsection, shall be based on the typical fee charged by a medical provider to perform 
an impairment rating in the local area where the examination is conducted.”  Iowa Code 
§ 85.39(2); Tweeten v. Tweeten, 999 N.W.2d 270, 282 (Iowa 2023).   
Iowa Code section 85.39(2), as amended, allows an employee with a 
compensable injury to be reimbursed the reasonable cost of an examination. See Iowa 
Code § 85.39(2)(2017); Mid American Construction LLC v. Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d 838, 849 
(Iowa 2024). Under the statute, an examination may include reviewing medical records, 
conducting a physical exam, opining on causation, assessing permanent impairment, 
assigning restrictions, and recommending further care. Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d at 851.  
The burden is on the claimant to show the reasonableness of his or her chosen 
physician’s IME fee. Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d at 852; see also Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e)(stating “Ordinarily, the burden of proof on an issue is upon the party who 
would suffer loss if the issue were not established.”); Pexa v. Auto Owners Ins. Co., 686 
N.W.2d 150, 156 (Iowa 2004)(stating “the plaintiff has the burden to prove the 
reasonable value of the [medical] service rendered”).  The reasonableness of an IME 
fee is a question of fact to be decided by the commissioner. See Quaker Oats Co. v. 
Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 154 (Iowa 1996). The reasonableness of an IME fee must be 
based upon the typical fee charged by a medical provider performing an impairment 
rating in the local area where the IME is conducted. Iowa Code § 85.39 (2); Sandlin, 2 
N.W.3d at 852.   
In this case, I found that Mr. Wilson proved he sustained a low back injury that 
arose out of and in the course of his employment.  Defendants are responsible for 
claimant’s related medical care for this work injury.  Therefore, I conclude claimant 
proved a compensable claim and is entitled to pursue reimbursement of an independent 
medical evaluation pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.39. 
As noted, the evaluation sought by claimant must occur after a physician retained 
by the defendants has evaluated claimant’s permanent disability, or lack thereof, as the 
case may be.  In this instance, Dr. Schmitz was retained and authorized by defendants.

He offered an opinion pertaining to permanent impairment, MMI, and work restrictions 
on May 12, 2023.  Claimant did not obtain an IME with Dr. Bansal until September 10, 
2024.  Therefore, I conclude claimant has proven the pre-requisites to obtain 
reimbursement of an independent medical evaluation fee. 
With respect to the amount of the IME fee, claimant must prove that Dr. Bansal’s 
fee was reasonable based upon the typical fee charged by a medical provider 
performing an impairment rating in the local area where the IME is conducted.  Iowa 
Code section 85.39(2); Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d at 852. Dr. Bansal’s billing statement 
specifically provides: 
The charges that I have set forth, which include my records review, review 
of history, causation analysis, physical examination, measurements, 
testing, impairment rating calculations, MMI assessment restriction 
assessment, future medical care assessment and the drafting of the IME 
report in this case is a reasonable and typical fee charged by medical 
providers in my local area. 
(Claimant’s Ex. 6, p. 32)  There is no contrary evidence in this case regarding the 
reasonableness of Dr. Bansal’s fee.  Therefore, I conclude that the full amount of Dr. 
Bansal’s IME fee, $3,279.00, should be assessed pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.39. 
Finally, claimant seeks assessment of costs against defendant employer.  
Assessment of costs is within the discretion of the agency.  Iowa Code section 86.40 
(2023)4.  Claimant failed to prevail on his claim for an award of permanent disability.  
However, claimant received an award of his independent medical evaluation fees.  
Given defendants’ refusal to voluntarily pay this IME fee, litigation and hearing were 
necessary for claimant to recover an amount he was owed.  I conclude it is reasonable 
and appropriate to assess claimant’s request for his filing fee ($125.30) under these 
circumstances.  876 IAC 4.33(7).  
The only other cost sought by claimant is assessment of Dr. Bansal’s report fee.  
Dr. Bansal’s fee was awarded pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.39.  Therefore, the 
request for it to be assessed as a cost is denied.
