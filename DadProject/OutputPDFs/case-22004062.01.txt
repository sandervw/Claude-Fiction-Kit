Claimant Chantell Emunah seeks workers’ compensation benefits from the 
defendants, employer 81N Prime LLC (81N Prime) and insurance carrier Accident Fund 
Insurance Company of America (Accident Fund) for three alleged work injuries: 
 Traumatic injury to the left wrist;

any permanent disability resulted from it and denying the alleged work injuries of left

A .  
C a u s a t i o n.  
The Iowa Workers’ Compensation Act “covers any and all personal injuries” 
relating to an employee’s work. Hanson v. Dickinson, 188 Iowa 728, 176 N.W. 823, 824 
(1920). Under section 85.3(1), an employer covered by the Act must “provide, secure, 
and pay compensation according to the provisions of this chapter for any and all 
personal injuries sustained by an employee arising out of and in the course of the 
employment.” The legislature has codified a partial definition of “personal injury” in 
section 85.61(5) that is not applicable in the current case. Rather, the expanded 
definition of “personal injury” as articulated by the Iowa Supreme Court in its seminal 
Almquist v. Shenandoah Nurseries, Inc. governs: 
[A]n injury to the body, the impairment of health, or a disease, not 
excluded by the act, which comes about, not through the natural building 
up and tearing down of the human body, but because of a traumatic or 
other hurt or damage to the health or body of an employee.... The injury to 
the human body here contemplated must be something, whether an 
accident or not, that acts extraneously to the natural processes of nature, 
and thereby impairs the health, overcomes, injures, interrupts, or destroys 
some function of the body, or otherwise damages or injures a part or all of 
the body.  
Dunlavey v. Econ. Fire & Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 850–51 (Iowa 1995) (quoting 
Almquist, 218 Iowa 724, 732, 254 N.W. 35, 39 (1934)) (citations omitted in Dunlavey). 
“In order for an injury to be compensable in Iowa, there must be ‘a connection 
between the injury and the work.’” Lakeside Casino v. Blue, 743 N.W.2d 169, 173 (Iowa 
2007) (quoting Meyer v. IBP, Inc., 710 N.W.2d 213, 221 (Iowa 2006)). To recover, a 
claimant must prove by a preponderance of the evidence that the injury for which the 
claimant is seeking workers’ compensation arose: 
1) Out of the claimant’s employment; and 
 
2) In the course of the claimant’s employment. St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 
N.W.2d 646, 652 (Iowa 2000) (citing Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 
143, 150 (Iowa 1996)); see also Miedema v. Dial Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309, 311 
(Iowa 1996).  
“‘The two tests are separate and distinct.’” Blue, 743 N.W.2d at 174 (quoting 
Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311). Under Iowa law, “‘both must be satisfied in order for an 
injury to be deemed compensable.’” Id. (quoting Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311).  
“An injury ‘arises out of’ employment if there is a causal connection between the 
employment and the injury.” Id. at 652 (citing Bailey v. Batchelder, 576 N.W.2d 334, 338 
(Iowa 1998)). “‘In other words, the injury must not have coincidentally occurred while at 
work, but must in some way be caused by or related to the working environment or the 

EMUNAH V. 81N PRIME LLC 
Page 10 
 
conditions of [the] employment.’” Blue, 743 N.W.2d at 174. (quoting Miedema, 551 
N.W.2d at 311).  
“Taking the words themselves, one is first struck by the fact that in the 
‘arising’ phrase the function of the employment is passive while in the 
‘caused by’ phrase it is active. When one speaks of an event ‘arising out 
of employment,’ the initiative, the moving force, is something other than 
the employment; the employment is thought of more as a condition out of 
which the event arises than as the force producing the event in affirmative 
fashion.” 
Meyer v. IBP, Inc., 710 N.W.2d 213, 223 (Iowa 2006) (quoting 1 Arthur Larson & Lex K. 
Larson, Larson’s Workers’ Compensation Law (2005) § 3.06, at 3-7 to 3-8 (emphasis in 
original)). 
Here, the parties agree Emunah sustained an acute injury while working for 81N 
Prime on the stipulated injury date. Their dispute regards what the fall caused. Emunah 
claims the blunt force from her fall caused carpal tunnel syndrome, a left shoulder injury, 
and a left wrist injury distinct from the carpal tunnel syndrome. The defendants contend 
the fall caused a left wrist injury separate from carpal tunnel syndrome and that is all. 
As found above, the weight of the evidence shows Emunah sustained a work 
injury to her left wrist on the stipulated injury date that is distinct from carpal tunnel 
syndrome. Emunah has failed to meet her burden of proof on the question of whether 
the stipulated work injury caused left carpal tunnel syndrome or a left shoulder injury. 
There is an insufficient basis from which to conclude that Emunah sustained left carpal 
tunnel syndrome or a left shoulder injury on January 17, 2022.

D i s a b i l i t y  B e n e f i t s .  
Workers’ compensation is “a creature of statute.” Darrow v. Quaker Oats Co., 
570 N.W.2d 649, 652 (Iowa 1997). This means an injured employee’s “right to workers' 
compensation is purely statutory.” Downs v. A & H Const., Ltd., 481 N.W.2d 520, 527 
(Iowa 1992).  And “it is the legislature’s prerogative to fix the conditions under which the 
act’s benefits may be obtained.” Darrow, 570 N.W.2d at 652. 
The Iowa Supreme Court has held: 
The legislature enacted the workers' compensation statute primarily for the 
benefit of the worker and the worker's dependents. Therefore, we apply 
the statute broadly and liberally in keeping with the humanitarian objective 
of the statute. We will not defeat the statute's beneficent purpose by 
reading something into it that is not there, or by a narrow and strained 
construction. 
Gregory v. Second Inj. Fund of Iowa, 777 N.W.2d 395, 399 (Iowa 2010) (quoting 
Holstein Elec. v. Breyfogle, 756 N.W.2d 812, 815–16 (Iowa 2008) (citations omitted)).

“Although the workers' compensation statute is to be liberally construed in favor 
of the worker, the statute is not to be expanded by reading something into it that is not 
there.” Downs v. A & H Const., Ltd., 481 N.W.2d 520, 527 (Iowa 1992) (citing Cedar 
Rapids Cmty. Sch. v. Cady, 278 N.W.2d 298 (Iowa 1979)). “To determine legislative 
intent, we look to the language chosen by the legislature and not what the legislature 
might have said.” Ramirez-Trujillo v. Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 N.W.2d 759, (Iowa 2016) 
(citing Schadendorf v. Snap-On Tools Corp., 757 N.W.2d 330, 337 (Iowa 2008)). The 
“broad purpose of workers’ compensation” is “to award compensation (apart from 
medical benefits), not for the injury itself, but the disability produced by a physical 
injury.” Bell Bros. Heating & Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d 193, (Iowa 2010) 
(citing 4 Arthur Larson & Lex K. Larson, Larson’s Workers’ Compensation Law § 80.02, 
at 80–2 (2009)).  
An employer covered by the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Act must “provide, 
secure, and pay compensation according to the provisions of this chapter for any and all 
personal injuries sustained by an employee arising out of and in the course of the 
employment, and in such cases, the employer shall be relieved from other liability for

1 .  
Te m p o r a r y  D i s a b i l i t y  B e n e f i t s .  
The Iowa Supreme Court considered how Iowa Code section 85.33 applies when 
an injured employee returns to work with reduced hours following a work injury. 
Employees who return to work for partial days are limited to TPD benefits. Ellingson v. 
Fleetguard, Inc., 599 N.W.2d 440, 446 (Iowa 1999), overruled on other grounds 
by Waldinger Corp. v. Mettler, 817 N.W.2d 1 (Iowa 2012). TTD benefits under Iowa 
Code section 85.33(2) are only available to employees who miss full days due to a work 
injury. Id.  
The parties agree that the stipulated work injury caused temporary disability 
during a period of recovery. Specifically, they paid to Emunah 19.14 weeks of temporary 
total disability (TTD) benefits at the rate of $461.03 per week and $418.61 in temporary 
partial disability (TPD) benefits. On the hearing report, Emunah did not define the time 
period(s) during which she is seeking additional temporary disability benefits. Even so, 
the evidence shows the defendants paid Emunah TTD through September 9, 2022, 
shortly after Dr. Paulson opined that Emunah’s left carpal tunnel syndrome was not 
work related. There is an insufficient basis in the record from which to conclude Emunah 
missed work after September 9, 2022, due to the left wrist injury separate from her 
diagnosis of carpal tunnel syndrome. 
2 .  
P e r m a n e n t  D i s a b i l i t y  B e n e f i t s .  
Iowa Code section 85.34(2) governs permanent partial disabilities that result from 
work injuries. The statute provides:

The compensation shall be based upon the extent of the disability and 
upon the basis of eighty percent per week of the employee’s average 
spendable weekly earnings, but not more than a weekly benefit amount, 
rounded to the nearest dollar, equal to one hundred eighty-four percent of 
the statewide average weekly wage paid employees as determined by the

The statute further provides that work “all cases of permanent partial disability 
compensation shall be paid” based on the paragraphs in subsection 2.  
As found above, there is an insufficient basis from which to conclude the alleged 
left carpal tunnel syndrome or left shoulder injury arose out of and in the course of 
Emunah’s employment with 81N Prime and therefore are not compensable under the 
Iowa Workers’ Compensation Act. Moreover, as discussed above, no doctor but Dr. 
Sassman has assigned a permanent impairment rating of Emunah’s left upper extremity 
and there is an insufficient basis in the record from which to identify what impairment 
may be related to the left wrist injury that is distinct from the left carpal tunnel syndrome. 
Therefore, Emunah has failed to establish entitlement to permanent partial disability 
(PPD) benefits under the statute. 
C . 
M e d i c a l  B e n e f i t s .  
For all injuries compensable under Iowa Code chapter 85 or 85A, the employer 
must “furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, chiropractic, podiatric, 
physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance, and hospital services and supplies therefor 
and shall allow reasonably necessary transportation expenses incurred for such 
services.” Iowa Code § 85.27(1). This responsibility is coupled with the right to choose 
care for the employee’s work injury. Id. at § 85.27(4). “[A]n employer who authorizes 
care is responsible for the cost of care up to the time when the employer notifies the 
employee it is no longer authorizing care.” Ramirez-Trujillo v. Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 
N.W.2d 759, 771 (Iowa 2016). 
The statute obligates the employer to monitor the care and determine if, and 
when, it no longer wishes to authorize care. Id. at 771. “An employer can easily 
reconsider whether it wishes to authorize further care when an authorized medical 
provider indicates an employee requires no further care for a workplace injury or when 
the employer authorizes a new provider to take over an employee’s care.” Id. at 772. 
“Section 85.27(4) balances this minimal burden with a significant corresponding 
benefit—a means of extinguishing the employer's ongoing obligation to pay for medical 
expenses following its acknowledgment of compensability and exercise of the right to 
choose care.” Id. 
“Nothing in the statute prevents an employee from obtaining unauthorized care” 
and “reimbursement for expenses incurred in seeking unauthorized care upon an 
adjudication of compensability.” Id. at 773 (citing Bell Bros. Heating & Air Conditioning v. 
Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d 193, 205–06 (Iowa 2010) and R.R. Donnelly & Sons v. Barnett, 670

N.W.2d 190, 197 (Iowa 2003)). The Commissioner has the authority to order the 
employer to pay for unauthorized care. Id. (citing W. Side Transp. v. Cordell, 601 
N.W.2d 691, 693 (Iowa 1999)). To recover expenses incurrent in seeking unauthorized 
care, the employee must prove, by a preponderance of the evidence that the care was:  
1) Reasonable; and 
2) Beneficial. Id. (citing Bell Bros., 779 N.W.2d at 206).  
The determination of whether unauthorized care was reasonable and beneficial 
is based on the totality of the circumstances. Id. “[T]he reasonableness of unauthorized 
treatment can normally only be fully evaluated in light of the effectiveness of the 
treatment.” Bell Bros., 779 N.W.2d at 206 (citing Linn Care Ctr. v. Cannon, 704 P.2d 
539, 540 (1985)). “[U]nauthorized medical care is beneficial if it provides a more 
favorable medical outcome than would likely have been achieved by the care authorized 
by the employer.” Id. 
Emunah sought unauthorized care. However, because she has failed to meet her 
burden of proof on causation, there is an insufficient basis in the record from which to 
link the care she sought after the defendants ceased providing care to the stipulated 
work injury. There is an insufficient basis in the record from which to conclude Emunah 
is entitled to payment of additional medical expenses under the Act. 
D . 
I M E .  
After the hearing and submission of post-hearing briefs, the Iowa Supreme Court 
issued its opinion in MidAmerican Construction LLC v. Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d 838 (Iowa 
2024). The opinion addresses IME reimbursement under Iowa Code section 85.39 after 
the 2017 amendments to the statute. Neither party moved to reopen the record for 
submission of additional evidence or supplemental briefing in the wake of the Sandlin 
opinion. 
Emunah seeks reimbursement for Dr. Sassman’s examinations and IME report. 
The defendants make two arguments against reimbursement. This decision addresses 
them in turn.  
Emunah seeks reimbursement for the cost of Dr. Sassman’s examinations. The 
defendants argue that Emunah is only entitled to reimbursement for the cost of the 
impairment rating component of the examination. In doing so, they do not dispute the 
reasonableness of Dr. Sassman’s costs—that is, whether they are in line with those 
charged by occupational medical doctors in central Iowa for an IME of an injured 
employee—only that Emunah is limited to reimbursement for solely the hearing 
impairment portion of her IME.  
In two unreported opinions considering the effect of the 2017 amendments to 
section 85.39(2), the Iowa Court of Appeals held that an injured employee was not 
entitled to reimbursement for an IME, but only for the impairment rating. See 

EMUNAH V. 81N PRIME LLC 
Page 14 
 
MidAmerican Const. LLC v. Sandlin, No. 22-0471 (Iowa Ct. App., Feb. 22, 2023); see 
also P.M. Lattner Mfg. Co. v. Rife, No. 22-1421 (Iowa Ct. App., Jun. 7, 2023). The Iowa 
Supreme Court granted further review on both cases and on February 9, 2024, issued 
decisions reversing the Court of Appeals. See Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d 838; see also P.M. 
Lattner Mfg. Co. v. Rife, 2 N.W.3d 859 (Iowa 2024). 
The court addresses the legal issue in Sandlin, and Rife relies on that case in its 
holding, so this decision’s analysis will focus on that opinion. See Rife, 2 N.W.3d at 867. 
In Sandlin, the court considered the argument that the defendants put forth with respect 
to whether an injured employee is entitled to the reimbursement for the costs of the IME 
or only the component part of the impairment rating. Id. at 846. The Iowa Supreme 
Court held, “The court of appeals erred by limiting reimbursement to the impairment 
rating alone, without including the companying examination.” Id. The court concluded 
that an injured employee is entitled to the reasonable cost of the examination to 
evaluate permanent impairment, which encompasses the records review, consultation 
with other physicians, physical examination, testing, and written report with opinions on 
causation, permanent impairment, and work restrictions. See id. at 849–52. Thus, the 
defendants’ argument that Emunah is limited to reimbursement for only that part of the 
IME that relates to Dr. Sassman’s impairment rating is rejected.  
As well, the defendants contend that Emunah is only entitled to reimbursement of 
one IME, not two. The statutory text of section 85.39(2) does not expressly limit an 
injured employee to one IME. Rather, the statutory text ties the right to reimbursement 
for an IME by a doctor of the employee’s choosing to an opinion on permanent 
impairment by a doctor chosen by the employer which the employee feels is too low. 
Thus, the question of how many IMEs to which an injured employee is entitled under 
section 85.39(2) hinges on the facts of the case.  
The parties’ dispute implicates the following sentence in Iowa Code section 
85.39(2): “An employer is not liable for the cost of such an examination if the injury for 
which the employee is being examined is determined not to be a compensable injury.” 
Here, Emunah sustained a work injury and after that injury, underwent care with multiple 
physicians that have provided different diagnoses and opinions on causation. The 
determination on what is compensable and what is not hinges on the findings of fact 
regarding these opinions. 
The defendants obtained an opinion on permanent impairment from Dr. Han on 
May 3, 2022, under section 85.39(1). Emunah disagreed and underwent an IME with 
Dr. Sassman on July 6, 2022, under section 85.39(2). The injury at issue was 
compensable because Emunah received TTD and TPD for time off work caused by the 
work injury that spurred the examinations and opinions on permanent impairment. 
Therefore, Emunah is entitled to reimbursement for the expenses relating to the July 6, 
2022 IME. According to Dr. Sassman’s IME report, this is one hour for the July 2, 2022 
exam and 4.5 hours reviewing records and preparing the report at an hourly rate of 
$740.00 for a total of $4,070.00. (Ex. 5, p. 1)

After Dr. Sassman’s first IME, Emunah requested additional care, which the 
defendants provided before ultimately obtaining an opinion on causation from Dr. 
Paulson dated August 29, 2022, with respect to her carpal tunnel syndrome. Emunah 
disagreed with the no-causation opinion and returned to Dr. Sassman who performed a 
second in-person evaluation that took one hour, reviewed additional medical records, 
and supplemented her report, which took another hour combined. (Ex. 5, p. 1) At 
$740.00 per hour, this cost $1,480.00. (Ex. 1, p. 5)  
As found above, there is an insufficient basis in the record from which to 
conclude Emunah’s carpal tunnel syndrome or other complaints are causally related to 
the stipulated work injury. Put otherwise, Emunah failed to show this condition is 
compensable. Because the second evaluation and records review related to complaints 
that are not compensable, they may not be reimbursed under the statute.
