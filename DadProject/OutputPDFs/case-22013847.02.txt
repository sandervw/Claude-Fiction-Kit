STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Harold Conger, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits against Manley Construction, LLC, employer.  
This case was heard on October 23, 2024, and considered fully submitted on 
November 6, 2024, upon the simultaneous filing of briefs.  
 The record consists of Joint Exhibits 1–2, Claimant’s Exhibits 1–9, and the 
testimony of claimant and Jess Manley.  
Defendant employer appeared pro se. No insurer appeared, and defendant 
represented at hearing that there was no insurer at the time of the incident.  
ISSUES 
1. Whether the alleged injury is a cause of temporary disability from August 13, 
2020 to November 16, 2020;  
 
2. The extent of claimant’s industrial disability;  
 
3. Whether there is a causal connection between claimant’s injury and the 
medical expenses claimed by claimant; 
 
4. The rate of compensation; 
 
5. Whether claimant is entitled to reimbursement of the IME pursuant to Iowa 
Code section 85.39; 
 
6. Assessment of costs.

STIPULATIONS 
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised 
or discussed in this decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations. 
The parties agree claimant sustained an injury arising out of and in the course of 
his employment with defendant employer on August 13, 2020. This injury resulted in a 
period of temporary disability as well as permanent disability. The parties further agree 
that the disability is industrial in nature.

Claimant, Harold Conger, was a 55-year-old person at the time of the hearing. 
He was single with no dependents. His educational background consists of graduation 
from high school in 1987. He was an average student.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury actually occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 
employment. Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 150 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. 
Dial Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309, 311 (Iowa 1996). The words “arising out of” refer to the 

CONGER V. MANLEY CONSTRUCTION, LLC 
Page 7 
 
cause or source of the injury. The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 
circumstances of the injury. 2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124, 128 (Iowa 
1995).  An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists 
between the injury and the employment. Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. The injury must 
be a rational consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely 
incidental to the employment. Koehler Elec. v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1, 3 (Iowa 2000); 
Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it 
happens within a period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may 
be when performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties 
or doing an activity incidental to them. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d at 150. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 
621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 
(Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
The parties agree claimant sustained a whole body injury arising out of a work 
incident on August 13, 2020, but dispute his benefit rate and entitlement to other 
benefits.  
Claimant argues that he is entitled to a benefit rate of $620.68 per week based 
on a 40-hour workweek paid at $25.00 per hour. Defendants contest the benefit rate 
proposed by claimant and refer to the actual earnings paid as evidenced by Claimant's 
Exhibit 5.   Claimant testified that while he ordinarily worked 40 hours per week, this 
was impacted by weather issues. In the twelve weeks preceding the injury, claimant’s 
wages varied a great deal and therefore are the best evidence of claimant’s customary 
wages. The unrebutted evidence shows claimant was paid an average of $611.98 per 
week. This figure is adopted as the gross weekly wage. At all times material hereto, 
claimant was single and entitled to one exemption. Based on the foregoing, the weekly 
benefit rate is $393.70.

Claimant seeks reimbursement for medical bills detailed in Claimant’s Exhibit 6.  
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance, and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Iowa Code section 85.27.  Holbert 
v. Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening October 1975). 
Defendant disputes that it is responsible for medical bills as no communication 
was received about the outstanding bills. A notice defense is an affirmative one that 
would need to be specifically pled and proven by defendant. See Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). No such defense was pled. Further, part of defendant’s responsibility as an 
employer is to pay or reimburse an injured worker for reasonable medical expenses.  
Defendant accepted and stipulated claimant sustained a work injury from a fall on 
August 13, 2020. The medical bills contained in Claimant’s Exhibit 6 correlate with the 
care he received for that injury. Those medical bills are awarded.  
Defendant argues that claimant is not entitled to additional benefits after October 
4, 2020, as claimant was deemed fit to work with restrictions. It appears defendant is 
arguing claimant rejected light-duty work. Claimant was released to return to work on 
October 6, 2020, with restrictions of no lifting greater than 25 pounds. Defendant 
testified that they were prepared to accommodate those restrictions. Claimant argues 
he was effectively terminated because he was not provided transportation. There was 
nothing in an employment agreement between claimant and defendant that required 
defendant to provide transportation. Claimant provided no case law to support this 
argument. Defendant's refusal to provide transportation is not found to be a termination 
of claimant’s employment. Claimant’s driver’s license was not valid, but it also appears 
he made no efforts to find transportation. .  
No additional temporary benefits are owed after October 16, 2020, the date on 
which defendant and claimant discussed claimant’s return to work with 
accommodations. IAC 85.33 
Permanent benefits begin when the claimant is returned to work or medically 
capable of returning to substantially similar work.  
When an injured worker has been unable to work during a period of recuperation 
from an injury that did not produce permanent disability, the worker is entitled to 
temporary total disability benefits during the time the worker is disabled by the injury.  
Iowa Code § 85.33(1) (2015).  Those benefits are payable until the employee has 
returned to work or is medically capable of returning to work substantially similar to the 
work performed at the time of injury.  Id.

Claimant was released to return to work on November 16, 2020, without 
restrictions. Therefore, the date of commencement of PPD benefits is November 16, 
2020.  
Claimant argues that he is entitled to an award of 30 percent or more of 
permanent partial disability benefits. Defendant argues that the PPD is limited. Claimant 
has a high school education with a work history of performing mostly heavy-duty 
physical manual labor. He is currently working in construction, but testified he leaves 
much of the heavier work to his partner. Defendant testified that he has seen claimant 
lift motorcycle saddle bags and that claimant’s current work includes climbing and 
working on scaffolding and engaging in general construction work. Dr. Bansal assigned 
a total of 12 percent whole person impairment due to the short-term memory loss, left 
shoulder injury and impairment related to the L4 transverse process fracture. Claimant 
was released to work without restrictions and has returned to the same work as he did 
prior to the work injury. He is motivated to return to work. Together, these factors 
support a finding that claimant has sustained a 15 percent permanent partial disability.  
Claimant seeks reimbursement of Dr. Bansal’s IME. The impediment to this is 
that defendant did not retain a physician and obtain its own IME. Iowa Code Section 
85.39 requires a triggering IME from a physician retained by defendant. There was no 
such triggering IME in this case. However, an expert witness fee can be recovered 
through the assessment of costs.

The bill of Dr. Bansal is a summary billing without an itemization for the time 
spent preparing the report versus the time spent examining claimant and reviewing the 
records. As such, an award cannot be given for Dr. Bansal’s fee under 4.33 which 
allows for only the report to be assessed.  
Under the language of Iowa Admin. Code r. 876—4.33(6), claimants can be 
awarded the costs of obtaining two medical reports.  However, the only taxable costs 
are the reports themselves, not the underlying examination needed to draft the reports.  
See Des Moines Area Reg’l Transit Auth. v. Young, 867 N.W.2d 839, 846-847 (Iowa 
2015). 
Claimant has prevailed in this suit and per the discretion afforded by the 
undersigned, the costs are awarded including but not limited to the filing fee and the 
hearing transcript cost. See 876 IAC 4.33.  
 
Defendant employer was not insured at the time of the claimant’s injury.
