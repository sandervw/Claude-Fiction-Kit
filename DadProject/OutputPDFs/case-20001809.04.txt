STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Angel Ocampo-Ramos, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits against Green Star Construction, LLC, employer, and Union 
Insurance Company, insurer.  
This case was heard on September 11, 2024, and considered fully submitted on 
October 23, 2024, upon the simultaneous filing of briefs.  
The record consists of Joint Exhibits 1-16, Claimant’s Exhibits 1-13, Defendants’ 
Exhibits A-J, along with the testimony of Claimant and Angel Ocampo, Jr.  
ISSUES 
1. The nature and extent of industrial disability sustained as a result of the 
February 3, 2020 work injury; 
 
2. Whether Claimant is entitled to healing period benefits from May 27, 2021, 
through October 18, 2021; 
 
3. The proper commencement date for permanent partial disability benefits and 
Defendants’ credit against any award of benefits. 
 
4. Whether there was a subsequent intervening event that is the cause of

STIPULATIONS 
 
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised 
or discussed in this decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.  
 
The parties stipulated that Claimant sustained a work-related injury on or about 
February 3, 2020. They agree that the injury was the cause of temporary disability and 
permanent disability, although the extent of disability for both is in dispute. The 
permanent disability is industrial in nature.  
 
At time of the injury, Claimant’s gross earnings were $1,057.00 per week. He 
was married and entitled to eight exemptions. The weekly benefit rate is $728.63.  
 
Defendants waive all affirmative defenses but for a subsequent intervening 
event.  
 
Claimant seeks taxation of costs, and Defendants agree that the costs itemized 
in Exhibit 12 have been paid by Claimant.  
FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
Claimant, Angel Ocampo-Ramos, was a 38-year-old person at the time of the 
hearing. His educational background consists of completion of the eleventh grade. 
Following high school, he entered the work force. His work history consists primarily of 
construction, carpentry and framing jobs. 
 
Claimant’s medical history is unremarkable with no documented low back 
problems prior to his accepted work injury of February 3, 2020, although he does have a 
prosthetic eye. The prosthetic eye did not impair Claimant’s ability to carry out the 
essential functions of his work.  
 
On April 15, 2019, Claimant began working for defendant-employer as a 
carpenter. His duties included rebuilding and installing windows and doors, leveling 
floors, installing laminate flooring, and painting. These were physically demanding tasks 
that required the ability to lift heavy materials such as doors and windows which 
weighed between 50 pounds and 150 pounds. At the time of his injury, Claimant was 
working 40 hours per week earning $27.00 per hour.  
 
On February 3, 2020, Claimant was helping to unload a trailer when he lifted an 
18 to 20 foot beam weighing approximately 150-200 pounds and felt a pop in his lower 
back. (Hearing Transcript, page 19)  He continued working, but his back pain worsened 
throughout the day. (Id.)  He left the site to purchase a back brace and went home. (Hr.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e). 
 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury actually occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 
employment. Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 150 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. 
Dial Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309, 311 (Iowa 1996). The words “arising out of” refer to the 
cause or source of the injury. The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 
circumstances of the injury. 2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124, 128 (Iowa 
1995).  An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists 
between the injury and the employment. Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. The injury must 
be a rational consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely 
incidental to the employment. Koehler Elec. v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1, 3 (Iowa 2000); 
Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it 
happens within a period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may 
be when performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties 
or doing an activity incidental to them. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d at 150.

Page 13 
 
 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 
621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 
(Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
 
The parties stipulate Claimant sustained an injury to his low back arising out of 
and in the course of his employment.  
 
Since claimant has an impairment to the body as a whole, an industrial disability 
has been sustained.  Industrial disability was defined in Diederich v. Tri-City Ry. Co. of 
Iowa, 219 Iowa 587, 591, 258 N.W. 899, 902 (1935) as follows:  "It is therefore plain that 
the Legislature intended the term 'disability' to mean 'industrial disability' or loss of 
earning capacity and not a mere 'functional disability' to be computed in terms of 
percentages of the total physical and mental ability of a normal man." 
 
Functional impairment is an element to be considered in determining industrial 
disability which is the reduction of earning capacity, but consideration must also be 
given to the injured employee's age, education, qualifications, experience, motivation, 
loss of earnings, severity and situs of the injury, work restrictions, inability to engage in 
employment for which the employee is fitted and the employer's offer of work or failure 
to so offer.  McSpadden v. Big Ben Coal Co., 288 N.W.2d 181, 192 (Iowa 1980); Olson 
v. Goodyear Service Stores, 255 Iowa 1112, 1120, 125 N.W.2d 251, 256 (1963); Barton 
v. Nevada Poultry Co., 253 Iowa 285, 110 N.W.2d 660, 663 (1961). 
 
Compensation for permanent partial disability shall begin when it is medically 
indicated that maximum medical improvement from the injury has been reached and 
that the extent of loss or percentage of permanent impairment can be determined by the 
use of the guides to the evaluation of permanent impairment, published by the American 
Medical Association, as adopted by the Commissioner. Iowa Code section 85.34(2)

The parties dispute the commencement date of PPD benefits.  
 
Iowa Code section 85.34(1) provides that healing period benefits are payable to 
an injured employee who has suffered permanent partial disability until (1) the employee 
has returned to work; (2) it is medically indicated that significant improvement from the 
injury is not anticipated; or (3) the employee is medically capable of returning to 
employment substantially similar to the employment in which the employee was 
engaged at the time of injury, whichever occurs first.  The healing period can be 
considered the period during which there is a reasonable expectation of improvement of 
the disabling condition.  See Armstrong Tire & Rubber Co. v. Kubli, 312 N.W.2d 60, 65 

OCAMPO-RAMOS V. GREEN STAR CONSTRUCTION, LLC 
Page 16 
 
(Iowa Ct. App. 1981).  Healing period benefits can be interrupted or intermittent.  Teel v. 
McCord, 394 N.W.2d 405, 406 (Iowa 1986). 
 
The proper commencement date for permanent partial disability benefits is the 
date set by Dr. Chen. Dr. Chen continued to treat Claimant for pain management even 
after Claimant had been released by Dr. Dery. Dr. Chen’s specialty was in the area of 
pain management, and he provided additional coping techniques and advice to 
encourage Claimant to be more functional. Despite Dr. Dery releasing Claimant, there 
was still a reasonable expectation of improvement of Claimant’s debilitating pain 
condition while treating with Dr. Chen. While Claimant did not improve until several 
years later, Dr. Chen believed Claimant could improve and that fulfills the standard of 
reasonable expectation of improvement. The date of MMI is October 18, 2021.
