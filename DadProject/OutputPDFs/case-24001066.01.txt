On March 15, 2024, this matter came before the Court for hearing. Petitioner Latoyia 
Towns (“Towns”) was represented by attorney Dennis Currell. Respondent Silver Oaks Nursing 
and Rehabilitation Center was represented by attorney Tyler Block. Silver Oaks Nursing and 
Rehabilitation Center utilize CCMSI as their insurance carrier, throughout this opinion they shall 
be known collectively as “Silver Oaks”. After hearing the arguments of Counsel and reviewing 
the court file, including the briefs filed by the parties and the Certified Administrative Record 
(“Cert. Rec.”), the Court enters this Order.  
I. 
BACKGROUND FACTS. 
At issue in this particular case is a right shoulder injury Towns sustained on April 18, 2023, 
while performing her duties at Silver Oaks. As Towns was lifting a patient in the nursing home, 
she felt a pop and felt pain in her right shoulder radiating to her right lateral chest. Cert. Rec. 25. 
She immediately reported the injury to her Director of Nursing. After Towns’ shift ended, she 
reported to her supervisor that she would be seeking treatment. She first sought treatment at Mercy 
Urgent Care, a walk-in clinic. Id. at 24. She was referred to the Mercy Hospital Emergency Room, 
who referred her to physical therapy within the hospital. Id. at 24–26. Before Towns began physical 
therapy, she experienced difficulty getting authorization for treatment due to Silver Oaks’ failure

II. 
STANDARD OF REVIEW. 
 
In administrative proceedings, the Court’s review is governed by Iowa Code Section 
17A.19. The party challenging the agency’s action bears the burden of demonstrating the action's 
invalidity and resulting prejudice. Iowa Code § 17A.19(8)(a). This can be shown several different 
ways, including proof the action was ultra vires; legally erroneous; unsupported by substantial 
evidence in the record when that record is viewed as a whole; or otherwise, unreasonable, arbitrary, 
capricious, or an abuse of discretion. See Iowa Code § 17A.19(10).  
The district court serves in an appellate role to correct any errors of law made by the 
agency. Grundmeyer v. Weyerhaeuser Co., 649 N.W.2d 744, 748 (Iowa 2002). “Accordingly, we 
will not defer to the commissioner's interpretation of section 85.27(4) and will substitute our own 
judgment for that of the commissioner should we conclude the commissioner's interpretation rests 
on an error at law.” Ramirez-Trujillo v. Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 N.W.2d 759, 770 (Iowa 2016) 
(citing Iowa Code § 17A.19(11)(b); Renda v. Iowa Civil Rights Com’n, 784 N.W.2d 8, 14–15.).  
When the issue on appeal is one of fact, the Court is bound by the agency’s factual 
determinations unless, when the Court’s record is viewed as a whole, the findings are not supported 
by substantial evidence. Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(f); see also Mycogen Seeds v. Sands, 686 
N.W.2d 457, 464-65 (Iowa 2004).

“When that record viewed as a whole” means that the adequacy of the evidence in the 
record before the court to support a particular finding of fact, must be judged in light of all 
the relevant evidence in the record cited by any party that detracts from the findings, as 
well as all of the relevant evidence in the record cited by any party that supports it . . .  
 
Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(f)(3). Substantial evidence is defined as evidence of the quality and 
quantity “that would be deemed sufficient by a neutral, detached, and reasonable person, to 
establish the fact at issue when the consequences resulting from the establishment of that fact are 
understood to be serious and of great importance.” Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(f)(1); Mycogen, 686 
N.W.2d at 464. “Under chapter 17A, a court’s task on judicial review is not to determine whether 
the evidence might support a particular factual finding; rather, it is to determine whether the 
evidence supports the finding made.” Burton v. Hilltop Care Ctr., 813 N.W.2d 250, 263–64 (Iowa 
2012). 
“Accordingly, we will not defer to the commissioner's interpretation of section 85.27(4) 
and will substitute our own judgment for that of the commissioner should we conclude the 
commissioner's interpretation rests on an error at law.” Ramirez-Trujillo,878 N.W.2d at 770 (Iowa 
2016) (citing Iowa Code § 17A.19(11)(b); Renda v. Iowa Civil Rights Com’n, 784 N.W.2d 8, 14–
15.).  
The application of the law to the facts is also an enterprise vested in the Commissioner.  
Mycogen, 686 N.W.2d at 465. Accordingly, the Court will reverse only if the Commissioner's 
application was “irrational, illogical, or wholly unjustifiable.” Id.; Iowa Code § 
17A.19(10)(f)(3)(m).  This standard requires the Court to allocate some deference to the 
Commissioner's application of law to the facts, but less than it gives to the agency's findings of 
fact.  Larson Mfg. Co. v. Thorson, 763 N.W.2d 842, 850 (Iowa 2009).

A. Interpretation of Section 85.27(4) 
Towns argues the Commissioner applied the incorrect legal standard when determining if 
Towns had met the notice requirement under Iowa Code Section 85.27(4). The Commissioner’s 
relevant analysis for this issue is as follows: 
I would first note that there is no proof in the record that the claimant expressed a 
dissatisfaction with her care before filing the original notice and petition concerning 
application for alternate care. Iowa Code section 85.27 (4) requires that a claimant express 
their dissatisfaction with care, in writing, prior to the filing of a petition seeking 
alternative medical care. 
 
Cert. Rec. at 8 (emphasis added).  
The relevant portion of Iowa Code Section 85.27(4) is as follows: 
the employer is obliged to furnish reasonable services and supplies to treat an injured 
employee, and has the right to choose the care. If the employer chooses the care, the 
employer shall hold the employee harmless for the cost of care until the employer notifies 
the employee that the employer is no longer authorizing all or any part of the care and the 
reason for the change in authorization. . . . The treatment must be offered promptly and be 
reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the employee. If the 
employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the care offered, the employee should 
communicate the basis of such dissatisfaction to the employer, in writing if requested, 
following which the employer and the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably 
suited to treat the injury. If the employer and employee cannot agree on such alternate care, 
the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable proofs of the necessity therefor, 
allow and order other care. In an emergency, the employee may choose the employee’s 
care at the employer’s expense, provided the employer or the employer’s agent cannot be 
reached immediately.  
 
 
Towns further argues the Commissioner placed an additional requirement on Towns, one 
not imposed by the statute, requiring Towns to notify Silver Oaks in writing of her dissatisfaction 
with her medical care. While Silver Oaks argues that the Commissioner “did not state that the 
failure to provide notice in writing was required,” the Commissioner’s analysis on this issue

concludes “[the lack of writing] alone could be fatal to the claimant’s request for alternate medical 
care.” Cert. Rec. at 8. If the lack of written notice of dissatisfaction could be determinative then 
the Commissioner is stating that written notice is required, in opposition to Silver Oaks’ 
arguments.   
Iowa Code Section 85.27(4) does not require Towns to communicate her dissatisfaction to 
Silver Oaks in writing. The plain language of the statute states “in writing if requested”, the 
Commissioner’s interpretation is an error in law. This Court substitutes the Commissioner’s 
judgment and determines that any and all communications of Towns’ dissatisfaction should be 
considered by the Commissioner in the notice analysis. 
The analysis completed by the Commissioner only addresses the emails between Towns 
and Silver Oaks about the evaluation with Dr. Bollier and wages, never addressing the email sent 
from Towns on July 7, 2023 requesting that Silver Oaks furnish authority for the “plan of care 
commenced by the authorized PCP”. Cert. Rec. at 29.  
The period in which Towns needed to express dissatisfaction with treatment is also at issue. 
There were clearly issues with the initial setup of the claim, authorizing treatment with physical 
therapy, the MRI scheduling, and the referral to the PCP, with a four-month lag between the date 
of injury and the date of Silver Oaks’ requested orthopedic evaluation with Dr. Bollier. The July 
7, 2023 email to Silver Oaks was sent prior to the notice about Dr. Bollier’s evaluation in August. 
Further analysis is needed to determine whether Towns’ dissatisfaction must be expressed after 
Silver Oaks communicated that they wanted Towns to seek treatment with Dr. Bollier or whether 
the July 7, 2023 email serves to notify Silver Oaks that Towns was planning to seek treatment 
under her PCP, who she believed was an authorized provider.

B. Arbitrary and Capricious – Delay in Treatment and Failure to Follow Precedent. 
  
The Court will condense Towns’ two arguments regarding whether the Commissioner’s 
decision was arbitrary and capricious into one issue addressed under this section.  
Towns argues that Silver Oaks improperly interfered in an authorized provider’s 
recommended course of treatment.  
The employer's right to control medical care attaches under the statute when the employer 
acknowledges compensability following notice and furnishes care to the employee, and it 
remains with the employer under the statute until the employer denies the injury is work-
related, withdraws authorization of the care, or until the commissioner orders alternative 
care.  
 
Bell Bros. Heating & Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d 193, 207 (Iowa 2010) (citing Iowa 
Code § 85.27). “As a general rule the employer, not the employee, is permitted to choose the 
medical care to be furnished.” Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 433, 436 (Iowa 
1997) (citing Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122, 123 (Iowa 1995)).  
However, we think [the right to select medical care] is a qualified right because the 
treatment must be (1) prompt, (2) reasonably suited to treat the injury, and (3) without 
undue inconvenience to the claimant. . . . [I]f the treatment the employer offers fails to 
meet any one of these qualifications, the commissioner has the authority to order alternate 
care, including care from a doctor chosen by the claimant. 
 
W. Side Transp. v. Cordell, 601 N.W.2d 691, 693 (Iowa 1999). The three reasons listed constitute 
abandonment of care. The Commissioner held that Silver Oaks did not abandon care since they 
“eventually provided authorization for some care,” namely the physical therapy appointments. 
Cert. Rec. 8. Towns’ argument rests on the presumption that Sharma was an authorized provider,
