STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Steven Weirick, claimant, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from Johnson Controls International, PLC, employer and Old 
Republic Insurance Company, insurance carrier, as defendants.  Hearing was held via 
Zoom videoconference on December 11, 2023.     
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision.  In the hearing report the defendants disputed that claimant’s disability is 
properly classified as an industrial disability.  (Hearing Report, numbered paragraph 5) 
However, in their post-hearing brief defendants no longer dispute claimant’s entitlement 
to industrial disability; rather, defendants dispute claimant is entitled to industrial 
disability in excess of the 10 percent body to the whole already conceded and paid by 
the defendants.  (Defendants’ Post-Hearing Brief, p. 1) Additionally, the hearing report 
states there is a dispute regarding claimant’s gross earnings at the time of the injury.  
(Hearing Report, numbered paragraph 6.)  In claimant’s post-hearing brief he stipulated 
to the defendants’ gross earnings.  (Claimant’s Post-Hearing Brief, p. 2) Thus, the 
claimant’s weekly workers’ compensation rate is no longer in dispute.     
Claimant, Steven Weirick, was the only witness to testify live at trial.  The 
evidentiary record also includes Joint Exhibits 1-5, Claimant’s Exhibits 1-7, and

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony.  The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 

WEIRICK V. JOHNSON CONTROLS INTERNATIONAL, PLC 
Page 8 
 
also relevant and material to the causation question.  The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances.  The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); 
Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995).  Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994).  Unrebutted expert medical 
testimony cannot be summarily rejected.  Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 
N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 1994).   
Since claimant has an impairment to the body as a whole, an industrial disability 
has been sustained.  Industrial disability was defined in Diederich v. Tri-City Ry. Co. of 
Iowa, 219 Iowa 587, 258 N.W. 899 (1935) as follows: "It is therefore plain that the 
Legislature intended the term 'disability' to mean 'industrial disability' or loss of earning 
capacity and not a mere 'functional disability' to be computed in terms of percentages of 
the total physical and mental ability of a normal man." 
Functional impairment is an element to be considered in determining industrial 
disability which is the reduction of earning capacity, but consideration must also be 
given to the injured employee's age, education, qualifications, experience, motivation, 
loss of earnings, severity and situs of the injury, work restrictions, inability to engage in 
employment for which the employee is fitted and the employer's offer of work or failure 
to so offer. McSpadden v. Big Ben Coal Co., 288 N.W.2d 181 (Iowa 1980); Olson v. 
Goodyear Service Stores, 255 Iowa 1112, 125 N.W.2d 251 (1963); Barton v. Nevada 
Poultry Co., 253 Iowa 285, 110 N.W.2d 660 (1961). 
Compensation shall be paid in relation to 500 weeks as the disability bears to the 
body as a whole. Section 85.34. 
As revised in 2017, the statute also requires consideration of the length of time 
before claimant retires as a factor in assessing industrial disability.  At the time of the 
injury, Weirick was an older worker with a less extensive work life remaining.  
Considering all of the relevant factors of industrial disability set forth in the statute and 
outlined by the Iowa Supreme Court, I found that Weirick proved he sustained a 20 
percent loss of future earning capacity. This is equivalent to a 20 percent industrial 
disability and entitles claimant to an award of 100 weeks of permanent partial disability 
benefits.  Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v).  The parties stipulated that any permanency 
benefits should commence on August 8, 2020.  Accordingly, I conclude permanent 
disability benefits commence on August 8, 2020 and are due weekly thereafter until the 
100 weeks are paid in full.  Iowa Code section 86.34(2)(v).

Iowa Code section 10A.315(4) provides: 
a. If a denial, a delay in payment, or a termination of benefits occurs 
without reasonable or probable cause or excuse known to the 
employer or insurance carrier at the time of the denial, delay in 
payment, or termination of benefits, the workers’ compensation 
commissioner shall award benefits in addition to those benefits

terminated without reasonable or probable cause or excuse.   
b. The workers’ compensation commissioner shall award benefits under 
this subsection if the commissioner finds both of the following facts:  
(1) The employee has demonstrated a denial, delay in payment, or 
termination in benefits.  
(2) The employer has failed to prove a reasonable or probable cause 
or excuse for the denial, delay in payment, or termination of benefits. 
c. In order to be considered a reasonable or probable cause or excuse 
under paragraph “b”, an excuse shall satisfy all of the following criteria: 
(1) The excuse was preceded by a reasonable investigation an 
evaluation by the employer or insurance carrier into whether benefits 
were owed to the employee. 
(2) The results of the reasonable investigation and evaluation were the 
actual basis upon which the employer or insurance carrier 
contemporarily relied to deny, delay payment of, or terminate benefits. 
(3) The employer or insurance carrier contemporaneously conveyed 
the basis for the denial, delay in payment , or termination of benefits to 
the employee at the time of the denial, delay , or termination of

Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d 229, 235 (Iowa 1996). The supreme court has stated, for

When an employee’s claim for benefits is fairly debatable based on a good faith 
dispute over the employee’s factual or legal entitlement to benefits, an award of penalty 
benefits is not appropriate under the statute. Whether the issue was fairly debatable

employer, would have supported the employer's denial of compensability. Gilbert v. USF 
Holland, Inc., 637 N.W.2d 194 (Iowa 2001).

Weirick is seeking penalty benefits for defendants’ unreasonably delaying 
payment of the PPD benefits.  Based on the above findings of fact, I conclude claimant 
has demonstrated by a preponderance of the evidence that $14,411.05 of PPD benefits 
were delayed.  There was a delay from the time claimant reached MMI until the time 
defendants requested an impairment rating.  There was also a delay from when the 
impairment rating was rendered until when the defendants issued the PPD check.   
Once claimant demonstrates there was a delay in payment of the PPD benefits 
the burden shifts to the defendants to show there was a reasonable or probable cause 
or excuse for the delay in payment.  Defendants offer no explanation or excuse for why 
either of these delays occurred.  Therefore, I conclude defendants have failed to 
demonstrate that there was an explanation or excuse for the delay.  Thus, I conclude 
defendants unreasonably delayed PPD benefits in the amount of $14,411.05. 
Claimant has proven a delay in payment of $14,411.05 of benefits prior to the 
hearing.  Defendants have not offered any evidence to establish that a timely 
investigation into the amount of permanency was conducted, that notice of the basis for 
delay was given to claimant, or that the basis for the delay was reasonable.  The 
undisputed evidence in this case demonstrates that the delay of benefits by defendants 
is unreasonable.   
Considering the purposes of the penalty benefits statute, the length of the delay, 
and lack of evidence of prior penalty awards against these defendants, I conclude that a 
substantial penalty is not necessary for both punitive purposes as well as to deter this 
and other defendants from similar conduct in the future.  Iowa Code section 10A.315(4) 
permits a penalty up to 50 percent of the benefits unreasonably delayed or denied.  In 
this instance, the defendants have unreasonably delayed PPD benefits in the amount of 
$14,411.05 prior to the hearing.  I find that a penalty totaling $5,000.00 is appropriate 
under these circumstances. 
Next, claimant is seeking reimbursement for Dr. Bansal’s IME in the amount of 
$2,859.00 (Cl. Ex. 6, p. 46) 
Section 85.39 permits an employee to be reimbursed for subsequent 
examination by a physician of the employee's choice where an employer-retained 
physician has previously evaluated “permanent disability” and the employee believes 
that the initial evaluation is too low.  The section also permits reimbursement for 
reasonably necessary transportation expenses incurred and for any wage loss 
occasioned by the employee attending the subsequent examination. 
Based on the above findings of fact, I conclude that the prerequisites of section 
85.39 were met.  On the hearing report defendants dispute claimant’s entitlement to 
reimbursement for the IME.  However, in their post-hearing brief defendants do not 
argue that claimant is not entitled to reimbursement.  Because the prerequisites of 
section 85.39 were met, I conclude claimant has demonstrated entitlement to 
reimbursement for the full amount of the IME.  Thus, defendants shall reimburse
