STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Kevin Buttrey, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from the City of Des Moines, self-insured defendant.  This matter 
was heard on April 8, 2025, with a final submission date of May 20, 2025. 
The record in this case consists of Claimant’s Exhibits 1-3, Defendant’s Exhibits 
A-F, and the testimony of claimant. 
STIPULATIONS 
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised 
or discussed in this decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.  
ISSUES 
1. The extent of claimant’s entitlement to permanent partial disability benefits. 
 
2. Costs. 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
Claimant was 63 years old at the time of hearing.  Claimant graduated from high 
school.  Claimant began working for the City of Des Moines in November 1994.  
(Hearing Transcript, page 8)  The first 10 years at Des Moines claimant worked in 
garbage pick-up.  He then did 3 years on an asphalt crew.  The last 10 years of his 
employment with Des Moines he worked as a street sweeper.  (Defendant’s Exhibit B, 
page 2; Ex. A, p. 1)

Claimant testified that as a street sweeper operator he was exposed to noise 
from the garbage trucks, street sweepers, rollers, loaders and dump trucks.  He said the 
City provided optional hearing protection for him.  He said he had difficulty using the 
hearing protection when at work. Earmuffs caused him to sweat, and his ear plugs 
would just fall out.  (Tr., p. 9) 
Claimant said that he first noticed having a hearing loss in 2018 or 2019.  He said 
he began noticing his loss in restaurants and in places where there was a lot of 
background noise.  (Tr., pp. 9-10) 
On September 30, 2022, claimant retired from his job with the City of Des 
Moines.  (Tr., pp. 8-9) 
On May 28, 2024, claimant was evaluated by Timothy Simplot, M.D., with Iowa 
ENT Center for evaluation of possible work-related hearing loss issues.  Claimant 
indicated exposure to loud noises while working with the City of Des Moines.  He 
noticed progressive hearing loss over time.  Claimant also had complaints of tinnitus.  
(Ex. B, p. 2)  Claimant was assessed as having bilateral sensorineural hearing loss and 
subjective tinnitus bilaterally.  (Ex. B, p. 4) 
In a June 7, 2024, letter, Dr. Simplot gave his opinions of claimant’s hearing loss 
following evaluation.  Dr. Simplot had a 2014 hearing test for claimant that showed 
signs of mid and high frequency hearing loss.  Between 2014 and 2022 claimant had 
some progression of lower frequency hearing loss with high frequency hearing loss 
showing stability.  Dr. Simplot opined that claimant had at least a portion of his hearing 
loss related to work with the City of Des Moines.  He also opined that at least part of his 
tinnitus complaints were related to his work for the City of Des Moines.  (Ex. C, p. 8) 
Based on the AMA Guides to the Evaluation of Permanent Impairment, Fifth 
Edition, Dr. Simplot found that claimant had a 3 percent permanent impairment 
regarding his tinnitus.  He also recommended claimant have a trial of hearing aid 
amplification.  (Ex. C, p. 9) 
Claimant testified that in August 2024, the City gave him hearing aids for his 
hearing loss.  He said this helped with his hearing.  (Tr., pp. 11-12) 
In a January 9, 2025 report, Richard Tyler, Ph.D., gave his opinions of claimant’s 
hearing loss following a document review and interview with claimant by phone.  Dr. 
Tyler did not examine claimant in person.  He did not conduct any physical hearing tests 
on claimant.  (Claimant’s Exhibit 1, page 1; Tr., p. 20)  
Dr. Tyler opined claimant’s hearing loss was mostly in higher frequencies. He 
opined that based on claimant’s hearing loss at 500 hertz (Hz), 1,000 Hz, 2,000 Hz, and 
3,000 Hz, claimant had a 20 percent hearing loss under Iowa Code section 85B.9A.  
(Ex. 1, p. 4)

He also opined claimant’s hearing impairment at 4 kHz, 6 kHz, and 8 kHz was 59 
percent.  (Ex. 1, p 11) 
Dr. Tyler opined that claimant had tinnitus.  (Ex. 1, p 9)  He also appeared to 
indicate that based on the Guides, claimant had a 25 percent permanent impairment 
regarding his tinnitus.  (Ex. 1, p. 11) 
In a January 27, 2025 report, Dr. Simplot indicated he had reviewed Dr. Tyler’s 
report regarding claimant’s hearing loss and tinnitus.  Dr. Simplot noted that he used the 
Tables for assessing hearing loss, as detailed in Rule 876 IAC 8.11(3) as specified 
under Iowa Code section 85B.  Based on the tables found at 876 IAC 8.11(3), claimant 
had a 9.9 percent binaural hearing loss.  Dr. Simplot had no explanation for Dr. Tyler’s 
opinion that claimant had a 20 percent binaural hearing loss.  (Ex. F, pp. 15-16) 
Dr. Simplot noted that, under the Guides, claimant can be rated up to an 
additional 5 percent hearing loss based on tinnitus.  Based on claimant’s symptoms, Dr. 
Simplot believed claimant had a 3 percent impairment for his tinnitus under the Guides.  
(Ex. F, p. 16)  Dr. Simplot indicated it appeared Dr. Tyler used his own set of ratings for 
determining permanent impairment for tinnitus.  (Ex. F, pp. 16-17) 
In a February 24, 2025 letter, Dr. Tyler indicated he had reviewed Dr. Simplot’s 
January 27, 2025 report.  He also indicated he spoke again to claimant on the phone.  
Dr. Tyler indicated he did not believe the Guides were an adequate method for 
assessing claimant’s hearing loss and noted, “It is inappropriate that the guidelines 
ignore the importance of high-frequency hearing.”  (Ex. 2, p. 18) 
He suggested that the physicians who wrote the Guides were possibly biased 
and that the authors of the Guides did not fully appreciate the consequences of hearing 
loss and tinnitus.  (Ex. 2, pp. 18, 23) 
Claimant testified that he has constant ringing in both ears due to tinnitus.  (Tr., 
p. 12)   
Claimant said that he when he retired from Des Moines, he anticipated to return 
to the work force.  He said that he had discussed returning to work with four different 
employers.  (Tr., p. 13)  Claimant testified he plans to return to work part-time, but not 
full-time.  (Tr., pp. 16-18) 
CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The first issue to be determined is the extent of claimant’s entitlement to 
permanent partial disability benefits. 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e). 
Since claimant has an impairment to the body as a whole, an industrial disability 
has been sustained.  Industrial disability was defined in Diederich v. Tri-City Ry. Co. of

Iowa, 219 Iowa 587, 591, 258 N.W. 899, 902 (1935) as follows:  "It is therefore plain that 
the Legislature intended the term 'disability' to mean 'industrial disability' or loss of 
earning capacity and not a mere 'functional disability' to be computed in terms of 
percentages of the total physical and mental ability of a normal man." 
Functional impairment is an element to be considered in determining industrial 
disability which is the reduction of earning capacity, but consideration must also be 
given to the injured employee's age, education, qualifications, experience, motivation, 
loss of earnings, severity and situs of the injury, work restrictions, inability to engage in 
employment for which the employee is fitted and the employer's offer of work or failure 
to so offer.  McSpadden v. Big Ben Coal Co., 288 N.W.2d 181, 192 (Iowa 1980); Olson 
v. Goodyear Service Stores, 255 Iowa 1112, 1120, 125 N.W.2d 251, 256 (1963); Barton 
v. Nevada Poultry Co., 253 Iowa 285, 110 N.W.2d 660, 663 (1961). 
Compensation for permanent partial disability shall begin when it is medically 
indicated that maximum medical improvement from the injury has been reached and 
that the extent of loss or percentage of permanent impairment can be determined by the 
use of the guides to the evaluation of permanent impairment, published by the American 
Medical Association, as adopted by the Commissioner. Iowa Code section 85.34(2)   
Occupational hearing loss alone is compensated as a percentage of 175 weeks 
by use of the formula set forth in Iowa Code sections 85B.6 and 85B.9.  Tinnitus is 
compensated industrially as an injury to the body as a whole.  Ehteshamfer v. UTA 
Engineered Systems Div., 555 N.W.2d 450 (Iowa 1996)  A claim for hearing loss and 
tinnitus from the same long-term noise exposure has the same injury date and is one 
loss and one disability.  Meier v. John Deere Dubuque Works, File No. 5002128 (App. 
July 22, 2004) 
Dr. Simplot found that claimant had a 9.9 percent binaural hearing loss by using 
testing data and by applying Iowa Code section 85B and the tables found in Rule 876 
IAC 8.11(3) and the worksheet found in Rule 876 IAC 8.11(5). (Ex. A; Ex. F, pp. 15-16)  
Dr. Tyler opined that claimant had a 20 percent rating for hearing loss.  (Ex. 1, p. 4)  It is 
unclear what audiometrics Dr. Tyler used as they are not detailed in his report.   
Dr. Tyler did not examine the claimant personally.  He appears to use his own 
data to determine the extent of claimant’s hearing loss.  Given the above facts, it is 
found that Dr. Simplot’s rating for claimant’s binaural hearing loss is more convincing 
than that of Dr. Tyler.  It is found that claimant has a 9.9 percent age-related binaural 
hearing loss.   
Dr. Simplot found that claimant had a 3 percent permanent impairment to the 
body as a whole for his tinnitus.  Dr. Simplot used the AMA Guides, Fifth Edition, to 
reach that value.  (Ex. F, p. 16) 
In his report, Dr. Tyler seems to opine that claimant has a 25 percent permanent 
impairment to the body as a whole for his tinnitus.  (Ex. 1, p. 9)  Further in his report, Dr. 
Tyler opines that claimant has a 62 percent permanent impairment due to his tinnitus.

(Ex. 1, p. 11)  Dr. Tyler indicates in his report that he uses his own tinnitus rating system 
“. . . based on my personal judgment of the worst instance of tinnitus I have seen. . .”  
(Ex. 1, pp. 6-9) 
Dr. Tyler’s rating for claimant’s tinnitus appears to offer two different ratings.  His 
ratings for tinnitus are ambiguous and unclear.  As detailed above, Dr. Tyler did not do 
any testing on claimant.  He spoke with claimant over the phone.  Dr. Tyler uses his 
own scale to evaluate tinnitus.  This scale is not presented in the Guides.  Given these 
facts, Dr. Simplot’s rating for claimant’s tinnitus is found to be more convincing than Dr. 
Tyler’s.  Claimant has a 3 percent permanent impairment to the body as a whole for his 
tinnitus. 
At the time of hearing claimant was 63 years old.  He graduated from high 
school.  Both parties agree claimant’s hearing loss and tinnitus arose out of and in the 
course of his employment with the City of Des Moines.  Claimant has been found to 
have a 9.9 percent binaural hearing loss and a 3 percent permanent impairment for 
tinnitus.  Claimant was 61 years old when he voluntarily retired from Des Moines.  He 
testified he has been looking for part-time work with a number of employers.  Claimant 
credibly testified he was not able to find work immediately due to issues with hearing 
loss.  He said he has not been able to seek employment until after he received his 
hearing aids in 2024.  (Tr., pp. 13-15)  The hearing loss that claimant has is a 
permanent disability.  Given all relevant factors, it is found that claimant has a 15 
percent industrial disability. 
The last issue to be determined is costs. 
 “All costs incurred in the hearing before the commissioner shall be taxed in the 
discretion of the commissioner.”  Iowa Code § 10A.328(2023).  Iowa Administrative 
Rule 876—4.33(86) outlines what costs may be assessed at hearing. It states as 
follows:  
Costs taxed by the workers’ compensation commissioner or a deputy 
commissioner shall be (1) attendance of a certified shorthand reporter or 
presence of mechanical means at hearings and evidential depositions, (2) 
transcription costs when appropriate, (3) costs of service of the original 
notice and subpoenas, (4) witness fees and expenses as provided by Iowa 
Code sections 622.69 and 622.72, (5) the costs of doctors’ and 
practitioners’ deposition testimony, provided that said costs do not exceed 
the amounts provided by Iowa Code sections 622.69 and 622.72, (6) the 
reasonable costs of obtaining no more than two doctors’ or practitioners’ 
reports, (7) filing fees when appropriate, including convenience fees 
incurred by using the WCES payment gateway, and (8) costs of persons 
reviewing health service disputes. Costs of service of notice and subpoenas 
shall be paid initially to the serving person or agency by the party utilizing 
the service. Expenses and fees of witnesses or of obtaining doctors’ or 
practitioners’ reports initially shall be paid to the witnesses, doctors or 
practitioners by the party on whose behalf the witness is called or by whom

ORDER 
THEREFORE IT IS ORDERED: 
That defendant shall pay claimant 75 weeks of permanent partial disability 
benefits at the rate of eight hundred seventy-nine and 95/100 dollars ($879.95) per 
week commencing on September 30, 2022. 
 That defendant shall pay accrued weekly benefits in a lump sum together with 
interest at an annual rate equal to the one-year treasury constant maturity published by 
the federal reserve in the most recent H15 report settled as of the date of injury, plus 
two percent.  
That defendant shall be given credit for benefits previously paid.   
That defendant shall pay costs. 
That defendant shall file subsequent reports of injury as required under Iowa 
Code section 876 IAC 3.1(2).
