B. 
Proceedings before the agency. 
 
Petitioner filed his claim for workers’ compensation benefits on May 8, 2023.  He 
claimed workplace injuries to both knees.  He claimed that the date of the injury was May 12, 
2021.  (Petition). 
 
Mercy offered opinions from Drs. Hart and Whited as part of the evidence in the case.  
Both opinion letters were letters drafted by Mercy’s counsel and were signed by the respective 
doctors.  Dr. Hart’s letter stated that Dr. Hart had talked to counsel prior to him writing the letter.  
Dr. Hart reported that he had reviewed several medical records including, but not limited to, a 
hospital emergency room record of June 8, 2021, and a report from Dr. Cearlock on June 21, 
2021.  Dr. Hart stated that he could not state to a reasonable degree of medical certainty that 
petitioner’s alleged work injury on May 12 or his job duties were a significant and/or substantial 
contributing factor to the right knee MRI finding or his recommendation for right knee surgery.  
(Mercy Exhibit A). 
 
Dr. Whited stated that petitioner had reported to him that he had aggravated his left knee 
in early 2021 when he fell while walking on ice.  He further stated that petitioner never reported 
a work injury to him.  Dr. Whited repeated his opinion that bilateral knee replacements were 
justified based on his prior diagnosis of severe bilateral knee osteoarthritis, complete medial joint 
space loss, and bone-on-bone contact.  Dr. Whited opined that neither petitioner’s work duties 
with Mercy nor his alleged work injury on May 12, 2021 were a significant or substantial 
contributing factor in the pain, complaints, symptoms, or need for surgery in either knee.  (Mercy 
Exhibit B).

STANDARD OF REVIEW 
Chapter 17A of the Iowa Code governs judicial review of administrative agency action. 
The district court acts in an appellate capacity to correct errors of law on the part of the agency. 
Meyer v. IBP, Inc., 710 N.W.2d 213, 219 (Iowa 2006).  The court “may grant relief if the agency 
action has prejudiced the substantial rights of the petitioner, and the agency action meets one of 
the enumerated criteria contained in section 17A.19(10)(a) through (n).” Burton v. Hilltop Care 
Cntr., 813 N.W.2d 250, 256 (Iowa 2012) (quoting Evercom Sys., Inc. v. Iowa Utilities Bd., 805 
N.W.2d 758, 762 (Iowa 2011)). Where an agency has been “clearly vested” with a fact-finding 
function, the appropriate “standard of review [on appeal] depends on the aspect of the agency's 
decision that forms the basis of the petition for judicial review”—that is, whether it involves an 
issue of 1) findings of fact, 2) interpretation of law, or 3) application of law to fact. Burton, 813 
N.W.2d at 256.  
The courts use a substantial evidence standard when considering challenges to findings of 
fact in agency decisions.  A reviewing court can only disturb factual findings if they are “not 
supported by substantial evidence in the record before the court when that record is reviewed as a 
whole.”  Burton, 813 N.W.2d at 256 (quoting Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(f)).  The Iowa Supreme 
Court has outlined the court’s guidelines when reviewing substantial evidence claims under the

17A.19 standard as follows: 
When reviewing a finding of fact for substantial evidence, we judge the finding in 
light of all the relevant evidence in the record cited by any party that detracts from 
that finding as well as all of the relevant evidence in the record cited by any party 
that supports it.  Our review of the record is fairly intensive, and we do not simply 
rubber stamp the agency finding of fact. 
 
Evidence is not insubstantial merely because different conclusions may be drawn 
from the evidence. To that end, evidence may be substantial even though we may 
have drawn a different conclusion as fact finder. Our task, therefore, is not to 
determine whether the evidence supports a different finding; rather, our task is to 
determine whether substantial evidence, viewing the record as a whole, supports 
the findings actually made. 
 
Cedar Rapids Cmty. Sch. Dist. v. Pease, 807 N.W.2d 839, 845 (Iowa 2011) (internal citations and 
quotation marks omitted). 
CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
A workers’ compensation claimant must show that an injury occurred in the course of 
and arose out of employment to be compensable.  Miedema v. Dial Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309, 
310–11 (Iowa 1996) (citing Iowa Code § 85.3(1).  The first part of the test, whether the injury 
occurred “in the course of” employment, refers to the time, place, and circumstances of the 
injury.  See Lakeside Casino v. Blue, 743 N.W.2d 169, 174 (Iowa 2007).  The second part of the 
test is whether the injury arose out of claimant’s employment.  This requires a distinct and 
independent inquiry.  Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311.  Injuries that occur in the course of 
employment or on the employer's premises do not necessarily arise out of that employment.  Id.  
Rather, the employee must show that the nature of the employment exposes the employee to the 
risk of such an injury.  Lakeside, 743 N.W.2d at 174. 
 
Expert opinions are particularly important to decide these questions.  The Iowa Supreme 
Court has discussed the standard in which the court review the workers’ compensation 
commission’s evaluation of experts as follows:

Medical causation is essentially within the domain of expert testimony.  The 
commissioner, as trier of fact, has a duty to weigh the evidence and measure the 
credibility of witnesses.  The weight given to expert testimony depends on the 
accuracy of the facts relied upon by the expert and other surrounding 
circumstances.  Also, an expert's opinion is not necessarily binding upon the 
commissioner if the opinion is based on an incomplete history.  Ultimately, 
however, the determination of whether to accept or reject an expert opinion is 
within the peculiar province of the commissioner. 
 
Cedar Rapids Cmty. Sch. Dist. v. Pease, 807 N.W.2d 839, 845 (Iowa 2011) (quotes, internal 
quotes, and emphasis omitted). 
 
Petitioner’s argument is directed at the completeness of the history known by each expert.  
Dr. Bansal collected a more complete medical history than the other two doctors.  Petitioner 
argued that Dr. Bansal gave the only credible opinion as to causation because the other two 
doctors only reviewed partial medical histories.  Petitioner also pointed to incorrect or 
incomplete information in Dr. Whited’s opinion regarding the timing of petitioner slipping on ice 
and whether petitioner told him that the injuries arose from the workplace.  Petitioner further 
argued that the two treating physicians only provided opinions as framed by defense counsel as 
opposed to preparing a report on their own. 
 
There is no question that an expert’s opinion can be impacted by the completeness of the 
information reviewed.  With that said, the deputy reasonably weighed the credibility of the 
respective experts in this case.  Mercy did not hire Drs. Hart or Whited.  Rather, petitioner chose 
to see them following a referral from his personal physician.  Both personally evaluated and 
treated him at the time he was reporting knee pain.  They may not have reviewed the medical 
history to the extent of Dr. Bansal, but they personally examined petitioner, ordered x-rays and 
other imaging, and discussed options with him thereafter.  Their opinions are consistent with 
each other.  In fact, Dr. Bansal agreed with Dr. Whited’s diagnosis of osteoarthritis (at least as to 
the left knee).  The deputy also fairly considered inconsistent reporting from petitioner.  The

decisions of the deputy and the commissioner to accept the opinions of the two treating doctors 
over that of the IME doctor is within their province.  Their decisions are supported by substantial

At hearing, petitioner cited to a decision from one of my esteemed colleagues2 in 
Culpepper v. CNH Industrial America, LLC et al., Polk County No. CVCV066801.  The Iowa 
Court of Appeals issued a decision affirming Judge McLellan’s decision just yesterday.  See 
Culpepper, No. 24-2040 (Iowa App. October 29, 2025).  However, Culpepper is distinguishable 
from this case.  In Culpepper, the deputy had cited to two statements from a treating 
neurosurgeon in support of a finding that the injury was not work-related.  However, the deputy 
did not discuss a third statement from the same doctor in which the doctor opined to within a 
reasonable degree of medical certainty that the claimant’s work activities resulted in an 
aggravation to his spine necessitating his need for surgery.  The court of appeals affirmed the 
district court’s finding that the agency decision was not supported by substantial evidence 
because the deputy had relied on the treating doctor’s statements, but ignored the doctor’s 
statement as to the key issue in the case. 
 
The same is not true here.  Drs. Hart and Whited have never opined that petitioner’s knee 
conditions were caused by his employment with Mercy.  They have only offered opinions that 
they could not find to a degree of medical certainty that petitioner’s employment at Mercy 
caused his knee pain or conditions.  Accordingly, Culpepper is not analogous to this case. 
RULING 
 
The agency decision is affirmed.  Petitioner shall pay the costs of this action.
