STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Jose Guerra, filed petitions in arbitration for workers’ compensation 
benefits against Clean Air Enviro Services, as employer, and Ace American Insurance 
Company, as insurance carrier.  These cases came before the undersigned for an 
arbitration hearing on January 18, 2024, via Zoom.   
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the hearing.  On the 
hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  Those stipulations were 
accepted and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be made 
or discussed.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.  
The evidentiary record includes Joint Exhibits 1 through 13, Claimant’s Exhibits 1 
through 8, and Defendants’ Exhibits A through G.  Claimant testified on his own behalf.  
The evidentiary record closed on February 12, 2024.  All parties filed their post-hearing 
briefs on February 16, 2024, at which time the case was deemed fully submitted to the 
undersigned.  
On July 18, 2024, the agency filed an arbitration decision concluding that Mr. 
Guerra carried his burden of proving he was owed temporary partial disability benefits 
based on lost overtime hours.  In the decision, the undersigned requested Mr. Guerra 
submit a detailed explanation with citations to the record as to the extent of temporary 
partial disability owed between April 22, 2022, and June 22, 2023.  Mr. Guerra 
submitted his supplemental brief on July 29, 2024.  Defendants submitted their

ISSUES 
The parties submitted the following disputed issues for resolution: 
1. The extent of claimant’s entitlement to temporary disability benefits; 
2. The extent of claimant’s entitlement to permanent disability benefits; 
3. Whether claimant is entitled to an award of medical expenses; 
4. Whether claimant is entitled to alternate medical care under Iowa Code 
section 85.27;  
5. Penalty benefits; and 
6. Whether costs should be assessed against either party. 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned, having considered all of the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
Jose Guerra was 52 years of age on the date of the evidentiary hearing.  Guerra 
was born in Mexico where he attended a technical school and obtained a machinist 
certificate. (See Hearing Transcript, pages 38-39)  He immigrated to the United States 
in 2001. (Hearing Transcript, page 39)  An interpreter was present at hearing; however, 
Guerra has a good understanding of the English language.  Guerra has attended 
English as a Second Language (ESL) courses in California and Iowa.  In California, 
Guerra attended three ESL classes per week for three years. (Hr. Tr., pp. 39-40)  In 
Iowa, he attended three ESL classes per week for approximately two years. (Hr. Tr., p. 
40)  Guerra described his ability to speak the English language as “80 percent.”  He 
described his ability to understand the English language as “85 percent.” (Id.) 
Guerra’s employment history is set forth in Claimant’s Exhibit 2.  The vast 
majority of his employment history has involved manual labor.  Prior to working for the 
defendant employer, Guerra worked as a general laborer for Quality Manufacturing from 
2008 to 2017. (Claimant’s Exhibit 2, page 28)  Guerra worked as a Press Operator, 
Roller Operator, and Robotic Welder Operator. (Id.)  During this time, Guerra made 
additional income by selling various items through the online retailer eBay. (Id.)  Guerra 
continued to sell items on eBay between 2017 and 2021.  He also worked for his friend 
installing carpet and vinyl planks. (Hr. Tr., pp. 44-45) 
Guerra began his employment with Clean Air in October 2021. (Hr. Tr., p. 46)  
Initially, Guerra was hired to clean floors and throw out garbage; however, he was 
quickly offered a position in mechanical cleaning. (Hr. Tr., p. 46)  As a mechanical

According to Guerra, he experienced progressive neck pain following the April

In response, Guerra testified that he consistently reported his neck pain to Dr. 
Berg, but he had no control over what Dr. Berg chose to include in his medical records.

November 23, 2022, appointment, and an injection was offered and rejected once.

While these concerns do not prove Dr. Berg omitted Guerra’s cervical spine

Dr. Kuhnlein’s opinions have their flaws as well.  First, his causation opinion 
relies in part on physical therapy records that were not submitted into evidence.  Dr. 
Kuhnlein’s opinion also relies in part on his finding of a positive Spurling’s maneuver. 
(Ex. 1, p. 18)  Notably, Guerra demonstrated full range of motion and a negative 
Spurling’s maneuver at 14 of the 15 appointments where the cervical spine was 
physically examined. (JE1, pp. 2, 5, 7, 11, 14, 17, 27, 37, 47, 49, 51, 53)  Second, Dr. 
Kuhnlein’s report fails to adequately address the lack of diagnostic imaging and 
objective evidence to support a finding of radiculopathy.  Lastly, Dr. Kuhnlein 
determined that Guerra was a poor historian and documented several examples of the 
same during his examination.  Such a finding is consistent with the undersigned’s 
observations at hearing.  Guerra was evasive with his answers on cross-examination 
and routinely provided that he “did not know” to a number of questions regarding his 
medical treatment.  I did not find Guerra to be a credible witness. 
When comparing the expert reports in this case, it is difficult to overlook the 
amount of time Dr. Berg spent with Guerra when compared to Dr. Kuhnlein.  Over the 
course of a year, Dr. Berg examined Guerra on 18 occasions.  During this time, Dr. 
Berg ordered and interpreted several x-rays, as well as two MRIs of the left shoulder, 
two CT scans of the left scapula, one EMG of the left upper extremity, and one MRI of 
the cervical spine.  Dr. Berg clearly performed an extensive workup of Guerra’s alleged 
conditions.   
Additionally, Dr. Kuhnlein’s causation opinion is predicated on the accuracy of 
Guerra’s statements during the independent medical examination.  I did not find 
Guerra’s testimony credible.  With this in mind, I afford more weight to the causation 
opinions of Dr. Berg and accept Dr. Berg’s opinion that Guerra did not sustain an injury 
to the cervical spine as a result of the April 22, 2022, or June 28, 2022, work injuries.  
Having reached this finding, I further accept Dr. Berg’s opinion that Guerra reached 
maximum medical improvement on June 22, 2023. 
With respect to permanent impairment, Dr. Berg assigned two percent left upper 
extremity impairment; however, Dr. Berg did not conduct an updated examination of 
Guerra prior to drafting his impairment assessment.  Instead, he relied on the range of 
motion measurements collected by Dr. Kuhnlein and Ms. Wrabek.  I do not find Dr. 
Berg’s impairment rating convincing.  Instead, I adopt the 15 percent left upper 
extremity impairment rating assigned by Dr. Kuhnlein. (Ex. 1, p. 20)

routinely worked more than 40 hours per week prior to his left shoulder injury.  Payroll 
records show that Guerra worked an average of 53.39 hours per week prior to his injury.  
Guerra testified that the defendant employer did not allow him to work overtime when he 
returned to light duty work.  He further testified that there was overtime work available

his gross weekly wages between April 22, 2022, and June 22, 2023.  Based on Exhibit

In his supplemental brief, Guerra submitted a detailed explanation with citations 
to the record as to the extent of temporary partial disability owed.  The parties agree Mr. 
Guerra worked an average of 53.39 hours per week in the 14 weeks leading up to the 
date of injury.  The parties further agree that Guerra is owed $4,947.19 in temporary 
partial disability benefits for the time period between May 1, 2022, through October 23, 
2022.  However, the parties disagree as to the extent of Guerra’s entitlement to 
temporary partial disability benefits from October 24, 2022, to June 22, 2023. 
Importantly, Guerra did not submit payroll records for the time period of October 
24, 2022, to June 22, 2023.  Instead, Mr. Guerra calculated his gross weekly wage from 
July 3, 2022, through October 23, 2022, and utilized the same to estimate the amount of 
temporary partial disability benefits owed for the time period between October 24, 2022,

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible. George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony.  The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the 
disability.  Supportive, lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, 
therefore, is also relevant and material to the causation question.  The weight to be 
given to an expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the 
accuracy of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding 
circumstances.  The expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. St. 
Luke's Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 
410 (Iowa 2001); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted expert 
medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, 
Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 1994). 
The first issue to be addressed is whether the claimant is entitled to temporary 
partial disability benefits from April 23, 2022, through June 22, 2023.  Having found that 
Guerra established he earned less than his gross weekly wages between April 22, 
2022, and June 22, 2023, I find claimant is entitled to an award of temporary partial 
disability benefits during this period of time.  Again, Guerra is directed to submit a

claim is denied.

as he is not yet at maximum medical improvement.   
Section 85.34(1) provides that healing period benefits are payable to an injured 
worker who has suffered permanent partial disability until (1) the worker has returned to 
work; (2) the worker is medically capable of returning to substantially similar

employment; or (3) the worker has achieved maximum medical recovery. The healing 
period can be considered the period during which there is a reasonable expectation of 
improvement of the disabling condition. See Armstrong Tire & Rubber Co. v. Kubli, 312 
N.W.2d 60 (Iowa App. 1981). Healing period benefits can be interrupted or intermittent. 
Teel v. McCord, 394 N.W.2d 405 (Iowa 1986).

Under the Iowa Workers' Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is 
compensated either for a loss or loss of use of a scheduled member under Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(a)-(u) or for loss of earning capacity under section 85.34(2)(v). The 
extent of scheduled member disability benefits to which an injured worker is entitled is 
determined by using the functional method. Functional disability is “limited to the loss of 
the physiological capacity of the body or body part.” Mortimer v. Fruehauf Corp., 502 
N.W.2d 12, 15 (Iowa 1993); Sherman v. Pella Corp., 576 N.W.2d 312 (Iowa 1998). 
In all cases of permanent partial disability described in paragraphs “a” 
through “u”, or paragraph “v” when determining functional disability and 
not loss of earning capacity, the extent of loss or percentage of permanent 
impairment shall be determined solely by utilizing the guides to the 
evaluation of permanent impairment, published by the American medical 
association, as adopted by the workers' compensation commissioner by 
rule pursuant to chapter 17A. Lay testimony or agency expertise shall not 
be utilized in determining loss or percentage of permanent impairment 
pursuant to paragraphs “a” through ““u”, or paragraph “v” when 
determining functional disability and not loss of earning capacity.

Next, Guerra asserts that he is entitled to temporary partial disability benefits for

An employee is entitled to appropriate temporary partial disability benefits during 
those periods in which the employee is temporarily, partially disabled. An employee is 
temporarily, partially disabled when the employee is not capable medically of returning 
to employment substantially similar to the employment in which the employee was 
engaged at the time of the injury, but is able to perform other work consistent with the 
employee's disability. Temporary partial benefits are not payable upon termination of 
temporary disability, healing period, or permanent partial disability simply because the 
employee is not able to secure work paying weekly earnings equal to the employee's 
weekly earnings at the time of the injury. Iowa Code section 85.33(2).

estimates.  As such, I found Guerra failed to prove he is entitled to temporary partial 
disability benefits for the time period between October 24, 2022, and June 22, 2023.

Lastly, claimant asserts he is entitled to penalty benefits for defendants’ delay in 
paying TPD benefits and delay in seeking an impairment assessment from Dr. Berg. 
Iowa Code section 10A.315(4)(a) provides: 
If a denial, a delay in payment, or a termination of benefits occurs without 
reasonable or probable cause or excuse known to the employer or 
insurance carrier at the time of the denial, delay in payment, or termination 
of benefits, the workers' compensation commissioner shall award benefits 
in addition to those benefits payable under this subchapter, or chapter 85, 
85A, or 85B, up to fifty percent of the amount of benefits that were denied, 
delayed, or terminated without reasonable or probable cause or excuse. 
In Christensen v. Snap-On Tools Corp., 554 N.W.2d 254 (Iowa 1996), and 
Robbennolt v. Snap-On Tools Corp., 555 N.W.2d 229 (Iowa 1996), the supreme court 
said: 
Based on the plain language of section [10A.315(4)]1 , we hold an 
employee is entitled to penalty benefits if there has been a delay in 
payment unless the employer proves a reasonable cause or excuse. A 
reasonable cause or excuse exists if either (1) the delay was necessary 
for the insurer to investigate the claim or (2) the employer had a 
reasonable basis to contest the employee's entitlement to benefits. A 
“reasonable basis” for denial of the claim exists if the claim is “fairly 
debatable.” 
Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 260.

to the current location of this statute are substituted to avoid confusion.

An issue of law is fairly debatable if viable arguments exist in favor of each party.  
Covia v. Robinson, 507 N.W.2d 411 (Iowa 1993).  An issue of fact is fairly debatable if 
substantial evidence exists which would support a finding favorable to the employer.  
Gilbert v. USF Holland, Inc., 637 N.W.2d 194 (Iowa 2001). 
If the employer fails to show reasonable cause or excuse for the delay or denial, 
the commissioner shall impose a penalty in an amount up to 50 percent of the amount 
unreasonably delayed or denied.  Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 254.  The factors to be 
considered in determining the amount of the penalty include the length of the delay, the 
number of delays, the information available to the employer and the employer's past 
record of penalties.  Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 238. 
The question here is whether the defendants were reasonable in their decision 
not to have the claimant evaluated for permanent disability; that is, whether such is a 
matter that is fairly debatable so as to excuse the defendants' failure to investigate this 
aspect of the claim. 
In Dr. Berg’s June 22, 2023, note releasing Guerra from treatment, he stated, 
“there is certainly no doubt that Jose has sustained some impairment to his left upper 
extremity. […] I have not been asked to determine any impairment with Jose. I certainly 
can do this based on the medical records and the many months that I have cared for 
Jose.” (JE1, p. 55)  Despite Dr. Berg’s statements, defendants did not request an 
impairment rating from Dr. Berg until December 19, 2023.  Defendants ignored the 
requirements to pay permanent partial disability as required by Iowa Code section 
85.34(2) with no medical basis for doing so. Defendants' failure to ask the questions 
necessary to make an informed decision on the issue of whether there was a 
permanent impairment and resulting disability is clearly unreasonable.  Again, given Dr. 
Berg’s statements, the question of whether claimant’s injury resulted in permanent 
impairment was not fairly debatable. 
Defendants have failed to establish the existence of a reasonable or probable 
cause or excuse for its delay. As such, penalty benefits are warranted on this basis. 
Defendants volunteered eight (8) weeks of benefits pursuant to Dr. Berg’s rating on 
January 18, 2024.  The length of delay between June 22, 2023, and January 18, 2024, 
is approximately 30 weeks.   
The purpose of Iowa Code section 86.13 is both punishment for unreasonable 
conduct but also deterrence for future cases. Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 237. In this 
regard, this agency is given discretion to determine the amount of the penalty imposed 
with a maximum penalty of 50 percent of the amount of the delayed, or denied, benefits. 
Christensen v. Snap-On Tools Corp., 554 N.W.2d 254, 261 (Iowa 1996). In exercising 
its discretion, the agency must consider factors such as the length of the delays, the 
number of delays, the information available to the employer regarding the employee's 
injury and wages, and the employer's past record of penalties. Meyers v. Holiday 
Express Corp., 557 N.W.2d 502, 505 (Iowa 1996).
