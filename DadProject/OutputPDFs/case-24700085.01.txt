STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
Claimant, Scott Chapman, filed a petition for arbitration against the Second Injury 
Fund of Iowa. The hearing occurred before the undersigned on February 19, 2025. This 
case proceeded to a live video hearing via Zoom. 
 
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the hearing. In the 
hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations. All of those stipulations 
were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration decision, and no factual 
or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised or discussed in this 
decision. The parties are bound by their stipulations. 
 
The evidentiary record consists of Joint Exhibits 1 through 5, Claimant’s Exhibits 
1 through 10, and Defendant’s Exhibits AA through CC. Claimant testified on his own 
behalf. No other witnesses were called to testify. The evidentiary record closed at the 
conclusion of the evidentiary hearing on February 19, 2025. The case was considered 
fully submitted upon submission of post-hearing briefs on April 4, 2025.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
The undersigned, having considered all of the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
 
The claimant at the center of this case is Scott Chapman, a 61-year-old individual 
who resides in Buford, Georgia. (Hearing Transcript, page 12)  Chapman is a high 
school graduate. (Hr. Tr., p. 13)  His employment history largely consists of being a 
professional truck driver for several companies.  These include Hanson & Adkins Auto 
Transport, Total Transport, White Tail Auto Transport, Waggonners Trucking, SDS Auto 
and Ron Nick Enterprises. (Exhibit 2, pp. 18-19) 
 
Since 2015, Chapman has been transporting and delivering automobiles to 
dealerships for Mid-Western Car Carriers. (Ex. 2, p. 18)  The job requires him to 
inspect, load, and unload vehicles onto a “double decker” semi-truck. (Hr. Tr., pp. 14-
15)  To load the vehicles, Chapman must strap down tires and use a tie down bar to 
secure vehicles to the trailer. (Hr. Tr., p. 15) 
 
On December 1, 2020, Chapman injured his left shoulder after loading a car onto 
his Mid-Western Car Carriers semi-truck.  As he exited the vehicle, he stepped onto the 
trailer’s side, slipped, lost his balance, and fell away from the trailer.  In an attempt to 
catch himself, he grabbed a post with his left hand, which jerked his arm out of its 
socket. (Hr. Tr., p. 22)  Chapman was transported via ambulance and received 
treatment at Corewell Health Dearborn Hospital Emergency Room in Dearborn, 
Michigan.  Alec Lesko, D.O. assessed Chapman with a left shoulder dislocation. (Joint 
Exhibit 1, p. 3)  Dr. Lesko performed a closed reduction of the left shoulder and referred 
Chapman for an orthopedic evaluation. (JE1, p. 1) 
 
A December 22, 2020, MRI of the left shoulder revealed a Bankart tear and 
rotator cuff tear. (JE2, pp. 5-6) 
 
Charles Morris, M.D. performed a left shoulder arthroscopy with rotator cuff 
repair, labral repair, and biceps tenodesis on January 28, 2021. (JE2, pp. 7-8)  He 
subsequently placed Chapman at maximum medical improvement on August 13, 2021.  
Based on a slight loss of motion, Dr. Morris assigned 5 percent left upper extremity 
impairment. (JE2, p. 10)  The impairment assessment was calculated using the AMA 
Guides, Fifth Edition; however, Dr. Morris did not provide the rationale for the 5 percent 
impairment rating. (Id.)

The initial disputed issue is whether Chapman has proven he sustained a first 
qualifying injury to his left arm as a result of the December 1, 2020, injury.  Specifically, 
Chapman asserts he sustained injuries to both his left arm and left shoulder on 
December 1, 2020.  Chapman’s argument is not supported by the medical evidence or 
applicable legal precedent.   
 
The medical records do not contain any diagnosis, complaints, or objective 
findings specific to the left arm independent of the left shoulder.  Instead, the evidentiary 
record demonstrates that Chapman sustained a rotator cuff tear, a labral tear, and a 
proximal biceps tear, all of which are classified as shoulder injuries.   
 
Legal precedent – as will be discussed in the Conclusions of Law section – does 
not support a finding that an injury to the proximal biceps is an injury to the arm for 
purposes of calculating permanent partial disability benefits.  I see no reason why the 
same logic would not apply when assessing whether an injury to the proximal biceps 
qualifies as an arm injury for purposes of the Second Injury Fund benefits.  Chapman’s 
attempt to classify his condition as an arm injury is unpersuasive.  I find that Chapman 
failed to prove he sustained a distinct injury to the left arm as a result of the December 
1, 2020, injury.  This finding renders all other issues moot. 
 
CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
Chapman seeks an award of benefits from the Second Injury Fund of Iowa. Iowa's 
version of a second injury fund is found in Iowa Code sections 85.63 through 85.69. The 
heart of the statutory scheme is found in section 85.64(1), which states: 
 
If an employee who has previously lost, or lost the use of, one hand, one 
arm, one foot, one leg, or one eye, becomes permanently disabled by a 
compensable injury which has resulted in the loss of or loss of use of 
another such member or organ, the employer shall be liable only for the

Page 5 
 
degree of disability which would have resulted from the latter injury if there 
had been no pre-existing disability. In addition to such compensation, and 
after the expiration of the full period provided by law for the payments 
thereof by the employer, the employee shall be paid out of the “Second 
Injury Fund” created by this division the remainder of such compensation 
as would be payable for the degree of permanent disability involved after 
first deducting from such remainder the compensable value of the 
previously lost member or organ. 
 
Before the Fund’s liability is triggered, three requirements must be met. First, the 
employee must have lost, or lost the use of, a hand, arm, foot, leg, or eye. Second, the 
employee must sustain a loss, or loss of use of, another specified member or organ 
through a compensable injury. Third, permanent disability must exist as to both the 
initial injury and the second injury. 
 
Other than the express requirement that the second loss be the result of 
permanent disability caused “by a compensable injury,” the text of section 85.64(1) 
provides no basis from which to create different standards for what constitutes a 
qualifying loss to an enumerated body part based on whether it happened first or 
second in time. See Iowa Code section 85.64(1). Moreover, the Iowa Supreme Court 
held it would be “senselessly inconsistent” to apply different standards based on the 
order of occurrence. Gregory v. Second Injury Fund, 777 N.W.2d 395, 400 (Iowa 2010). 
Therefore, the case law delineating the contours of what constitutes a qualifying loss 
under the statute is generally applicable regardless of when such a loss took place. See 
Id. 
 
The matter at hand involves injuries to the rotator cuff and the proximal biceps.  
These muscles and tendons have previously been determined to be part of the 
“shoulder” pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(n).  Our Supreme Court provided 
analysis of this issue in Chavez v. MS Technology LLC, 972 N.W.2d 662 (Iowa 2022). 
The claimant in Chavez felt a pop in her right shoulder, and suffered a full thickness 
rotator cuff tear, which retracted to the glenoid. Id. at 668. She also was diagnosed with 
severe AC arthrosis, tendonitis, and tearing of the biceps tendon. The Commissioner 
and court ruled that the shoulder includes the shoulder structure, including injuries to 
the tendons, ligaments, muscles, and articular surfaces connected to the glenohumeral 
joint. Id. The court ruled that the legislature did not intend to limit the definition of the 
shoulder solely to the glenohumeral joint. Id. The Commissioner and court also noted 
that the claimant suffered biceps tendonitis and tearing, and that she underwent a 
biceps tenotomy along with her shoulder surgery. Id. at 671. Notably, the Court noted 
that Ms. Chavez did not suffer a permanent impairment to her right arm due to her 
biceps injury. Id. at 671.  Accordingly, I find Chapman’s injuries to the rotator cuff and 
proximal biceps are injuries to the shoulder pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(n). 
 
Because a shoulder injury is not one of the enumerated scheduled member 
injuries listed in Iowa Code section 85.64, the claimant cannot recover any benefits from

the Fund based solely on an injury to his shoulder. West v. Second Injury Fund of Iowa,

I found that claimant failed to prove he sustained a separate and distinct left arm 
injury as a result of the December 1, 2020, work injury.  Accordingly, I conclude that 
claimant failed to carry his burden of proof to establish a first qualifying injury to the left 
arm.
