Petitioner Norie C. Smith (Claimant) filed a Petition for Judicial Review in this Iowa Code 
chapter 17A proceeding on August 10, 2022 (the Petition), alleging a final agency decision 
denying her workers’ compensation benefits was erroneous. In person oral argument was held on 
April 12, 2024. Attorney Eric Luthens appeared for Petitioner. Attorneys Timothy Wegman, 
Joseph Barron and Christopher Spencer (argued) represented Respondents. Oral argument was not 
reported. 
After reviewing the court file and the administrative record in light of the relevant law, and 
after hearing the respective arguments of counsel, the court enters the following Order dismissing 
the Petition in its entirety and affirming the final agency decision in its entirety for the reasons 
discussed below. 
                BACKGROUND FACTS AND PROCEEDINGS 
 
On December 28, 2019, Claimant filed a petition (the agency petition) with the Iowa 
workers’ compensation commissioner (the Commissioner) for benefits related to an injury she 
sustained on November 27, 2017, while working for Respondent TPI Iowa, LLC. (TPI), a company 
that makes wind turbines. On February 7, 2022, Deputy Commissioner Gerrish-Lanpe (the 
Deputy) issued an Arbitration Decision finding Claimant failed to meet her burden of proof that

                                   STANDARD OF REVIEW 
 
Judicial review of an administrative agency action is governed by the Iowa Administrative 
Procedure Act (IAPA), Iowa Code chapter 17A. An agency decision shall be reversed or modified 
if the agency decision is unsupported by substantial evidence, affected by error of law or 
unreasonable, arbitrary or capricious.  Iowa Code § 17A.19(8). 
 
The court is bound by agency findings of fact if they are supported by substantial evidence 
in the record as a whole. Meyer v. IBP, Inc., 710 N.W.2d 213, 218 (Iowa 2006). The workers’ 
compensation commissioner is the trier of fact and is charged with weighing witness credibility, 
weighing the evidence, and deciding factual issues. Arndt v. City of Le Claire, 728 N.W.2d 389, 
394-95 (Iowa 2007). The court will broadly and liberally apply agency findings to uphold, rather 
than overturn, an agency decision. Stephenson v. Furnas Elec. Co., 522 N.W.2d 828, 831 (Iowa 
1994). The reviewing court may disregard the agency’s conclusions if it decides, after reviewing 
the entire record, the direct and circumstantial evidence is so compelling that a reasonable mind 
would find the evidence inadequate to reach the same conclusions. Ringland Johnson, Inc. v. 
Hunecke, 585 N.W.2d 269, 272 (Iowa 1998). The record before the agency must be viewed as a 
whole to determine if there is substantial evidence to support the workers’ compensation 
commissioner’s decision. 2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124, 126 (Iowa 1995). The court 
will reverse the application of law to facts if it is “irrational, illogical, or wholly unjustifiable.” 
Neal v. Annette Holdings, Inc., 814 N.W.2d 493, 518 (Iowa 2012) (quoting Lakeside Casino v. 
Blue, 743 N.W.2d 169, 173 (Iowa 2007)). The workers’ compensation commissioner’s application

                                                CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
The predominant issue raised by Claimant on judicial review is that the Commissioner 
erred in finding her work injury did not result in any permanent condition. Claimant’s remaining 
two assignments of error flow from the first contention. She urges the Commissioner further erred 
by not ordering payment of benefits for certain of Claimant’s past medical expenses and by failing 
to award Claimant costs. 
 
Claimant’s first contention deals with agency findings of fact and will be reviewed under 
the substantial evidence standard. The agency is not required to discuss every single finding or all 
of the application of law to facts. The agency only needs to provide a decision detailed enough for 
a reviewing court to work backwards to determine the findings that needed to be made. Schutjer 
v. Algona Manor Care Ctr., 780 N.W.2d 540, 560-61 (Iowa 2010). Further, the court in this 
instance must try to uphold the decision made in terms of the findings of fact and the application 
of law to facts. Coghlan v. Quinn Wire & Iron Works, 164 N.W.2d 848, 852 (Iowa 1969); Larson 
Mfg. Co. v. Thorson, 763 N.W.2d 842, 850 (Iowa 2009); Christensen v. Iowa Bd. of Educ. Exam’rs, 
831 N.W.2d 179, 192 (Iowa 2013). 
 
Claimant bears the burden of showing that the claimed injury is a proximate cause of 
Claimant’s condition or injury. Ayers v. D & N Fence Co., Inc., 731 N.W.2d 11, 17 (Iowa 2007). 
For a cause to be proximate, it must be a substantial factor in bringing about the result. (Id.). 
Questions of causal connection between the condition and the alleged injury are essentially within 
the domain of expert testimony. Dunlavey v. Econ. Fire & Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). An expert opinion, even an uncontroverted expert opinion, may be accepted or rejected by

the Commissioner. Sondag v. Ferris Hardware, 220 N.W.2d 903, 907-08 (Iowa 1974). 
 
In the instant matter the agency found the opinions of Dr. Miller and Dr. Neff more 
persuasive than the opinions offered by Dr. Stoken. Dr. Miller and Dr. Neff both offered opinions 
that Claimant sustained only a temporary injury to her back. Dr. Miller provided treatment to 
Claimant following the incident. Dr. Neff is an orthopaedic specialist. The opinions of Dr. Miller 
and Dr. Neff alone are more than adequate evidence to support the agency’s findings and its 
conclusion that Claimant only sustained a temporary injury when she slipped and fell.   
Only Dr. Stoken assigned any permanent functional impairment. It was within the purview 
of the agency to weigh the competing expert opinions. The court will not second guess the weight 
afforded those opinions under the substantial evidence standard. An agency decision is not 
considered to be unsupported by substantial evidence merely because inconsistent conclusions can 
be drawn from the same evidence. Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(f); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, et al., 621 
N.W.2d 410, 417 (Iowa 2001). Claimant urges the court to re-evaluate the evidence in this case to 
reach a more favorable conclusion for her. The court respectfully declines to do so under the 
standard of deference afforded to the agency’s findings of fact. 
Claimant ignores the opinions of Dr. Miller in asking the court to overturn the agency’s 
factual findings. Dr. Miller treated Claimant following her injury before releasing her from care. 
Dr. Miller opined that the injury resulted in no permanent impairment and did not result in the 
need for restrictions. Claimant’s arguments to diminish the weight the agency afforded to Dr. 
Miller’s opinions are unconvincing on judicial review. 
Claimant argues that it was error for the agency to note contradictions between Dr. Stoken’s 
earlier examination for another injury claim and her more recent examination. Such findings are 
consistent with the record as a whole and fair inferences to draw from the evidence. It was not 
error for the agency to consider those earlier opinions in weighing the competing expert opinions

Claimant also faults the agency for pointing out that Dr. Stoken performed a one-time 
examination. However, this appears to be a correct statement based upon the record. Dr. Stoken 
performed an IME at Claimant’s request. The agency could consider this in weighing competing 
expert opinions. The Deputy specifically concluded: 
 
Claimant did sustain a fall and she did go on to receive physical therapy for 
her back and neck pains. Dr. Neff attributes this pain primarily to claimant’s 
underlying degenerative condition. Dr. Stoken’s opinions are somewhat 
contradictory to her previous opinion. Dr. Stoken placed claimant in the sedentary 
work category following claimant’s left shoulder injury and rotator cuff repair. 
However, for the fall, Dr. Stoken found claimant to be capable of light duty work 
which suggests that claimant’s condition was better in 2021 than it was in 2018 
when Dr. Stoken examined claimant for the shoulder injury. Further, there was no 
mention of neck and back pain in the August 6, 2018, examination of claimant 
which Dr. Stoken conducted for the purposes of evaluating the left shoulder. (see 
DE D). Under the current section of the report, claimant made no mention of the 
fall in November 2017, or any lingering problems related to the fall of November 
2017. 
 
 
The picture painted in Dr. Stoken’s report is of an employee who sustained 
a serious debilitating injury to the left shoulder and left arm with no problems 
related to the back and neck which is consistent with the argument advanced by the 
defendants and their experts that claimant sustained only a temporary exacerbation 
of a pre-existing condition. 
 
 
Based on the report of Dr. Stoken in August 2018 along with the opinions 
of Dr. Miller and Dr. Neff, it is found that claimant sustained a temporary 
exacerbation of a pre-existing degenerative condition that was resolved in mid-
2018. The claimant has not carried her burden of proof as it relates to the issue of 
permanency.

                                                                CONCLUSION 
The agency did not err in accepting the opinions of Dr. Miller and Dr. Neff over those of 
Dr. Stoken. The agency was charged with weighing the evidence. Claimant’s belief that the 
evidence could support a different determination is not adequate to reverse the agency’s findings.

Based upon the opinions of Dr. Neff and Dr. Miller, the finding that Claimant only suffered a 
temporary injury that resolved without any permanent disability is supported by the evidence in 
the record when the record is viewed as a whole. 
This finding is dispositive of the remaining issues raised by Claimant on judicial review. 
Given that Claimant only suffered a temporary injury, it was not error to decline to award Claimant 
benefits for past medical care. Likewise, as Claimant was not the prevailing party on her issues 
before the agency, it was not an abuse of discretion by the agency to decline to award Claimant 
costs. 
                                                 ORDER 
 
IT IS THEREFORE ORDERED, ADJUDGED AND DECREED that the Petition is 
dismissed in its entirety, and the Appeal Decision of the Commissioner affirming the Arbitration 
Decision is affirmed in its entirety.

proceeding are assessed to Claimant.
