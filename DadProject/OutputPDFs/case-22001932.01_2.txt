II. 
STANDARD OF REVIEW 
According to Iowa Code Section 17A.19(10)(f), when parties challenge the 
factual findings of administrative agencies, such as the workers' compensation 
commission, the district court reviews the record to determine whether those 
findings are supported by substantial evidence when the court views the record as a 
whole. Substantial evidence is “the quantity and quality of evidence that would be 
deemed sufficient by a neutral, detached, and reasonable person to establish the 
fact at issue when the consequences resulting from the establishment of that fact 
are understood to be serious and of great importance.36”  
When conducting a substantial evidence review, the court must affirm, even 
if the evidence in the record might support a different finding.37 This standard 
accords a fair amount of deference to the agency. Usually, courts only overturn 
factual findings when they are irrational or arbitrary.38 However, to receive such

proving notice under section 85.23 to Culpepper. Section 85.23 requires 
employees, or someone acting on their behalf, to notify their employers of an 
injury within ninety days from the date the employee "knew or should have known

"the employee becomes aware that the injury impacts his or her employment."45

may be relevant to the question, the employee's awareness that the injury affects 
her work is ultimately dispositive.46 This construction of the rule is critical when 
determining a date of injury in cumulative injury cases such as this one.

In response to an inquiry by Culpepper’s counsel, Dr. Abernathey authored

traumatic injuries and  cumulative injury here, he opined, "[a]lthough there was not 
a specific traumatic injury, the work activities were a substantial contributing factor 
to his current condition and need for care.”96  
Unfortunately, neither the deputy nor the commissioner acknowledged or 
discussed this highly relevant evidence. Not only is the evidence highly relevant, 
but it is also uncontradicted. Under Iowa law, a deputy must at least address and

activity is not entitled to deference. Accordingly, the court reverses the findings of
