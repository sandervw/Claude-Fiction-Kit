STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
Christy Spranger-Jones, claimant, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from Kimco Facility Services, LLC, employer, and Everest 
National Insurance Co., insurance carrier, as defendants.  The hearing was held on 
December 11, 2023.  Pursuant to an order from the Iowa Workers’ Compensation 
Commissioner, this case was heard via videoconference using Zoom with all parties 
and the court reporter appearing remotely.   
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.   
Christy Spranger-Jones was the only witness to testify live at the trial.  The 
evidentiary record also includes Joint Exhibits 1-12, Claimant’s Exhibits 1-9, and 
Defendants’ Amended Exhibits A-D.  The parties submitted post-hearing briefs on 
January 16, 2024.  Defendants filed the hearing transcript on April 10, 2024.   
On April 29, 2024, the undersigned issued a show cause order.  This order 
indicated that two of the parties’ stipulations were legally inaccurate and could not be 
accepted by the agency.  The order gave the parties two weeks to submit new 
stipulations for claimant’s weekly rate and for the date she returned back to work 
following the injury, or to request leave to submit additional evidence on those issues.

ISSUES 
The parties identified the following disputed issues on the hearing report: 
1. Whether claimant sustained an injury that arose out of and in the course of her 
employment with Kimco Facility Services, LLC, on December 30, 2020.   
 
2. Whether the alleged injury resulted in any permanent disability; and if so, 
 
3. The nature and extent of claimant’s entitlement to permanent disability benefits. 
 
4. The commencement date for permanent partial disability benefits if any are 
awarded.  
 
5. Whether the claimant is entitled to temporary disability or healing period benefits 
because of the alleged injury. 
 
6. Whether claimant is entitled to penalty benefits.  
 
7. Whether claimant is entitled to payment of the medical expenses listed in 
Claimant’s Exhibits 6 and 7. 
 
8. Whether claimant is entitled to alternate medical care. 
 
9. Whether claimant is entitled to recover the cost of an independent medical 
examination pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.39. 
 
10. Assessment of costs.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the

employment.  Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. Dial 
Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309 (Iowa 1996).  The words “arising out of” refer to the cause or 
source of the injury.  The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 
circumstances of the injury.  2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124 (Iowa 1995).  
An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists between the 
injury and the employment.  Miedema, 551 N.W.2d 309.  The injury must be a rational 
consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely incidental to 
the employment.  Koehler Electric v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1 (Iowa 2000); Miedema, 551 
N.W.2d 309.  An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it happens within a 
period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may be when 
performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties or doing 
an activity incidental to them.  Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony.  The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question.  The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances.  The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); 
Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995).  Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994).  Unrebutted expert medical 
testimony cannot be summarily rejected.  Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 
N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 1994). 
When an expert's opinion is based upon an incomplete or incorrect history, it is 
not necessarily binding on the commissioner or the court. It is then to be weighed, 
together with other facts and circumstances, the ultimate conclusion being for the finder 
of the fact.  Musselman v. Central Telephone Company,154 N.W.2d 128 (Iowa 
1967); Bodish v. Fischer, Inc., 257 Iowa 516, 133 N.W.2d 867 (1965).  The 
commissioner as trier of fact has the duty to determine the credibility of the witnesses 
and to weigh the evidence, together with the other disclosed facts and circumstances, 
and then to accept or reject the opinion.  Dunlavey, 526 N.W.2d 845. 
While a claimant is not entitled to compensation for the results of a preexisting 
injury or disease, its mere existence at the time of a subsequent injury is not a defense. 
Rose v. John Deere Ottumwa Works, 247 Iowa 900, 76 N.W.2d 756 (1956). If the

Iowa Code section 85.33 governs temporary disability benefits, and Iowa Code 
section 85.34 governs healing period and permanent disability benefits.  Dunlap v. 
Action Warehouse, 824 N.W.2d 545, 556 (Iowa Ct. App. 2012).  As a general rule,

of recovery from the condition.  Id.  An award of healing period benefits or total 
temporary disability benefits is not dependent on a finding of permanent impairment, 
however which type of benefit a claimant receives depends on whether or not the 
employee has sustained permanent disability.  Dunlap, 824 N.W.2d at 556.  Jones has 
proven entitlement to permanent disability benefits in this action; thus, the temporary 
benefits she seeks are healing period benefits. Id.

An employee is entitled to temporary partial disability benefits during those 
periods in which the employee is temporarily, partially disabled.  An employee is 
temporarily, partially disabled when the employee is not capable medically of returning

temporary partial reduction in earning ability” as a result of the work injury. Iowa Code §

current low back pain, as well as a material aggravation of her pre-exiting lipoma.  The 
majority of the expenses listed in Jones’s summary are for treatment she received for 
those conditions.  However, there are no corresponding medical records for two of the 
items listed in her expense sheet: 1) a visit to James Lindley, M.D., on March 30, 2021, 
and 2) a visit to John Dooley, M.D., on October 18, 2023.  (Cl Ex. 7, pp. 27-28).  Given 
the lack of evidence I cannot determine whether there is a causal connection between 
those dates of service and the work injury.  The remainder of the medical expenses 
listed are compensable.  If Jones has paid the remaining medical expenses listed in 
Claimant’s Exhibits 6 and 7, she is entitled to reimbursement of those expenses.  If 
Jones has not paid those expenses, defendants are ordered to pay the listed providers 
directly and/or satisfy any liens asserted for payment of those expenses. Krohn v. State 
of Iowa, 420 N.W.2d 463, 465 (Iowa 1988); Caylor v. Employers Mut. Casualty Co., 337 
N.W.2d 890, 894 (Iowa App.1983).  
 
Jones also asserts a claim for alternate medical care.  Specifically, she seeks 
further care as recommended by Dr. Pugely and Dr. Taylor. (See Claimant’s Post-
Hearing Brief, p. 37).  In his report, Dr. Pugely recommended Jones see a pain doctor 
for epidural steroid injections, topical pain medications and/or muscle relaxants, and 
potentially a TENS unit trial.  Dr. Taylor recommended Jones treat with Dr. Dooley, have 
an evaluation with a pain psychologist, and attend physical therapy.  Iowa Code section 
85.27(4) provides, in relevant part: 
For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish reasonable 
services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has the right to 
choose the care . . . . The treatment must be offered promptly and be 
reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the 
employee. If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the care 
offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such dissatisfaction 
to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the employer and 
the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited to treat the 

SPRANGER-JONES V. KIMCO FACILITY SERVICES, LLC 
Page 20 
 
 
 
injury. If the employer and employee cannot agree on such alternate care, 
the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable proofs of the 
necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
Id.; see also, Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 433, 436-37 (Iowa 
1997). Under this code section, the employer must furnish “reasonable medical services 
and supplies and reasonable and necessary appliances to treat an injured employee.” 
Stone Container Corp. v. Castle, 657 N.W.2d 485, 490 (Iowa 2003) (emphasis in 
original). Such employer-provided care “must be offered promptly and be reasonably 
suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the employee.” Iowa Code § 
85.27(4). 
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment - and seeking alternate care - 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable. See e.g., 
Iowa R. App. P. 14(f)(5); Bell Bros. Heating and Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d 
193, 209 (Iowa 2010); Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122, 124 (Iowa 1995). An 
injured employee dissatisfied with the employer-furnished care (or lack thereof) may 
share the employee’s discontent with the employer and if the parties cannot reach an 
agreement on alternate care, “the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable 
proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and order the care.” Id. “Determining what care is 
reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.” Long, 528 N.W.2d at 123; Reynolds, 
562 N.W.2d at 436. As the party seeking relief in the form of alternate care, the 
employee bears the burden of proving that the authorized care is unreasonable. Long, 
528 N.W.2d at 124; Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d at 209; Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d at 436. Because 
“the employer’s obligation under the statute turns on the question of reasonable 
necessity, not desirability,” an injured employee’s dissatisfaction with employer-provided 
care, standing alone, is not enough to find such care unreasonable. Long, 528 N.W.2d 
at 124. 
Jones argues she is entitled to alternate medical care because defendants are 
not currently providing any treatment. (Claimant’s Post-Hearing Brief, p. 37).  While that 
assertion is correct, Jones has already received the treatment recommended by Dr. 
Pugely and Dr. Taylor.  Since her evaluation with Dr. Pugely, she has been evaluated 
by two different pain specialists, and received epidural steroid injections to her lumbar 
spine and left SI joint, a blood patch, medial branch blocks, several different pain 
medications, muscle relaxers, a TENS unit, physical therapy, chiropractic treatment, 
and osteopathic manipulation. The alternate care process is not available for care that 
has already taken place.  See Moline v. Nordstrom, File No. 1273226 (Alt. Care, 
December 21, 2000); Donisi v. Norrell Services, File No. 1276161 (Alt. Care, August 8, 
2000); Mobayed v. AMS Services, Inc., File No. 1168048 (Alt. Care, May 20, 1997); and 
Massie v. Madison Avenue Dairy Queen, File No. 1055168 (Alt. Care, November 3, 
1995). On this record, it appears Jones has already received reasonable care as 
recommended by Dr. Pugely and Dr. Taylor.  Her request for alternate medical care is 
denied.   
 
Jones requests reimbursement of the IME performed by Dr. Taylor.  Iowa Code 
section 85.39 permits an employee to be reimbursed for a subsequent examination by a 

SPRANGER-JONES V. KIMCO FACILITY SERVICES, LLC 
Page 21 
 
 
 
physician of the employee's choice where an employer-retained physician has 
previously evaluated “permanent disability” and the employee believes that the initial 
evaluation is too low. The Iowa Supreme Court has provided a literal interpretation of 
the plain language of Iowa Code section 85.39, stating that section only allows the 
employee to obtain an independent medical evaluation at the employer’s expense if an 
evaluation of permanent disability has already been made by an employer retained 
physician.  Des Moines Area Reg’l Transit Auth. v. Young, 867 N.W.2d 839, 847 (Iowa 
2015).  
 
Dr. Pugely, defendants’ IME physician, evaluated Jones in October 2021.  He, 
however, did not assign her an impairment rating.  Instead, he indicated she was not yet 
at MMI from the work fall and needed additional treatment. (JE 7, p. 106).  Dr. Taylor’s 
IME report was issued in September 2023.  No employer retained doctor provided a 
permanent disability rating prior to Dr. Taylor issuing his report.  Given this, Jones does 
not meet the requirements for IME reimbursement under Iowa Code section 85.39.6   
In Young, the Supreme Court noted that in cases where Iowa Code section 85.39 
is not triggered to allow for IME reimbursement, a claimant can still be reimbursed 
expenses associated with the preparation of a written report as a cost under 
administrative rule 876 IAC 4.33 because the report is used as evidence in lieu of the 
doctor’s testimony.  Young, 867 N.W.2d at 846-47.  However, only the cost of the report 
can be assessed, not the underlying exam or treatment. Id. 
Iowa Administrative Rule 4.33 outlines what costs may be assessed at hearing. It 
provides, as follows:  
Costs taxed by the workers’ compensation commissioner or a deputy 
commissioner shall be (1) attendance of a certified shorthand reporter or 
presence of mechanical means at hearings and evidential depositions, (2) 
transcription costs when appropriate, (3) costs of service of the original 
notice and subpoenas, (4) witness fees and expenses as provided by Iowa 
Code sections 622.69 and 622.72, (5) the costs of doctors’ and 
practitioners’ deposition testimony, provided that said costs do not exceed 
the amounts provided by Iowa Code sections 622.69 and 622.72, (6) the 
reasonable costs of obtaining no more than two doctors’ or practitioners’ 
reports, (7) filing fees when appropriate, including convenience fees 
incurred by using the WCES payment gateway, and (8) costs of persons 
reviewing health service disputes.
