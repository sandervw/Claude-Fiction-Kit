STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A. The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Glenn Lanpher.   
The alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on July 15, 2024.  
Claimant appeared through his attorney Matthew Dake.  Defendants appeared through 
their attorney Steve Spencer.  The proceedings were digitally recorded. That recording 
constitutes the official record of this proceeding.  
Pursuant to the Commissioner’s July 21, 2023, Order, the undersigned has been 
delegated authority to issue a final agency decision in this alternate medical care 
proceeding. Therefore, this ruling is designated final agency action, and any appeal of 
the decision would be to the Iowa District Court pursuant to Iowa Code section 17A. 
The hearing record consists of: 
 Claimant’s Exhibits 1-4 
 Defendants’ Exhibit A 
There was no witness testimony at hearing.  Counsel for both parties provided 
argument.  The record closed at the end of the alternate medical care telephonic 
hearing.

ISSUE 
The issue presented for resolution is whether the claimant is entitled to alternate 
medical care in the form of: 
 Authorization to attend physical therapy and occupational therapy at 
Jones Regional Medical Center in Anamosa.  
FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned having considered all the evidence in the record finds: 
On May 7, 2024, Claimant sustained a work-related injury to his right leg. (See 
Petition; Hearing Testimony).   According to claimant’s counsel, as a result of the work 
injury, a portion of claimant’s right lower extremity was amputated. (Hearing Testimony).  
Defendants admitted liability for the injury and authorized medical care. (Hearing 
Testimony).  None of claimant’s medical records were submitted into the hearing record, 
and the claimant did not testify at the hearing; thus, the agency’s understanding of 
claimant’s medical treatment is cobbled together from emails exchanged between the 
parties’ representatives.  According to claimant’s counsel, he is currently receiving 
wound care at Jones Regional Medical Center in Anamosa, Iowa, but neither 
representative could recall the name of claimant’s authorized provider.  (Hearing 
Testimony; See also Exhibit A; Petition).

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Under Iowa law, an employer who has accepted compensability for a workplace 
injury has a right to control the care provided to the injured employee.  Ramirez-Trujillo 
v. Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 N.W.2d 759, 769 (Iowa 2016).  The relevant statute provides 
as follows: 
For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish 
reasonable services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has the

Page 5 
 
right to choose the care. . . . The treatment must be offered promptly and 
be reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the 
employee. If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the care 
offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such dissatisfaction 
to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the employer and 
the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited to treat the 
injury. If the employer and employee cannot agree on such alternate care, 
the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable proofs of the 
necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
Iowa Code § 85.27(4).   
Defendant’s “obligation under the statute is confined to reasonable care for the 
diagnosis and treatment of work-related injuries.” Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 
N.W.2d 122, 124 (Iowa 1995) (emphasis in original).  In other words, the “obligation 
under the statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.”  (Id.). 
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  See Iowa Code § 85.27(4). By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment 
and seeking alternate care, claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care 
is unreasonable.  See Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e); Long, 528 N.W.2d at 124.  
Ultimately, determining whether care is reasonable under the statute is a question of 
fact.  Long, 528 N.W.2d at 123. 
Under Iowa Code section 85.27, claimant bears the burden of providing 
“reasonable proofs of the necessity” to order alternate care.  At the hearing, claimant’s 
counsel argued that having therapy in Clarence would be uncomfortable for the 
claimant.  (Hearing Testimony).  Claimant, however, was not present at the hearing to 
provide any evidence to support that assertion.  Claimant’s counsel also stated that 
claimant was already receiving wound care at Jones Regional Medical Center, and it 
would be a serious inconvenience for him to have to travel to another facility for therapy. 
(Id.).  Past decisions from the Iowa Supreme Court and this agency have generally 
found care located within a 50-mile radius of the claimant’s residence to be reasonable.  
See Bitner v. Cedar Falls Construction Co., File No. 5013852 (Alt. Care. September 24, 
2004); see also, Trade Professionals. Inc., v. Shriver, 661 N.W.2d 119, 124 (Iowa 2003) 
(more than 100 miles is an undue inconvenience); Schrock v. Corkery Waste Disposal, 
Inc., File No. 1133725 (Alt. Care June 26, 1996) (120-mile round trip excessive); 
Schulte v. Vocational Services of Area Residential Care, File No. 1134342 (Alt. Care 
September 6, 1996) (care more than 70 miles away unreasonable).  Claimant did not 
submit any evidence of the distance between his residence and Anamosa, where Jones 
Regional Medical Center is located.  Defendants’ Exhibit A, however, indicates the 
distance is comparable to driving from his home to Clarence.  (Ex. A, p. 1).  Claimant’s 
exhibit 4 indicates driving from his residence to Clarence, where the Millennial facility is

located, is a distance of approximately 24-31 miles. (Cl Ex. 4, p. 1).  He also submitted 
the distance between Clarence and Anamosa; that is a distance of 26-33 miles.  (Id. at 
2).  If these distances were added together, they could conceivably equal a distance of 
more than 50 miles from claimant’s residence, depending upon the route one chooses 
to take.  However, there is no evidence in the record that claimant is going to be 
required to attend wound care and physical therapy appointments in the same day, 
which could potentially require driving over 50 miles.  There is, in fact, no evidence at all 
in the record about claimant’s wound care appointments, i.e., their frequency, length 
and/or duration.  There is also no factual evidence in the record providing details for 
claimant’s proposed therapy.2  Given this deficient record, the undersigned cannot find 
claimant met his burden to prove the therapy in Clarence is unduly inconvenient.  While 
there is certainly the potential for it to become unreasonable, the record before the 
undersigned does not prove it is unreasonable.    
Claimant’s last argument is that defendants authorized Jones Regional Medical 
Center to provide care for his work injury, and they cannot transfer his care to a different 
facility without showing that the care provided at Jones Regional Medical Center is 
unreasonable or inappropriate.  Claimant does not provide supporting case law for this 
argument.  However, even if claimant had provided legal support for this contention, the 
hearing record is too sparse for the undersigned to entertain this argument.  At hearing, 
neither counsel could provide the name of claimant’s authorized medical provider at 
Jones Regional Medical Center.  Additionally, neither party introduced any medical 
notes into the hearing record.  In short, the undersigned has no evidence of what 
defendants have or have not already authorized, with the exception of the wound care 
mentioned in defendants’ June 26, 2024 email.  Given this record, I cannot determine 
whether defendants are inappropriately interfering in claimant’s care as he appears to 
be alleging.  There is no evidence in the record that claimant’s authorized provider 
ordered his therapy to take place at Jones Regional Medical Center, nor is there 
anything in the workers’ compensation statute that requires defendants to provide all 
ancillary treatment at the same facility where claimant’s causally related treatment 
began.  
Under the Long decision, defendants can take cost into consideration when 
choosing between providers.  See Long, 528 N.W.2d at 124.  That consideration does 
not make the therapy they are offering unreasonable.  Nor has claimant provided 
sufficient evidence that the therapy is unduly inconvenient under our existing case law.  
Under the record currently before the undersigned, claimant’s request for alternate 
medical care is denied.  
 
THEREFORE IT IS ORDERED: 
Claimant's petition for alternate medical care is denied.
