RULING ON PETITION FOR 
JUDICIAL REVIEW 
 
 
 This is a judicial review proceeding in which the petitioners seek review of an 
alternate medical care decision issued on February 19, 2025, which in part required the 
petitioners to file a denial of liability through the EDI (Electronic Data Interchange) 
system and send the respondent a letter stating the reasons for the denial.  The petitioners 
argue that this requirement in the order goes beyond the scope of Iowa Code §85.27 and 
876 IAC 4.48 and constituted an abuse of the agency’s discretion. 
 
The appropriate standard of review for this court is governed by Iowa Code 
§17A.19(10).   The parties agree that the deputy’s decision to order the filing of a denial 
letter implicates the application of law to fact.  As the application of law to fact is vested 
in the discretion of the agency, it is only to be reversed if found to be irrational, illogical 
or wholly unjustifiable.  Jacobson Transp. Co. v. Harris, 778 N.W.2d 192, 196 (Iowa 
2010); Iowa Code §17A.19(10)(m) (2023): 
A decision is irrational when it is not governed by or 
according to reason.  A decision is illogical when it is 
contrary to or devoid of logic.  A decision is unjustifiable 
when it has no foundation in fact or reason.

A brief summary of the factual background of this dispute would be as follows:  
The respondent sustained a work-related injury on June 28, 2024, in which she alleged 
she had injured both her left hip and low back.  The petitioners accepted responsibility for 
the left hip injury, but denied liability for the low back.  A dispute developed regarding 
the scheduling of treatment for the left hip, resulting in the respondent filing a petition for 
alternate medical care.  A hearing on that petition was held before the deputy on February 
18, 2025; part of the record made at the hearing included medical records from the 
treating orthopedist stating that the work-related incident “would have created enough 
force to aggravate this pre-existing condition and perhaps create now the existence of the 
radiculopathy that she is experiencing.”  D0006, Certified Record, Exh. 3 at 1 
(4/11/2025). 
 
The deputy rendered his alternate medical care decision the day following the 
hearing.  The deputy granted the petition as to the left hip condition, but dismissed that 
part of the petition dealing with the low back injury, as compensability for that injury was 
contested, citing R. R. Donnelly & Sons v. Barnett, 670 N.W.2d 190, 197-98 (Iowa 2003) 
and 876 IAC 4.48(7) (“Application cannot be filed under this rule if the liability of the 
employer is an issue. If the application is filed where the liability of the employer is an 
issue, the application will be dismissed without prejudice”).  However, the deputy also 
noted that “new information (presumably the aforementioned orthopedist’s report) has

been brought to light that may require additional investigation by defendants.  D0006 at

letter pursuant to 876 IAC 3.1(2) within ten days of the order.  D0006 at 14. 
 
A timely petition for judicial review was filed by the petitioners on February 28, 
2025.  D0001, Petition for Judicial Review (2/28/2025).  However, prior to filing the 
judicial review action, the petitioners complied with the deputy’s directive to send the 
respondent a second denial letter regarding the low back injury.  D0008, Petitioners’ 
Judicial Review Brief at 6 (5/28/2025).  The respondent argues that this action rendered 
the pending judicial review action moot.  For the reasons noted below, the court disagrees 
and will address the merits of the dispute. 
 
Historically, voluntary compliance with a ruling required the dismissal of any 
appeal from that ruling.  Northern Natural Gas Co. v. Knop, 524 N.W.2d 668, 670 (Iowa 
Ct.App. 1994) (citing Credit Indus. Co. v. Bendixen, 255 Iowa 1020-1021-22, 125 
N.W.2d 262, 262-63 (1963)).  However, that holding “is no longer as severely applied as 
it once was.”  Id. (citing Millsap v. Cedar Rapids Civil Serv. Comm’n, 249 N.W.2d 679, 
683 (Iowa 1977)).  The issue of appellate waiver is now viewed in the context of whether 
the performance is under some sort of compulsion, see Hegtvedt v. Prybil, 223 N.W.2d 
186, 188-89 (Iowa 1974) (receiver ordered by pay judgment debt) or done “to avoid 
unnecessary costs and expenses.” Peoples Trust & Sav. Bank v. Security Sav. Bank, 815 
N.W.2d 744, 754 (Iowa 2012) (payment of judgment debt “to avoid the hassles resulting 
from execution” did not cut off rights to pursue appeal).

In the present case, the petitioners argue that they complied with the deputy’s 
directive as “an exercise of good faith [and] to avoid additional litigation surrounding 
enforcement of the order during the pendency of the appeal process.”  D0012, 
Petitioners’ Judicial Review Reply Brief at 3 (7/3/2025).  While the notion of acting in 
“good faith” suggests that the petitioners were under no compulsion, financial or 
otherwise, to file the second denial letter, the court is willing to accept that the actions of 
the petitioners in complying with the deputy’s order is sufficient analogous to the 
situations outlined above to not constitute a waiver of their right to pursue judicial review 
of the deputy’s decision. 
 
On the merits of the dispute, it must be remembered that the underlying agency 
proceeding-a hearing on a petition for alternate medical care-is deemed to be a “fast-
track” hearing submitted on an expedited basis and wholly unavailable when 
compensability for the injury is disputed.  See West Central Cooperative v. Sullivan, 
2022 WL 3906810 *9 (Iowa Ct.App., Case No. 21-1559, filed August 31, 2022); R. R. 
Donnelly, 670 N.W.2d at 196 (“[T]he issue of compensability is totally removed from the 
alternate medical care process”).  As noted earlier, once compensability becomes an 
issue, that portion of the request for alternate medical care must be dismissed.  Id. at 197-
98.  As applied to this case, the deputy correctly dismissed that portion of the petition for 
alternate medical care pertaining to the disputed low back claim.  By the same token, the 
agency no longer could address the issue of compensability in the context of a request for 
alternate medical care; that would have to be addressed in the related contested case 
proceeding.  Sullivan, 2022 WL 3906810 at *9.  As a result, the deputy abused his

discretion by ordering the second denial letter—a request that is clearly outside the scope 
of the alternate medical care proceeding. 
 
The respondent counters this argument by urging that the deputy was only acting 
within his authority when the nature of the respondent’s low back claim had changed and 
was consistent with the petitioners’ ongoing duty of investigation.  See 
Bridgestone/Firestone and Old Republic Ins. Company v. Dalton, 2016 WL 742806 *3 
(Iowa Ct.App., Case No. 15-0571, filed February 24, 2016).  These issues are of course 
viable when dealing with the disputed issue of compensability in the contested case 
proceeding.  Id. (failure to initiate further investigation proper basis to assess penalty 
benefits).  They have no relevance in the context of an alternate medical care proceeding, 
however.  By directing further action in the context of that part of the injury claim that 
had already been dismissed, the deputy abused his discretion and that decision should be 
reversed. 
 
IT IS THEREFORE ORDERED that the portion of the deputy’s alternate 
medical care decision rendered on  February 19, 2025 dealing with the directive to the 
petitioners to issue a denial letter is reversed.  The remainder of that order remains in full 
force and effect.  This matter is remanded to the agency for further proceedings 
consistent with this ruling.  The costs associated with this proceeding are assessed to the 
respondent.
