compensation benefits against Erlbacher Bros., Inc., employer, and Service American 
Indemnity Company, insurer.  
This case was heard on October 28, 2024, and considered fully submitted on 
December 11, 2024, upon the simultaneous filing of briefs.  
 The record consists of Joint Exhibits 1–8, Claimant’s Exhibits 1–11, Defendants’ 
Exhibits A–E, and testimony of the claimant.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury actually occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 
employment. Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 150 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. 
Dial Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309, 311 (Iowa 1996). The words “arising out of” refer to the 
cause or source of the injury. The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 

WALTON V. ERLBACHER BROS., INC. 
Page 11 
 
circumstances of the injury. 2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124, 128 (Iowa 
1995).  An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists 
between the injury and the employment. Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. The injury must 
be a rational consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely 
incidental to the employment. Koehler Elec. v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1, 3 (Iowa 2000); 
Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it 
happens within a period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may 
be when performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties 
or doing an activity incidental to them. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d at 150. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 
621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 
(Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
The parties agree Claimant sustained a whole body injury arising out of a work 
incident on December 11, 2019. The primary dispute is the extent of such injury. 
Claimant asserts he is permanently and totally disabled either under the standard 
disability constructs or under the odd-lot doctrine. Conversely, Defendants maintain 
Claimant can return to employment in areas similar to ones in which he performed 
before such as driving, dispatch and customer service.

opined that based on Claimant’s need to alternatively sit and stand there are no jobs 
available to him unless he was provided significant accommodations by an employer. 
Dr. Patra believed that Claimant was unemployable due to his mental health.  
Defendants argue that Claimant has provided no proof that he is unable to pass 
a DOT licensure. He has not attempted to pass nor has he sought out a physician to do

The next question is the extent of Defendants’ credit. Benefits due to an 
employee under a permanent and total disability begin on the date of the injury and any 
overpayment shall be credited against the liability of the employer for any future weekly 
benefits due pursuant to subsection 2, for any current or subsequent injury to the same 
employee. Iowa Code § 85.34; Hanson v. Fortune Brands Home & Security, File No. 
21012709.01 (Arb. May 31, 2024) (citing Iowa Code § 85.34(3)(a)). 
Defendants assert that they have overpaid Claimant 51.311 weeks in both 
temporary partial and temporary total disability benefits. (See Defendants’ Brief) 
However, due to the finding that Claimant is permanently and totally disabled, the issue 
of credits applied to future benefits for this injury is not applicable. Claimant seeks a 
penalty benefit. If weekly compensation benefits are not fully paid when due, section 
10A.315(4) requires that additional benefits be awarded unless the employer shows 
reasonable cause or excuse for the delay or denial.  Iowa Code § 10A.315(4) (2023); 
Robbennolt v. Snap-on Tools Corp., 555 N.W.2d 229, 236-37 (Iowa 1996).  
“Benefits may be delayed, without penalty, during such time as an employer’s 
liability is “fairly debatable”. Kiesecker v. Webster City Custom Meats, Inc., 528 N.W.2d 
109, 111 (Iowa 1995).  A delay attributable to the time required to perform a reasonable 
investigation is not unreasonable.  Id.  
It also is not unreasonable to deny a claim when a good faith issue of law or fact 
makes the employer’s liability fairly debatable.  Rodda v. Vermeer Mfg., 734 N.W.2d 
480, 483 (Iowa 2007).  An issue of law is fairly debatable if viable arguments exist in 
favor of each party.  Covia v. Robinson, 507 N.W.2d 411, 412 (Iowa 1993).  An issue of 
fact is fairly debatable if substantial evidence exists which would support a finding 
favorable to the employer.  Gilbert v. USF Holland, Inc., 637 N.W.2d 194, 199 (Iowa 
2001).  
“An employer’s bare assertion that a claim is ‘fairly debatable’ does not make it 
so.”  Meyers v. Holiday Express Corp., 557 N.W.2d 502, 505 (Iowa 1996).  The 
employer must assert facts upon which the commissioner could reasonably find that the 
claim was “fairly debatable.”  Id.   
If the employer fails to show reasonable cause or excuse for the delay or denial, 
the commissioner shall impose a penalty in an amount up to 50 percent of the amount 
unreasonably delayed or denied.  Christensen v. Snap-on Tools Corp., 554 N.W.2d 
254, 259 (Iowa 1996).  The factors to be considered in determining the amount of the 
penalty include the length of the delay, the number of delays, the information available 
to the employer and the employer’s past record of penalties.  Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d 
at 238.

Defendants’ brief, they argued that there were likely mailing issues that accounted for 
the delays, although no such issues were presented at hearing. While the delays were
