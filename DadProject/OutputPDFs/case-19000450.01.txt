STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Dirk Markle, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers' 
compensation benefits from Christian Retirement Services, Inc. (Christian), employer, 
and West Bend Mutual Insurance, insurer, both as defendants.  This matter was heard 
on August 3, 2023, with a final submission date of September 22, 2023. 
The record in this case consists of Joint Exhibits 1 through 9, Claimant’s Exhibits 
1 through 7, Defendants’ Exhibits A through Q, and the testimony of claimant.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
The first issue to be determined is whether claimant’s injury resulted in a 
permanent disability.  The parties agree that claimant sustained an injury arising out of 
and in the course of employment on July 8, 2019, when he bent over to paint.  Claimant 
alleges the injury resulted in a permanent disability and the need for bilateral total knee 
replacements, surgery to his upper extremities and depression.  Defendants contend 
claimant’s injury was temporary in nature.   
 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based. A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause. A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible. George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996). 
 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability. 
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); 
Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995). Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted expert medical 
testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 
N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 1994).

Page 8 
 
 “Medical causation presents a question of fact.” Cedar Rapids Cmty. Sch. Dist. 
v. Pease, 807 N.W.2d 839, 844 (Iowa 2011). “A cause is proximate if it is a substantial 
factor in bringing about the result.  It only needs to be one cause; it does not have to be 
the only cause.”  Blacksmith v. All-American, Inc., 290 N.W.2d 348, 354 (Iowa 1980) 
(internal citations omitted).   
“[W]here an accident occurs to an employee in the usual course of [the 
employee’s] employment, the employer is liable for all consequences that naturally and 
proximately flow from the accident.”  Oldham v. Scofield & Welch, 222 Iowa 764, 266 
N.W. 480, 482 (1936).  
While a claimant is not entitled to compensation for the results of a preexisting  
injury or disease, its mere existence at the time of a subsequent injury is not a 
defense.  Rose v. John Deere Ottumwa Works, 247 Iowa 900, 76 N.W.2d 756 (1956).  If 
the claimant had a preexisting condition or disability that is materially aggravated,  
accelerated, worsened or lighted up so that it results in disability, claimant is entitled to  
recover.  Nicks v. Davenport Produce Co., 254 Iowa 130, 115 N.W.2d 812 (1962);  
Yeager v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co., 253 Iowa 369, 112 N.W.2d 299 (1961).  A 
material aggravation, worsening, lighting up or acceleration of any prior condition is a 
compensable event.  Ziegler v. United States Gypsum Co., 252 Iowa 613; 106 N.W.2d 
591 (1961).  A claimant must show that the injury proximately caused the medical 
condition sought to be compensable.   A cause is “proximate” when it is a substantial 
factor in bringing about that condition.  Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 
(Iowa 1994); Blacksmith v. All-American, Inc., 290 N.W.2d 348 (Iowa 1980).

right knee was a sequela injury leading to a TKR on the right.  He further contends that 
his upper extremity problems are sequela for his right knee injury.  Finally, he contends 
that his depression is also caused by his numerous injuries.

Claimant was counseled in December of 2017 to have bilateral total knee 
replacements.  Notes from Dr. Noiseux’s office indicate that claimant did not believe his

replacements was not caused or materially aggravated by the July 2019 incident of 
bending.  The causation opinions of Dr. Whited and Dr. Segal are found not convincing.   
Given this record, claimant has failed to carry his burden of proof his July 8, 
2019, injury was a substantial factor in bringing about the need for bilateral total knee 
replacements resulting in a permanent disability.  Blacksmith, 290 N.W.2d 354. 
As claimant has failed to carry his burden of proof his injury resulted in a 
permanent disability and the need for bilateral total knee replacement, all other issues 
are found to be moot.
