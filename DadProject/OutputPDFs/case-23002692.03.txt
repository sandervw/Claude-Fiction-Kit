STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Keith Rath (“Rath”), claimant, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from LRS of Iowa, LLC a/k/a LRS Recycling (“LRS”), employer, 
American Zurich Insurance Company, insurance carrier, and the Second Injury Fund of 
Iowa (the Fund) as defendants.  Hearing was held via Zoom videoconference on 
December 3, 2024.     
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.  
Keith Rath was the only witness to testify live at trial.  The evidentiary record also 
includes joint exhibits 1-9, claimant’s exhibits 1, pages 1-25, 29-30 and exhibits 2-7, 
defendant-employer exhibits A-G, and Fund exhibits AA-HH.   
The Fund objected to claimant’s exhibit 1, page 26 through 28, a November 12, 
2024 supplemental report from Dr. Taylor as untimely.  (Transcript pages 14-17) 
Claimant did not dispute that the report was served after the 30-day exchange deadline.

has specifically held that sanctions for violation of any agency rule or order is a 
discretionary decision made by the agency and that the courts will defer to that 
discretion unless it is arbitrary or capricious.  Hagen v. Serta/National Bedding Co.,

will be excluded if the objecting party shows that receipt of the evidence would be 
unfairly prejudicial.  876 IAC 4.19(3)(e) I found that the Fund demonstrated that 
admission of the untimely supplemental report from Dr. Taylor would be unfairly 
prejudicial to the Fund and therefore sustained the Fund’s objection.  Claimant’s exhibit 
1, pages 26 to 28 were excluded from evidence.

ISSUES 
 
The parties submitted the following issues for resolution: 
1. Whether claimant sustained an injury that arose out of and in the course of 
his employment on November 22, 2022 to his right arm and/or cervical 
spine/neck. 
2. The nature and extent of permanent disability claimant is entitled to receive 
from the defendant-employer. 
3. Whether penalty benefits are appropriate for unreasonable delay of weekly 
benefits. 
4. Whether defendants are responsible for medical benefits pursuant to Iowa 
Code section 85.27. 
5. Whether claimant is entitled to reimbursement for an IME under Iowa Code 
section 85.39. 
6. Whether the liability of the Second Injury Fund of Iowa was triggered?  If so, 
claimant’s entitlement to benefits. 
7. Assessment of costs.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned, having considered all the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
 
 Claimant, Keith Rath, sustained an injury to his right shoulder which arose out of 
and in the course of his employment with LRS of Iowa, LLC on November 22, 2022.  
(Hearing Report, numbered paragraph 2) Rath also contends that as the result of the 
work injury he sustained an injury to his right arm and neck; the right arm and neck 
claims are disputed.  (Id.)  For purposes of Rath’s claim against the Fund, the parties 
agreed he sustained an injury to his right leg on April 28, 2017.  (Hearing Report, 
numbered paragraph 10(a)) 
 
At the time of the hearing Rath was 64 years old and resided in Cedar Rapids, 
Iowa.  (Tr. p. 38) Rath began working at Johnson County Refuse as a garbage truck 
driver in March 2015.  (Tr. p. 39) LRS purchased Johnson County Refuse on December 
1, 2021; Rath’s job and duties remained the same.  (Tr. pp. 39-40)  
 
On November 22, 2022 Rath was operating a rear-loading garbage truck 
dumping commercial debris.  (Tr. p. 44) His duties included driving the garbage truck to 
various sites and performing heavy lifting.  (Tr. p. 42) Rath felt a pop in his right 
shoulder and felt immediate pain while he was loading a large piece of countertop by 
hand into the back of the garbage truck.  His shoulder was painful for the rest of his day 
causing him to struggle to steer the truck or lift his right arm.  (Tr. p. 45) He did not feel 
any pain in his left neck area when he felt the pop in his shoulder.  However, later that 
night he did have pain in the left side, the trapezial area up into the neck.  (Tr. pp. 45-
46) He reported his injury to his employer the next day.  (Tr. p. 46) 
 
There was an approximately three-week delay before Rath was sent for medical 
treatment.  (Tr. p. 46) On December 15, 2022, Rath saw Patrick G. Hartley, M.D. at 
University of Iowa Occupational Health.  (JE5, pp. 74-84) Rath reported that on 
November 22, 2022 he was loading countertops into the rear of the garbage truck.  As 
he was lifting the countertop he felt a pop in his right anterior shoulder.  He had 
progressive pain, aggravated by certain motions.  The only thing that alleviates his 
discomfort is not performing activities with his right arm.  He rated his pain as 9/10.  He 
has some radiation of shoulder pain into his right upper extremity.  He also reported 
some discomfort along upper border of trapezius bilaterally, greater on the right.  
Turning his head to the right increased his posterior neck, trapezius/parascapular pain.  
(JE5, p. 74) Dr. Hartley’s assessment was acute pain of the right shoulder and 
impingement syndrome of the right shoulder.  He recommended Tylenol, ice, MRI of the 
right shoulder, and a referral to physical therapy.  Dr. Hartley restricted Rath to no reach 
or lift above shoulder with right arm, limited reach away from the trunk with right arm, 
lift/push/pull carry up to 10 pounds with right arm close to his trunk, and lift/push/pull up 
to 5 pounds occasionally with right arm away from his trunk.  (JE5, pp. 77-78)

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 

RATH V. LRS OF IOWA, LLC A/K/A LRS RECYCLING/SECOND INJURY FUND OF 
IOWA 
Page 19 
 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony.  The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question.  The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances.  The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001);

Claimant alleges entitlement to penalty benefits due to late payment of four

employer proves a reasonable cause or excuse.  [Iowa Code § 86.13 
(2015).]  A reasonable cause or excuse exists if either (1) the delay was 
necessary for the insurer to investigate the claim or (2) the employer had a 
reasonable basis to contest the employee’s entitlement to benefits.  A 
“reasonable basis” for denial of the claim exists if the claim is “fairly 
debatable.”  Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 260.

(1) If the employer has a reason for the delay and conveys that reason to 
the employee contemporaneously with the beginning of the delay, no 
penalty will be imposed if the reason is of such character that a reasonable
