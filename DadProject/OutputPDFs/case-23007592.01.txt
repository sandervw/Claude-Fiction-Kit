STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Tomas Transito, claimant, filed a petition for arbitration against Lennox 
Industries, Inc., as the employer, and Indemnity Insurance Company of North America, 
as the insurance carrier.  This case came before the undersigned for an arbitration 
hearing on June 18, 2024.  Pursuant to an order from the Iowa Workers’ Compensation 
Commissioner, this case was heard via a Zoom videoconference.   
The parties filed a hearing report prior to the commencement of the hearing.  On 
the hearing report, the parties entered into numerous stipulations.  Those stipulations 
were accepted and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be 
made or discussed.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations. 
The evidentiary record includes Joint Exhibits 1 through 7, Claimant’s Exhibits 1 
through 6, and Defendants’ Exhibits A through B.  Claimant testified on his own behalf.  
No other witnesses testified live at the hearing.  The evidentiary record closed at the 
conclusion of the arbitration hearing and the case was considered fully submitted at that 
time.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned, having considered all of the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
Tomas Transito, claimant, sustained a stipulated right knee injury as a result of 
his work activities at Lennox Industries, Inc., on January 25, 2023.  Claimant asserts 
that he sustained both temporary and permanent disability as a result of the injury.  
Defendants dispute whether the injury caused either temporary or permanent disability. 
Claimant’s medical records demonstrate that he slipped on a mat on the floor at 
the Lennox plant on January 25, 2023.  Mr. Transito did not report the injury 
immediately but developed symptoms over the course of the next week and ultimately 
reported the injury to the employer. 
Lennox sent claimant to an occupational medicine physician, Sherman Jew, 
D.O., who evaluated claimant on February 14, 2023.  Dr. Jew diagnosed claimant with 
right knee pain and declared claimant’s right knee condition to be the result of a work-
related injury.  Dr. Jew initiated conservative care and placed claimant on restricted, 
light duty work.  He ultimately ordered an MRI of claimant’s right knee and referred 
claimant to an orthopaedic surgeon, Steven A. Aviles, M.D. 
Dr. Aviles evaluated Mr. Transito on April 4, 2023.  Dr. Aviles diagnosed claimant 
with severe right knee osteoarthritis with a severely macerated medial meniscus with 
loose bodies in the right knee as well.  (Joint Exhibit 5, page 35)  Dr. Aviles 
recommended against an arthroscopic surgical procedure, however.  (Joint Ex. 5, pp. 
35-37)  He opined that there was a low percentage chance that an arthroscopic surgery 
would provide meaningful benefit to claimant’s right knee.  (Joint Ex. 5, p. 37)  Dr. Aviles 
adamantly opined that claimant’s right knee osteoarthritis is not related to the January 
25, 2023 work injury.  (Joint Ex. 5, p. 39)  He also opined that Mr. Transito achieved 
maximum medical improvement after his steroid injection on April 3, 2023.  (Joint Ex. 5, 
p. 40) 
After Dr. Aviles opined that claimant’s right knee condition was not related to his 
work injury, defendants denied further liability.  (Joint Ex. 5, p. 39)  Mr. Transito sought

Mr. Transito also obtained an independent medical evaluation prior to hearing.  
Mark Kirkland, D.O., evaluated claimant on April 3, 2024.  (Claimant’s Exhibit 1)  Dr. 
Kirkland documented a history in which claimant reported he landed on his right knee at 
work on January 25, 2023.  He documented that claimant had no prior pain or injuries in 
his right knee before January 25, 2023.  (Claimant’s Ex. 1, p. 4) 
Dr. Kirkland’s evaluation noted that claimant was able to do deep knee bends 
and squat but performed those maneuvers with pain in the right knee.  Ultimately, Dr. 
Kirkland diagnosed claimant with osteoarthritis of the right knee as well as a medial 
meniscus tear with surgical intervention via a medial meniscectomy.  (Claimant’s Ex. 1, 
p. 4)  Dr. Kirkland opined, “Mr. Transito fell at work, January 25, 2023, landing on his 
right knee which resulted in a right knee medial meniscal tear.”  (Claimant’s Ex. 1, p. 4) 
Dr. Kirkland specifically opined that the January 25, 2023 incident at work “was a 
substantial factor to either cause or aggravate Mr. Transito’s right knee condition.”  
(Claimant’s Ex. 1, p. 4)  It is not apparent or clear from Dr. Kirkland’s report whether he 
considered the osteoarthritis diagnosis to be caused or materially aggravated by the 
January 25, 2023 incident at work.  Dr. Kirkland only specifically references the 
meniscal injury as being caused or aggravated by the work incident.   
Ultimately, I find the opinions of Dr. Kirkland to be most convincing on the 
causation issue.  There is no evidence in this record that Mr. Transito had a prior right 
knee injury or prior right knee pain before the January 25, 2023 incident.  After that 
incident, Mr. Transito reported an injury and ultimately submitted to an arthroscopic 
surgical procedure.  Dr. Kirkland’s causation opinion is consistent with this history and 
convincing.  I find that Mr. Transito proved his meniscus injury and resulting treatment, 
including surgery, are causally related to the January 25, 2023 work incident.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Defendants stipulated that claimant sustained a work-related injury of the right 
leg as a result of his work activities on January 25, 2023.  (Hearing Report)  However, 
defendants disputed whether the work injury caused either temporary or permanent 
disability. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa App. 1997); 
Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa App. 1996). 
A possibility of causation is not sufficient; a probability is necessary. Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996) (citing Holmes v. Bruce 
Motor Freight, Inc., 215 N.W.2d 296, 297 (Iowa 1974)). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 

TRANSITO V. LENNOX INDUSTRIES, INC. 
Page 5 
 
 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 
621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 
(Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa App. 1994). 
While a claimant is not entitled to compensation for the results of a preexisting 
injury or disease, its mere existence at the time of a subsequent injury is not a defense.  
Rose v. John Deere Ottumwa Works, 247 Iowa 900, 908, 76 N.W.2d 756, 760-61 
(1956).  If the claimant had a preexisting condition or disability that is materially 
aggravated, accelerated, worsened or lighted up so that it results in disability, claimant 
is entitled to recover.  Nicks v. Davenport Produce Co., 254 Iowa 130, 135, 115 N.W.2d 
812, 815 (Iowa 1962); Yeager v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co., 253 Iowa 369,374-75,  
112 N.W.2d 299, 302 (1961). 
In this case, I accepted the medical opinion of Dr. Kirkland as most convincing 
and credible.  Therefore, I also found that claimant proved he sustained both a 
temporary and permanent disability as a result of the January 25, 2023 work injury.  I 
conclude claimant is entitled to an award of healing period benefits as well as 
permanent partial disability benefits. 
Iowa Code section 85.34(1) provides that healing period benefits are payable to 
an injured employee who has suffered permanent partial disability until (1) the employee 
has returned to work; (2) it is medically indicated that significant improvement from the 
injury is not anticipated; or (3) the employee is medically capable of returning to 
employment substantially similar to the employment in which the employee was 
engaged at the time of injury, whichever occurs first.  The healing period can be 
considered the period during which there is a reasonable expectation of improvement of 
the disabling condition.  See Armstrong Tire & Rubber Co. v. Kubli, 312 N.W.2d 60, 65 
(Iowa App. 1981).  Healing period benefits can be interrupted or intermittent.  Teel v. 
McCord, 394 N.W.2d 405, 406 (Iowa 1986). 
In this instance, I accepted the opinion of Dr. Delaney and found that claimant 
proved he was medically taken off work beginning on June 5, 2023.  Claimant remained 
off work through November 19, 2023 and was released by Dr. Hamilton to return to 
work without restrictions on November 20, 2023.  I conclude claimant proved 
entitlement to healing period benefits from June 5, 2023 through November 19, 2023.  
Iowa Code section 85.34(1). 
The next issue to consider is the claim for permanent partial disability.  Under the 
Iowa Workers' Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is compensated either for 
a loss or loss of use of a scheduled member under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(a)-(u) or 
for loss of earning capacity under section 85.34(2)(v).  The extent of scheduled member 

TRANSITO V. LENNOX INDUSTRIES, INC. 
Page 6 
 
 
disability benefits to which an injured worker is entitled is determined by using the 
functional method.  Functional disability is "limited to the loss of the physiological 
capacity of the body or body part.”  Mortimer v. Fruehauf Corp., 502 N.W.2d 12, 15 
(Iowa 1993); Sherman v. Pella Corp., 576 N.W.2d 312 (Iowa 1998). 
The parties stipulated that claimant’s injury is limited to a right leg scheduled 
member injury.  (Hearing Report)  Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(p) provides that an injury 
to the leg should be compensated on a scheduled member basis using a 220-week 
schedule.   
Having accepted Dr. Kirkland’s opinion and found that claimant proved he 
sustained a two percent permanent functional impairment of the right leg as a result of 
the January 25, 2023 work injury, I conclude claimant proved the injury caused 
permanent disability to claimant’s right leg.   
Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(x) provides: 
In all cases of permanent partial disability described in paragraphs “a” 
through “u,” or paragraph “v” when determining functional disability and 
not loss of earning capacity, the extent of loss or percentage of permanent 
impairment shall be determined solely by utilizing the guides to the 
evaluation of permanent impairment, published by the American medical 
association, as adopted by the workers’ compensation commissioner by 
rule pursuant to chapter 17A.  Lay testimony or agency expertise shall not 
be utilized in determining loss or percentage of permanent impairment 
pursuant to paragraphs “a” through “u,” or paragraph “v” when determining 
functional disability and not loss of earning capacity. 
This agency complied with the requirement in Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(x) by 
enacting agency rule 876 IAC 2.4.  Rule 2.4 adopts the fifth edition of the AMA Guides 
for purposes of determining permanent functional disability of scheduled member 
injuries. 
Having accepted Dr. Kirkland’s opinion as the most credible and convincing in 
this evidentiary record, I conclude claimant proved entitlement to permanent partial 
disability benefits equivalent to two percent of the right leg pursuant to the AMA Guides 
to the Evaluation of Permanent Impairment, Fifth Edition.  As noted above, a leg injury 
is compensated using a 220-week schedule.  Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(p).  Two 
percent of 220 weeks totals 4.4 weeks.  Therefore, I conclude claimant proved 
entitlement to 4.4 weeks of permanent partial disability benefits.  Iowa Code section 
85.34(2)(p), (w). 
Permanent partial disability benefits “begin when it is medically indicated that 
maximum medical improvement from the injury has been reached and that the extent of 
loss or percentage of permanent impairment can be determined by use of the guides to 
the evaluation of permanent impairment.”  Iowa Code section 85.34(2).  In this case, I 
accepted the opinion of Dr. Kirkland that claimant achieved maximum medical

improvement on December 5, 2023.  Accordingly, I conclude that permanent partial 
disability benefits should commence in this case on December 5, 2023.  Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2). 
Mr. Transito also submitted a claim for past medical expenses contained in 
Claimant’s Exhibit 5.   
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance, and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Section 85.27.  Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening October 1975). 
In the hearing report, defendants stipulated that the medical expenses submitted 
are causally connected to the medical condition upon which this claim of injury is based.  
Having accepted that stipulation and having concluded that claimant proved the work 
injury caused the need for additional medical treatment, including the arthroscopic 
surgery on his right knee, I conclude claimant proved entitlement to payment, or 
reimbursement, of the submitted medical expenses in Claimant’s Exhibit 5. 
The final disputed issue is whether costs should be assessed against either 
party.  Costs are assessed at the discretion of the agency.  Iowa Code section 10A.328.  
I concluded that claimant proved the January 25, 2023 right knee injury caused both 
temporary and permanent disability.  Therefore, it is appropriate to assess claimant’s 
costs in some amount. 
Mr. Transito seeks assessment of his filing fee ($128.75) as a cost.  I conclude it 
is reasonable and permissible to assess the filing fee as a cost in this case.  876 IAC 
4.33(7).   
ORDER 
THEREFORE, IT IS ORDERED: 
The employer and insurance carrier shall pay claimant healing period benefits 
from June 5, 2023 through November 19, 2023. 
The employer and insurance carrier shall pay claimant four point four (4.4) weeks 
of permanent partial disability benefits commencing on December 5, 2023. 
All weekly benefits shall be paid at the stipulated rate of seven hundred thirty-two 
and 94/100 dollars ($732.94) per week. 
The employer and insurance carrier shall be entitled to credit for all benefits paid 
to date, as stipulated to in the hearing report.
