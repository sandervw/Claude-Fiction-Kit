STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A.  The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Ruth Thomas.  
Claimant appeared personally and through Attorney Christopher Spaulding.  Defendant 
appeared through Attorney Jessica Voelker. 
 
The alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on October 23, 2025.  The 
proceedings were digitally recorded through Google Meet.  That recording constitutes 
the official record of this proceeding.  Pursuant to the Commissioner’s Order, the 
undersigned has been delegated authority to issue a final agency decision in this 
alternate medical care proceeding.  Therefore, this ruling is designated final agency 
action and any appeal of the decision would be to the Iowa District Court pursuant to 
Iowa Code section 17A. 
 
The record consists of claimant’s exhibit 1 and defense exhibits A through C, 
which were filed prior to the hearing.  After the hearing, via email, counsel for both 
parties agreed that these exhibits are appropriately part of the agency record.   
 
The defendant does not dispute liability for claimant’s left knee condition which 
resulted from her work injury.  The claimant did participate in the hearing as well and 
provided some information.  She was not sworn in for the hearing; thus, her statements 
are not considered testimony.  I specifically find it was unnecessary to take sworn 
testimony from the claimant because prior to the hearing, the defendant agreed to 
provide future transportation expenses for evaluation and referral by her authorized 
treating physician, regardless of the legal issue presented.

ISSUES 
 
There are two issues presented for determination. 
 
The first issue is whether transportation expenses are an appropriate subject of 
an alternate medical care claim. 
 
The second issue presented for resolution is whether the claimant is entitled to 
future transportation expenses for her medical appointments. 
 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
The claimant, Ruth Thomas, sustained an injury which arose out of and in the 
course of her employment on November 28, 2022.  This condition has impacted her left 
knee and has necessitated ongoing treatment. 
 
Ms. Thomas relocated to Illinois in approximately September 2025, and has 
sought treatment through Christian Community Health.  She has been referred to an 
orthopedist there named Dr. Sarah Wallace.  The defendant has indicated that it will 
accept Dr. Wallace as an authorized treatment provider.  In her statements on the 
record, it is clear that Ms. Thomas is not entirely satisfied with this for a variety of 
reasons.  That issue is not what is in dispute at this hearing. 
 
Ms. Thomas has been frustrated with her treatment.  Since moving to Illinois, she 
has had difficulty making it to her medical appointments, some authorized, some not.  
She does not have a working vehicle so she uses rideshare (e.g., Uber or Lyft) for 
transportation.  She clearly feels as though she has been required to jump through an 
unreasonable number of hoops to secure care.  In any event, she stated she has had 
past authorized appointments where the defendant has not reimbursed her for the 
necessary ride to the appointment.  The defendant stated to the agency that it is willing 
to provide such transportation in the future to her authorized physician.  The difficulty at 
this hearing is that the parties had not agreed upon exactly who that was.  At the 
hearing, defense counsel designated Dr. Wallace as the authorized physician and 
agreed to provide transportation for these visits.  Ms. Thomas was also receiving 
physical therapy from a provider she referred to as ACI, which Ms. Thomas stated is 
very close to her home. 
 
REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Iowa Code Section 85.27 (2013).

By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See 
Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  Determining what care is 
reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.  Id.  The employer’s obligation turns 
on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.  Id.; Harned v. Farmland 
Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983).   
 
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995). 
 
An employer’s statutory right is to select the providers of care and the employer 
may consider cost and other pertinent factors when exercising its choice. Long, at 124. 
An employer (typically) is not a licensed health care provider and does not possess 
medical expertise. Accordingly, an employer does not have the right to control the 
methods the providers choose to evaluate, diagnose and treat the injured employee. An 
employer is not entitled to control a licensed health care provider’s exercise of 
professional judgment. Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 866389 (Declaratory 
Ruling, May 19, 1988). An employer’s failure to follow recommendations of an 
authorized physician in matters of treatment is commonly a failure to provide reasonable 
treatment. Boggs v. Cargill, Inc., File No. 1050396 (Alt. Care, January 31, 1994). 
 
The first issue is whether future transportation expenses is a valid subject of an 
alternate medical care petition.  Defendant cited the case Morris v. Knight 
Transportation, File No. 5027965 (Alt Care May 27, 2009).  Having reviewed this case, I 
find that transportation expenses are an appropriate subject of an alternate care 
proceeding. 
 
The second issue is whether Ms. Thomas is entitled to transportation expenses 
for future care with an authorized treatment provider.  This issue is not in dispute.  She 
is.  The defendant agrees she is. 
 
It is my sincere hope that Ms. Thomas is able to use this order to follow through 
with any medical treatment for her left knee she may need.  I can certainly understand 
her frustration.  Under Iowa law, the employer has a right to direct her medical care.  
This is the law.  This means that the employer has an obligation to select a physician to 
direct her medical treatment.  Therefore, the employer has to know who that medical 
provider is.  In this case, the employer has agreed that Dr. Wallace is the most 
appropriate treatment provider to serve as a gatekeeper.  Ms. Thomas should be able to 
get an appointment with Dr. Wallace and then receive any and all reasonable and 
necessary medical care that Dr. Wallace recommends in the future.  For this to work, 
Ms. Thomas will need to communicate quickly with her attorney and the defendant

ORDER 
 
THEREFORE IT IS ORDERED: 
 
The claimant's petition for alternate medical care is GRANTED at this time.  The 
defendant shall authorize and pay for transportation, including rideshare expenses, for
