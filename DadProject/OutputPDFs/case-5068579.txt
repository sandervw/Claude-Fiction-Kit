STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Dawn Persinger, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from defendants M.A. Mortenson, Company, as employer, and 
Arch Insurance Group, as insurer. The hearing occurred before the undersigned on 
April 21, 2020. 
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the hearing. In the 
hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations. All of those stipulations 
were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration decision, and no factual 
or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised or discussed in this 
decision. The parties are bound by their stipulations. 
The evidentiary record consists of: Joint Exhibits 1 through 9 and Claimant’s 
Exhibits 10 through 18.  Defendants did not submit any non-medical exhibits. Claimant 
testified on his own behalf, and no other witnesses were called to testify. The 
evidentiary record closed on April 21, 2020, and the case was considered fully 
submitted upon submission of post-hearing briefs on May 21, 2020. 
ISSUES 
The parties submitted the following disputed issues for resolution: 
1. Whether the stipulated injury caused permanent disability; 
2. Whether the claimant’s stipulated October 25, 2017, work injury should be 
compensated with permanent disability benefits as a scheduled member 
injury to the right shoulder, or as an unscheduled injury; 
3. The extent of claimant’s entitlement to permanent disability, if any.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned, having considered all of the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
Dawn Persinger is a 57-year-old individual, who lives in Fremont, Nebraska. 
(Hearing Transcript, page 10)  She attended school in Adams, Minnesota through the 
10th grade. (Hr. Tr., p. 11)  She would go on to obtain her High School Equivalency 
Diploma at 18 years of age. (Id.)   
Ms. Persinger began working as a general laborer for the defendant employer in 
October 2017. (Hr. Tr., p. 23)  On October 25, 2017, Ms. Persinger sustained an injury 
as she was cleaning the cell, or motor, of a wind turbine.  At the time of the injury, 
claimant was laying on her back and reaching with her left hand to clean as much of the 
cell as she could. (Hr. Tr., pp. 24-26)  Claimant testified she felt a sharp pain in her left 
shoulder.  The employer accepted the injury and authorized medical treatment.   
When a conservative course of treatment, including medication management and 
physical therapy, failed to alleviate claimant’s symptoms, claimant was referred to David 
Buck, M.D., an orthopaedic surgeon. (Joint Exhibit 1, page 2)  Dr. Buck diagnosed 
claimant with “left shoulder pain with questionable SLAP tear and biceps tendinitis, 
impingement syndrome.” (JE1, p. 1)  Dr. Buck would eventually recommended surgical 
intervention. (JE1, page 2)  The operative notes indicate that Dr. Buck performed a left 
shoulder arthroscopy with arthroscopic labral repair, including capsulorrhaphy; 
extensive debridement of an articular-sided cuff tear, with takedown of bursitis and 
adhesions; and a subacromial decompression and bursectomy. (JE2, p. 9) 
Fortunately, the surgical intervention was successful at reducing Ms. Persinger’s 
symptoms.  By her July 9, 2018, follow-up appointment, claimant was experiencing only 
mild discomfort, and mild end range stiffness. (See Ex. 11, p. 44)  She reported to Dr. 
Buck that she was back to working around the house, which included weed-eating and 
mowing the lawn. (See Ex. 11, p. 44)  Following his July 9, 2018, examination, Dr. Buck 
released claimant to full duty work, and provided relatively broad restrictions, including, 
“common sense with activities” and “do not overdo heavy repetitive overhead activities.” 
(See Ex. 11, p. 44)   
After claimant’s physical therapist expressed concern with claimant’s ability to 
return to full duty work, Dr. Buck issued a new work note on July 13, 2018, limiting 
claimant’s overhead lifting ability to 30-pounds and recommending a work hardening 
program. (See JE8, p. 35)   
Claimant attended a Functional Capacity Evaluation (FCE) on November 19, 
2018. (JE7, p. 27)  According to Neal Wachholtz, D.P.T., Ms. Persinger demonstrated 
the ability to perform work activities within the medium physical demand level.  More 
specifically, Ms. Persinger demonstrated the ability to lift and carry 50 pounds on an 
occasional basis, and 30 pounds on a frequent basis when performed near waist level 
and below. (Id.)  Mr. Wachholtz recommended that claimant limit the lifting of objects to

shoulder level to 30 pounds on an occasional basis, and 15 pounds on a frequent basis. 
He further recommended she limit overhead lifting to 15 pounds on an occasional basis,

Dr. Buck adopted the permanent restrictions outlined by the FCE report and

Mortenson was unable to return claimant to work after Dr. Buck adopted the 
findings of the November 2018 FCE report as claimant’s permanent restrictions. (Ex.13, 
pp. 54-55)  
Claimant sought an independent medical examination, performed by Sunil 
Bansal, M.D., on January 24, 2020. (Ex. 11, p. 41)  Dr. Bansal assigned a 9 percent 
impairment rating to the left upper extremity for loss of range of motion, which is 
equivalent to 5 percent of the whole person. (Ex. 11, pp. 48-49)  He agreed with the 
permanent restrictions assigned by the November 2018 FCE report, and added 
claimant should not perform frequent forward reaching with the left arm. (Ex. 11, p. 49) 
Dr. Bansal was also asked to address whether claimant’s injuries involved 
anatomical structures proximal to the glenohumeral joint. (Ex. 10, pp. 38-40)  Dr. Bansal 
opined that from an anatomical standpoint, the rotator cuff tears claimant sustained 
were located proximal to the glenohumeral joint. (Ex. 11, pp. 48, 51-52)  Dr. Bansal also 
provided an anatomic drawing with his report that demonstrates the origins and 
termination points of the supraspinatus and subscapularis tendons and muscles. (Ex. 
11, p. 51) There is no contrary medical evidence or explanation of the anatomic 
structures surrounding the glenohumeral joint. Therefore, I accept Dr. Bansal’s medical 
explanation regarding the supraspinatus and subscapularis tendons and muscles.  
Claimant has proven that the torn rotator cuff tendons originate with their corresponding 
muscles proximal to the glenohumeral joint.   
Dr. Bansal provides no analysis or explanation of the anatomy of the shoulder 
joint or supporting and surrounding anatomic parts.  In fact, no physician provided an 
analysis or explanation of the anatomy of the shoulder joint or supporting and 
surrounding anatomic parts.

The initial disputed factual issue for the undersigned to address is whether 
claimant has proven that the stipulated work injury of October 25, 2017, resulted in 
permanent disability.  The defendants appear to concede claimant sustained permanent 
disability; however, they assert claimant’s permanent disability is limited to the 8 percent 
impairment rating provided by Dr. Buck.  Regardless, the evidentiary record includes 
two expert reports which conclude claimant sustained permanent impairment as a result 
of the October 25, 2017, work injury.  The evidentiary record is void of any evidence to 
the contrary.  As such, I find claimant met her burden of proving she sustained 
permanent disability as a result of the stipulated work injury.   
Defendants assert claimant is not entitled to industrial disability benefits as 
claimant’s injury is to a scheduled member. I agree with defendants that claimant’s left 
shoulder injury does not extend into the body as a whole.  As a result of this finding, I 
find claimant is not entitled to industrial disability benefits. 
While their opinions are substantially similar, I accept Dr. Bansal’s opinions in 
this case as the most convincing and credible.  Dr. Bansal’s analysis is reasonable.  He 
appears to have had access to all relevant medical records.  I find Dr. Bansal’s 
permanent restrictions to be reasonable and appropriate.  Therefore, I accept the 
restrictions assigned to claimant by Dr. Bansal.  I likewise accept the 9 percent 
impairment rating assigned to claimant by Dr. Bansal and find that Ms. Persinger 
sustained a permanent functional impairment rating equivalent to 9 percent of the left 
upper extremity.  Again, the physicians provided similar impairment ratings.  I have 
accepted Dr. Bansal’s impairment rating over the impairment rating of Dr. Buck because 
Dr. Bansal actually documented the deficits in claimant’s range of motion and applied 
the same to the AMA Guides.  Dr. Buck assigned 6 percent impairment for occult 
instability and 2 percent impairment for loss of motion.  He did not provide what deficits 
lead to the 2 percent impairment for loss of range of motion.   
CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
As an initial matter, many of the disputes in this case arise out of the legislature’s 
2017 amendments to Iowa Code section 85.34.  As a result, a number of the 
determinations turn on this agency’s interpretation of the legislative changes.  I 
recognize the Iowa Supreme Court has stated that this agency lacks the authority to 
interpret workers’ compensation statutes.  See e.g., Ramirez-Trujillo v. Quality Egg, 
LLC, 878 N.W.2d 759, 770 (Iowa 2016).  Practically speaking, however, the agency acts 
as the front-line authority in interpreting statutory workers’ compensation provisions, 
particularly when statutory amendments are enacted.  Thus, while the appellate courts 
will have the final say, statutory interpretation by this agency is inevitable.   
The question to be determined in this case is whether the injuries to claimant's 
rotator cuff, and specifically the supraspinatus and subscapularis tendons, constitute 
“shoulder” injuries under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(n) or should be considered injuries 
to anatomic body parts proximal to the shoulder and compensated as unscheduled 
injuries under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v).

The parties stipulate that claimant sustained an injury to the left shoulder area on 
October 25, 2017. Claimant asserts that the injury extends beyond the left shoulder into 
the body as a whole. Specifically, claimant argues that the affected areas from this 
injury are proximal to the glenohumeral joint and, as such, the injury should be 
determined to be an unscheduled injury compensated with industrial disability pursuant 
to Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v) (2017).  According to claimant, the term “shoulder” 
was defined by this agency in the case of Pack v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co., File No. 
865057 (Arb. September 29, 1994).  In fact, claimant contends that the term “shoulder” 
is clear on its face, given the definition provided in Pack.  Claimant alternatively asserts 
the statute is ambiguous and should be liberally interpreted in favor of the injured 
worker. Griffen Pipe Products Co. v. Guarino, 663 N.W.2d 862, 864 (Iowa 2003). 
Defendants contend that the injury is limited to the left shoulder and should be 
compensated as a scheduled member injury pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(n) 
(2017).  Defendants further assert “loss of shoulder” has acquired an “approved usage” 
by virtue of receiving an authoritative construction by the Iowa Supreme Court.  In this 
regard, defendants contend that the Iowa Supreme Court has previously considered a 
rotator cuff injury to be an injury to the shoulder. 
Our workers' compensation statute provides compensation for permanent partial 
disability for injuries to specific parts of the body pursuant to an established schedule. 
See Iowa Code § 85.34(2) (1995); Second Injury Fund of Iowa v. Nelson, 544 N.W.2d 
258, 269 (Iowa 1995). This schedule sets the compensation at a percentage of a 
workers' average weekly earnings over a certain number of weeks based on the 
location of the injury. Id. The purpose of scheduling injuries is to provide certainty of 
compensation after a specific injury has been sustained by a worker and to avoid 
controversies. Gilleland v. Armstrong Rubber Co., 524 N.W.2d 404, 407 (Iowa 1994).  It 
has long been understood that an injury must be compensated as a scheduled injury if 
the legislature saw fit to list the injured body part in Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(a)-(u). 
Williams v. Larson Construction Co., 125 N.W.2d 248 (Iowa 1963).  A permanent partial 
disability to an area of the body not identified in the schedule is compensated as an 
injury to the body as a whole. Nelson, 544 N.W.2d at 269. This disability is 
compensated like industrial disability, taking into account the loss of earning capacity. 
Gilleland, 524 N.W.2d at 407.  The amount of compensation for an unscheduled injury 
is often much greater than for a scheduled injury.   
The Iowa Legislature enacted significant amendments to the Iowa workers' 
compensation laws, which took effect July 1, 2017. Of particular relevance to this case, 
the Iowa Legislature modified section 85.34 by adding the shoulder to the list of 
scheduled members.  The new subsection states, in its entirety: “For the loss of a 
shoulder, weekly compensation is paid based on four hundred weeks.” Iowa Code § 
85.34(2)(n).  Unfortunately, the amendment did not define what constitutes a “shoulder” 
injury.  Making matters worse, the legislature did not include a statement of the general 
assembly’s legislative intent in adopting the amendment to Iowa Code section 85.34.  
The use of the generic term “shoulder” has resulted in uncertainty as to the meaning of 
the statute at the agency level. See Arroyo v. Smithfield Foods, Inc., File No. 5066288

(Arbitration, February 6, 2020); Agee v. EFCO Corp., Inc., File No. 5065304, 5064099 
(Arbitration, October 22, 2019); Chavez v. Technology, L.L.C., File No. 5066270 
(Arbitration, February 5, 2020); Deng v. Farmland Foods, Inc., File No. 5061883 
(Arbitration, February 25, 2020); Smidt v. JKB Restaurants, LLC, File No. 5067766 
(Arbitration, May 6, 2020). 
Fortunately, the recent appeal decision of Deng v. Farmland Foods, Inc., File No. 
5061883 (App. September 29, 2020), has provided this agency with some clarity and 
has set a precedent for “new” shoulder claims moving forward. 
In Deng, the commissioner determined that the term “shoulder” under section 
85.34(2)(n) is not limited to the glenohumeral joint.  Additionally, the commissioner 
found that the muscles that make up the rotator cuff are included within the definition of 
“shoulder” under section 85.34(2)(n). Deng v. Farmland Foods, Inc., File No. 5061883 
(App. Sept. 29, 2020).  The commissioner explained: 
Given the entwinement of the glenohumeral joint and the muscles that 
make up the rotator cuff, including the infraspinatus, and the importance of 
the rotator cuff to the function of the joint, I find the muscles that make up 
the rotator cuff are included within the definition of “shoulder” under 
section 85.34(2)(n). 
Deng at 11.     
Given the commissioner’s findings in Deng, I find that claimant’s injury is limited 
to the left shoulder.  As such, I find claimant’s injury will be compensated as a 
scheduled member injury pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(n).  In the event the 
claimant believes an inequity exists in the current statutory scheme, it is a matter for the 
legislature to consider and not this agency.  The place to make new legislation, or 
address unwanted consequences of old legislation, lies in the state legislature. When it 
comes to statutory interpretation, this agency’s role is limited to applying the law’s 
demands as faithfully as we can in the cases that come before us. 
The next issue to be determined is the extent of claimant’s permanent disability.  
Injuries to the rotator cuff are considered injuries to the shoulder. Deng, File No. 
5061883 (App. 2020).  Shoulder injuries are compensated on a 400-week schedule and 
are limited to the functional impairment rating.  Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(n).   
The right of a worker to receive compensation for injuries sustained which arose 
out of and in the course of employment is statutory. The statute conferring this right can 
also fix the amount of compensation to be paid for different specific injuries, and the 
employee is not entitled to compensation except as provided by statute. Soukup v 
Shores, 268 N.W. 598 (Iowa 1936). 
Where an injury is limited to a scheduled member the loss is measured 
functionally, not industrially. Graves v. Eagle Iron Works, 331 N.W.2d 116 (Iowa 1983). 
The 2017 statutory amendments also change prior case law, which held that functional

disability was not limited to an impairment rating.  As revised, Iowa Code section 
85.34(2)(x) provides that when determining functional disability, the extent of loss or 
percentage of permanent impairment shall be determined solely by utilizing the guides 
to the evaluation of permanent impairment, published by the American medical 
association, as adopted by the workers' compensation commissioner by rule pursuant to 
chapter 17A. Lay testimony or agency expertise shall not be utilized in determining loss 
or percentage of permanent impairment. Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(x). 
Having adopted the impairment rating assigned by Dr. Bansal, I found claimant 
proved a functional loss of 9 percent to the left upper extremity.  Pursuant to Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(n), claimant is entitled to a proportional award equivalent to 9 percent 
of 400 weeks.  Therefore, I conclude that claimant is entitled to an award of 36 weeks of 
permanent partial disability benefits, commencing on November 26, 2018. Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(n).   
ORDER 
THEREFORE, IT IS ORDERED: 
Defendants shall pay unto claimant thirty-six (36) weeks of permanent partial 
disability benefits commencing on November 26, 2018, at the weekly rate of six hundred 
ninety-nine and 25/100 dollars ($699.25).  
Defendants shall pay all accrued benefits in a lump sum. 
Defendants shall receive a credit for all benefits previously paid.   
Defendants shall pay interest on unpaid weekly benefits awarded herein as set 
forth in Iowa Code section 85.30. Defendant shall pay accrued weekly benefits in a 
lump sum together with interest at an annual rate equal to the one-year treasury 
constant maturity published by the federal reserve in the most recent H15 report settled 
as of the date of injury, plus two percent. 
Defendants shall file subsequent reports of injury (SROI) as required by this 
agency pursuant to rules 876 IAC 3.1(2) and 876 IAC 11.7.
