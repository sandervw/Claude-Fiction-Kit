STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
The claimant, Paula Hildman, filed a petition for arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from self-insured employer Trinity Health Corporation d/b/a 
MercyOne Waterloo Medical Center (“Mercy”).  Dillon Carpenter appeared on behalf of 
the claimant.  Lee Hook appeared on behalf of the defendant.   
 
The matter came on for hearing on August 13, 2024, before Deputy Workers’ 
Compensation Commissioner Andrew M. Phillips.  Pursuant to an order of the Iowa 
Workers’ Compensation Commissioner, the hearing occurred electronically via Zoom.  
The hearing proceeded without significant difficulty.  
 
The record in this case consists of Joint Exhibits 1-14, Claimant’s Exhibits 1-4, 
and Defendant’s Exhibits A-D.  All of the exhibits were received into evidence without 
objection.   
Dennis Rogers, the claimant’s husband, testified on behalf of the claimant.  The 
claimant also testified on her own behalf.  Maria Taylor was appointed the official 
reporter and custodian of the notes of the proceeding.  The evidentiary record closed at 
the conclusion of the hearing, and the matter was considered fully submitted following 
briefing by the parties on September 13, 2024.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned, having considered all of the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
Paula Hildman, the claimant, was 70 years old at the time of the hearing.  
(Testimony).  She is married.  (Testimony).  She is a high school graduate.  
(Testimony).  She then attended the Allen School of Nursing, where she earned a 
nursing degree.  (Testimony).  In 1984, she became a registered nurse, a designation 
she held until 2023.  (Testimony).  Around 1993, Ms. Hildman began working for Mercy 
as a registered nurse.  (Testimony).   
There is one chiropractic record that predates the alleged date of injury, which is 
a January 4, 2019, visit to O’Hara Chiropractic.  (Joint Exhibit 1:1).  The record notes 
“LBP,” which generally means low back pain, for four days preceding the treatment.  (JE 
1:1).   
In 2019, Ms. Hildman worked for Mercy in Waterloo, Iowa, as a nurse facilitator in 
the surgical department.  (Testimony).  As a staff nurse, she communicated with 
patients, moved patients in carts, prepared operating rooms, moved tanks, lifted metal 
instrument trays, helped transfer patients, and insured equipment was properly 
sterilized.  (Testimony).  This job required her to twist, turn, and lift.  (Testimony).   
While assisting with a surgery on January 9, 2019, Ms. Hildman tripped over a 
stirrup used to support a patient’s foot.  (Testimony).  This caused her to fall onto her 
right side.  (Testimony).  She felt immediate pain in her right leg, knee, hip, buttocks, 
shoulder, and face.  (Testimony).

Permanent Partial Disability 
 
The parties previously stipulated that the claimant’s January 9, 2019, work injury 
was a cause of permanent disability.  Thus, there are three issues concerning the 
claimant’s entitlement to permanent partial disability benefits: 1. Whether the claimant is 
entitled to functional disability benefits pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v) or 
industrial disability benefits; 2. The extent of the claimant’s permanent disability; and, 3. 
The commencement date for permanent partial disability.   
Generally, permanent partial disability falls into two categories.  A scheduled 
member, as defined by Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(a) – (u), or a loss of earning 
capacity, also known as industrial disability, as defined by Iowa Code section 
85.34(2)(v).  Lauhoff Grain Co. v. McIntosh, 395 N.W.2d 834 (Iowa 1986); Blacksmith v. 
All-American, Inc., 290 N.W.2d 348 (Iowa 1980); Dailey v. Pooley Lumber Co., 233 
Iowa 758, 10 N.W.2d 569 (1943); Soukup v. Shores Co., 222 Iowa 272, 268 N.W. 598 
(1936); Diederich v. Tri-City Ry. Co. of Iowa, 219 Iowa 587, 258 N.W. 899 (1935).   
Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v) provides an alternative to the scheduled member 
and/or industrial disability compensation methods.  Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v) 
states, in relevant part:  
If an employee who is eligible for compensation under this paragraph 
returns to work or is offered work for which the employee receives or would 
receive the same or greater salary, wages, or earnings than the employee 
received at the time of the injury, the employee shall be compensated based 
only upon the employee’s functional impairment resulting from the injury, 
and not in relation to the employee’s earning capacity.   
In determining whether the above provision of Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v) 
applies, there is a comparison between the claimant’s pre- and post-injury salary, 
wages, or earnings.  McCoy v. Menard, Inc., File No. 1651840.01 (App. April 9, 2021).  
A claimant’s hourly wage must be considered in tandem with the actual hours worked 
by that claimant or offered by the employer.  Id.  The Commissioner noted in McCoy 
that there remained unanswered questions about when and how to take the “snapshot 
of a claimant’s post-injury earnings.”  Id.  The Commissioner attempted to clarify some 
of these questions in a subsequent appeal decision.  See Vogt v. XPO Logistics Freight, 
File No. 5064694.01 (App. June 11, 2021).  Specifically, the Commissioner noted, 

HILDMAN V. TRINITY HEALTH CORPORATION d/b/a MERCYONE WATERLOO 
MEDICAL CENTER 
Page 23 
 
“[f]rom the standpoint of logic and fairness, the post-injury ‘snapshot’ of claimant’s 
salary, wages or earnings should occur at the time of the hearing, just as industrial 
disability is measured as the evidence stands at the time of the hearing.”  Id.  The 
Commissioner further opined that performing a comparison based solely on the 
claimant’s initial return to work “could lead to unfair and illogical results.”  Id.   
The defendant provided wage records for the claimant dating back to 2018.  See 
DE C:1-31.  In 2018, the claimant earned seventy-four thousand seven hundred sixty-
six and 41/100 dollars ($74,766.41) in gross wages.  (DE C:10).  She worked 1,409.37 
regular hours and was on call for 790.75 hours.  (DE C:10).  She was injured on 
January 9, 2019.  In 2019, Ms. Hildman earned sixty-seven thousand ten and 46/100 
dollars ($67,010.46), worked 1,337.63 regular hours and was on call for 618.50 hours.  
(DE C:23).  In 2020, Ms. Hildman worked until voluntarily retiring in March of 2020.  (DE 
C:25-31).   
It is plain from the evidence that Ms. Hildman worked less following her January 
9, 2019, injury.  The defendant’s own exhibits show that she worked fewer regular hours 
in 2019, when compared to 2018.  She also earned less in gross wages as a result.  
The defendant admits in their posthearing brief that Ms. Hildman only missed one day of 
work following her January 9, 2019, fall, and that she continued to work in her same 
position following the work injury.  There was not specific evidence elicited or provided 
to indicate another explanation for a non-injury related reason that Ms. Hildman’s hours 
and/or gross earnings were less in 2019 than they were in 2018.  The evidence, taken 
as a snapshot at the time of the hearing, shows that the claimant worked less hours, 
and earned less following her work injury.  Considering the decisions in both McCoy and 
Vogt, and the evidence in this case, the claimant is entitled to permanent partial 
disability compensation based upon an industrial disability analysis.   
Industrial disability was defined Diederich v. Tri-City R. Co. of Iowa, 219 Iowa 
587, 591, 258 N.W. 899, 902 (1935) as follows: “[i]t is therefore plain that the 
Legislature intended the term ‘disability’ to mean ‘industrial disability’ or loss of earning 
capacity and not a mere ‘functional disability’ to be computed in terms of percentages of 
the total physical and mental ability of a normal man.”   
 
Functional impairment is an element to be considered in determining industrial 
disability which is the reduction of earning capacity; however, consideration must also 
be given to the injured employee’s age, education, qualifications, experience, 
motivation, loss of earnings, severity and situs of the injury, work restrictions, inability to 
engage in employment for which the employee is fitted, and the employer’s offer of work 
or failure to so offer.  McSpadden v. Big Ben Coal Co., 288 N.W.2d 181, 192 (Iowa 
1980); Olson v. Goodyear Service Stores, 255 Iowa 1112, 1120, 125 N.W.2d 251, 256 
(1963); Barton v. Nevada Poultry Co., 253 Iowa 285, 110 N.W.2d 660, 663 (1961).

There is a dispute as to the proper commencement date for permanent partial 
disability benefits.  The claimant asserts that May 31, 2022, is the proper 
commencement date, while the defendant asserts that October 9, 2019, is the proper 
commencement date.   
Compensation for permanent partial disability shall begin when it is medically 
indicated that MMI from the injury has been reached, and that the extent of loss or 
percentage of permanent impairment can be determined by use of the Guides.  See 
Iowa Code section 85.34(2).  The Guides define MMI as when an impairment is “well 
stabilized and unlikely to change substantially in the next year with or without medical 
treatment.”  See Guides, pg. 2.   
On August 21, 2019, Dr. Naylor opined that the claimant achieved MMI for her 
right hip.  On October 21, 2019, Dr. Naylor opined that Ms. Hildman achieved MMI for 
her low back issues as of her appointment with Dr. Abernathey on October 9, 2019, 
during which she declined proceeding with surgery on her low back.  On December 5, 
2019, Dr. Naylor then walked-back his opinion on December 5, 2019, in noting that he 
was delaying the issuance of an impairment rating “until [Ms. Hildman] is maximized in 
her care,” as he felt she may need a second opinion for surgical options relating to her 
ongoing low back issues.   
Dr. Segal opined in his October 30, 2023, IME report that May 31, 2022, was a 
more appropriate date for the claimant to have reached MMI.  He felt that Dr. Naylor’s 
opinions regarding MMI were premature, as Ms. Hildman continued to treat following 
this date, and that the imaging studies performed after this date showed progression in 
her lower back issues.  Dr. Segal felt that May 31, 2022, was a more accurate date, as 
this was Ms. Hildman’s final visit with Dr. Afzal.   
Dr. Naylor’s opinions are rather muddled.  He issued an impairment rating as of 
his October 21, 2019, visit, yet felt that the October 9, 2019, visit with Dr. Abernathey 
was a more appropriate date for the claimant to have achieved MMI considering her 
decision to decline surgery on her lower back.  Then, he walked-back his opinion on 
December 5, 2019, in stating that an impairment rating should be delayed until Ms. 
Hildman was “maximized in her care” including a potential second opinion for her low 
back issues.  This change in opinion indicates that Ms. Hildman’s impairment was not 
“well stabilized and unlikely to change substantially in the next year with or without 
medical treatment.”  See Guides, pg. 2.  On this issue, the clear opinions of Dr. Segal 
are more persuasive.  Therefore, I find that the claimant achieved MMI as of May 31, 
2022.  Permanent disability benefits should thus commence on that date.

The claimant is seeking reimbursement or an order for the defendant to pay for 
certain medical expenses and/or to hold the claimant harmless “as it relates to any 
subrogation issues raised by any third-party for expenses previously paid for treatment 
associated with the injuries in this case.”  See Claimant’s Posthearing Brief, pg. 15.  In 
their posthearing brief, the defendant agrees to pay outstanding balances for a March 
11, 2020, office visit with Dr. Naylor, a November 24, 2020, MRI of the lumbar spine, 
and a September 23, 2019, office visit with Dr. Abernathey, totaling eighty-six and 
75/100 dollars ($86.75).  However, the defendant disputes payment for unauthorized 
chiropractic visits with Dr. Welsh at Hitchins Chiropractic (Radiance Chiropractic).  The 
defendant alleges that these visits were not authorized and were not causally connected 
to the work injury.   
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance, and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers’ compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Iowa Code 85.27.  Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening, October 1975).   
 
Pursuant to Iowa Code 85.27, claimant is entitled to payment of reasonable 
medical expenses incurred for treatment of a work injury.  Claimant is entitled to an 
order of reimbursement if he/she has paid those expenses.  Otherwise, claimant is 
entitled only to an order directing the responsible defendants to make such payments 
directly to the provider.  See Krohn v. State, 420 N.W.2d 463 (Iowa 1988).   
 
In cases where the employer’s medical plan covers the medical expenses, 
claimant is entitled to an order of reimbursement only if he has paid treatment costs; 
otherwise, the defendants are ordered to make payments directly to the provider.  See 
Krohn, 420 N.W.2d at 463.  Where medical payments are made from a plan to which 
the employer did not contribute, the claimant is entitled to a direct payment.  Midwest 
Ambulance Service v. Ruud, 754 N.W.2d 860, 867-68 (Iowa 2008) (“We therefore hold 
that the commissioner did not err in ordering direct payment to the claimant for past 
medical expenses paid through insurance coverage obtained by the claimant 
independent of any employer contribution.”).  See also Carl A. Nelson & Co. v. Sloan, 
873 N.W.2d 552 (Iowa App. 2015)(Table) 2015 WL 7574232 15-0323.   
The employee has the burden of proof to show medical charges are reasonable 
and necessary, and must produce evidence to that effect.  Poindexter v. Grant’s Carpet 
Service, I Iowa Industrial Commissioner Decisions, No. 1, at 195 (1984); McClellon v. 
Iowa S. Util., 91-92, IAWC, 266-272 (App. 1992).    

HILDMAN V. TRINITY HEALTH CORPORATION d/b/a MERCYONE WATERLOO 
MEDICAL CENTER 
Page 28 
 
The employee has the burden of proof in showing that treatment is related to the 
injury.  Auxier v. Woodward State Hospital School, 266 N.W.2d 139 (Iowa 1978), 
Watson v. Hanes Border Company , No. 1 Industrial Comm’r report 356, 358 (1980) 
(claimant failed to prove medical charges were related to the injury where medical 
records contained nothing related to that injury)  See also Bass v. Veith Construction 
Corp., File No 5044438 (App. May 27, 2016)(Claimant failed to prove causal connection 
between injury and claimed medical expenses); Becirevic v. Trinity Health, File No. 
5063498 (Arb. December 28, 2018) (Claimant failed to recover on unsupported medical 
bills) 
 
Nothing in Iowa Code section 85.27 prohibits an injured employee from selecting 
his or her own medical care at his or her own expense following an injury.  Bell Bros. 
Heating and Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d 193, 205 (Iowa 2010).  In order to 
recover the reasonable expenses of the care, the employee must still prove by a 
preponderance of the evidence that unauthorized care was reasonable and beneficial.  
Id.  The Court in Bell Bros. concluded that unauthorized medical care is beneficial if it 
provides a “more favorable medical outcome than would likely have been achieved by 
the care authorized by the employer.”  Id.

An employer is only liable to reimburse an employee for the cost of an

The Iowa Supreme Court determined that an “examination” required “to perform 
an impairment rating” must include certain steps as required under the AMA Guides.  
Mid American Construction, LLC v. Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d 838 (Iowa 2024).  These steps 
include: determining whether an injured worker achieved MMI, reviewing past medical 
records to ascertain knowledge as to any past injuries to the injured worker, opining as 
to whether the work injury caused the impairment, conducting a physical examination of 
the claimant in order “to assess the employee’s status and perform accurate 
measurements…”, and determining a diagnosis.  Id.   
Defendants are responsible only for reasonable fees associated with claimant’s 
independent medical examination.  The claimant has the burden of proving the 
reasonableness of the expenses incurred for the examination.  See Schintgen v. 
Economy Fire & Casualty Co., File No. 855298 (App. April 26, 1991).  This burden may 
be met either by “…evidence of the fees typically charged in that locality to support or 
challenge the fee charged in a particular case…” or by agency expertise in adjudicating 
claims in the local area where the examination was conducted.  Mid American 
Construction, LLC v. Sandlin, 2 N.W.3d 838 (Iowa 2024).   
An opinion finding a lack of causation is tantamount to a zero percent impairment 
rating.  Kern v. Fenchel, Doster & Buck, P.L.C., 2021 WL 3890603 (Iowa App. 2021).     
It is well accepted Iowa law that a claimant is only entitled to reimbursement for 
one IME pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.39.  Larson Mfg. Co. Inc. v. Thorson, 763 
N.W.2d 842, 861 (Iowa 2009).   
In this case, Dr. Naylor made two opinions as to permanent impairment on the 
claimant.  The first concerned her right hip, which he determined had no permanent 
impairment.  The second concerned her lower back on October 21, 2019.  He later

The claimant then had an IME physical examination with Dr. Segal on September

Costs 
Claimant seeks an award of costs.  Costs are to be assessed at the discretion of 
the deputy commissioner hearing the case.  See 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.33; 
Iowa Code 10A.328.  876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.33(6) provides:  
[c]osts taxed by the workers’ compensation commissioner or a deputy 
commissioner shall be (1) attendance of a certified shorthand reporter or 
presence of mechanical means at hearings and evidential depositions, (2) 
transcription costs when appropriate, (3) costs of service of the original 
notice and subpoenas, (4) witness fees and expenses as provided by Iowa 
Code sections 622.69 and 622.72, (5) the costs of doctors’ and 
practitioners’ deposition testimony, provided that said costs do not exceed 
the amounts provided by Iowa Code sections 622.69 and 622.72, (6) the 
reasonable costs of obtaining no more than two doctors’ or practitioners’ 
reports, (7) filing fees when appropriate, including convenience fees 
incurred by using the WCES payment gateway, and (8) costs of persons 
reviewing health service disputes.   
Pursuant to the holding in Des Moines Area Regional Transit Authority v. Young, 
867 N.W.2d 839 (Iowa 2015), only the report of an IME physician, and not the 
examination itself, can be taxed as a cost according to 876 IAC 4.33(6).  The Iowa 
Supreme Court reasoned, “a physician’s report becomes a cost incurred in a hearing 
because it is used as evidence in lieu of the doctor’s testimony,” while “[t]he underlying 
medical expenses associated with the examination do not become costs of a report 
needed for a hearing, just as they do not become costs of the testimony or deposition.”  
Id.  (noting additionally that “[i]n the context of the assessment of costs, the expenses of 
the underlying medical treatment and examination are not part of the costs of the report

or deposition”).  The commissioner has found this rationale applicable to expenses 
incurred by vocational experts.  See  Kirkendall v. Cargill Meat Solutions Corp., File No. 
5055494 (App. December 17, 2018); Voshell v. Compass Group, USA, Inc., File No. 
5056857 (App. September 27, 2019).
