STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Adam Davis, claimant, filed a petition for arbitration against Blain Supply 
Company a/k/a Blain’s Farm & Fleet, as the employer, and West Bend Mutual 
Insurance, as the insurance carrier.  This case came before the undersigned for an 
arbitration hearing on February 26, 2024.  The case proceeded to a live video hearing 
via Zoom.   
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the hearing. On the 
hearing report, the parties entered into numerous stipulations. Those stipulations were 
accepted and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be made 
or discussed. The parties are now bound by their stipulations. 
The evidentiary record includes Joint Exhibits 1 through 9, Claimant’s Exhibits 1 
through 11, and Defendants’ Exhibits A through C.  All exhibits were received without 
objection. 
Claimant testified on his own behalf. No other witnesses were called to testify. 
The evidentiary record closed at the conclusion of the evidentiary hearing.  All parties 
served their post-hearing briefs on April 1, 2024, at which time this case was deemed 
fully submitted to the undersigned.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned, having considered all of the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
 
Adam Davis sustained a work-related injury to his right shoulder on September 
17, 2020.  The primary issue in this case is whether Mr. Davis also sustained a neck 
injury as a result of the stipulated September 17, 2020, incident.  
On the date of hearing, Mr. Davis was a 37-year-old gentleman living and 
working in Dubuque, Iowa. (Hearing Transcript, page 11; Defendants’ Exhibit B, pages 
2, 6)  In terms of his educational background, Mr. Davis obtained his Certificate of High 
School Equivalency from Northeast Iowa Community College. (Ex. B, p. 4)  He has not 
sought any additional formal education.   
Mr. Davis’ employment history largely consists of work as an automotive 
mechanic. (Ex. B, pp. 6-10)  In November 2018, Mr. Davis began working as a service 
manager for Blain’s Farm & Fleet.  As a service manager, Mr. Davis supervised and 
assigned tasks to service center employees, provided technical assistance and training, 
and, on occasion, performed all the duties of a service technician. (Claimant’s Exhibit 6, 
page 22; Hr. Tr., pp. 13-14)  The position required Mr. Davis to perform horizontal and 
overhead lifting of products up to 100 pounds. (Ex. 6, p. 22)  Mr. Davis testified he had 
no problems, symptoms, or limitations with his right wrist, right arm, or neck when he 
was hired. (Hr. Tr., pp. 14-15) 
 
Mr. Davis sustained a work-related injury while assisting an associate with an 
alignment adjustment on September 17, 2020. (Hr. Tr., p. 15)  In order to adjust the 
alignment, the vehicle had to be raised up onto a lift or rack that is approximately five 
feet off the ground. (Hr. Tr., pp. 15-16)  According to Mr. Davis, the lift has a detachable 
step for employees to use. (Hr. Tr., pp. 16-17)  After completing the alignment, Mr. 
Davis typically uses the detachable step to climb down and check his measurements on 
a computer. (Hr. Tr., pp. 15-16)  On the date of injury, the detachable step “flipped out” 
from underneath Mr. Davis’ foot and he fell to the cement floor of the service bay. (Hr. 
Tr., p. 17)  On the way down, Mr. Davis asserts his neck made contact with a wall and 
he landed on his right wrist. (Hr. Tr., p. 17)  Mr. Davis submitted video evidence of the 
stipulated injury. (Claimant’s Exhibit 9)

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The fighting issue in this case is whether claimant’s disability is a scheduled 
disability to the shoulder under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(n) or an unscheduled 
disability under Section 85.34(2)(v).  In this regard, claimant asserts that his right 
shoulder injury extends into his neck and the body as a whole.   
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible. George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony.  The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question.  The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy

of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances.  The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke's Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc, v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); 
Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995); Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods. Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994).   
While a claimant is not entitled to compensation for the results of a preexisting 
injury or disease, its mere existence at the time of a subsequent injury is not a defense. 
Rose v. John Deere Ottumwa Works, 247 Iowa 900, 76 N.W.2d 756 (1956).  If the 
claimant had a preexisting condition or disability that is materially aggravated, 
accelerated, worsened or lighted up so that it results in disability, claimant is entitled to 
recover. Nicks v. Davenport Produce Co., 254 Iowa 130, 115 N.W.2d 812 (1962); 
Yeager v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co., 253 Iowa 369, 112 N.W.2d 299 (1961). 
Under the Iowa Workers' Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is 
compensated either for a loss or loss of use of a scheduled member under Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(a)-(u) or for loss of earning capacity under section 85.34(2)(v).  The 
extent of scheduled member disability benefits to which an injured worker is entitled is 
determined by using the functional method.  Functional disability is “limited to the loss of 
the physiological capacity of the body or body part.” Mortimer v. Fruehauf Corp., 502 
N.W.2d 12, 15 (Iowa 1993); Sherman v. Pella Corp., 576 N.W.2d 312 (Iowa 1998).   
An injury to a scheduled member may, because of after effects or compensatory 
change, result in permanent impairment of the body as a whole. Such impairment may 
in turn be the basis for a rating of industrial disability. It is the anatomical situs of the 
permanent injury or impairment which determines whether the schedules in section 
85.34(2)(a)-(u) are applied. Lauhoff Grain v. McIntosh, 395 N.W.2d 834 (Iowa 1986); 
Blacksmith v. All-American, Inc., 290 N.W.2d 348 (Iowa 1980); Dailey v. Pooley Lumber 
Co., 233 Iowa 758, 10 N.W.2d 569 (1943). Soukup v. Shores Co., 222 Iowa 272, 268 
N.W. 598 (1936). 
In this case, Mr. Davis offered the report of Dr. Mathew, who assigned 
permanent impairment to the cervical spine; however, he failed to address whether the 
September 17, 2020, work injury caused Mr. Davis’ cervical spine condition or materially 
aggravated, accelerated, or lit up a pre-existing cervical spine condition.  I did not find 
Dr. Mathew’s opinion to be thorough or convincing.  Without a credible expert opinion, I 
found insufficient evidence that Mr. Davis sustained a neck injury as a result of the 
September 17, 2020, work accident.   
There is no credible evidence that Mr. Davis sustained a permanent injury to any 
body part other than the right shoulder as a result of the September 17, 2020, work 
injury.  Therefore, I conclude that claimant failed to carry his burden of proof to establish 
he sustained a permanent injury to any body part other than his right shoulder.  I further 
conclude claimant’s injury should be compensated as a scheduled member injury to the 
right shoulder. Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(n) (2018)

The Iowa legislature enacted significant amendments to the Iowa workers’ 
compensation laws, which took effect in July 2017. As part of those amendments, the 
legislature specified that injuries to the shoulder should be compensated as a scheduled 
member injury on a 400-week schedule. Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(n) (2018). 
In all cases of permanent partial disability described in paragraphs “a” through 
“u”, or paragraph “v” when determining functional disability and not loss of earning 
capacity, the extent of loss or percentage of permanent impairment shall be determined 
solely by utilizing the guides to the evaluation of permanent impairment, published by 
the American Medical Association, as adopted by the workers' compensation 
commissioner by rule pursuant to chapter 17A. Lay testimony or agency expertise shall 
not be utilized in determining loss or percentage of permanent impairment pursuant to 
paragraphs “a” through “u”, or paragraph “v” when determining functional disability and 
not loss of earning capacity. Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(x) 
I found that claimant sustained a fifteen percent loss of function in his right 
shoulder as a result of the September 17, 2020, work injury.  Claimant is entitled to an 
award of permanent partial disability benefits equivalent to the proportional loss of his 
shoulder. Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(w); Blizek v. Eagle Signal Company, 164 N.W.2d 
84 (Iowa 1969).  Fifteen percent (15%) of 400 weeks is 60 weeks.  Claimant is therefore 
entitled to an award of 60 weeks of permanent partial disability benefits against 
defendants as a result of the September 17, 2020, right shoulder injury. 
The next issue to be decided is whether the claimant is entitled to alternate 
medical care under Iowa Code section 85.27.  More specifically, Mr. Davis is requesting 
that defendants be ordered to authorize and provide ongoing care for his right shoulder 
and neck injuries. 
In a claim for alternate medical care, the employee has the burden of proving that 
the care provided by the employer was unreasonable. Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 
N.W.2d 122, 123 (Iowa 1995). The determination is based upon what it reasonably 
necessary, not on what is desired by the employee. (Id. at 124) It is not enough for the 
employee to show that the care requested by the employee is itself “reasonable.” The 
employer provided care must be deemed unreasonable. Lynch Livestock, Inc. v. 
Bursell, 870 N.W.2d 274 (Iowa App. 2015). 
Having found the claimant failed to prove he sustained a neck injury on 
September 17, 2020, I find the care requested for the claimant’s complaints of neck pain 
are denied.  The claimant is entitled to ongoing medical care for all causally related 
conditions pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.27. 
The final disputed issue is whether costs should be assessed against either 
party. Costs are assessed at the discretion of the agency. Iowa Code section 10A.328.  
Because claimant was successful in his claim, I conclude it is appropriate to assess 
claimant's costs in some amount.
