STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
Claimant Shannon McCain filed a petition in arbitration seeking worker’s 
compensation benefits against Qwest Corporation, employer, and Insurance Company 
of the State of PA, insurer, for an accepted work injury date of May 13, 2004. The case 
came before the undersigned for an arbitration hearing on May 6, 2024. Pursuant to an 
order of the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commissioner, this case proceeded to a live 
video hearing via Zoom, with all parties and the court reporter appearing remotely. The 
hearing proceeded without significant difficulties. 
The parties filed a hearing report prior to the commencement of the hearing. On 
the hearing report, the parties entered into numerous stipulations. Those stipulations 
were accepted and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be 
made or discussed. The parties are now bound by their stipulations. 
The evidentiary record includes Joint Exhibits 1 through 5, Claimant’s Exhibits 1 
through 3, and Defendants’ Exhibits A through H.  
Claimant testified on his own behalf. The evidentiary record closed at the 
conclusion of the evidentiary hearing on May 6, 2024. The parties submitted post-
hearing briefs on June 21, 2024, and the case was considered fully submitted on that 
date.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned, having considered all of the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
Claimant’s testimony was consistent as compared to the evidentiary record, and 
his demeanor at the time of hearing gave the undersigned no reason to doubt his 
veracity. Claimant is found credible. 
At the time of hearing, claimant was a 49-year-old person. (Hearing Transcript, p. 
8) Claimant has worked for CenturyLink, formerly Qwest, since April 16, 2000. He is 
employed as a network technician. (Tr., p. 9) His job involves getting phone lines to 
people’s houses to provide telephone and internet services. The phone lines can be 
buried underground or above ground via telephone poles.  
Over the course of his employment, claimant has sustained a couple of work-
related injuries. In 2001, he slipped on ice, landing on his chest. He testified that he had 
a lot of pain in his sternum/ribs and back, but did not have much treatment at the time of 
that injury. (Tr., pp. 9-13) He testified that after that injury his condition never fully 
resolved, as different activities would set it off. (Tr., pp. 13-14) However, he was 
released from care and allowed to return to work with no restrictions by May of 2002. 
See Shannon McCain v. Qwest Corp., File No. 5020712, p. 2 (Arb., August 7, 2008) 
Claimant sustained a second work injury on May 13, 2004. (Tr., p. 13) At that 
time, he was working at a house when he had to move a large boulder out of the way. 
He said that movement set off the prior injury, in his opinion, and he requested medical 
care. (Tr., p. 14)  
The original case went to hearing before former Deputy Workers’ Compensation 
Commissioner Ron Pohlman on May 21, 2008. To briefly summarize claimant’s care 
prior to the arbitration hearing, he began treating with Douglas Sedlacek, M.D., shortly 
after the injury. (Joint Exhibit 1, p. 3) He explained to Dr. Sedlacek that he had an initial 
injury in 2001, after which he developed left sternal pain and left thoracic pain. (Jt. Ex. 1, 
p. 1) He had conservative treatment and his evaluations were normal, but he continued 
to have pain. However, he tolerated it and continued to work with no additional 
treatment. With the 2004 injury, however, he felt his pain was aggravated, and at the 
time he first saw Dr. Sedlacek he said it was “markedly affecting” him. He could not get 
better after the 2004 incident, and activities were “markedly aggravating” his pain. He 
complained of mid thoracic pain, which he described as radiating out into his left side.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence. Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based. A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause. A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible. George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 

MCCAIN V. QWEST CORPORATION 
Page 15 
 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 
621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 
(Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
In this case, I found that based on the majority of the evidence, including 
claimant’s credible testimony and the medical records, claimant’s ongoing symptoms 
are related to the original work injury on May 13, 2004. I accepted the opinion of Dr. 
Kline, a long-term authorized treating provider, over Dr. Chen, who saw claimant on two 
occasions for IMEs. Because claimant’s current symptoms are related to the original 
work injury, he is entitled to ongoing medical care. 
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance, and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury. Iowa Code section 85.27. Holbert 
v. Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening October 1975). 
 
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable. Long v. 
Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122, 123 (Iowa 1995). Determining what care is 
reasonable under the statute is a question of fact. Id. The employer’s obligation turns on 
the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability. Id. at 124; Harned v. Farmland 
Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98, 101 (Iowa 1983). In Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d 
at 437, the court approvingly quoted Bowles v. Los Lunas Schools, 109 N.M. 100, 106, 
781 P.2d 1178, 1184 (N.M. Ct. App. 1989): 
[T]he words “reasonable” and “adequate” appear to describe the same 
standard. 
[The New Mexico rule] requires the employer to provide a certain standard 
of care and excuses the employer from any obligation to provide other 
services only if that standard is met.  We construe the terms "reasonable” 
and “adequate” as describing care that is both appropriate to the injury and 
sufficient to bring the worker to maximum recovery. 

MCCAIN V. QWEST CORPORATION 
Page 16 
 
The commissioner is justified in ordering alternate care when employer-
authorized care has not been effective, and evidence shows that such care is “inferior or 
less extensive” care than other available care requested by the employee. Long, 
528 N.W.2d at 124; Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 437. 
In this case, defendants withdrew authorization for medical care on February 20, 
2020, when they denied that claimant’s ongoing symptoms were related to the work 
injury. As such, defendants are not currently offering care. Claimant has requested 
ongoing pain management with Dr. Kline, which is a reasonable request as Dr. Kline 
has been treating claimant since Dr. Sedlacek retired in 2018. Therefore, claimant’s 
request for alternate care is granted, and defendants shall immediately authorize 
ongoing pain management with Dr. Kline. 
Claimant seeks reimbursement of medical expenses, reflected in Claimant’s 
Exhibit 2. The claimant has the burden to prove that claimed medical expenses are 
related to the alleged work injury. Zineta Kudic v. IOC Black Hawk Co., File Nos. 
5066504; 5066505 (App. Dec. 17, 2020). Defendants argue that claimant has not met 
this burden. 
In reviewing Exhibit 2, some of the expenses are for treatment dates prior to 
defendants’ denial of the claim. An employer cannot revoke authorization for medical 
care retroactively to avoid liability for expenses previously incurred. Ramirez-Trujillo v. 
Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 N.W.2d 759, 776 (Iowa 2016). As such, all medical expenses in 
Exhibit 2 prior to the February 20, 2020 denial are defendants’ responsibility. 
With respect to the remaining medical expenses, once defendants denied 
compensability for claimant’s alleged injuries, they lost the right to choose the medical 
providers for that care during the period of denial. “[T]he employer has no right to 
choose the medical care when compensability is contested.” Bell Bros. Heating and Air 
Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d 193, 204 (Iowa 2010). Further, when compensability 
is contested, “the employer cannot assert an authorization defense in response to a 
subsequent claim by the employee for the expenses of the alternate medical care.”      
R. R. Donnelly & Sons v. Barnett, 670 N.W.2d at 197-198 (Iowa 2003). As such, 
defendants are responsible for all medical care claimant has received related to the 
work injury. Review of Claimant’s Exhibit 2 does not reveal any medical expenses that 
are not related to the 2004 injury. As such, defendants are responsible for all medical 
expenses found in Claimant’s Exhibit 2.  
Claimant is not entitled to reimbursement for medical bills unless claimant shows 
that they were paid from his own funds. See Caylor v. Employers Mutual Casualty Co., 
337 N.W.2d 890 (Iowa Ct. App. 1983). Otherwise, claimant is entitled only to an order 
directing the responsible defendants to make such payments directly to the provider. 
See Krohn v. State, 420 N.W.2d 463 (Iowa 1988). Where medical payments are made 
from a plan to which the employer did not contribute, the claimant is entitled to a direct 
payment. Midwest Ambulance Service v. Ruud, 754 N.W.2d 860, 867-68 (Iowa 2008) 
(“We therefore hold that the commissioner did not err in ordering direct payment to the 
claimant for past medical expenses paid through insurance coverage obtained by the 

MCCAIN V. QWEST CORPORATION 
Page 17 
 
claimant independent of any employer contribution.”) See also: Carl A. Nelson & Co. v. 
Sloan, 873 N.W.2d 552 (Iowa Ct. App. 2015) (Table) 2015 WL 7574232 15-0323. 
Defendants shall therefore reimburse claimant for all out-of-pocket medical 
expenses related to the 2004 injury, and make payment directly to the provider for any 
outstanding balances. 
The final issue is costs. Assessment of costs is a discretionary function of this 
agency. Iowa Code § 10A.328. Costs are to be assessed at the discretion of the deputy 
commissioner or workers’ compensation commissioner hearing the case. 876 IAC 4.33. 
Claimant seeks reimbursement for a phone conference with Dr. Kline, in the amount of 
$250.00, and the cost of the deposition transcript, in the amount of $86.70. (Cl. Ex. 3, 
pp. 39-40) Both are allowable costs. Therefore, I use my discretion to award claimant 
$336.70 in costs.
