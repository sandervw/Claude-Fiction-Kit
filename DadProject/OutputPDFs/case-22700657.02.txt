STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Charles Drees, claimant, filed a petition in arbitration and seeks workers’ 
compensation benefits from defendant, John Deere Waterloo Works, as the self-insured 
employer.  Mr. Drees also seeks benefits from the Second Injury Fund of Iowa.  Hearing 
was held on January 22, 2025 via Zoom videoconference.   
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised 
or discussed in this decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations. 
The evidentiary record includes Joint Exhibits 1 through 14, Claimant’s Exhibits 1 
through 6, Employer’s Exhibits A through K and Second Injury Fund Exhibits AA 
through DD.  All exhibits were received into the evidentiary record without objection.    
Claimant testified on his own behalf.  The employer called Brian Burkhardt, its 
wage employment administrator, to testify.  No other witnesses testified live at the 
hearing.  The evidentiary record closed at the conclusion of the arbitration hearing.  
Counsel requested the opportunity to file post-hearing briefs, and the case was 
considered fully submitted to the undersigned upon the filing of those briefs on February 
28, 2025.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned, having considered all of the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
Charles Drees, claimant, is a 38-year-old gentleman, who lives in Waterloo, 
Iowa.  Mr. Drees is a high school graduate, who attended an alternative school and 
worked his way to a degree in spite of a learning disability.  Mr. Drees testified that he 
earned C’s and D’s in high school and had difficulties with reading and comprehension, 
attending special education classes.  Since graduating from high school, Mr. Drees has 
worked as a kitchen manager, in the warehouse at Target, as a delivery driver, in a 
packing job, in a seasonal position in food service at a theme park, and as an assistant 
manager since this work injury. 
Claimant asserts a claim against the employer John Deere Waterloo Works 
(hereinafter “Deere”) for an injury on February 15, 2022.  Mr. Drees asserts he 
sustained a traumatic injury to his left knee on the date of injury and developed a low 
back injury as a sequela of that knee injury. 
Mr. Drees began his employment with Deere in January 2022 and worked most 
of his time with Deere in training.  In total, Mr. Drees worked 15 days at Deere, of which

assigned to production assembly work.  His main position was installing wiring

pivoted on his left knee to get a part and felt pain in his left knee.  (Transcript, page 20)

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
A personal injury contemplated by the workers’ compensation law means an 
injury, the impairment of health or a disease resulting from an injury which comes about, 
not through the natural building up and tearing down of the human body, but because of 
trauma.  The injury must be something that acts extraneously to the natural processes 
of nature and thereby impairs the health, interrupts or otherwise destroys or damages a 
part or all of the body.  Although many injuries have a traumatic onset, there is no 
requirement for a special incident or an unusual occurrence.  Injuries which result from 
cumulative trauma are compensable.  Increased disability from a prior injury, even if 
brought about by further work, does not constitute a new injury, however.  St. Luke’s 
Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 650-51 (Iowa 2000); Ellingson v. Fleetguard, Inc., 599 
N.W.2d 440, 444 (Iowa 1999); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 
845, 850-51 (Iowa 1995); McKeever Custom Cabinets v. Smith, 379 N.W.2d 368, 373-
74 (Iowa 1985).  An occupational disease covered by chapter 85A is specifically 
excluded from the definition of personal injury.  Iowa Code section 85.61(4)(b); Iowa 
Code section 85A.8; Iowa Code section 85A.14. 
 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is

proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
A possibility of causation is not sufficient; a probability is necessary. Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996) (citing Holmes v. Bruce 
Motor Freight, Inc., 215 N.W.2d 296, 297 (Iowa 1974)). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 
621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 
(Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
In this case, it is stipulated that claimant sustained an initial injury that arose out 
of and in the course of his employment with Deere on February 15, 2022.  However, the 
parties dispute whether claimant’s second knee surgery is causally related to the 
February 15, 2022 work injury at Deere.  I accepted the medical opinions of Drs. Naylor 
and Fields as most credible in this record.  Accordingly, I found that claimant failed to 
prove the second knee surgery was causally related to the initial work injury. 
The parties also submitted a dispute regarding claimant’s low back claim.  I 
accepted the opinions of Dr. Fields as most credible on the low back issue and rejected 
those of Dr. Bansal.  In doing so, I found that claimant also failed to prove a causal 
connection between his February 15, 2022 work injury at Deere and his claimed low 
back injury.  Claimant is not entitled to benefits related to either his second knee surgery 
or his low back injury claim.

Deere asserts a notice defense pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.23.  Deere did not 
specifically brief the notice issue in their post-hearing brief and presumably waived that

Deere’s medical clinic for his left knee pain on February 24, 2022.  That office note 
indicates, “He also is reporting a L knee injury that occurred on Monday or Tuesday of 
last week.”  (Joint Ex. 9, p. 107)  The Deere clinic office note records the specific facts 
of how claimant’s left knee injury occurred on February 15, 2022.  Deere clearly had

Mr. Drees contends he is entitled to healing period benefits pursuant to Iowa 
Code section 85.34(1).    Healing period compensation describes temporary workers’ 
compensation weekly benefits that precede an allowance of permanent partial disability 
benefits.  Ellingson v. Fleetguard, Inc., 599 N.W.2d 440, 446 (Iowa 1999).  Healing 
period benefits are payable to an injured worker who has suffered permanent partial 
disability until the first to occur of three events.  Iowa Code § 85.34(1) (2015).  These 
are:  (1) the worker has returned to work; (2) the worker medically is capable of 
returning to substantially similar employment; or (3) the worker has achieved maximum 
medical recovery.  Id.  Maximum medical recovery is achieved when healing is 
complete, and the extent of permanent disability can be determined.  Armstrong Tire & 
Rubber Co. v. Kubli, 312 N.W.2d 60, 65 (Iowa Ct. App. 1981).  Neither maintenance 
medical care nor an employee's continuing to have pain or other symptoms necessarily 
prolongs the healing period.  Id. 
The hearing report notes that Deere paid temporary partial disability benefits 
from June 25, 2022 through August 9, 2022 and temporary total disability benefits from 
August 10, 2022 through August 17, 2022.  There does not appear to be a dispute 
about this issue, other than potential underpayment of the weekly rate.  The 
undersigned leaves it to the parties to calculate any underpayment of benefits based 
upon the weekly rate determined in this decision.  Should additional dispute exist and 
further calculations be required by the undersigned, the parties should file a request for 
rehearing and provide their respective calculations of the underpayment of healing 
period benefits due.  However, the undersigned is confident counsel can calculate and 
come to a mutual agreement as to any underpayment of the healing period benefits 
based upon the weekly rate awarded in this decision.

concluded claimant failed to prove entitlement to any benefits for the alleged low back 
injury or the second knee surgery.

85.34(2)(v). The extent of scheduled member disability benefits to which an injured 
worker is entitled is determined by using the functional method. Functional disability is

"limited to the loss of the physiological capacity of the body or body part.” Mortimer v.

Having determined the weekly benefits to which claimant is entitled, I need to

However, claimant challenges inclusion of the earnings for the week of February 18,

The basis of compensation is the weekly earnings of the employee at the time of 
the injury.  Iowa Code § 85.36 (2015).  Weekly earnings are the gross salary, wages, or 
earnings to which an employee would have been entitled had the employee worked the 
customary hours for the full pay period in which the employee was injured as the 
employer regularly required for the work or employment.  Id.  The various subsections of 
section 85.36 set forth methods of computing weekly earnings depending upon the type 
of earnings and employment.  Id. 
The gross weekly earnings of an employee who has worked for the employer for 
less than the full thirteen calendar weeks immediately preceding the injury are 
determined by looking at the earnings of other similarly situated employees employed 
over that full period.  Iowa Code § 85.36(7) (2015).  If earnings of similar employees 
cannot be determined, the employee’s earnings shall be determined by averaging the 
employee's weekly earnings computed for the number of weeks that the employee has 
been in the employ of the employer.  Id. 
Having considered the specific dispute of the parties over inclusion of the 
earnings for the week of February 18, 2022, I found those earnings do not fairly reflect 
claimant’s typical earnings for the period immediately preceding claimant’s work injury.

be $753.56.  The parties stipulated claimant is single and entitled to one exemption.

Mr. Drees also seeks an award for reimbursement of his independent medical 
evaluation pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.39(2).  Specifically, claimant seeks award 
of his second independent medical evaluation fee with Dr. Bansal.  From this record, it 
appears Deere appropriately reimbursed claimant for Dr. Bansal’s initial independent

Claimant urges an award for his second independent medical evaluation.  He 
asserts that claimant required a second knee surgery and developed low back pain, 
thus necessitating a second IME by Dr. Bansal.  Mr. Drees urges, “It would be unjust to 
limit an injured worker like Mr. Drees to only one IME when injuries and symptoms 
evolve over time. It is unreasonable to assume his impairment remained the same after 
undergoing a second surgery, developing complications from limping, etc., and the 
onset of back pain.”  (Claimant’s Post-Hearing Brief, p. 31)  Instead, claimant urges, 
“Given the evolving nature of Mr. Drees’ condition and the need for a fair and accurate 
impairment evaluation, he is entitled to reimbursement for his second IME with Dr. 
Bansal.”  (Claimant’s Post-Hearing Brief, p. 31) 
Mr. Drees cites no legal authority that permits the award of a second IME.  Iowa 
Code section 85.39 permits claimant to obtain reimbursement for “the reasonable fee 
for a subsequent examination.”  In Des Moines Area Regional Transit Authority v. 
Young, 867 N.W.2d 839, 847 (Iowa 2015), the Iowa Supreme Court rejected a similar 
“fairness” argument noting that claimant argued, “the process unfairly limits her to one 
reimbursable, independent evaluation and could permit employers to sabotage the 
claim process…. Yet, these arguments have been impliedly rejected by the legislature 
in enacting section 85.39.” 
Similarly, in Larson Manufacturing Co., Inc. v. Thorson, 763 N.W.2d 842, 861 
(Iowa 2009), the Iowa Supreme Court specifically considered a request by an injured 
worker for reimbursement of more than one independent medical evaluation.  The Court 
noted, “Having paid for the prior examination, Larson contends the plain language of 
section 85.39 precludes its liability for the subsequent examination by Dr. Kuhnlein.  We 
agree.”  Id.  Accordingly, claimant’s argument in this case for reimbursement of a 
second independent medical evaluation has been squarely considered and rejected by 
the Iowa Supreme Court.  Id.  I conclude claimant failed to establish entitlement to 
reimbursement for Dr. Bansal’s second examination pursuant to Iowa Code section 
85.39(2). 
Claimant also asserts a claim for penalty benefits against Deere for 
unreasonable delay in payment of permanent partial disability benefits.   If weekly 
compensation benefits are not fully paid when due, section 10A.315(4) requires that 
additional benefits be awarded unless the employer shows reasonable cause or excuse 
for the delay or denial.  Iowa Code § 10A.315(4) (2023); Robbennolt v. Snap-on Tools 
Corp., 555 N.W.2d 229, 236-37 (Iowa 1996).  
“Benefits may be delayed, without penalty, during such time as an employer’s 
liability is “fairly debatable”. Kiesecker v. Webster City Custom Meats, Inc., 528 N.W.2d 

DREES V. JOHN DEERE WATERLOO WORKS/SECOND INJURY FUND OF IOWA 
Page 15 
 
 
109, 111 (Iowa 1995).  A delay attributable to the time required to perform a reasonable 
investigation is not unreasonable.  Id.  
It also is not unreasonable to deny a claim when a good faith issue of law or fact 
makes the employer’s liability fairly debatable.  Rodda v. Vermeer Mfg., 734 N.W.2d 
480, 483 (Iowa 2007).  An issue of law is fairly debatable if viable arguments exist in 
favor of each party.  Covia v. Robinson, 507 N.W.2d 411, 412 (Iowa 1993).  An issue of 
fact is fairly debatable if substantial evidence exists which would support a finding 
favorable to the employer.  Gilbert v. USF Holland, Inc., 637 N.W.2d 194, 199 (Iowa 
2001).  
“An employer’s bare assertion that a claim is ‘fairly debatable’ does not make it 
so.”  Meyers v. Holiday Express Corp., 557 N.W.2d 502, 505 (Iowa 1996).  The 
employer must assert facts upon which the commissioner could reasonably find that the 
claim was “fairly debatable.”  Id.   
If the employer fails to show reasonable cause or excuse for the delay or denial, 
the commissioner shall impose a penalty in an amount up to 50 percent of the amount 
unreasonably delayed or denied.  Christensen v. Snap-on Tools Corp., 554 N.W.2d 
254, 259 (Iowa 1996).  The factors to be considered in determining the amount of the 
penalty include the length of the delay, the number of delays, the information available 
to the employer and the employer’s past record of penalties.  Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d 
at 238. 
Dr. Naylor provided an impairment rating on February 26, 2023.  (Jt. Ex. 7, pp. 
79-81)  Deere conceded liability for the impairment rating on April 6, 2023.  It issued 
payment for all healing period and permanent partial disability benefits, with interest, 
pursuant to Dr. Naylor’s opinion and impairment rating on April 7, 2023.  (Claimant’s Ex. 
4, p. 36)  This represents a 40-day delay between the issuance of Dr. Naylor’s opinions 
and payment of the weekly benefits. 
There was certainly a competing causation opinion in this record that could have 
made defendant’s ongoing denial reasonable.  Deere clearly was continuing its 
investigation of the claim, both by seeking clarification of Dr. Naylor’s opinion as well as 
contemplating the competing opinions.  Deere appears to have acted in good faith in 
deciding to accept the left knee injury and pay benefits, as well as reimburse past 
medical expenses.  
I conclude Deere can establish it had a good faith basis for ongoing denial and 
that its denial was preceded by a reasonable investigation.  However, there is no 
communication from Deere to claimant explaining the basis for its ongoing denial or for 
its delay in payment of benefits after receipt of Dr. Naylor’s impairment rating report.  
Defendants bear the burden to establish all three prongs of the penalty statute to avoid 
imposition of a penalty when benefits are not paid on a timely basis.  Iowa Code section 
10A.315(4)(c).  Therefore, I conclude that an award of penalty benefits in some amount 
is appropriate.

The purpose of Iowa Code section 10A.315(4) is both punishment for 
unreasonable conduct but also deterrence for future cases.  Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d  
at 237.  In this regard, the Commission is given discretion to determine the amount of 
the penalty imposed with a maximum penalty of 50 percent of the amount of the 
delayed, or denied, benefits.  Christensen v. Snap-On Tools Corp., 554 N.W.2d 254, 
261 (Iowa 1996).  In exercising its discretion, the agency must consider factors such as 
the length of the delays, the number of delays, the information available to the employer 
regarding the employee’s injury and wages, and the employer’s past record of penalties.  
Meyers v. Holiday Express Corp., 557 N.W.2d 502, 505 (Iowa 1996).   
Having considered the relevant factors and the purposes of the penalty statute, I 
conclude that a section 86.13 penalty in the amount of $300.00 is appropriate in this 
case.  Given that Deere conducted a reasonable investigation and had a reasonable 
basis for ongoing denial prior to its acceptance and payment of weekly benefits, the 
punitive factor and the use of a penalty to avoid future similar conduct is lessened.  
Nevertheless, Deere failed to establish that it contemporaneously conveyed its good 
faith basis for denial or delay of benefits.  Exercising the agency’s discretion and 
considering the goals and purpose of the penalty statute, I conclude that a penalty in the 
amount of $300.00 is appropriate and sufficient to satisfy the goals of the statute.                   
Mr. Drees also asserts a claim against the Second Injury Fund of Iowa.  Claimant

Iowa Code section 85.64 governs Second Injury Fund liability.  Before liability of 
the Fund is triggered, three requirements must be met.  First, the employee must have 
lost or lost the use of a hand, arm, foot, leg, or eye.  Second, the employee must sustain 
a loss or loss of use of another specified member or organ through a compensable 
injury.  Third, permanent disability must exist as to both the initial injury and the second 
injury.   
The Second Injury Fund Act exists to encourage the hiring of handicapped 
persons by making a current employer responsible only for the amount of disability 
related to an injury occurring while that employer employed the handicapped individual 
as if the individual had had no preexisting disability.  See Anderson v. Second Injury 
Fund, 262 N.W.2d 789, 792 (Iowa 1978); 15 Iowa Practice, Workers’ Compensation, 
Lawyer, Section 17:1, p. 211 (2014-2015). 
The Fund is responsible for the industrial disability present after the second injury 
that exceeds the disability attributable to the first and second injuries.  Section 85.64.  
Second Injury Fund of Iowa v. Braden, 459 N.W.2d 467, 471 (Iowa 1990); Second Injury

The Second Injury Fund is responsible for the industrial disability resulting from 
the two qualifying injuries but is given credit against that industrial disability award for 
the functional impairment for each injury.  Industrial disability represents the claimant’s 
loss of future earning capacity.  When assessing industrial disability, functional 
impairment is an element to be considered.  However, consideration must also be given 
to claimant’s age, proximity to retirement, education, qualifications, inability to engage in 
employment for which the employee is fitted, ability to rehabilitate or retrain, the local

The final issue submitted for resolution is a request for costs.  Specifically, Deere 
seeks assessment for the cost of Dr. Fields’ report.  Defendant included this cost at 
Deere’s Ex. G, p. 56.  The invoice from Dr. Fields lumps all charges for review of 
medical records and drafting his report together for a total of 5.75 hours, or $4,025.00.   
Agency rule 876 IAC 4.33(6) permits assessment of “the reasonable costs of 
obtaining no more than two doctors’ or practitioners’ reports.”  However, cost statutes 
are construed strictly.  Des Moines Area Regional Transit Authority v. Young, 867 
N.W.2d 839, 846 (Iowa 2015).  Rule 876 IAC 4.33(6) only permits the assessment of 
the cost of obtaining a report for hearing.  The process of obtaining a report is separate 
from the expense and process of obtaining an examination.  Only that portion of the 
expense related to preparing a report in lieu of hearing testimony or a medical 
deposition is permitted as a cost.  Id. at 844-845.   
Dr. Fields’ invoice does not delineate, or break down, the amount of time or 
expense necessary for medical records review versus report preparation.  His report 
also lumps in the amount of time he spent on both tasks.  It is not possible in this record 
to determine the precise amount of time or expense related to preparation of Dr. Fields’ 
report versus other related medical tasks.  I decline to speculate as to the amount of 
time or expense that is associated with preparation of the report.  Therefore, I decline to 
assess defendant’s requested cost.
