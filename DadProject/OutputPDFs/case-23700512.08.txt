STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A.  The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Jerimy Matheny.  
Claimant appeared personally and through attorney, Nate Willems.  Defendants 
appeared through their attorney, Bryan Brooks. 
 
The alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on July 2, 2025.  The 
proceedings were digitally recorded through Google Meet.  That recording constitutes 
the official record of this proceeding.  Pursuant to the Commissioner’s Order, the 
undersigned has been delegated authority to issue a final agency decision in this 
alternate medical care proceeding.  Therefore, this ruling is designated final agency 
action and any appeal of the decision would be to the Iowa District Court pursuant to 
Iowa Code section 17A. 
 
The record consists Claimant’s Exhibits 1 through 7 and Defense Exhibits A 
through B, which were received without objection.  The claimant testified live and under 
oath.  In addition, I have taken administrative notice of File Nos. 23700512.02 through 
23700512.07.  The defendants do not dispute liability for claimant’s work injury. 
 
ISSUE 
 
The issue presented for resolution is whether the claimant has proven that 
defendants abandoned medical care.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
Jerimy Matheny testified live and under oath at the audio hearing.  His testimony 
is found to be credible. 
 
The parties have stipulated that Mr. Matheny sustained an injury which arose out 
of and in the course of his employment on October 4, 2022.  The defendants have 
authorized medical treatment. 
 
This is the seventh Application for Alternate Medical Care filed by Mr. Matheny.  
He alleges that defendants have a long pattern of failing to timely authorize the care he 
requires.  In his petition, he requests a ruling that defendants have “abandoned care” 
allowing him to seek treatment of his own choosing at defendants’ expense.  
Alternatively, he requests the agency order defendants to provide the care 
recommended by their authorized physicians. 
 
The claimant has two conditions for which he seeks treatment.  First, he requires 
further evaluation of his low back.  A treating physician, Chad Abernathey, M.D., 
recommended evaluation at the University of Iowa Hospitals and Clinics for this 
condition.  This was the subject of a previous alternate care claim which resulted in a 
Consent Order that defendants would authorize and provide such treatment.  Second, 
his authorized treating physicians have recommended a pain psychology examination.  
This was also previously recommended and the subject of a Consent Order. 
 
The last Consent Order was entered in March 2025.  I ordered the defendants to 
provide treatment with a pain psychologist.  This was previously ordered and agreed 
upon in January 2025. 
 
At hearing, the defendants argued that they have authorized this treatment and 
expressed frustration that the treatment has not occurred.  In essence, the defendants 
argue that they have authorized the treatment, but for reasons beyond their control, the 
medical providers have not been able to set up these appointments for Mr. Matheny.  
To this end, the defendants provided exhibits demonstrating their efforts since the last 
Consent Order in March to arrange the appointments.  (See Defendants’ Exhibits A and 
B) 
 
Mr. Matheny testified credibly that he has contacted both medical providers in 
question:  UIHC and St. Luke’s Pain Psychology.  He testified that both providers 
informed him that they had not received approval from the carrier (at least back when 
he contacted them in April and May 2025).  His testimony is supported by Claimant’s 
Exhibits 1 through 7, which demonstrate his counsel’s efforts to secure his treatment 
through correspondence.

REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Iowa Code Section 85.27 (2013). 
 
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See 
Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  Determining what care is 
reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.  Id.  The employer’s obligation turns 
on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.  Id.; Harned v. Farmland 
Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983).   
 
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995). 
 
An employer’s statutory right is to select the providers of care and the employer 
may consider cost and other pertinent factors when exercising its choice. Long, at 124. 
An employer (typically) is not a licensed health care provider and does not possess 
medical expertise. Accordingly, an employer does not have the right to control the 
methods the providers choose to evaluate, diagnose and treat the injured employee. An 
employer is not entitled to control a licensed health care provider’s exercise of 
professional judgment. Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 866389 (Declaratory 
Ruling, May 19, 1988). An employer’s failure to follow recommendations of an 
authorized physician in matters of treatment is commonly a failure to provide reasonable 
treatment. Boggs v. Cargill, Inc., File No. 1050396 (Alt. Care, January 31, 1994). 
 
Very little is actually disputed in this claim.  The claimant is seeking treatment 
recommended by the authorized medical providers, and the defendants profess to 
agree with this treatment.  In fact, defendants have offered some evidence that they 
have provided written authorization to the treatment providers of this fact.  Nevertheless, 
the treatment has not occurred.  The treatment has been delayed for months.  
 
The employer’s right to direct medical care is a fundamental cornerstone of the 
Iowa’s workers’ compensation system.  With this significant right to direct care comes 
the responsibility to ensure that the treatment is offered promptly and without undue 
inconvenience.  Based upon the record before me, it appears that the right people from 
the insurance bureaucracy have been unable to communicate effectively with the 
correct people from the medical bureaucracy in order to allow this to happen.  Medical

and/or insurance bureaucratic delay is not a legal excuse for the failure to provide the 
care in a timely, convenient manner.  I cannot find in this record, however, that 
defendants have abandoned care.  The defense exhibits demonstrate to my satisfaction 
that the carrier has at least acted in good faith to arrange these specific appointments.  I 
do find though, that the care has not been offered promptly and claimant is entitled to 
alternate care. 
 
Regarding the specific treatment required at this time, Mr. Matheny is granted the 
authority to arrange these appointments on his own, at the employer’s expense.  My 
concern in allowing this remedy is that the medical provider may refuse to arrange an 
appointment which is not arranged by the defendant carrier.  The claimant should 
provide this decision to the medical providers, as well as the contact information for 
defense counsel in the event that the provider needs specific authorization from the 
insurance carrier. 
 
ORDER 
 
THEREFORE IT IS ORDERED: 
 
The claimant's petition for alternate medical care is GRANTED.  The claimant 
may arrange appointments at the UIHC and St. Luke’s Pain Psychology as set forth 
above.  This care is authorized.
