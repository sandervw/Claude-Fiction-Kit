STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Teresa Walton, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits against Compass Group, employer, and New Hampshire 
Insurance Company, insurance carrier, both as defendants.  
This case was heard on January 23, 2025, and considered fully submitted on 
February 21, 2025, upon the simultaneous filing of briefs.  
The record consists of Joint Exhibits 1-6, Claimant’s Exhibits 1-6, Defendants’ 
Exhibits A-F, and testimony of claimant. 
There are two underlying legal proceedings related to the case at bar. On June 
24, 2021, File No.1663689.02 was heard and considered fully submitted on July 22, 
2021. At issue was whether claimant sustained a left knee, left hip and low back injury 
arising out of and in the course of her employment. The arbitration decision found that 
claimant did sustain injuries to her left knee, left hip and low back arising from a work 
incident on December 27, 2018. This decision was appealed to the commissioner and 
upheld as to the issues of causation and extent.1  
Claimant then sought additional medical care, File No. 1663689.06. Defendants 
denied claimant the right to additional medical care on the grounds that the requested 
care was not causally related to the December 27, 2018, work injury. Based on the

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e) 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury actually occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 
employment. Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 150 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. 
Dial Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309, 311 (Iowa 1996). The words “arising out of” refer to the 
cause or source of the injury. The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 
circumstances of the injury. 2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124, 128 (Iowa 
1995).  An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists 
between the injury and the employment. Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. The injury must 
be a rational consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely 
incidental to the employment. Koehler Elec. v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1, 3 (Iowa 2000); 
Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it 
happens within a period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may 
be when performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties 
or doing an activity incidental to them. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d at 150. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable

Page 8 
 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 
621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 
(Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
Defendants’ argument is twofold. First, claimant’s post-December 2018 work has 
aggravated her condition, and defendants are not responsible for aggravated symptoms 
caused by other employment. Second, even if the symptoms claimant currently suffers 
are causally connected to the December 2018 injury, claimant is not entitled to 
additional treatment as she has testified that the treatment is not helpful.  
Claimant left defendant employer in January 2000 due to a falling out with her 
supervisor. She obtained full-time employment with Sevita f/k/a REM. As a caretaker in 
the employ of Sevita, claimant goes to specific homes and provides in-home care for 
clients. This includes cooking their meals, bathing them, showering them and putting 
them to bed. (Tr., pp. 28-29) Her shift is overnight from 2:30 PM on Thursday to 9:00 
AM on Friday and 12:00 PM Sunday to 9:00 AM Monday. (DE F:47) She described the 
clients as high functioning with the ability to walk and talk. (DE F:48)  
Claimant testified at hearing that the work she currently does aggravates her low 
back and left hip pain that developed from the December 2018 injury. (Tr., p. 30) This 
testimony does not give rise to a superseding cause. Hayward v. P.D.A. Inc., 573 
N.W.2d, 29, 32 (Iowa 1997)  To conclude that an event constitutes a superseding 
cause, there must be a later-occurring event that breaks the chain of causal events.  
(Id.)  However, there is no new injury identified by any of the experts that is the origin of 
claimant’s complaints. Dr. Kim initially found that a repeat MRI was warranted, but after

being informed that claimant had obtained other employment since leaving defendant 
employer, Dr. Kim opined no further medical treatment would be causally related to, or 
necessitated by, claimant’s remote work injury. Dr. Kim did not explain why or how the 
symptoms claimant currently suffers relate to the new employment as opposed to an

In July 2023, Dr. Kim acknowledged that claimant’s lumbar spine showed an L4-

Dr. Chen concluded that claimant’s current complaints arose from personal 
muscle imbalances developed from a long history of personal and general health factors 
including physical and muscular deconditioning, general soft-tissue inflexibility, and a 
lack of a consistent stretching exercise program.  
Dr. Chen’s opinions do not reflect claimant’s ongoing complaints since the 
December 2018 knee fracture. It was the knee fracture and period of immobilization that 
led to the altered gait pattern and the hip and low back pain. While claimant’s knee pain 
has largely been resolved by surgery, the hip and low back pain have lingered, 
exacerbated by work and activity. The pain symptoms have been causally linked by her 
treating healthcare professionals who have been following claimant for years. Ms. 
Collum and Dr. Mathew both agreed that claimant’s work injury was a substantial 
contributing factor for the treatment that Dr. Mathew and Ms. Collum provided in 2021, 
2022, 2023, and 2024.  
The claimant’s current condition as it relates to the low back and left hip arose 
out of the December 2018 injury. Activity at home and at work, for a different employer, 
aggravate the symptoms claimant developed following the December 2018 injury.  
Thus, a substantial factor in claimant’s current symptoms is the December 2018 injury. 
The work injury does not need to be the only cause of claimant’s symptoms. Instead, it 
must be a substantial factor in bringing about the symptoms.  
Based on the medical opinions of Dr. Mathew and Ms. Collum, as well as 
claimant’s consistent and ongoing complaints, it is found that claimant’s current 
condition, specifically pain and discomfort and lack of function in the low back and left 
hip, is related to the work injury of December 2018.

When an employer contests liability, the employee can recover the unauthorized 
medical costs by proving by a preponderance of the evidence that such care was 
reasonable and beneficial under the totality of the circumstances. Bell Bros., 779 
N.W.2d 193, 206 (2010)   
Defendants contested liability in the July 27, 2023, alternate care petition. 
Claimant testified that the care provided by Dr. Mathew was helpful but not lasting. Dr. 
Mathew opined that the treatment he was providing and that was being sought in the 
alternate care proceeding was reasonable and necessary. Claimant did not get lasting 
relief from the injections or therapy, but the injections did provide relief for a short time. 
The Bell Bros. decision does not require the treatment be lasting in order to be deemed 
beneficial. Physical therapy was less successful, but it was part of the treatment 
regimen that was recommended by Dr. Mathew and was reasonable and beneficial 
under the totality of circumstances in providing claimant with coping skills to continue to 
work and function.  It is found that the care provided from June 9, 2021, through March 
20, 2024 by Dr. Mathew and Ms. Collum was reasonable and beneficial under the 
totality of circumstances.

charge was not entered into evidence at the June 21, 2021, arbitration hearing as it was 
not available at the date of the hearing. It should be reimbursed herein and is not 
disallowed simply because it was not an itemized bill at the time of the June 24, 2021, 
hearing.

The claimant also seeks ongoing treatment from Dr. Mathew, Dr. Magid and 
Associates of PM&R for her low back and hip symptoms.  
Under Iowa law, the employer is required to provide care to an injured employee 
and is permitted to choose the care.  Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 
562 N.W.2d 433, 435 (Iowa 1997). 
[T]he employer is obliged to furnish reasonable services and supplies to 
treat an injured employee, and has the right to choose the care.  The 
treatment must be offered promptly and be reasonably suited to treat the 
injury without undue inconvenience to the employee.  If the employee has 
reason to be dissatisfied with the care offered, the employee should 
communicate the basis of such dissatisfaction to the employer, in writing if 
requested, following which the employer and the employee may agree to 
alternate care reasonably suited to treat the injury.  If the employer and 
employee cannot agree on such alternate care, the commissioner may, 
upon application and reasonable proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and 
order other care.  Id. 
 
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  Long v. 
Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122, 123 (Iowa 1995).  Determining what care is

Page 12 
 
reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.  Id.  The employer’s obligation turns 
on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.  Id. at 124; Harned v. Farmland 
Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98, 101 (Iowa 1983).  In Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d 
at 437, the court approvingly quoted Bowles v. Los Lunas Schools, 109 N.M. 100, 106, 
781 P.2d 1178, 1184 (N.M. Ct. App. 1989): 
[T]he words “reasonable” and “adequate” appear to describe the same 
standard. 
[The New Mexico rule] requires the employer to provide a certain standard 
of care and excuses the employer from any obligation to provide other 
services only if that standard is met.  We construe the terms "reasonable” 
and “adequate” as describing care that is both appropriate to the injury and 
sufficient to bring the worker to maximum recovery. 
The commissioner is justified in ordering alternate care when employer-authorized care 
has not been effective, and evidence shows that such care is “inferior or less extensive” 
care than other available care requested by the employee.  Long, 528 N.W.2d at 124; 
Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 437. 
Defendants current proffer of medical care is no medical care and, therefore, is 
“inferior or less extensive” than the care requested by claimant.  
As it was previously found that claimant’s current complaints are causally 
connected to the work injury of December 2018, claimant is entitled to ongoing care 
from Dr. Mathew, who is a pain management specialist, and Dr. Magid, whose osteo-
manipulation has provided the most relief, and any other care recommended by Dr. 
Mathew and/or Dr. Magid or Associates of PM&R.  
Claimant also seeks reimbursement for the IME of Dr. Mathew. However, Iowa 
Code section 85.39 is not applicable for a medical bill only claim. (See Iowa Code 
section 85.39)
