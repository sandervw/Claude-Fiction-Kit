STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Joseph Demers, claimant, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from the Second Injury Fund of Iowa (“the Fund”), as defendant.  
It should be noted that Demers filed a second petition against employer, Schuster 
Company, and its insurance carrier, Travelers Indemnity Company of Connecticut, and 
the Fund.  (See Agency File No. 23009349.01) The two petitions were consolidated and 
set for hearing on October 23, 2024.  At the time of the hearing, Demers moved to 
dismiss File No. 23009349.01.  The unresisted motion was granted.  Therefore, the 
defendant employer and insurance carrier did not participate in the hearing.  The 
claimant and the Fund proceeded to hearing via Zoom videoconference on October 23, 
2024.         
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised 
or discussed in this decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.  
Joseph Demers was the only witness to testify live at trial.  The evidentiary 
record also includes Joint Exhibits 1-6, Claimant’s Exhibits 1-6, and Defendant’s 
Exhibits AA-GG.  Defendant objected to Claimant’s Exhibit 1, pages 27A and 27B, an 
October 7, 2024, report from Steven G. McCloy, M.D., because the report was not 
produced to defendant until October 7, 2024, after the 30-day exchange deadline.  
Defendant argued that receipt of the evidence would be unfairly prejudicial to the 
defendant because they received the report a mere 16 days prior to the hearing.  Dr. 
McCloy is not a treating physician in this case.  The report was the first report to assign 
an impairment rating pursuant to the AMA Guides, Fifth Edition, with regard to the 
alleged first date of loss in this matter.  Defendant argues that claimant had ample 
opportunity to timely obtain the report and took no steps to minimize the surprise and 
unfair prejudice to the defendant.  I find that claimant producing a report from a non-

ISSUES 
 
The parties submitted the following issues for resolution: 
1. Whether claimant sustained a fist qualifying loss to his left lower extremity in 
2014. 
 
2. Whether claimant sustained a second qualifying loss to his right lower 
extremity/foot on December 29, 2021. 
 
3. If so, the amount of benefits claimant is entitled to receive from the Second 
Injury Fund of Iowa. 
 
4. The amount of credit the Fund is entitled to receive against any award.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
The question of medical causation is “essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony.” Cedar Rapids Cmty. Sch. Dist. v. Pease, 807 N.W.2d 839, 844-45 (Iowa 
2011). The commissioner, as the trier of fact, must “weigh the evidence and measure 
the credibility of witnesses.” Id. The trier of fact may accept or reject expert testimony, 
even if uncontroverted, in whole or in part. Frye, 569 N.W.2d at 156. When considering 
the weight of an expert opinion, the fact-finder may consider whether the examination 
occurred shortly after the claimant was injured, the compensation arrangement, the 
nature and extent of the examination, the expert's education, experience, training, and 
practice, and “all other factors which bear upon the weight and value” of the 
opinion. Rockwell Graphic Sys., Inc. v. Prince, 366 N.W.2d 187, 192 (Iowa 1985). 
Unrebutted expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland 
Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994).  
Claimant seeks benefits from the Second Injury Fund of Iowa.  Iowa Code 
section 85.64 governs Second Injury Fund liability.  Before liability of the Fund is 
triggered, three requirements must be met.  First, the employee must have lost or lost 
the use of a hand, arm, foot, leg, or eye.  Second, the employee must sustain a loss or 

DEMERS V. SECOND INJURY FUND OF IOWA 
Page 9 
 
loss of use of another specified member or organ through a compensable injury.  Third, 
permanent disability must exist as to both the initial injury and the second injury.   
The Iowa Supreme Court has explained the concept of the Second Injury Fund.  
The Court stated,  
The milieu in which the second or subsequent injury fund statute operates 
is the situation of an individual within the labor force, whether or not he is 
at the moment an ‘employee’ within the meaning of the Workmen's 
Compensation Act, who is suffering from some condition of permanent 
disability that affects his employability. The source of this pre-existing 
disability is normally of no importance but it must be permanent and must 
tend to act as a hindrance to the individual's ability to obtain or retain 
effective employment. Such individuals are what society commonly refers 
to as ‘the handicapped,‘ and the primary purpose of second injury fund 
statutes is to encourage the hiring of the handicapped. 
 
Anderson v. Second Inj. Fund, 262 N.W.2d 789, 791 (Iowa 1978). 
 
The Iowa Supreme court has addressed what constitutes a first qualifying loss.  
The Court stated,  
To qualify as a first loss for purposes of section 85.64, the first loss does 
not need to be work-related. Shank, 516 N.W.2d at 812. Additionally, the 
first loss need not be a total loss or loss of use. Irish v. McCreary Saw Mill, 
175 N.W.2d 364, 368 (Iowa 1970). In 1990 the Fund again asked this 
court to hold that the first loss must be “at minimum, ‘a substantial loss 
that acts as a handicap to one's employability.’ ” Second Injury Fund v. 
Braden, 459 N.W.2d 467, 469 (Iowa 1990). In Braden we decided not to 
retreat from our prior position and held that a scheduled injury under 
section 85.34(2) affecting a hand, arm, foot, leg, or eye is a qualifying first 
loss. Id. at 469–70. The reason for allowing a scheduled injury affecting a 
hand, arm, foot, leg, or eye to be a qualifying first loss is because a 
scheduled injury evaluates an injured person's permanent partial disability 
by determining the loss of physiological capacity of the affected body part. 
Bergeson, 526 N.W.2d at 547. We see no reason to retreat from our long-
standing law that a scheduled injury under section 85.34(2) affecting a 
hand, arm, foot, leg, or eye is a qualifying loss. 
Second Inj. Fund of Iowa v. George, 737 N.W.2d 141, 146 (Iowa 2007). 
Under the Iowa Workers' Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is 
compensated either for a loss or loss of use of a scheduled member under Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(a)-(u) or as an unscheduled injury pursuant to the provisions of section 
85.34(2)(v). An injury to the lower extremity is considered a scheduled member injury 
under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(p). The extent of scheduled member disability 
benefits to which an injured worker is entitled is determined by using the functional 
method. Functional disability is “limited to the loss of the physiological capacity of the 

DEMERS V. SECOND INJURY FUND OF IOWA 
Page 10 
 
body or body part.” Mortimer v. Fruehauf Corp., 502 N.W.2d 12, 15 (Iowa 1993); 
Sherman v. Pella Corp., 576 N.W.2d 312, 320 (Iowa 1998). 
The first question is whether claimant lost the use of one of the enumerated 
members, in this case his left lower extremity.  Claimant bears the burden of proving 
that permanent impairment resulted from the first injury, whether by a permanency 
rating, and/or by other credible evidence of permanency or work restrictions.  Haynes v. 
Second Injury Fund, 547 N.W.2d 11, 14 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996).   
Iowa Code section 85.34(x) permanent disabilities states: 
x. In all cases of permanent partial disability described in paragraphs “a” 
through “u”, or paragraph “v” when determining functional disability and 
not loss of earning capacity, the extent of loss or percentage of permanent 
impairment shall be determined solely by utilizing the guides to the 
evaluation of permanent impairment, published by the American 
medical association, as adopted by the workers' compensation 
commissioner by rule pursuant to chapter 17A. Lay testimony or agency 
expertise shall not be utilized in determining loss or percentage of 
permanent impairment pursuant to paragraphs “a” through “u”, or 
paragraph “v” when determining functional disability and not loss of

This agency has cautioned against impairment ratings that do not comply with 
the AMA Guides, Fifth Edition. See Hill v. Vermeer Corp., File No. 5066032 (App. 
January 30, 2020). In this case, Dr. McCloy offered his opinions based on the AMA 
Guides, 6th Edition.  Furthermore, for the reasons set forth above, I found Dr. McCloy’s 
opinions carry no weight.  Based on the above findings of fact, I conclude Dr. McCloy’s 
various impairment ratings for the left lower extremity did not meet the criteria set forth 
in the Fifth Edition of the AMA Guides.  Thus, I conclude claimant has failed to prove 
that prior to the work injury he sustained a scheduled injury under section 85.34(2) 
affecting a hand, arm, foot, leg, or eye.  
In the alternative, claimant argues that he does not need an impairment rating to 
establish a first qualifying loss. In support of his position he states that Iowa Courts have 
opined that a medical impairment rating is not an absolute legal requirement to establish 
a qualifying injury.  See Second Injury Fund of Iowa v. George, 737 N.W.2d 141 (Iowa 
2007).  A deputy can properly find a qualifying loss from other evidence such as the fact 
of surgery, activity restrictions, a medically advised job change or loss of a job.  See Id.   
The Fund contends that due to the 2017 legislative addition of subparagraph 
(2)(x) to section 85.34, an impairment rating under the Guides is required to prove a first 
qualifying loss.  The Fund argues that because the record is void of any expert 
assigning impairment that complies with the Guides, Fifth Edition, claimant failed to 
carry his burden to demonstrate he sustained a first qualifying loss.       
I recognize the Iowa Supreme Court has repeatedly stated this agency lacks the 
legislature's expressly vested authority to interpret workers' compensation statutes. 
See, e.g., Ramirez-Trujillo v. Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 N.W.2d 759, 770 (Iowa 2016), 
reh'g denied (May 27, 2016). Practically speaking, however, this agency acts as the 
front-line authority in interpreting statutory workers' compensation provisions, 
particularly when statutory amendments are enacted. Thus, while the appellate courts 
have the final say, statutory interpretation by this agency is a necessary inevitability. 
The Fund argues that subsection x does apply to first alleged injuries for Fund 
purposes.  The Fund contends that the Second Injury Compensation Act is a 
subchapter of Chapter 85 and does not exist independent of other provisions within the 
chapter.  The Fund’s total potential exposure, credit, and commencement date are fixed 
using the schedule set forth in Iowa Code section 85.34.  The Fund’s liability for a 
claimant’s industrial disability is based upon a percentage of 500 weeks, pursuant to the 
compensation for unscheduled injuries set forth in Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v).  See, 
e.g., Second Injury Fund v. Neelans, 436 N.W.2d 355, 357 (Iowa 1989).  Likewise, the 
Fund’s liability for permanent and total disability benefits is assessed pursuant to Iowa 
Code section 85.34(3).  Second Injury Fund v. Shank, 516 N.W.2d 808, 814 (Iowa 
1994).  The Fund contends that when read in the context of the entire statute, the plain 
language of section 85.34(2)(x) applies to the first qualifying injuries, just the same as 
the second qualifying injuries.

The Fund points out that the agency must determine the percentage of 
permanent impairment for a first injury in order to determine what credit the Fund is 
entitled to receive against an award of industrial disability.     
In this case, even if after the 2017 legislative changes an impairment rating under 
the Guides is not required to prove a first qualifying loss, I conclude claimant has still 
failed to carry his burden of proof.  Claimant contends he lost the use of one of the 
enumerated members, specifically his left lower extremity.  Claimant does not have any 
physician-imposed restrictions related to his left lower extremity.  Further, based on the 
above findings of fact, I conclude claimant has not demonstrated that his left lower 
extremity has acted as a hindrance to his ability to obtain or retain effective 
employment. As such, he has not proven a first qualifying loss.  Because claimant failed 
to carry his burden of proof to demonstrate by a preponderance of the evidence that he 
sustained a first qualifying loss, I conclude that liability of the Fund has not been 
triggered. As a result, all remaining issues are rendered moot.
