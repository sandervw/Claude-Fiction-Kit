STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
On January 15, 2026, the claimant filed a petition for alternate medical care 
pursuant to Iowa Code 85.27(4) and 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.48.  The 
defendant filed an answer admitting liability for the injuries to the claimant’s left wrist. 
The undersigned presided over the hearing held electronically and recorded 
digitally on January 28, 2026.  That recording constitutes the official record of the 
proceeding pursuant to 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.48(12).  The claimant 
participated personally and through her attorney, Thomas Wertz.  The defendant 
participated through their attorney, Andrew Duffelmeyer.   
Prior to the hearing, the claimant submitted two pages of exhibits, marked as 
Exhibit 1.  The defendant submitted ten pages of exhibits, marked as Exhibits A through 
I.  The exhibits were received into the record without objection.  The claimant testified 
on her own behalf.  Felicia Hall and Sonja James testified on behalf of the defendant.   
 
On December 12, 2025, the Interim Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commissioner 
issued an order delegating authority to deputy workers’ compensation commissioners, 
such as the undersigned, to issue final agency decisions on applications for alternate 
care.  Consequently, this decision constitutes final agency action, and there is no 
appeal to the commissioner.  Judicial review in a district court pursuant to Iowa Code 
Chapter 17A is the avenue for an appeal. 
ISSUE 
The issue under consideration is whether the defendant should be ordered to 
allow the claimant to leave work one hour early in order to attend physical therapy 
appointments.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
Kyla Farr, the claimant, testified that she is a single mother to four children.  
(Testimony).  Her oldest child is 15 years old, while the youngest is 20 months old.  
(Testimony).  She resides in a home owned by the father of her youngest child.  
(Testimony).  She is in a relationship with the father of her youngest child.  (Testimony).   
 
Ms. Farr works for the University of Iowa Hospital.  (Testimony).  Prior to her 
injury, she worked as a cardiac nurse.  (Testimony).  At the time of the hearing, and for 
other time periods between her injury and the time of the hearing, she worked light duty 
in the CMU/VMU unit.  (Testimony).  This consists of watching patients on video 
monitors in order to make sure that they do not get out of bed.  (Testimony).  Ms. Farr 
indicated that she worked light duty with two other individuals, and that these individuals 
would periodically take breaks as allowed; however, two people would remain on duty in 
order to monitor the patients under their supervision.  (Testimony).   
Prior to her injury, and prior to the hearing, while on light duty, Ms. Farr worked a 
weekend shift on Saturday and Sunday nights from 7:00 p.m. to 7:30 a.m.  (Testimony).  
She testified that this was so that she could remain at home with her children during the 
week.  (Testimony).  On the weekends, her youngest child’s father cares for the child, 
which allows her to work these shifts.  (Testimony).   
 
Felicia Hall, an adjuster from Sedgwick testified that Ms. Farr’s workers’ 
compensation claim is an “accepted claim,” and that the defendant is providing Ms. Farr 
with care as recommended by her treating physician.  (Testimony).  This care includes 
twice-weekly physical therapy sessions.  (Testimony).  Ms. Hall noted that Ms. Farr was 
free to choose physical therapy appointment times that fit her schedule.  (Testimony).  
Ms. Farr testified that she completed a round of physical therapy from February to July 
of 2025.  (Testimony).  During this time, she was allowed to leave work one hour early 
in order to attend her physical therapy appointments, and pick up her youngest child 
from the care of the child’s father.  (Testimony).   
 
Ms. Farr attended physical therapy on December 18, 2025.  (Defendant’s Exhibit 
A:1).  There is no indication on this therapy note as to what time her appointment 
occurred.  (DE A:1).   
 
On Tuesday, December 23, 2025, Ms. Farr attended physical therapy from 7:45 
a.m. to 8:25 a.m.  (DE B:2).  She then attended physical therapy on Monday, December 
29, 2025, from 8:40 a.m. to 9:20 a.m.  (DE C:3).  Ms. Farr’s next appointment was at an 
unlisted time on January 2, 2026.  (DE D:4).  On Friday, January 9, 2026, Ms. Farr 
attended physical therapy from 7:45 a.m. to 8:30 a.m.  (DE E:5).  Ms. Farr next had 
physical therapy on Monday, January 12, 2026, from 7:50 a.m. to 8:30 a.m.  (DE F:6).   
 
On January 12, 2026, the defendant provided a temporary light duty offer of work 
to Ms. Farr.  (DE G:8; H:9).  In response to this, Ms. Farr emailed her supervisor that 
she would take the weekend hours offered in the light duty offer, but that she “…would 
need to leave by [6:30 a.m.] to make it to [physical therapy] on time.”  (DE G:7).

Ms. Farr then attended physical therapy at an unlisted time on Thursday, January

request, they would have done so, as they did in the past.  (Testimony).   
 
Ms. Farr indicated that there have been times where her older children could 
watch her youngest child so that she could attend therapy.  (Testimony).  However, 
these times were mostly due to the children being on winter break from school.  
(Testimony).  Ms. Farr indicated that her youngest child’s father periodically watched the 
child so that she could attend therapy at days or times that are not early on Monday 
mornings.  (Testimony).  However, she further testified that this presented a hardship to 
the father, as he works at a mechanic-type business that has traffic and requires 
attention.  (Testimony).  She indicated that the child’s father could only watch the child 
when his schedule allowed for it.  (Testimony).  Ms. Farr testified that she had no one to 
watch her youngest child when she attends physical therapy, unless she were allowed 
to leave work at 6:30 a.m., in order to attend physical therapy appointments starting at 
7:30 a.m.  (Testimony).  Ms. Farr indicated that she has not sought a babysitter or 
daycare for childcare during her appointments, and she seemed resistant to this idea.  
(Testimony).     
CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Iowa Code 85.27(4) provides, in relevant part: 
For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish reasonable 
services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has the right to 
choose the care….  The treatment must be offered promptly and be 
reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the 
employee.  If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the care 
offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such dissatisfaction 
to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the employer and 
the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited to treat the 
injury.  If the employer and employee cannot agree on such alternate care,

the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable proofs of the 
necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
Iowa Code 85.27(4). See Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 433 (Iowa 
1997).   
“Iowa Code section 85.27(4) affords an employer who does not contest the 
compensability of a workplace injury a qualified statutory right to control the medical 
care provided to an injured employee.”  Ramirez-Trujillo v. Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 
N.W.2d 759, 769 (Iowa 2016) (citing R.R. Donnelly & Sons v. Barnett, 670 N.W.2d 190, 
195, 197 (Iowa 2003)).  “In enacting the right-to-choose provision in section 85.27(4), 
our legislature sought to balance the interests of injured employees against the 
competing interests of their employers.”  Ramirez-Trujillo, 878 N.W.2d at 770-71 (citing 
Bell Bros., 779 N.W.2d at 202, 207; IBP, Inc. v. Harker, 633 N.W.2d 322, 326-27 (Iowa 
2001)).   
The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except where the 
employer has denied liability for the injury.  Iowa Code section 85.27; Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening, October 16, 1975).  An employer’s right to select 
the provider of medical treatment to an injured worker does not include the right to 
determine how an injured worker should be diagnosed, evaluated, treated, or other 
matters of professional medical judgment.  Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 
866389 (Declaratory Ruling, May 19, 1988).  Reasonable care includes care necessary 
to diagnose the condition, and defendants are not entitled to interfere with the medical 
judgment of its own treating physician.  Pote v. Mickow Corp., File No. 694639 (Review-
Reopening Decision, June 17, 1986).   
The employer must furnish “reasonable medical services and supplies and 
reasonable and necessary appliances to treat an injured employee.”  Stone Container 
Corp. v. Castle, 657 N.W.2d 485, 490 (Iowa 2003)(emphasis in original).  Such 
employer-provided care “must be offered promptly and be reasonably suited to treat the 
injury without undue inconvenience to the employee.”  Iowa Code section 85.27(4).  
More specifically in Long, the court noted that “the employer’s obligation under the 
statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.”  Long v. Roberts 
Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122, 128 (Iowa 1995).   
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment - and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See e.g. 
Iowa R. App. P. 14(f)(5); Bell Bros. Heating and Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d 
193, 209 (Iowa 2010); Long, 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  More specifically in Long, 
the court noted that “the employer’s obligation under the statute turns on the question of 
reasonable necessity, not desirability.”  Long, 528 N.W.2d at 128.   
“Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.”  
Long, 528 N.W.2d at 123; Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 436.  As the party 
seeking relief in the form of alternate care, the employee bears the burden of proving

that the authorized care is unreasonable.  Id. at 124; Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d at 209; Pirelli-
Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 436.  Because “the employer’s obligation under the 
statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability,” an injured 
employee’s dissatisfaction with employer-provided care, standing alone, is not enough 
to find such care unreasonable.  Id.  However, if substantial evidence exists that the 
care authorized by the employer was ineffective, it may be found that the care was not 
reasonable.  Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 437.   
In this matter, the claimant alleges that it is unreasonable for the defendant to not 
accommodate her leaving work one hour early, at 6:30 a.m., in order to attend physical 
therapy appointments beginning at 7:30 a.m. on Monday mornings.  This allows her 
youngest child’s father to care for the child until Ms. Farr completes her appointment.  
The defendant argues that they have provided reasonable care, insofar as they agree to 
provide Ms. Farr with two physical therapy appointments per week, at the 
recommendation of a treating provider.   
As Deputy Christenson noted in Hanawalt v. American Bottling Co., “…there is 
no precedent indicating it is unreasonable to not offer childcare as a part of medical 
care.”  See Hanawalt v. American Bottling Co., 2024 WL 436322 (Alt. Care Jan. 25, 
2024).  While I sympathize with Ms. Farr’s position regarding the need for childcare in 
order to attend medical appointments, the question is whether the care provided by the 
defendant is reasonable.  Ms. Farr would like to be released from work one hour early in 
order to have physical therapy on Monday mornings at 7:30 a.m., in order to 
accommodate her and her partner’s schedules to care for their young child.  This is a 
preference or a desire.   
As noted in our caselaw, the question in alternate care cases turns on 
reasonability and not desirability.  Ms. Farr has not shown that the defendant has acted 
unreasonably in providing two physical therapy appointments per week, at the direction 
of the treating provider.  In fact, the defendant has allowed Ms. Farr to seek physical 
therapy at any time or date of her preference during the week.  Ms. Farr desires to leave 
work early in order to complete the physical therapy appointments to comport with her 
partner’s work schedule.  By allowing Ms. Farr to seek two physical therapy 
appointments on days and times of her choosing, the defendant has shown that their 
provision of care is not unduly inconvenient to Ms. Farr.   
Ms. Farr wants to have appointments at the times desirable to her.  Ms. Farr is 
free to seek care during times which she could obtain childcare for her daughter, either 
from her partner or another source.  The defendant has not acted unreasonably, nor is 
their provision of physical therapy at the recommendation of the treating provider 
unreasonable. 
The claimant has not shown that the care provided by the defendant is 
unreasonable.
