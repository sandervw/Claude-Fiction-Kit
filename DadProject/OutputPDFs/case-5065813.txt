STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Che Gomez, claimant, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from Lawson Enterprises, Inc., employer and Markel Insurance 
Company, insurance carrier as defendants.  Hearing was held on June 26, 2020.  This 
case was scheduled to be an in-person hearing occurring in Des Moines.  However, due 
to the outbreak of a pandemic in Iowa, the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commissioner 
ordered all hearings to occur via video means, using CourtCall.  Accordingly, this case 
proceeded to a live video hearing via CourtCall with all participants also appearing 
remotely.     
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised 
or discussed in this decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.  
Che Gomez was the only witness to testify live at trial.  The evidentiary record 
also includes joint exhibits JE1-JE9, claimant’s exhibits 1-11, and defendant’s exhibits 
A-H.  All exhibits were received without objection.  The evidentiary record closed at the 
conclusion of the arbitration hearing.       
The parties submitted post-hearing briefs on July 20, 2020, at which time the 
case was fully submitted to the undersigned.

ISSUES 
The parties submitted the following issues for resolution: 
1. Whether claimant sustained an injury to his left hip as the result of the 
September 29, 2016 work injury. 
2. Whether claimant has reached Maximum Medical Improvement (MMI). 
3. If claimant has reached MMI, what is his entitlement to permanency benefits. 
4. Claimant’s entitlement to temporary weekly benefits.   
5. The appropriate weekly workers’ compensation rate. 
6. Assessment of costs 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned, having considered all of the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
 
Claimant, Che Gomez, has alleged he injured his back and left hip as the result 
of the September 29, 2016 work injury.  The defendants do not dispute that Mr. Gomez 
injured his back as the result of the September 29, 2016 work injury, but defendants do 
dispute that his left hip complaints are related to the work injury.   
 
At the time of the hearing, Mr. Gomez was 39 years old.  He attended high 
school until his sophomore year.  He obtained his GED in 1997.  He also attended 
approximately one and a half years of classes at Des Moines Area Community College.  
He stopped attending classes in order to work and support his family.  (Testimony) 
 
Mr. Gomez began working for defendant employer, Lawson Enterprises, Inc. 
(“Lawson”) in April of 2016.  At the time he was hired, he did not have any physical 
limitations placed on his activities by any medical provider.  He described himself as 
being in good shape.  He enjoyed regular exercise, including jogging.  He participated in 
road runs and marathons.  He also participated in gladiator assault type runs.  These 
consist of 7 mile runs with obstacle courses.   (Testimony)   
 
Mr. Gomez was hired by Lawson as a truck driver.  His duties involved driving a 
dump truck from point A to point B.  Approximately two months into his employment, he 
switched to a truck driver/laborer and received a raise.  His duties as a truck 
driver/laborer included his driving duties, plus laborer duties such as busting out 
concrete, operating a Bobcat, setting forms, puddling concrete, cutting concrete, and 
carrying chunks of concrete.  Up until the September 29, 2016 injury, Mr. Gomez was 
able to perform his duties without any difficulty.  (Testimony)   
 
It is noted that Mr. Gomez did have some medical treatment and injuries prior to 
the September 29, 2016 work injury.  Mr. Gomez was involved in a motorcycle accident 
in 2000.  In 2009 he sought treatment for a lumbar strain.  In 2011 he had low back pain 
following a motor vehicle accident; he underwent physical therapy for several months.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 
6.14(6)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996). 

GOMEZ V. LAWSON ENTERPRISES, INC. 
Page 16 
 
 
While a claimant is not entitled to compensation for the results of a preexisting 
injury or disease, its mere existence at the time of a subsequent injury is not a defense.  
Rose v. John Deere Ottumwa Works, 247 Iowa 900, 76 N.W.2d 756 (1956).  If the 
claimant had a preexisting condition or disability that is materially aggravated, 
accelerated, worsened or lighted up so that it results in disability, claimant is entitled to 
recover.  Nicks v. Davenport Produce Co., 254 Iowa 130, 115 N.W.2d 812 (1962); 
Yeager v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co., 253 Iowa 369, 112 N.W.2d 299 (1961). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony.  The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question.  The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances.  The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); 
Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995).  Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994).  Unrebutted expert medical 
testimony cannot be summarily rejected.  Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 
N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 1994). 
Based on the above findings of fact, I conclude that Mr. Gomez did injure his left 
hip as the result of the September 29, 2016 work injury.   
Next, we turn to the issue of whether Mr. Gomez has reached maximum medical 
improvement.  I conclude that Mr. Gomez has not reached maximum medical 
improvement from his work injury on September 29, 2016.  While Dr. Schmitz’s opinion 
as to MMI may have been correct in 2017, I conclude that, in light of more recent facts 
and the more recent opinions of two physicians, it is not correct now.  Additional 
treatment after the date of the hearing may successfully help Mr. Gomez’s disability.  
Consequently, an assessment of permanent disability cannot be performed at this time.  
The issue of permanent disability is bifurcated to a later date.     
We now turn to Mr. Gomez’s entitlement to temporary weekly benefits.  Injured 
workers are entitled to temporary total/healing period benefits under Iowa Code section 
85.33(1) or section 85.34(1) for an absence from work during a recovery period until 
claimant returns to work; until claimant is medically capable of returning to substantially 
similar work to the work he was performing at the time of the injury; or, until it is 
indicated that significant improvement from the injury is not anticipated whichever 
occurs first. 
It is well settled in Iowa that a healing period may be intermittent. Waldinger 
Corp. v. Mettler, 817 N.W.2d 1 (Iowa 2012); Teel v. McCord, 394 N.W.2d 405 (Iowa 
1986). Healing period may terminate and then begin again. Willis v. Lehigh Portland 
Cement Co., I-2 Iowa Ind. Comm'r Decisons 485 (Review Reopening 1984); Clemens v. 

GOMEZ V. LAWSON ENTERPRISES, INC. 
Page 17 
 
 
Iowa Veterans Home, I-1 Iowa Industrial Comm'r Decisions 35 (Review Reopening 
1984); Riesselman v. Carroll Health Center, III Iowa Ind. Comm'r Report 209 (App. 
1982); Junge v. Century Engineering Corp., II Iowa Industrial Comm'r Report 219 (App. 
1981).  In multiple healing period scenarios, permanent partial disability is due and 
payable after the end of the first healing period and this is the time interest on unpaid 
benefits begins. Teel v. McCord, 394 N.W.2d 405 (Iowa 1986).  In this case, I conclude 
Mr. Gomez is entitled to temporary total/healing period benefits from October 4, 2016 
through January 9, 2017 and January 30, 2017 through March 22, 2017. 
Next, we must determine the appropriate weekly workers’ compensation rate in 
this case.  Section 85.36 states the basis of compensation is the weekly earnings of the 
employee at the time of the injury.  The section defines weekly earnings as the gross 
salary, wages, or earnings to which an employee would have been entitled had the 
employee worked the customary hours for the full pay period in which the employee 
was injured as the employer regularly required for the work or employment.  The various 
subsections of section 85.36 set forth methods of computing weekly earnings 
depending upon the type of earnings and employment. 
If the employee is paid on a daily or hourly basis or by output, weekly earnings 
are computed by dividing by 13 the earnings over the 13-week period immediately 
preceding the injury.  Any week that does not fairly reflect the employee’s customary 
earnings is excluded, however.  Section 85.36(6).  Based on the above findings of fact, I 
conclude claimant’s gross weekly wages at the time of the injury were $932.63.  Based 
on the Division of Workers’ Compensation Disability Compensation Rate tables, an 
individual who is single and entitled to three exemptions has a weekly workers’ 
compensation rate of $585.46.   
All costs incurred in the hearing before the commissioner shall be taxed in the 
discretion of the commissioner.  876 IAC 4.33.  Defendants are assessed costs as set 
forth above in the amount of $1,015.57.
