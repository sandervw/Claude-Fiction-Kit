STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A.  The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Lajuannah Stoglin.  
On September 20, 2024, Ms. Stoglin filed her application for alternate care, seeking 
treatment for her upper and mid back, as well as her right shoulder. Claimant appeared 
personally and through attorney, Nick Shaull.  Defendants appeared through their 
attorney, Nick Pellegrin. 
The alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on October 1, 2024.  The 
proceedings were digitally recorded on agency recording software, Quality Management 
Suite.  That recording constitutes the official record of this proceeding.  Pursuant to the 
Commissioner’s Order, the undersigned has been delegated authority to issue a final 
agency decision in this alternate medical care proceeding.  Therefore, this ruling is 
designated final agency action and any appeal of the decision would be to the Iowa 
District Court pursuant to Iowa Code section 17A. 
The record consists of Claimant’s Exhibits 1 through 10, which were received 
without objection, as well as the sworn testimony of claimant.  The defendants do not 
dispute liability for claimant’s November 10, 2023, work injury insofar as it has impacted 
her right shoulder.  The defendants have not filed an answer as required by 876 IAC

ISSUE 
The issue presented for resolution is whether the claimant is entitled to alternate 
medical care. 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
The claimant sustained an injury to her right shoulder on or about November 10, 
2023.  On that date she was assisting with a lift of a patient and felt pain in her upper 
back and shoulders.  Ultimately, the employer authorized treatment with her primary 
care provider, Amber Galey, M.D.  As set forth above, Ms. Stoglin contends that she 
has sustained conditions in her left shoulder, mid and upper back, as well as her right 
shoulder.  Thus far, the defendants have refused to accept liability for her left shoulder 
or her mid/upper back. 
Ms. Stoglin testified credibly that defendants arranged an appointment for her to 
see Dr. Galey on or about August 29, 2024.  On that date, Dr. Galey diagnosed chronic 
right shoulder pain and recommended a referral to “orthopedics” for this condition.  
(Claimant’s Exhibit 1)  The note references that she had been seen at ORA previously 
for her left shoulder.  Ms. Stoglin testified credibly that after this appointment she was 
contacted by ORA to arrange an appointment.  She further testified that ORA was 
having difficulty getting the appointment authorized through workers’ compensation.  
She admitted that at this time, she does not have any specific written referral to ORA.  
Nevertheless, I believe her that Dr. Galey did specifically refer her to ORA.  Her attorney 
requested the treatment on or about September 13, 2024. 
On September 19, 2023, defense counsel wrote to claimant’s counsel. 
Regardless of the authorization and procedural history involved, please 
note that my clients remain willing to have Ms. Stoglin evaluated for both 
the accepted right shoulder and the newly alleged left shoulder injury.  
Please note that my clients are in the process of scheduling an evaluation 
with Dr. Matthew Bollier at the University of Iowa to evaluate both the 
alleged right and left shoulder injuries.  As part of the evaluation Dr. Bollier 
will be asked to advise what, if any, additional treatment he is 
recommending that is causally related to the alleged work injury, among 
other issues.  I will notify you once an appointment date and time with Dr. 
Bollier has been scheduled.

Defense counsel made a number of additional statements on the record.  As of

The issue is whether the care provided by the defendants is reasonable. 
REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Iowa Code Section 85.27 (2013). 
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See 
Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  Determining what care is 
reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.  Id.  The employer’s obligation turns 
on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.  Id.; Harned v. Farmland 
Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983).   
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995). 
An employer’s statutory right is to select the providers of care and the employer 
may consider cost and other pertinent factors when exercising its choice. Long, at 124.

An employer (typically) is not a licensed health care provider and does not possess 
medical expertise. Accordingly, an employer does not have the right to control the 
methods the providers choose to evaluate, diagnose and treat the injured employee. An 
employer is not entitled to control a licensed health care provider’s exercise of 
professional judgment. Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 866389 (Declaratory 
Ruling, May 19, 1988). An employer’s failure to follow recommendations of an 
authorized physician in matters of treatment is commonly a failure to provide reasonable 
treatment. Boggs v. Cargill, Inc., File No. 1050396 (Alt. Care Dec. January 31, 1994). 
Based upon the record before me, I find that Dr. Galey is the authorized treating 
physician.  I further find that she has made a specific treatment referral to ORA.  This is 
based upon the highly credible testimony of Ms. Stoglin which is at least somewhat 
corroborated by Dr. Galey’s medical note.  She has been treated by ORA in the past for 
her left shoulder which has been denied at this time.  It appears that an appointment 
with ORA would have either already occurred or at least have been scheduled had the 
defendants approved the same.  There is no timeline at all for an appointment with the 
University of Iowa Hospitals and Clinics. 
I find that it is unreasonable to refuse to authorize the treatment recommended 
by her authorized treating physician. 
With respect to claimant’s treatment for her mid and/or upper back, the 
defendants have refused to accept liability for these conditions, stating they are still 
under investigation. 
Before any benefits can be ordered, including medical benefits, compensability of 
the claim must be established, either by admission of liability or by adjudication.  The 
summary provisions of Iowa Code section 85.27 as more particularly described in rule 
876 IAC 4.48 are not designed to adjudicate disputed compensability of claim.   
 
The Iowa Supreme Court has held:   
We emphasize that the commissioner’s ability to decide the merits of a 
section 85.27(4) alternate medical care claim is limited to situations where 
the compensability of an injury is conceded, but the reasonableness of a 
particular course of treatment for the compensable injury is disputed. . . .  
Thus, the commissioner cannot decide the reasonableness of the 
alternate care claim without also necessarily deciding the ultimate 
disputed issue in the case:  whether or not the medical condition Barnett 
was suffering at the time of the request was a work-related injury.  
. . . 
Once an employer takes the position in response to a claim for alternate 
medical care that the care sought is for a noncompensatory injury, the 
employer cannot assert an authorization defense in response to a

Thus, the portion of claimant’s application seeking medical treatment for her 
mid/upper back must be dismissed because the defendants refuse to accept liability for 
the condition for which claimant seeks treatment.  The defendants thereby lose their 
right to control the medical care claimant seeks in this proceeding and the claimant is 
free to choose that care on her own.  Bell Bros. Heating and Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 
779 N.W.2d 193 (Iowa 2010). 
As a result of the denial of liability for the condition sought to be treated in this 
proceeding, claimant may obtain reasonable medical care from any provider for this 
treatment, but at claimant’s expense, and seek reimbursement for such care using 
regular claim proceedings before this agency. 
ORDER 
THEREFORE IT IS ORDERED: 
The claimant's application for alternate medical care insofar as it relates to her 
right shoulder condition is GRANTED.  Defendants shall immediately authorize an 
appointment with ORA. 
IT IS FURTHER ORDERED that this claim as it relates to the mid and/or upper 
back should be and is hereby dismissed without prejudice. 
IT IS FURTHER ORDERED that if claimant seeks to recover the charges 
incurred in obtaining the care for which defendants have refused to accept liability, 
defendants are barred from asserting lack of authorization as a defense for those 
charges.
