STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, David LeRette, Jr., filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from Master Blaster, Inc., employer and Travelers Property 
Casualty Company of America, insurance carrier, both as defendants.  This matter was 
heard on September 24, 2020 with a final submission date of October 22, 2020. 
The record in this case consists of Joint Exhibits 1-8, Claimant’s Exhibits 1-6, 
Defendants’ Exhibits A-J and the testimony of claimant. 
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised 
or discussed in this decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The first issue to be determined is whether claimant’s injury arose out of and in 
the course of employment. 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa Rule of Appellate 
Procedure 6.14(6).

The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury actually occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 
employment.  Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. Dial 
Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309 (Iowa 1996).  The words “arising out of” referred to the cause or 
source of the injury.  The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 
circumstances of the injury.  2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124 (Iowa 1995).  
An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists between the 
injury and the employment.  Miedema, 551 N.W.2d 309.  The injury must be a rational 
consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely incidental to 
the employment.  Koehler Electric v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1 (Iowa 2000); Miedema, 551 
N.W.2d 309.  An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it happens within a 
period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may be when 
performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties or doing 
an activity incidental to them.  Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony.  The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question.  The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances.  The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); 
Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995).  Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994).  Unrebutted expert medical 
testimony cannot be summarily rejected.  Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 
N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 1994). 
Iowa law does not allow an order of workers’ compensation benefits based on 
testimony that is not credible.  Prokop v. Frank’s Plastering Company, 257 Iowa 766, 
133 N.W.2d 878, 880 (Iowa 1965). 
Claimant testified he had a work injury on November 24, 2018.  He testified the 
injury occurred after unloading his truck after working at Offutt in Nebraska.  Claimant 
said he pulled a pallet of sandbags and later moved it with a fork truck.  He said he got 
underneath the truck to tighten an oil filter when he felt excruciating pain in the area of

his navel.  Claimant was later assessed as having a hernia.  Claimant testified that 
approximately one and a half months after the November 24, 2018 incident he hurt his 
lower back while getting out of bed.  (Ex. G, Depo. pp. 27-28) 
There are a number of inconsistencies with claimant’s testimony.  In his 
statement given to the insurance company shortly after the accident, claimant seems to 
indicate his injury occurred on a Wednesday.  (Ex. 3, p. 14)  At hearing, claimant 
testified the alleged injury occurred on a Saturday.  (Tr. pp. 34-35) 
At hearing, claimant testified he had immediate stabbing pain in the area of his 
navel when tightening an oil filter.  (Tr. p. 16)  At his first medical exam following the 
injury, on November 29, 2018, claimant indicated he had umbilical pain for three weeks.  
The records from the November 29, 2018 exam indicate no history of trauma.  There is 
no reference to a work injury.  (JE5, p. 27) 
On January 9, 2019, claimant was evaluated by Dr. Hovey for an umbilical 
hernia.  There is no indication in the record claimant’s hernia was the result of a trauma 
or was a work injury.  (JE4, pp. 17-22) 
On February 18, 2019, claimant was evaluated by Dr. Rodriguez.  At that time, 
claimant indicated, for the first time, he had an onset of back pain with an umbilical 
hernia that developed after moving a heavy object at work.  (JE5, p. 39)  Claimant 
testified at hearing his back pain did not begin until a month and a half after the 
November 24, 2018 incident while getting out of bed.  (Ex. J, Depo. pp. 27-28) 
Records indicate that claimant was sent a letter, on or about December 5, 2018, 
regarding signing a form excluding him from workers’ compensation coverage.  At 
hearing, claimant testified he never got the letter.  (Tr. p. 37)  In a recorded statement, 
claimant said he received the letter, but turned it over to his insurance agent.  (Ex. 3, p. 
21) 
Claimant alleges a hernia and a back injury occurring on November 24, 2018 
after moving a pallet of sand and tightening an oil filter under a truck.  Claimant’s first 
two medical visits make no reference to a work injury.  Claimant’s first two medical visits 
indicate the injury did not occur with trauma.  The medical exam on November 29, 2018, 
approximately five days after the alleged date of injury, indicates claimant had been 
having umbilical pain a few weeks prior.  Claimant testified his back pain began a month 
and a half after the alleged date of injury while getting out of bed.  Medical records 
indicate claimant’s back pain began at the time of the alleged injury on November 24, 
2018.  Claimant’s testimony at hearing regarding receipt of an exclusionary form from 
the insurer contradicts the statement he gave to the defendant insurer.  Given the 
numerous contradictions in the record, it is found that claimant has failed to carry his 
burden of proof he sustained an injury that arose out of and in the course of 
employment on November 24, 2018.
