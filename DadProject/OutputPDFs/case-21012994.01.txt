On September 13, 2024, the above-captioned matter came before the Court for hearing.  
Andrew Bribriesco represented the petitioner, Mecene Laguerre ("Mecene"). Respondents JBS 
USA, Inc. (“JBS”) and American Zurich Insurance Company ("Respondents") were represented

including the briefs filed by the parties and the certified administrative record (“Cert. Rec.”), the

II. 
STANDARD OF REVIEW. 
The standard of review the Court must apply when considering an agency's interpretation 
of a statute depends on whether the legislature has clearly vested that agency with interpretive 
authority to interpret the provision at issue.  Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(c) and (m).  If the 
commission is vested with the authority to interpret Chapter 85, then review is for erroneous 
application of the law under Iowa Code section 17A.19(10)(l).  However, if the commission is 
not clearly vested with the authority to interpret Chapter 85, the review is for the correction of 
errors of law.  Iowa Code § 17A.19(10)(c).  As the Iowa Supreme Court concluded, the 
legislature has not vested the workers’ compensation commissioner with interpretive authority 
over Chapter 85. P.M. Lattner Mfg. Co. v. Rife, 2 N.W.3d 859, 865 (Iowa 2024).  Thus, the 
Court’s review of Deputy Lunn’s interpretation is for corrections of errors of law.  Iowa Code § 
17A.19(10)(c).  
III. 
ANALYSIS. 
a. Scheduled Injury § 85.34(2). 
The question here is whether an injury, the effects of which are limited to a scheduled

member under section 85.34(2)(t), should be classified as unscheduled according to section 
85.34(2)(v) because the injury received a body-as-a-whole impairment rating and primarily 
affects the skin.  Since the classification of an injury as scheduled or unscheduled is "essentially 
a question of statutory interpretation," the Court looks at the plain language, read in context with 
the rest of the statute.  Bridgestone Americas, Inc. v. Anderson, 4 N.W.3d 676, 683 (Iowa 2024). 
If the statute is unambiguous, the court will evaluate the deputy’s interpretation against the plain 
text.  Only if the statute is ambiguous will the court turn to other tools of interpretation.  Holstine 
Elec. v. Breyfogle, 756 N.W.2d 812, 815 (Iowa 2008).  The Court notes that it is true, as Mecene 
emphasizes, that the worker’s compensation statute is to be interpreted liberally with the goal of 
compensating employees; however, the court's priority must be the text. Id. (quoting Hoyt v. 
Wendling Quarries, 2015 WL 576174, at *2 (Iowa Ct. App. Feb. 11, 2015)).  
Mecene uses the 2017 amendment to paragraph "x," which requires the commissioner to 
use the AMA guidelines for calculating impairment, and Chavez v. MS Technology LLC, 972 
N.W.2d 662, 669 (Iowa 2022), to argue that the legislature intended that scheduling conforms to 
the AMA Guide’ impairment rating system.  Iowa Code § 85.34(2)(x).  In other words, 
regardless of whether the effects of the injury are limited to members defined in paragraph "t," 
because the AMA Guide calculate the impairment rating for a degloving injury using a body-as-
a-whole rating, the injury should be unscheduled.  Mecene buttresses his interpretation with dicta 
from Chavez where the court observes that their conclusion that “shoulder” in paragraph “n” 
should be defined functionally aligns with the AMA Guide, “which is the guide for determining 
the extent of loss or percentage of impairment for permanent partial disabilities.” (quoting Iowa 
Admin Code. r. 876—2.4).  Nevertheless, the text refutes Mecene’s interpretation. 
Iowa Code section 85.34(2) governs compensation for permanent partial disabilities.  The

compensation method significantly impacts the level of compensation an employee can obtain 
for a permanent partial disability.  Bridgestone, 4 N.W.3d at682.  How compensation is 
calculated depends on whether the deputy classifies an employee's injury as scheduled or 
unscheduled. Scheduled injuries are those described or referenced in paragraphs "a" through "u" 
of section 85.34(2).  An injury may only be classified as unscheduled if it is not those described 
or referenced in paragraphs "a" through "u" of section 85.34(2).  Iowa Code § 85.34(2)(v). Here, 
the deputy concluded that Mecene’s injury was described or referenced in paragraph “t,” which 
covers “the loss of both arms, or . . .  both legs . . . or any to thereof caused by a single accident,” 
applied to Mecene’s injuries.  Iowa Code § 85.34(2)(t).  Thus, the question becomes whether 
Mecene's injury is described or referenced in paragraph “t.”  
Determining whether an injury or impairment is described or referenced by paragraph “t” 
depends on whether the impairment results in the loss of function of a member referenced or 
described in paragraph “t.”  Chavez 972 N.W.2d at 668-69 (quoting Floyd v. Quaker Oats 646, 
N.W.2d 105,109 (Iowa 2002)) (internal citation omitted).  Additionally, Iowa courts "look 
beyond the situs of the original injury and consider the impact of the injury on all parts of the 
body.”  Sandberg v. Rubbermaid Home Prod., 760 N.W.2d 210 (Table) 2008 WL 5234378 *4 
(Iowa Ct. App. 2008).  As the deputy observed in his opinion, determining whether an 
impairment affects the use of a member or impacts the other parts of the body reflects the 
commission’s long-standing approach to this issue.  D0003 at 77-78 (discussing various deputy 
rulings rejecting the contention that because the AMA Guides Tables rate skin disorders as body-
as-a-whole impairments, such injuries should be classified as unscheduled).  For example, in 
Bryan v. Kiowa Line Builders Inc., File No. 5062947 (Arb. March 2018), the deputy in that case 
rejected an argument that although scarring is localized to a scheduled member, because it is a

skin disorder and the skin itself is not a scheduled member, the injury should be classified as 
unscheduled. The deputy rejected the argument because the scarring and its functional effect 
were limited to one member, and there was no proof of a skin disorder that spread around the 
body. Id.  The plain text is, therefore, unambiguous: if the loss of function due to Mecene’s 
injuries is limited to two members covered by paragraph “t,” his injury cannot be classified as 
unscheduled under paragraph “v.”   
Applying the plain text to the facts found by the deputy leads the court to affirm the 
deputy’s interpretation.  Here, while the deputy agreed with Dr. Bansal’s body-as-a-whole 
impairment rating, he did not classify Mecene’s injury as an injury to two members caused by 
one accident under 85.34(2)(t) because “the injury does not extend into the body as a whole.”  
D0003 at 79.  Moreover, the deputy only found that the function of Mecene's right arm and right 
leg were affected. Id.  There was no evidence of the scarring or neurological effects of Mecene's 
injuries extending beyond their respective members. Id.  As discussed above, paragraph “t” 
describes or references arm and leg impairments.  Iowa Code § 85.34(2)(t).  Accordingly, 
Mecene's injuries are described and/or referenced in paragraphs "a" through “u.”  Therefore, 
Mecene's injury cannot be unscheduled under paragraph “v.”  
Turning to Mecene’s argument regarding section 85.34(2)(x), paragraph “x” states, 
“when determining functional disability and not loss of earning capacity, the extent of loss or 
percentage of permanent impairment shall be determined solely by utilizing the guides to the 
evaluation of permanent impairment, published by the American Medical Association.”  Iowa 
Code § 85.34(2)(x) (emphasis added).  The text of paragraph “x” is crystal clear.  AMA Guides 
must be used to determine functional disability or permanent impairment, not whether an injury 
is scheduled or unscheduled. Id.  Therefore, the text rejects Mecene's argument that paragraph

“x” shows that the legislature intended that the classification of injuries as scheduled or 
unscheduled should align perfectly with AMA Guides ratings.  
 Mecene’s interpretation of Chavez is also incorrect. Chavez did not hold that the 
legislature intended that scheduled and unscheduled injuries conform to the AMA Guides. 
Instead, Chavez is inapposite to Mecene’s position.  The crux of Chavez is:  When determining 
whether an injury is scheduled or unscheduled, the court looks to whether the injury impacts the 
function of a member referenced in paragraphs “a” through “u” of subsection 2.  972 N.W.2d at 
668-69 (“the “loss” referenced in section 85.34(2) includes the “loss of the use of a scheduled 
member.”  In other words, the loss of function.”).2  As discussed above, the only members on 
Mecene’s body that had a loss in function were those described in section 85.34(2)(t).  Therefore, 
section 85.34(2)(v) cannot apply. The Chavez court’s reference to the fact that impairment 
ratings must be calculated using AMA Guides tables was not an acknowledgment that the 
legislature intended to have scheduling classifications conform to AMA Guides table ratings. 
972 N.W.2d at 670.  Not only is it pure dicta, at most, it is merely confirmation by the court that 
its functional analysis of section 85.34(2)(n) was consistent with the rest of the text in that 
section. Id.  Further, even if the legislature intended that scheduling classification reflect the 
AMA Guides, the AMA Guides reject Mecene's interpretation.  The AMA Guides permit the 
conversion of body-as-a-whole impairment ratings.  D0003 at 78.3

Finally, Mecene’s invocation of exclusio unius and the overarching statutory purpose of 
helping workers recover are inapplicable.  Firstly, statutory purpose and canons of construction 
are interpretive tools.  Such tools are only relevant when the statute is ambiguous.  Holstine 
Elec., 756 N.W.2d at 815.  As the court has discussed in this opinion, section 85.34(2) is 
unambiguous.  Secondly, even if the statute were ambiguous, JBS correctly points out that 
adopting Mecene’s interpretation would lead to absurdity because anytime a claimant has a 
scheduled member injury, the effects of which are confined to that member, rated using the 
body-as-a-whole rating that scheduled member injury transforms into an unscheduled injury. 
D0012 at 24-25.   
Paragraph “v” illustrates the inaccuracy of Mecene’s interpretation.  Paragraph “v” states 
that to qualify as unscheduled, an injury must be other than those described or referred to in 
paragraphs "a" through "u."  Iowa Code § 85.34(2)(v).  The text reveals that the legislature 
intended that either a claimant has a scheduled or unscheduled injury, not both at the same time. 
This is not to say that claimants cannot have multiple scheduled or unscheduled injuries, but an 
injury that qualifies as a scheduled member injury cannot simultaneously also be unscheduled. 
The Court recognizes that the consequence of affirming Mecene's interpretation would be to 
affirm that an injury can be both scheduled and unscheduled.  It would be possible for a claimant 
to have an injury that qualifies as scheduled member injury under the plain text because the only 
resulting "loss of use" would be confined to one member.  Still then, however, because of a 
body-as-a-whole impairment rating, the injury would also be unscheduled.  Therefore, for the 
reasons laid out above, the deputy did not err in his interpretation of Iowa Code section 85.34(2).  
IV. 
DISPOSITION. 
Petitioner’s Petition for Judicial Review is hereby DENIED.
