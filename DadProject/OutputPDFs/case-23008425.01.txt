STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Tammy Renaud, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from defendants, Warren County, employer, West Bend Mutual 
Insurance Company, insurer, and the Second Injury Fund of Iowa (Fund), all as 
defendants.  This matter was heard on September 18, 2024, with a final submission 
date of November 27, 2024.   
The record in this case consists of Joint Exhibits 1-16, Claimant’s Exhibits 1-9, 
Defendants’ Employer and Insurer (Defendants) Exhibits A-K, Defendant Second Injury 
Fund’s Exhibits AA-DD, and the testimony of claimant.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
The first issue to be determined is whether claimant is entitled to temporary 
disability benefits.  

RENAUD V. WARREN COUNTY/SECOND INJURY FUND OF IOWA 
Page 9 
 
 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e). 
 
Healing period compensation describes temporary workers’ compensation 
weekly benefits that precede an allowance of permanent partial disability benefits.  
Ellingson v. Fleetguard, Inc., 599 N.W.2d 440, 446 (Iowa 1999).  Healing period benefits 
are payable to an injured worker who has suffered permanent partial disability until the 
first to occur of three events.  Iowa Code § 85.34(1) (2015).  These are:  (1) the worker 
has returned to work; (2) the worker medically is capable of returning to substantially 
similar employment; or (3) the worker has achieved maximum medical recovery.  Id.  
Maximum medical recovery is achieved when healing is complete, and the extent of 
permanent disability can be determined.  Armstrong Tire & Rubber Co. v. Kubli, 312 
N.W.2d 60, 65 (Iowa Ct. App. 1981).  Neither maintenance medical care nor an 
employee's continuing to have pain or other symptoms necessarily prolongs the healing 
period.  Id. 
 
The hearing report indicates claimant sought temporary disability from March 1, 
2024 through July 22, 2024.  At hearing, claimant’s counsel clarified this matter and 
indicated claimant was seeking temporary benefits from March 1, 2024, through March 
16, 2024.  (Tr., p. 5; Claimant’s Post-Hearing Brief, p. 29) 
 
Claimant was terminated by Warren County on March 1, 2024.  As detailed in the 
Findings of Fact, claimant was terminated for allegedly leaving a door unsecured, 
intentionally silencing an alarm, and lying about her actions.  (Ex. 5, p. 1; Ex. B) 
 
Claimant testified at hearing that she was not at fault for leaving the secured door 
open.  (Tr., pp. 56-57)  Claimant also refused to sign her termination papers.   
 
The termination report indicates, in part, that claimant was terminated for 
unlocking a security door after another officer secured the door and failing to notify 
anyone the door was unlocked.  Claimant indicated that this statement was correct.  
(Tr., pp. 57-58)  Claimant also testified that she was accommodated by Warren County 
up until the time of her termination.  (Tr., p. 58)  There is no evidence in the record 
claimant attempted to appeal her termination with Warren County.   
 
Defendants contend that claimant is not due temporary disability benefits due to 
termination from Warren County.   
 
Iowa Code section 85.33(3) precludes an employee who refuses suitable work 
offered by the employer, consistent with the employee's disability, from receiving 
temporary or healing period benefits during the period of refusal. Neal v. Annett 
Holdings, Inc., 814 N.W.2d 512, 520 (Iowa 2012). The employer bears the burden to 
establish the prerequisites of Iowa Code section 85.33(3). Koehler v. American Color 
Graphics, File No. 1248489 (App. Feb. 25, 2005). 
For misconduct to disqualify an employee from compensation, the misconduct 
must be tantamount to a refusal of suitable work. Reynolds v. Hy-Vee, Inc., File No. 

RENAUD V. WARREN COUNTY/SECOND INJURY FUND OF IOWA 
Page 10 
 
5046203 (App. Oct. 31, 2017). Termination by itself is not sufficient grounds to 
disqualify an employee from temporary benefits under Iowa Code section 85.33(3). 
Gully v. Liguria Foods, Inc., File No. 5063429 (App. Jan. 30, 2020) (citations omitted). 
The misconduct must be serious and the type of conduct that would cause any 
employer to terminate any employee. The misconduct must have a serious adverse 
impact on the employer. The misconduct must be more than the type of inconsequential 
misconduct that employers typically overlook or tolerate. An employee working with 
restrictions is not entitled to act with impunity toward the employer and the employer's 
interests. Nevertheless, not every act of misconduct justifies disqualifying an employee 
from workers' compensation benefits even though the employer may be justified in 
taking disciplinary action. Reynolds, at 2 
 
Claimant admitted to unlocking a door at the jail and forgetting to notify anyone 
the door was unlocked.  This action, in part, was found to jeopardize the security of the 
jail.  Claimant’s termination was signed by the Warren County Sheriff after consulting 
with the Warren County Attorney.  (Ex. B)  There is no evidence claimant attempted to 
appeal the termination.  I recognize claimant disagrees with the termination and refused 
to sign the termination report.  However, given the facts above, I find that claimant’s 
misconduct disqualifies her from temporary benefits.  Defendants have carried their 
burden of proof that claimant is not entitled to temporary benefits from March 1, 2024 
through March 16, 2024.   
 
The next issue to be determined is if claimant’s injury resulted in a permanent 
disability. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
A possibility of causation is not sufficient; a probability is necessary. Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996) (citing Holmes v. Bruce 
Motor Freight, Inc., 215 N.W.2d 296, 297 (Iowa 1974)). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 
621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 
(Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 

RENAUD V. WARREN COUNTY/SECOND INJURY FUND OF IOWA 
Page 11 
 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
 
The parties seem to agree that claimant sustained a work-related injury to her 
right knee and ankle that was permanent in nature. 
 
Dr. Vinyard opined that claimant had a 10 percent permanent impairment due to 
her right knee injury.  (Ex. H)  Dr. Bansal opined that claimant had a 5 percent 
permanent impairment to her right knee and a 4 percent permanent impairment to her 
right ankle.  It does not appear that defendants attempted to obtain a permanent 
impairment from Dr. Trout for claimant’s right ankle injury.  Dr. Vinyard treated 
claimant’s right knee for an extended period of time and performed surgery.  Dr. Bansal 
evaluated claimant once for an IME.  As a practical matter, Dr. Vinyard has a far better 
understanding of claimant’s medical history and presentation regarding her right knee 
injury.  Given this record, I found that claimant has a 10 percent permanent impairment 
to the right knee, and a 4 percent permanent impairment to the right ankle regarding the 
June 14, 2023 work injury. 
 
The record indicates that claimant has pre-existing mental health problems.  (JE 
11, p. 6)  Two experts have opined that claimant has PTSD as a result of the June 2023 
work injury.  Counselor Franco treated claimant for her mental health condition.  She 
indicated claimant’s PTSD “stems” from her assault while working on June 14, 2023.  
(JE 14, p. 3)  Dr. Bansal opined that claimant’s workplace incident on June 14, 2023, 
led to the development of PTSD.  (JE 16, p. 26)  No expert has rebutted these two 
opinions.  Based on this record, claimant has carried her burden of proof she sustained 
a mental health injury that arose out of and in the course of employment.

Functional impairment is an element to be considered in determining industrial 
disability which is the reduction of earning capacity, but consideration must also be 
given to the injured employee's age, education, qualifications, experience, motivation, 
loss of earnings, severity and situs of the injury, work restrictions, inability to engage in 
employment for which the employee is fitted and the employer's offer of work or failure

v. Goodyear Service Stores, 255 Iowa 1112, 1120, 125 N.W.2d 251, 256 (1963); Barton 
v. Nevada Poultry Co., 253 Iowa 285, 110 N.W.2d 660, 663 (1961). 
Compensation for permanent partial disability shall begin when it is medically

The next issue to be determined is rate. 
The basis of compensation is the weekly earnings of the employee at the time of 
the injury.  Iowa Code § 85.36 (2015).  Weekly earnings are the gross salary, wages, or 
earnings to which an employee would have been entitled had the employee worked the 
customary hours for the full pay period in which the employee was injured as the 
employer regularly required for the work or employment.  Id.  The various subsections of 
section 85.36 set forth methods of computing weekly earnings depending upon the type 
of earnings and employment.  Id.

If the employee is paid on a daily or hourly basis or by output, weekly earnings 
are computed by dividing by thirteen the earnings over the thirteen-week period 
immediately preceding the injury.  Iowa Code § 85.36(6) (2015).  Any week that does

The next issue to be determined is whether claimant is entitled to alternate

Iowa Code section 85.27(4) provides, in relevant part: 
For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish 
reasonable services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has 
the right to choose the care. . . .  The treatment must be offered promptly 
and be reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience 
to the employee.  If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the 
care offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such 
dissatisfaction to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the 
employer and the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited 
to treat the injury.  If the employer and employee cannot agree on such 
alternate care, the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable 
proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).

awarded as a remedy, claimant first must communicate the basis of the dissatisfaction 
with the care provided.  There is no evidence in the record that claimant communicated

communicate to the employer a basis for dissatisfaction with care, claimant has failed to 
carry her burden of proof she is entitled to alternate medical care at this time.

Iowa Code section 85.64 governs Second Injury Fund liability.  Before liability of 
the Fund is triggered, three requirements must be met.  First, the employee must have 
lost or lost the use of a hand, arm, foot, leg, or eye.  Second, the employee must sustain 
a loss or loss of use of another specified member or organ through a compensable 
injury.  Third, permanent disability must exist as to both the initial injury and the second 
injury.   
The Second Injury Fund Act exists to encourage the hiring of handicapped 
persons by making a current employer responsible only for the amount of disability 
related to an injury occurring while that employer employed the handicapped individual 
as if the individual had had no preexisting disability.  See Anderson v. Second Injury 
Fund, 262 N.W.2d 789, 792 (Iowa 1978); 15 Iowa Practice, Workers’ Compensation, 
Lawyer, Section 17:1, p. 211 (2014-2015). 
The Fund is responsible for the industrial disability present after the second injury 
that exceeds the disability attributable to the first and second injuries.  Section 85.64.  
Second Injury Fund of Iowa v. Braden, 459 N.W.2d 467, 471 (Iowa 1990); Second Injury 
Fund v. Neelans, 436 N.W.2d 355, 358 (Iowa 1989); Second Injury Fund v. Mich Coal 
Co., 274 N.W.2d 300, 304 (Iowa 1979).

The last issue to be determined is whether defendants are liable for a penalty 
under Iowa Code section 10A.315(4). 
In Christensen v. Snap-on Tools Corp., 554 N.W.2d 254 (Iowa 1996) and 
Robbennolt v. Snap-on Tools Corp, 555 N.W.2d 229 (Iowa 1996) the supreme court 
said: 
Based on the plain language of section 86.13,1 we hold an employee 
is entitled to penalty benefits if there has been a delay in payment unless 
the employer proves a reasonable cause or excuse.  [Iowa Code § 86.13 
(2015).]  A reasonable cause or excuse exists if either (1) the delay was 
necessary for the insurer to investigate the claim or (2) the employer had a 
reasonable basis to contest the employee’s entitlement to benefits.  A 
“reasonable basis” for denial of the claim exists if the claim is “fairly 
debatable.”  Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 260. 
The supreme court has stated: 
(1) If the employer has a reason for the delay and conveys that 
reason to the employee contemporaneously with the beginning of the delay, 
no penalty will be imposed if the reason is of such character that a 
reasonable fact finder could conclude that it is a "reasonable or probable 
cause or excuse" under Iowa Code section 86.13 [2015].  In that case, we 
will defer to the decision of the commissioner.  See Christensen, 554

(2) If no reason is given for the delay or if the “reason” is not one that 
a reasonable fact finder could accept, we will hold that no such cause or

(3) Reasonable causes or excuses include (a) a delay for the

When an employee’s claim for benefits is fairly debatable based on a good faith 
dispute over the employee’s factual or legal entitlement to benefits, an award of penalty 
benefits is not appropriate under the statute.  Gilbert v. USF Holland, Inc., 637 N.W.2d

investigation is not unreasonable.  Id.  
It also is not unreasonable to deny a claim when a good faith issue of law or fact

480, 483 (Iowa 2007).  An issue of law is fairly debatable if viable arguments exist in 
favor of each party.  Covia v. Robinson, 507 N.W.2d 411, 412 (Iowa 1993).  An issue of 
fact is fairly debatable if substantial evidence exists which would support a finding 
favorable to the employer.  Gilbert v. USF Holland, Inc., 637 N.W.2d 194, 199 (Iowa 
2001).  
“An employer’s bare assertion that a claim is ‘fairly debatable’ does not make it 
so.”  Meyers v. Holiday Express Corp., 557 N.W.2d 502, 505 (Iowa 1996).  The 
employer must assert facts upon which the commissioner could reasonably find that the 
claim was “fairly debatable.”  Id.
