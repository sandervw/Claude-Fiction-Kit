This matter came before the Court on a Petition for Judicial Review from a 
final decision of the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commission.  A hearing was held 
on February 16, 2024. Petitioner, Archer Daniels Midland (“ADM”) appeared through 
counsel, Brandon Lobberecht.  Respondent, Donald Tuttle, appeared through counsel 
Dennis Currell. Upon review of the court file and applicable law, the Court enters the 
following order: 
 
I. 
BACKGROUND FACTS  
Donald Tuttle sustained two work related injuries, one to his left knee, and the 
other a head injury. Tuttle suffered the injury to his left knee while working on 
January 21, 2018. He returned to work from that injury, and sustained his head 
injury on April 14, 2022. 
 
A. LEFT KNEE 
 
ADM authorized the University of Iowa Hospitals and Clinics (“UIHC”) as 
treating provider for Tuttle’s knee injury.  ADM accepted compensability of the knee 
claim and provided treatment, including left knee arthroscopic surgery on February 
28, 2018. In November 2020, the doctors at UIHC opined that Tuttle needed a left 
knee replacement.  Tuttle chose to get a second opinion at Mayo Clinic, and pursued 
care for his knee injury there. While Mayo Clinic believed knee replacement would 
be needed down the road, they provided knee injections to delay the need for that 
surgery; this was in light of concerns regarding surgical risks related to Tuttle’s

When Tuttle received his head injury, he was originally seen at a Unity Point

week.  Tuttle was directed to follow up with ADM’s provider, St. Luke’s Work Well.

know why Tuttle was there.  ADM attempted to reschedule an examination at Work 
Well, but Tuttle did not go to that appointment and instead continued treatment 
through Dr. Flory. Dr. Flory made multiple recommendations and referrals, including 
referrals to the Mayo Clinic and to Dr. Alzohaili, an endocrinologist who diagnosed 
Tuttle with pituitary dysfunction and growth hormone deficiency. Dr. Alzohaili 
recommended Tuttle receive regular and ongoing injections of human growth 
hormone (HGH) for the remainder of his life. 
 
Tuttle had an independent medical examination with Dr. Jonathon Fields on 
September 29, 2022. Dr. Fields provided an updated report on February 28, 2023, 
which recommended a second opinion to a local endocrinologist at Mayo Clinic or the 
University of Iowa, because Dr. Alzohaili is in Detroit, Michigan. Mayo Clinic 
declined to see Tuttle for a second opinion, and an endocrinologist at the University 
of Iowa did not have time to see Tuttle until last August 2023 at the earliest.

II. 
STANDARD OF REVIEW 
Final decisions rendered by the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commission are 
reviewed under Iowa Code Chapter 17A, the Iowa Administrative Procedures Act.1 
“Under the Act, [a court] may only interfere with the commissioner’s decision if it is 
erroneous under one of the grounds enumerated in the statute, and a party’s 
substantial rights have been prejudiced.”2 The standard of review depends on the 
type of error alleged by the Petitioner.3 
If the alleged error is one of fact, the standard of review is whether the findings 
are supported by substantial evidence.4 “[A] reviewing court can only disturb those 
factual findings if they are ‘not supported by substantial evidence in the record before 
the court when that record is reviewed as a whole.’”5 Additionally, in workers’ 
compensation cases, factual questions are “delegated by the legislature to the 
[C]ommissioner.”6 Consequently, the Court does not apply a “scrutinizing analysis” 
to factual findings of the Commissioner, but only reverses the Commissioner’s 
findings if they are not supported by substantial evidence.7 
“Evidence is substantial if a reasonable person would find the evidence 
adequate to reach the same conclusion.”8 The Court is “not to determine whether the 
evidence supports a different finding; rather, our task is to determine whether 
substantial evidence, viewing the record as a whole, supports the findings actually 
made.”9

If the claimed error is in the ultimate conclusion reached, “then the challenge 
is to the agency’s application of the law to the facts.”10 In workers’ compensation 
cases, “[t]he application of the law to the facts is also an enterprise vested in the 
[C]ommissioner.” As such, this Court “reverse[s] only if the [C]ommissioner's 
application was irrational, illogical, or wholly unjustifiable.”11  
 
III. 
TUTTLE’S PETITION FOR JUDICIAL REVIEW – KNEE INJURY 
A. STANDARD OF REVIEW 
 
Initially, the parties dispute the standard of review for the Deputy’s 
determination. ADM argues the Deputy failed to find ADM did not meet its statutory 
requirements under Iowa Code section 85.27(4), and therefore review is for errors at 
law. Tuttle counters that the Deputy made a finding of reasonableness—or, in this 
case, whether ADM’s care was unreasonable because Tuttle had the burden—which 
is a question of fact that must only be reviewed for whether substantial evidence 
supports the factual decision. The Court has long held that “[d]etermining what care 
is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.”12 
 
B. FINDING OF UNREASONABLENESS 
As in Long, “[t]he ultimate question is whether, in choosing the University of 
Iowa Hospitals . . . the employer met its obligation under Iowa Code section 85.27 ‘to 
furnish reasonable services’ for the treatment of claimant’s injuries.”13 The Deputy 
found that the doctors at UIHC still recommend total knee replacement. This was 
supported by a letter from Dr. Bollier at UIHC, which state’s Mayo Clinic’s 
“recommendation of knee replacement is the same as our opinion . . . There are just 
as many well-trained joint replacement surgeons in Iowa and our joint replacement 
specialists at The University of Iowa would be happy to take care of him.”14  Although

ADM argues on appeal that “[t]he only reason Mayo Clinic may have offered more or

submitted for the alternate medical care hearing include UIHC’s letter discussed

The Deputy accurately articulated the applicable law—that Tuttle had the 
burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable and it was a question of 
reasonable necessity, not desirability. And while the Deputy stated there were 
“documented concerns with claimant having a total knee replacement surgery, given 
his history of MRSA,” the Deputy never made an express finding that ADM’s care 
was unreasonable. The Deputy implied that ADM’s offered care—total knee 
replacement surgery—was unreasonable because surgery came with a significant 
risk and should be pushed out as far as possible, but that key finding is not expressly 
included in the Decision. The Deputy made findings supported by substantial 
evidence that Dr. Duck’s treatment at Mayo Clinic was “more extensive and better 
suited to treat claimant’s knee injury.” However, this finding only comes into play 
after a determination ADM’s care is unreasonable. Because that express finding is 
not in the Ruling, this case must be remanded on that issue. The Deputy 
Commissioner shall clarify whether ADM’s care plan of UIHC is unreasonable.

B. ALTERNATE MEDICAL CARE 
ADM contends that Mayo Clinic was the authorized treating provider for 
Tuttle’s head injury starting in February 2023, following the independent medical 
examination by Dr. Fields in September 2022. Mayo Clinic declined treatment, and 
an endocrinologist at the University of Iowa could not see Tuttle until August 2023. 
The Deputy found that this was an unreasonable delay in treatment, and therefore 
ADM’s offered treatment was not offered promptly. As discussed above, 
reasonableness is a fact question.20 Substantial evidence supports this finding, 
especially since ADM did nothing between setting a May 16, 2022, appointment at 
WorkWell, and the September 2022 IME. ADM’s request that Tuttle wait until 
August 2023 to see their doctor at the University of Iowa would have caused further 
delays in treatment. The Deputy’s found that the HGH injections provided benefit, 
and stopping the injections had a negative effect. The care authorized by ADM was

C. SCOPE OF AUTHORIZATION 
The  Deputy ordered ADM to authorize and pay for Tuttle’s treatment related 
to his head injury with Dr. Alzohaili, including authorization of the prescription for 
human growth hormone injections. ADM argues the Deputy ordered “ADM to 
authorize, in essence, any future and unknowable treatment recommendations of Dr. 
Alzohaili pertaining to his head injury claim” and that is a legally erroneous 
application of Iowa Code section 85.27(4). ADM cites no caselaw to support this 
preposition. ADM merely suggests “the Deputy should have simply ordered ADM to 
authorize and provide for the injections (either through May Clinic or, if it refuses to 
do so, through Dr. Alzohaili’s office).”  As discussed above, Mayo Clinic declined to 
provide a second opinion regarding Dr. Alzohaili’s treatment with HGH injections. 
The Deputy’s authorization was limited to “treatment related to [Tuttle’s] head 
injury,” including the prescription for HGH injections. This was not an abuse of 
discretion. 
 
 
V. 
DISPOSITION 
IT IS THE ORDER OF THE COURT that Decision of the Deputy 
Commissioner by REMANDED to the agency for a more specific finding regarding 
whether UIHC’s care was unreasonable with respect to the knee injury.  
IT IS FURTHER ORDERED that the Decision is AFFIRMED with respect to 
the alternate medical care decision for the head injury.
