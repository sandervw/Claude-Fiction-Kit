STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
The claimant, Holly Siefken, filed a petition for arbitration and seeks workers’ 
compensation benefits from International Paper Company, employer, and Old Republic 
Insurance, insurance carrier.  The claimant was represented by James Hoffman.  The 
defendants were represented by John Cutler. 
The matter came on for hearing on January 26, 2024, before deputy workers’ 
compensation commissioner Joe Walsh in Des Moines, Iowa via Zoom.  The record in 
the case consists of Joint Exhibits 1 through 5; Claimant’s Exhibits 1 through 2; and 
Defense Exhibits A through B, in addition to the claimant’s sworn testimony.  The record 
was held open until February 22, 2024, to allow the defendants to submit additional 
evidence on the issue of lost wages and mileage.  Those issues were resolved prior to 
the record being closed and are no longer part of this case.  Gina Castro served as the 
court reporter.  The matter was fully submitted on February 22, 2024. 
ISSUES 
The parties submitted the following issues for determination: 
1. The extent of permanent impairment in the right leg. 
2. Whether claimant is entitled to IME expenses.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The first question submitted is the extent of impairment in her right leg.  
The parties have stipulated that her disability is to her right lower extremity or 
right leg. 
The courts have repeatedly stated that for those injuries limited to the schedules 
in Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(a-t), this agency must only consider the functional loss of 
the particular scheduled member involved and not the other factors which constitute an 
“industrial disability.”  Iowa Supreme Court decisions over the years have repeatedly 
cited favorably the following language in the 66-year-old case of Soukup v. Shores Co., 
222 Iowa 272, 277; 268 N.W. 598, 601 (1936): 
The legislature has definitely fixed the amount of compensation that shall 
be paid for specific injuries . . . and that, regardless of the education or 
qualifications or nature of the particular individual, or of his inability . . . to 
engage in employment . . . the compensation payable . . . is limited to the 
amount therein fixed. 
Permanent partial disabilities are classified as either scheduled or unscheduled.  
A specific scheduled disability is evaluated by the functional method; the industrial 
method is used to evaluate an unscheduled disability.  Graves v. Eagle Iron Works, 331 
N.W.2d 116 (Iowa 1983); Simbro v. DeLong's Sportswear 332 N.W.2d 886, 887 (Iowa 
1983); Martin v. Skelly Oil Co., 252 Iowa 128, 133, 106 N.W.2d 95, 98 (1960). 
Thus, when the result of an injury is loss to a scheduled member, the 
compensation payable is limited to that set forth in the appropriate subdivision of Code 
section 85.34(2).  Barton v. Nevada Poultry Co., 253 Iowa 285, 110 N.W.2d 660 (1961).  
"Loss of use" of a member is equivalent to "loss" of the member.  Moses v. National 
Union C. M. Co., 194 Iowa 819, 184 N.W. 746 (1921). 
In all cases of permanent partial disability described in paragraphs 
“a” through “u”, or paragraph “v” when determining functional disability and

not loss of earning capacity, the extent of loss or percentage of permanent 
impairment shall be determined solely by utilizing the guides to the 
evaluation of permanent impairment, published by the American Medical 
Association, as adopted by the workers’ compensation commissioner by 
rule pursuant to chapter 17A.  Lay testimony or agency expertise shall not 
be utilized in determining loss of percentage of permanent impairment 
pursuant to paragraphs “a” through “u”, or paragraph “v” when determining 
functional impairment and not loss of earning capacity. 
Iowa Code Section 85.34(2)(x) (2023).  In other words, the law, as written, is not 
concerned with an injured worker’s actual functional loss as determined by the 
evidence, but rather the impairment rating as assigned by the adopted version of the 
AMA Guides.  The only function of the agency is to determine which impairment rating 
should be utilized.  The statute itself provides no guidance on which rating should be 
selected.  I interpret the statute to direct me to utilize the rating which most accurately 
reflects the injured workers’ actual functional disability.  The burden of proof for this lies 
with the injured worker.  I find that in this case, the parties have stipulated that Ms. 
Siefken’s disability is to her right leg and is analyzed under Section 85.34(2)(p) (2023). 
 
In this record, there are two impairment ratings.  I find both ratings are flawed 
and neither adequately evaluates Ms. Siefken’s significant loss of function of her right 
leg.  She spent four years in the healing process and underwent significant amounts of 
treatment, both surgery and significant physical therapy, in addition to numerous other 
treatment modalities.  She is still unable to stand for extended periods and her activities 
of daily living are significantly impacted.  The treating surgeon assigned a restriction 
forbidding her from walking up stairs while carrying anything.  Her actual loss of function 
is likely higher than 9 percent of her leg. 
 
Dr. Bansal rated her right ankle range of motion deficits but failed to consider her 
strength loss (peroneal nerve deficit).  The main flaw in his rating, however, is that he 
rated her when she was not at MMI.  Ms. Siefken, however, testified that her condition 
really did not improve even after that date.  (Tr., pp. 29-30)  I believe her testimony.  
Nevertheless, this flaw in the timing of the rating must be acknowledged. 
 
Dr. Femino was her treating surgeon.  He did rate her loss of strength (peroneal 
nerve deficit), however, failed to rate her range of motion deficits.  There is nothing in 
the record that he even measured her deficits when she reached MMI.  While Dr. 
Femino did perform the rating at an appropriate point in time (after she reached MMI at 
the conclusion of her treatment), I am unclear in this record why he failed to rate her 
range of motion deficits.  There are a number of possibilities, however, it is unexplained 
in this record.  Based upon the record before me, and specifically Ms. Siefken’s credible 
testimony, it seems highly likely that she still has moderate range of motion deficits. 
 
I find that the actual loss of function in her right leg is likely greater than 
measured by either of the impairment ratings pursuant to the AMA Guides, 5th edition.  
The statute, however, clearly forbids me from assessing her actual loss of function

based upon the evidence in the case.  Pursuant to section 85.34(2)(x), I am required to 
utilize a rating from the AMA Guides, even if the rating is incorrect or otherwise 
inadequate.  Ultimately, I find that the claimant has failed to meet her burden of proof 
that she has sustained an impairment beyond that recorded by her authorized surgeon. 
 
The final issue is IME expenses. 
Section 85.39 permits an employee to be reimbursed for subsequent 
examination by a physician of the employee's choice where an employer-retained 
physician has previously evaluated “permanent disability” and the employee believes 
that the initial evaluation is too low.  The section also permits reimbursement for 
reasonably necessary transportation expenses incurred and for any wage loss 
occasioned by the employee attending the subsequent examination. 
Defendants are responsible only for reasonable fees associated with claimant's 
independent medical examination.  Claimant has the burden of proving the 
reasonableness of the expenses incurred for the examination.  See Schintgen v. 
Economy Fire & Casualty Co., File No. 855298 (App. April 26, 1991). 
In this record, I cannot find that Ms. Siefken ever had an evaluation of permanent 
disability before she was evaluated by Dr. Bansal.  An evaluation of permanent disability 
need not be an impairment rating; it simply needs to be an evaluation which assesses 
an injured worker’s permanency.  In this case, Ms. Siefken was evaluated by Dr. Bansal 
who authored a report on December 1, 2021, roughly a year and a half before she 
reached MMI.  Dr. Bansal listed his date of service as October 22, 2021, which I 
understand to mean the date he examined her.  As set forth above, it is unclear 
precisely the date Dr. Femino last examined her.  October 22, 2021, however, is the 
same date she saw Dr. Femino for treatment purposes.  Dr. Femino did not evaluate 
her for permanent disability at this time.  In fact, the opposite is true.  He assigned 
temporary restrictions and further treatment.  (Jt. Ex. 4, p. 31)  While this treatment 
ultimately did not help improve her condition, it was undoubtedly a mistake for 
claimant’s counsel to arrange an IME at this time when the statute clearly requires the 
claimant is only entitled to an IME after an evaluation of permanency.  As such, she is 
not entitled to reimbursement of IME expenses. 
The claimant is also seeking costs under Iowa Code Section 10A.328.  Costs are 
discretionary.  See 876 Iowa Administrative Code Section 4.33.  Under Rule 4.33, 
however, the allowable cost has been interpreted to only include the cost of a medical 
report, not the cost of evaluation.  Dr. Bansal did not itemize his report fee which was 
$1,374.00.  (Cl. Ex. 1, p. 5)  Utilizing the discretion afforded to the agency, I find that 
$1,099.20 is allowable as a cost.  She is also entitled to her filing fee of $103.00.
