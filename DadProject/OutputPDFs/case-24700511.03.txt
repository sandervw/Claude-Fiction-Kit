STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
On May 2, 2025, the claimant filed a petition for alternate medical care pursuant 
to Iowa Code 85.27(4) and 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.48.  The defendants filed an 
answer admitting liability for injuries to the claimant’s left shoulder and left arm.  
The undersigned presided over the hearing held electronically and recorded 
digitally on May 13, 2025.  That recording constitutes the official record of the 
proceeding pursuant to 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.48(12).  The claimant 
participated through his attorney, Emily Schott Hood.  The defendants participated 
through their attorney, Julie Burger.   
Prior to the hearing, the claimant submitted ten pages of exhibits, marked as 
Exhibits 1-3.  The defendants submitted no exhibits.  The claimant’s exhibits were 
received into the record without objection. 
 
On July 21, 2023, the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commissioner issued an 
order delegating authority to deputy workers’ compensation commissioners, such as the 
undersigned, to issue final agency decisions on applications for alternate care.  
Consequently, this decision constitutes final agency action, and there is no appeal to 
the commissioner.  Judicial review in a district court pursuant to Iowa Code Chapter 17A 
is the avenue for an appeal.

ISSUE 
The issue under consideration is whether the defendants should be ordered to 
authorize and provide the claimant with an MRI of the left shoulder. 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
Edward Davidson is the claimant in this matter.  The claimant alleges that he was 
injured while working on November 1, 2022.   
 
The defendants initially denied liability for Mr. Davidson’s injuries.  During that 
time, his counsel represented that he sought care with Dr. Tedesco at UnityPoint Health 
Grinnell Orthopaedic Clinic.  (Claimant’s Exhibit 2:9).  It is unclear what treatment he 
had with Dr. Tedesco, as there are no records in the evidentiary record for this 
proceeding.  (CE 2:9).   
 
On March 25, 2025, the defendants had Mr. Davidson examined by Jonathon 
Fields, M.D., M.B.A., M.P.H., C.I.M.E., for an independent medical examination (“IME”).  
(CE 1:1-8).  Subsequent to the examination, Dr. Fields issued a report outlining his 
findings and opinions.  (CE 1:1-8).  At the outset of the report, Dr. Fields noted that he 
informed Mr. Davidson that their interaction did not constitute a doctor-patient 
relationship, that he was not Mr. Davidson’s treating provider, and that Dr. Fields was 
not providing him with any sort of treatment for his complaints.  (CE 1:1).  It is 
documented that Mr. Davidson understood these caveats.  (CE 1:1).   
 
In preparing his report, Dr. Fields reviewed a number of medical records.  (CE 
1:1).  These records were not summarized in detail in Dr. Fields’ report.  (CE 1:1-8).  Mr. 
Davidson noted that on November 1, 2022, he was working when he opened a door at 
the mill.  (CE 1:2).  Due to an air pressure issue, the door ripped open, throwing him into 
the building.  (CE 1:2).  While he was thrown, he held a “sissy bar.”  (CE 1:2).  He then 
felt a pop in his left biceps and left shoulder and noted immediate pain.  (CE 1:2).  He 
developed bruising and a deformity over his left bicep.  (CE 1:2).  Over the next few 
weeks, his pain dissipated and his bruising resolved.  (CE 1:2).  Mr. Davidson alleged 
that his injury was never documented by the defendant-employer.  (CE 1:2).  Since no 
treatment was offered, the claimant followed up with his primary care provider, Dr. 
Tedesco.  (CE 1:3).  At the time of the examination, Mr. Davidson had no work 
restrictions.  (CE 1:3).   
 
Mr. Davidson told Dr. Fields that he experienced a constant ache in his left 
shoulder, and would develop a “stinger” in his left shoulder with use.  (CE 1:4).  He had 
increased pain in his left biceps when attempting to lift anything.  (CE 1:4).  Upon 
examination, Dr. Fields observed that Mr. Davidson had no visible atrophy in his left 
arm.  (CE 1:5).  Dr. Fields noted that Mr. Davidson had a deformed left biceps, and the 
diameter of his biceps was uneven.  (CE 1:5).  Dr. Fields also noted an inability to 
palpate the distal biceps tendon in his left arm.  (CE 1:5).  Dr. Fields then documented 
range of motion in both of Mr. Davidson’s shoulders.  (CE 1:6).  Mr. Davidson displayed 
reduced range of motion in his left shoulder, especially when compared to his right

shoulder.  (CE 1:6).  Dr. Fields noted that Mr. Davidson had tenderness to palpation 
over his proximal left biceps tendons, but no tenderness over the AC joint.  (CE 1:6).   
 
Dr. Fields then responded to specific questions from defendants’ counsel.  (CE

and thus he could not assess any permanent impairment.  (CE 1:7).   
In a letter dated April 8, 2025, Claimant’s counsel represented to defendants’ 
counsel that Dr. Tedesco ordered an MRI, but that Mr. Davidson did not fit into the MRI 
machine due to his broad shoulders.  (CE 2:9).  According to claimant’s counsel, Dr. 
Tedesco was in the process of referring Mr. Davidson to an open MRI in Des Moines.  
(CE 2:9).  In the interim, defendants accepted liability, and claimant decided to “await an 
appointment scheduled by” defendants for an MRI.  (CE 2:9).  Claimant’s counsel 
requested that the defendants refer Mr. Davidson for an open MRI in this 
correspondence.  (CE 2:9).   
In response to Dr. Fields’ report, the defendants authorized Dr. Pape, an 
orthopedic physician, as an authorized treating provider.  They arranged for an 
appointment for Mr. Davidson with Dr. Pape on May 20, 2025.  (CE 3:10).  This 
appointment was so that Dr. Pape could evaluate Mr. Davidson’s condition, and 
recommend further treatment.  (CE 3:10).  During the hearing, counsel for the 
defendants represented that they would authorize an MRI for Mr. Davidson in the event 
that Dr. Pape recommends said treatment.   
  
CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Iowa Code 85.27(4) provides, in relevant part: 
For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish reasonable 
services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has the right to 
choose the care….  The treatment must be offered promptly and be 
reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the 
employee.  If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the care 
offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such dissatisfaction 
to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the employer and 
the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited to treat the 
injury.  If the employer and employee cannot agree on such alternate care,

the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable proofs of the 
necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
Iowa Code 85.27(4). See Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 433 (Iowa 
1997).   
“Iowa Code section 85.27(4) affords an employer who does not contest the 
compensability of a workplace injury a qualified statutory right to control the medical 
care provided to an injured employee.”  Ramirez-Trujillo v. Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 
N.W.2d 759, 769 (Iowa 2016) (citing R.R. Donnelly & Sons v. Barnett, 670 N.W.2d 190, 
195, 197 (Iowa 2003)).  “In enacting the right-to-choose provision in section 85.27(4), 
our legislature sought to balance the interests of injured employees against the 
competing interests of their employers.”  Ramirez, 878 N.W.2d at 770-71 (citing Bell 
Bros., 779 N.W.2d at 202, 207; IBP, Inc. v. Harker, 633 N.W.2d 322, 326-27 (Iowa 
2001)).   
The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except where the 
employer has denied liability for the injury.  Iowa Code section 85.27; Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening, October 16, 1975).  An employer’s right to select 
the provider of medical treatment to an injured worker does not include the right to 
determine how an injured worker should be diagnosed, evaluated, treated, or other 
matters of professional medical judgment.  Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 
866389 (Declaratory Ruling, May 18, 1988).  Reasonable care includes care necessary 
to diagnose the condition, and defendants are not entitled to interfere with the medical 
judgment of its own treating physician.  Pote v. Mickow Corp., File No. 694639 (Review-
Reopening Decision, June 17, 1986).   
The employer must furnish “reasonable medical services and supplies and 
reasonable and necessary appliances to treat an injured employee.”  Stone Container 
Corp. v. Castle, 657 N.W.2d 485, 490 (Iowa 2003) (emphasis in original).  Such 
employer-provided care “must be offered promptly and be reasonably suited to treat the 
injury without undue inconvenience to the employee.”  Iowa Code section 85.27(4).  
More specifically in Long, the court noted that “the employer’s obligation under the 
statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.”  Long v. Roberts 
Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122, 128 (Iowa 1995).   
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment - and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See e.g. 
Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e); Bell Bros. Heating and Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 
N.W.2d 193, 209 (Iowa 2010); Long, 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  More specifically in 
Long, the court noted that “the employer’s obligation under the statute turns on the 
question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.”  Long, 528 N.W.2d at 128.   
An injured employee dissatisfied with the employer-furnished care (or lack 
thereof) may share the employee’s discontent with the employer and if the parties 
cannot reach an agreement on alternate care, “the commissioner may, upon application

and reasonable proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and order the care.”  Id.  
“Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.”  Long, 528 
N.W.2d at 123; Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 436.  As the party seeking 
relief in the form of alternate care, the employee bears the burden of proving that the 
authorized care is unreasonable.  Id. at 124; Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d at 209; Pirelli-
Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 436.  Because “the employer’s obligation under the 
statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability,” an injured 
employee’s dissatisfaction with employer-provided care, standing alone, is not enough 
to find such care unreasonable.  Id.  However, if substantial evidence exists that the 
care authorized by the employer was ineffective, it may be found that the care was not 
reasonable.  Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 437.   
    Claimant’s counsel argued that not providing Mr. Davidson with an MRI based 
upon the recommendations of Dr. Fields was unreasonable conduct on behalf of the 
defendants.  They argued that the defendants asked Dr. Fields to opine on additional 
care needed by Mr. Davidson, and that Dr. Fields opined that Mr. Davidson needed an 
MRI.  Thus, their argument was that the defendants should be ordered to authorize and 
provide the MRI based upon Dr. Fields’ opinions.  In their theory, not ordering the MRI 
would result in less extensive care.  The claimant also points to Dr. Tedesco’s alleged 
recommendation of an MRI.   
There are three main issues with the claimant’s argument.  First, there are no 
records from the claimant’s visits to Dr. Tedesco in the record.  Therefore, it is difficult to 
assess his opinions.  Second, Dr. Tedesco was not an authorized provider, and the 
record lacks evidence to determine whether he was a treating provider.  Third, Dr. 
Fields was not an authorized treating provider.  Dr. Fields was retained by the 
defendants in order to conduct an IME.  As noted above, an IME is an independent 
medical examination, meaning an examination by an independent provider who is 
generally not a treating physician for the individual being examined.  Dr. Fields’ report 
begins with an admonition that he is not providing treatment to the claimant, nor is he 
establishing a doctor-patient relationship with Mr. Davidson.   
Based upon comments made during the hearing, the defendants initially denied 
Mr. Davidson’s allegations, and did not direct or provide medical care.  After reviewing 
the results of Dr. Fields’ IME, they accepted liability for the claim related to the 
claimant’s left arm and left shoulder.  Thus, they began to direct treatment.  In doing so, 
they authorized treatment with Dr. Pape as the new authorized treating provider.  The 
defendants noted during the hearing that they would abide by the treatment 
recommendations of Dr. Pape, including authorizing an MRI, if ordered.   
Based upon my review of the record, and the arguments of the parties, I find that 
the claimant has demonstrated mere dissatisfaction with care.  The claimant has not 
proven that the care offered by the defendants, by way of authorizing treatment with Dr. 
Pape rather than his desired MRI, is unreasonable.
