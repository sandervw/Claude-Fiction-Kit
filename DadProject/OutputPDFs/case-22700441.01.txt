STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
Claimant, Richard Brevik, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from Derners of Milford (Derners), employer, and Grinnell Mutual 
Reinsurance Company, insurer, both as defendants.  This matter was heard on August 
2, 2023, with a final submission date of October 13, 2023.   
 
The record in this case consists of Joint Exhibits 1 through 4, Claimant’s Exhibits 
1 through 2, Defendants’ Exhibits A through P, and the testimony of claimant and 
Joshua Peters. 
STIPULATIONS 
 
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised 
or discussed in this decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The first issue to be determined is whether claimant’s work injury of July 26, 
2020, resulted in a permanent disability to his left shoulder. 
 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based. A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause. A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible. George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996). 
 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability. 
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); 
Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995). Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted expert medical 
testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 
N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 1994). 
 
Claimant contends that he sustained a permanent disability to his left shoulder 
due to the July 26, 2020, work injury.  Defendants stipulate claimant had a work injury 
on July 26, 2020, but dispute the injury resulted in a permanent disability. 
 
Claimant was evaluated by Dr. Johnson for his left shoulder injury in November 2020.  
In his assessment of claimant in November 2020, Dr. Johnson noted “left shoulder 
acute on chronic rotator cuff tear.”  (JE 4, p. 25)  An acute condition is one that has a 
sudden onset.  A chronic condition is something that has continued on for an extended 
period of time.  (See “acute” https://www.medlineplus./gov/ency/acute (last visited 
February 19, 2024) (defining “acute” as “acute symptoms appear, change, or worsen 
rapidly.  It is the opposite of chronic.”)) (See also “chronic” 
https://www.medlineplus/gov/ency/chronic (last visited February 19, 2024) (defining 
“chronic” as “…something that continues over an extended period of time…Chronic is 
the opposite of acute.”))  The term “chronic rotator cuff tear” may be interpreted as a 
rotator cuff tear that has continued over an extended period of time.  However, the use 
of the word “acute” might also infer the rotator cuff tear occurred as a result of a sudden 
injury.  There is no evidence in the record that Dr. Johnson clarifies the phrase “left

shoulder acute on chronic rotator cuff tear.”  It is found that this term is ambiguous.  It is 
unclear if Dr. Johnson believed the rotator cuff tear was a chronic condition or if it 
occurred suddenly due to the July 2020 injury. 
Three other experts have opined regarding whether the July 26, 2020, incident 
resulted in a permanent disability. 
 
Dr. Bollier is an orthopedic specialist.  He performed a record review.  He 
indicated that he performs approximately 300 rotator cuff surgeries per year and 
reviews over 1000 shoulder MRIs each year.  (Ex. P, p. 95)  He opined that claimant’s 
rotator cuff tear was a chronic condition.  He based his opinion, in part, on his review of 
the MRI that showed atrophy in the rotator cuff muscle, suggesting a chronic, long-
standing rotator cuff tear.  He also indicated that the humeral head suggested a chronic 
condition.  Dr. Bollier also opined that given the fact that claimant did not take off from 
work following the July 2020 incident, this was indicative of a chronic condition.  (Ex. P, 
p. 96) 
 
Dr. Broghammer performed an IME on claimant.  He also opined that the July 26, 
2020, injury did not cause a permanent disability to claimant’s left shoulder.  (Exs. A, O) 
 
Dr. Bansal evaluated claimant once for an IME.  He opined that claimant’s July 
26, 2020, injury to the left shoulder resulted in a permanent disability.  Dr. Bansal’s 
causation opinion does not address issues raised by Dr. Bollier concerning the MRI 
showing atrophy of the rotator cuff muscle or the nature of the humeral head.  Dr. 
Bansal’s opinion fails to offer rationale for these diagnostic findings.  As a result, his 
opinion regarding permanent disability is found not convincing. 
 
Dr. Johnson’s assessment of claimant’s shoulder condition is ambiguous as to 
causation or permanent disability.  Dr. Bollier is an orthopedic specialist.  Dr. Bollier has 
a great deal of experience in rotator cuff surgeries and reviewing shoulder MRIs.  He 
opined claimant’s rotator cuff tear was chronic and not caused by the July 2020 
incident.  Dr. Bollier’s opinion is corroborated by Dr. Broghammer.  Dr. Bansal’s opinion 
of permanent disability is found not convincing.  Given this record, it is found that 
claimant has failed to carry his burden of proof his July 26, 2020, injury to his left 
shoulder resulted in a permanent disability. 
 
Claimant also contends that he sustained carpal tunnel syndrome as a result of 
his July 26, 2020, injury.  Claimant did not plead a carpal tunnel injury to his right upper 
extremity in his petition.  The only expert who found that claimant had a work-related 
carpal tunnel syndrome is Dr. Bansal.  Dr. Bansal opines that claimant’s July 26, 2020, 
injury to his left shoulder “may have” caused or aggravated carpal tunnel syndrome.  
(Ex. 1, p. 12)  Given this record, claimant has failed to carry his burden of proof he 
sustained a carpal tunnel syndrome that arose out of and in the course of his 
employment.

As claimant has failed to carry his burden of proof that he sustained a carpal 
tunnel syndrome that arose out of and in the course of employment, and that his left 
shoulder injury resulted in a permanent disability, the issues of entitlement to permanent 
partial disability benefits and claimant’s entitlement to alternate medical care are found 
moot. 
 
The final issue to be determined is whether claimant is entitled to reimbursement 
for Dr. Bansal’s IME. 
 
Section 85.39 permits an employee to be reimbursed for subsequent 
examination by a physician of the employee's choice where an employer-retained 
physician has previously evaluated “permanent disability” and the employee believes 
that the initial evaluation is too low.  The section also permits reimbursement for 
reasonably necessary transportation expenses incurred and for any wage loss 
occasioned by the employee attending the subsequent examination.  
 
Defendants are responsible only for reasonable fees associated with claimant's 
independent medical examination.  Claimant has the burden of proving the 
reasonableness of the expenses incurred for the examination.  See Schintgen v. 
Economy Fire & Casualty Co., File No. 855298 (App. April 26, 1991).  Claimant need 
not ultimately prove the injury arose out of and in the course of employment to qualify 
for reimbursement under section 85.39.  See Dodd v. Fleetguard, Inc., 759 N.W.2d 133, 
140 (Iowa App. 2008).  
 
The Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commissioner has noted that the Iowa 
Supreme Court adopted a strict and literal interpretation of Iowa Code section 85.39 in 
Des Moines Area Regional Transit Authority v. Young, 867 N.W.2d 839 (Iowa 2015) 
(hereinafter “DART”). See Cortez v. Tyson Fresh Meats, Inc., File No. 5044716 (App. 
December 2015). If an injured worker wants to be reimbursed for the expenses 
associated with a disability evaluation by a physician selected by the worker, the 
process established by the legislature must be followed. This process permits the 
employer, who must pay the benefits, to make the initial arrangements for the 
evaluation and only allows the employee to obtain an independent evaluation at the 
employer’s expense if dissatisfied with the evaluation arranged by the employer. DART, 
867 N.W.2d at 847 (citing Iowa Code § 85.39).  See also P.M. Lattner 
Manufacturing.Co. v. Rife, No. 22-1421, slip op. at 12 (Iowa Supreme Court) (February 
9, 2024).  
 
Defendants stipulated that claimant’s injury to his left shoulder on July 26, 2020, 
arose out of and in the course of employment.  Dr. Bollier, the employer-retained 
physician, found that claimant did not have any permanent impairment to the shoulder 
in a December 13, 2021 report.  Dr. Bansal found that claimant had permanent 
impairment in a July 17, 2023, report.  Claimant failed to carry his burden of proof that 
his carpal tunnel syndrome arose out of and in the course of employment.  Dr. Bansal 
charged $3,967.00 for his IME.  (Ex. 1, p. 14)  As claimant’s carpal tunnel syndrome is 
found not to have arisen out of and in the course of employment, defendants are
