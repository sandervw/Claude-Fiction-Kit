STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Jorge Morelos, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from Oak Grove Pork (Oak Grove), employer, and Western 
Agricultural Insurance Company, insurer, both of the defendants. This matter was heard 
on October 8, 2024 with the final submission date of November 5, 2024. Appearing as 
interpreter was Suzanne Wedeking.  
The record in this case consists of Joint Exhibits 1-3, Claimant’s Exhibits 1-5, 
Defendants’ Exhibits A-H, and the testimony of claimant.   
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of 
those stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration 
decision and no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised 
or discussed in this decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.  
ISSUES 
1. Whether the injury is a cause of permanent disability, and if so, 
 
2. The extent of claimant’s entitlement to permanent partial disability benefits. 
 
3. Costs.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The first issue to be determined is whether claimant’s injury resulted in a 
permanent disability  
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996).

A possibility of causation is not sufficient; a probability is necessary. Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996) (citing Holmes v. Bruce 
Motor Freight, Inc., 215 N.W.2d 296, 297 (Iowa 1974)). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 
621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 
(Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
Claimant had a lumbar fusion. All experts opined that claimant has had some 
degree of permanent impairment from his fall at work and subsequent lower back 
surgery. Given this record, claimant has carried his burden of proof that his injury 
resulted in a permanent disability. 
The next issue to be determined is the extent of claimant’s entitlement to 
permanent partial disability benefits.  
Since claimant has an impairment to the body as a whole, an industrial disability 
has been sustained.  Industrial disability was defined in Diederich v. Tri-City Ry. Co. of 
Iowa, 219 Iowa 587, 591, 258 N.W. 899, 902 (1935) as follows:  "It is therefore plain that 
the Legislature intended the term 'disability' to mean 'industrial disability' or loss of 
earning capacity and not a mere 'functional disability' to be computed in terms of 
percentages of the total physical and mental ability of a normal man." 
Functional impairment is an element to be considered in determining industrial 
disability which is the reduction of earning capacity, but consideration must also be 
given to the injured employee's age, education, qualifications, experience, motivation, 
loss of earnings, severity and situs of the injury, work restrictions, inability to engage in 
employment for which the employee is fitted and the employer's offer of work or failure 
to so offer.  McSpadden v. Big Ben Coal Co., 288 N.W.2d 181, 192 (Iowa 1980); Olson 
v. Goodyear Service Stores, 255 Iowa 1112, 1120, 125 N.W.2d 251, 256 (1963); Barton 
v. Nevada Poultry Co., 253 Iowa 285, 110 N.W.2d 660, 663 (1961). 
Compensation for permanent partial disability shall begin when it is medically 
indicated that maximum medical improvement from the injury has been reached and 
that the extent of loss or percentage of permanent impairment can be determined by the 
use of the guides to the evaluation of permanent impairment, published by the American 
Medical Association, as adopted by the Commissioner. Iowa Code section 85.34(2)

Claimant was 44 years old at the time of hearing. He went up to the sixth grade 
in Mexico. Claimant has worked primarily in the meat packing industry. Claimant does 
not speak English.  
Claimant fell off a ladder at work. Claimant had an L4-5 lumbar fusion as a result 
of that fall.  
Claimant was injured on April 20, 2021.  Approximately one month after the 
injury, claimant was terminated from his employment with Oak Grove. Defendant-
employer contends that claimant was terminated due, in large part, to harassing co-
workers, threatening to fight a coworker and taking excessive time to perform work 
duties. (Ex. 4, p. 26; Ex. G) 
Claimant had worked for Oak Grove for approximately four years prior to his 
injury. There is nothing in the record to indicate that claimant had harassed co-workers, 
threatened to fight co-workers, or took excessive time to complete work duties until one 
month after his work injury. Oak Grove initially denied claimant’s unemployment 
insurance benefits. Defendant employer failed to attend a hearing regarding claimant’s 
claim for unemployment insurance benefits.  As a result, claimant was granted 
unemployment insurance benefits. This record suggests claimant’s termination may 
have had something to do with his work injury. 
Three experts have opined to the degree of permanent impairment for claimant 
after his injury. Dr. Boulden evaluated claimant once for an IME. Dr. Boulden opined 
that claimant had a 10 percent permanent impairment to the body as a whole caused by 
his fall and subsequent surgery. He also found claimant had no permanent restrictions. 
There is no indication in the report how Dr. Boulden arrived at the figure of 10 percent 
for permanent impairment. There is no analysis how Dr. Boulden reached this figure. 
There is no reference to the Guides.  Based on this lack of analysis or reference to any 
Guides or treatise, it is found that Dr. Boulden's opinions regarding permanent 
impairment are not convincing. 
Dr. Rosenthal treated claimant for an extended period of time. He opined that 
claimant had 20 percent permanent impairment based upon a finding that claimant fell 
in the DRE lumbar category IV of the Guides. (JE 1, p. 46) 
Dr. Sassman evaluated claimant once for an IME. Dr. Sassman found that 
claimant had a 25 percent permanent impairment, finding that claimant’s condition fell in 
the DRE lumbar category V of the Guides.  (Ex. 1, p. 6) Dr.  Sassman evaluated 
claimant in January of 2024. Dr. Rosenthal evaluated claimant for his rating of 
permanent impairment in June 2022. Dr. Sassman's rating is based, in part, on a finding 
that claimant had atrophy in a calf muscle. Dr. Sassman's evaluation of permanent 
impairment is closer to the time of hearing and is a more accurate picture of claimant’s 
permanent impairment at the time of hearing. Dr. Sassman’s evaluation of permanent 
impairment is slightly more detailed than that of Dr. Rosenthal.  Based on this record, it 
is found that Dr. Sassman's evaluation of permanent impairment is more convincing

than that of Dr. Rosenthal.  Given this record, it is found that claimant has a 25 percent 
permanent impairment from his fall and subsequent lumbar surgery. 
Claimant underwent two FCEs.  One FCE found claimant gave invalid effort. The 
other FCE found claimant gave valid effort and limited him to lifting 20 pounds 
occasionally. Physical therapy records from March of 2022 found that claimant could lift 
50 pounds from floor to waist with no back pain. Because of the discrepancies between 
the two FCEs and the physical therapy records, I am unable to make a finding of fact 
regarding claimant’s permanent restrictions.   
At the time of hearing, the record indicated claimant was actively seeking work.  
The record suggests claimant sought work in 2021 and 2022, in part to allow him to 
continue to receive unemployment insurance benefits. The record indicates claimant did 
not seek work at all in 2023. 
A loss of earning capacity due to voluntary choice or lack of motivation to return 
to work is not compensable. Malget v. John Deere Waterloo Works, File No. 5048441 
(Remand Dec. May 23, 2018); Rus v. Bradley Puhrmann, File No. 5037928 (App. 
December 16, 2014); Gaffney v. Nordstrom, File No. 5026533 (App. September 7, 
2011); Main v. Quaker Oats, File No. 5017903 (App. December 19 2007); Snow v. 
Chevron Phillips Chemical Co., File No. 5016619 (App. October 25, 2007);  Copeland v. 
Boone Book and Bible Store, File No. 1059319 (App. November 6, 1997).  See also 
Brown v. Nissen Corp., 89-90 IAWC 56, 62 (App. 1989) (no prima facie showing that 
claimant is unemployable when claimant did not make an attempt for vocational 
rehabilitation). 
When all relevant factors are considered, it is found that claimant has a 40 
percent loss of earning capacity or a 40 percent industrial disability.  
The final issue is costs.  As claimant prevailed on most of the issues in this case, 
in my discretion I award costs to claimant. 
ORDER 
THEREFORE IT IS ORDERED:  
That defendants shall pay claimant 200 weeks of permanent partial disability 
benefits at the rate of four hundred sixty-three and 16/100 dollars ($463.16)

the federal reserve in the most recent H15 report settled as of the date of injury, plus 
two percent.  
That defendants shall be given credit for benefits previously paid. 
That defendants shall pay costs.
