STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
On May 23, 2024, the claimant filed a petition for alternate medical care pursuant 
to Iowa Code section 85.27(4) and 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.48.   
The defendants failed to file an appearance or an answer for the alternate 
medical care hearing.  The claimant filed proof of service documents, as well as 
documentation of communication with the defendant-insurer advising them of the filing 
of the original notice and petition concerning alternate medical care.   
The undersigned presided over the hearing held via telephone and recorded 
digitally on June 4, 2024.  That recording constitutes the official record of the 
proceeding pursuant to 876 Iowa Administrative Code 4.48(12).  Claimant participated 
through his attorney, Joseph Powell.  No representative of the defendants appeared.   
Prior to the hearing, the claimant submitted ten pages of exhibits, marked as 
Exhibits 1-2.   
On July 21, 2023, the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commissioner issued an 
order delegating authority to deputy workers’ compensation commissioners, such as the 
undersigned, to issue final agency decisions on applications for alternate care.  
Consequently, this decision constitutes final agency action, and there is no appeal to 
the commissioner.  Judicial review in a district court pursuant to Iowa Code Chapter 17A 
is the avenue for an appeal.

ISSUE 
The issue under consideration is whether an order for certain alternate medical 
care is appropriate.   
FINDINGS OF FACT 
Richard Sheehan, the claimant, alleges that he sustained injuries to his back that 
arose out of and in the course of his employment with the defendant-employer on April 
3, 2024.  Since the time of the injury, Mr. Sheehan treated with Eric Petersen, D.O.  
(Claimant’s Exhibit 1:1).   
During a visit with Dr. Petersen on April 18, 2024, Mr. Sheehan described pain in 
his back, including spasms.  (CE 1:1).  He also felt as though he had a golf ball in his 
right buttock.  (CE 1:1).   Pain progressed down his left leg.  (CE 1:1).  Dr. Petersen 
prescribed certain medications, and recommended an x-ray.  (CE 1:1-2).   
On April 25, 2024, Mr. Sheehan followed-up with Dr. Petersen regarding his low 
back pain and lumbar radiculopathy into his right leg.  (CE 1:3).  He attended one 
physical therapy visit and continued to take muscle relaxers.  (CE 1:3).  Dr. Petersen 
recommended that Mr. Sheehan continue physical therapy and taking muscle relaxers.  
(CE 1:4).   
Kimberly Thompson, D.O., examined Mr. Sheehan for his low back issue on May 
2, 2024.  (CE 1:5).  Dr. Thompson is located in the same clinic as Dr. Petersen.  (CE 
1:5).  Mr. Sheehan described a burning sensation in his low back, along with shooting 
pain down his right leg.  (CE 1:5).  Physical therapy had yet to yield positive results.  
(CE 1:5).  Mr. Sheehan could not have an MRI due to a prior surgical history.  (CE 1:5).  
Dr. Thompson recommended continued physical therapy and medications.  (CE 1:6).  If 
physical therapy did not provide improvement within one week, she contemplated a 
referral for a CT scan of the lumbar spine.  (CE 1:6).   
Claimant’s counsel e-mailed the defendant-insurer on May 14, 2024, requesting 
an update as to the scheduling of a recommended CT scan for the claimant.  (CE 2:2).  
Claimant’s counsel indicated that the e-mail served as his client’s notice of 
dissatisfaction with care should the CT scan not be approved.  (CE 2:2).   
In response to a check-box letter from claimant’s counsel, Dr. Petersen agreed 
on May 22, 2024, that a CT scan of the claimant’s low back was reasonable and 
necessary in order to diagnose Mr. Sheehan’s current back complaints.  (CE 1:7).   
Claimant’s counsel forwarded communications with the defendant-insurer dated 
May 22, 2024, in which he requests an update as to a CT scan requested by Dr. 
Petersen.  (CE 2:1-2).   
 
CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Iowa Code 85.27(4) provides, in relevant part:

For purposes of this section, the employer is obligated to furnish 
reasonable services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has the 
right to choose the care….  The treatment must be offered promptly and be 
reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the 
employee.  If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the care 
offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such dissatisfaction 
to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the employer and 
the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited to treat the 
injury.  If the employer and employee cannot agree on such alternate care, 
the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable proofs of the 
necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
Iowa Code 85.27(4). See Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 433 (Iowa 
1997).   
“Iowa Code section 85.27(4) affords an employer who does not contest the 
compensability of a workplace injury a qualified statutory right to control the medical 
care provided to an injured employee.”  Ramirez-Trujillo v. Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 
N.W.2d 759, 769 (Iowa 2016) (citing R.R. Donnelly & Sons v. Barnett, 670 N.W.2d 190, 
195, 197 (Iowa 2003)).  “In enacting the right-to-choose provision in section 85.27(4), 
our legislature sought to balance the interests of injured employees against the 
competing interests of their employers.”  Ramirez-Trujillo, 878 N.W.2d at 770-71 (citing 
Bell Bros., 779 N.W.2d at 202, 207; IBP, Inc. v. Harker, 633 N.W.2d 322, 326-27 (Iowa 
2001)).   
The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except where the 
employer has denied liability for the injury.  Iowa Code section 85.27; Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening, October 16, 1975).  An employer’s right to select 
the provider of medical treatment to an injured worker does not include the right to 
determine how an injured worker should be diagnosed, evaluated, treated, or other 
matters of professional medical judgment.  Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 
866389 (Declaratory Ruling, May 19, 1988).  Reasonable care includes care necessary 
to diagnose the condition, and defendants are not entitled to interfere with the medical 
judgment of its own treating physician.  Pote v. Mickow Corp., File No. 694639 (Review-
Reopening Decision, June 17, 1986).   
The employer must furnish “reasonable medical services and supplies and 
reasonable and necessary appliances to treat an injured employee.”  Stone Container 
Corp. v. Castle, 657 N.W.2d 485, 490 (Iowa 2003)(emphasis in original).  Such 
employer-provided care “must be offered promptly and be reasonably suited to treat the 
injury without undue inconvenience to the employee.”  Iowa Code section 85.27(4).   
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment - and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See e.g. 
Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e); Bell Bros. Heating and Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 
N.W.2d 193, 209 (Iowa 2010); Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).

An injured employee dissatisfied with the employer-furnished care (or lack thereof) may 
share the employee’s discontent with the employer and if the parties cannot reach an 
agreement on alternate care, “the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable 
proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and order the care.”  Id.  “Determining what care 
is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.”  Long, 528 N.W.2d at 123; Pirelli-
Armstrong Tire Co., 562 N.W.2d at 436.  As the party seeking relief in the form of 
alternate care, the employee bears the burden of proving that the authorized care is 
unreasonable.  Id. at 124; Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d at 209; Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co., 562 
N.W.2d at 436.  Because “the employer’s obligation under the statute turns on the 
question of reasonable necessity, not desirability,” an injured employee’s dissatisfaction 
with employer-provided care, standing alone, is not enough to find such care 
unreasonable.  Id.   
Mr. Sheehan treated consistently with Medical Associates of Maquoketa, P.C.  
This included seeing both Dr. Petersen and Dr. Thompson.  They both appear to have 
been authorized treating providers.  Dr. Petersen recommended that the claimant have 
a CT scan of his back in order to determine the cause of his ongoing symptoms.  He 
further opined that this would be reasonable and necessary treatment.   
Authorizing the CT scan of the back is reasonable and necessary based upon 
the evidence in the record.  Therefore, the original notice and petition concerning 
application for alternate medical care is granted.   
IT IS THEREFORE ORDERED: 
1. The claimant’s petition concerning application for alternate medical care is 
granted.   
  
2. The defendants shall, within five (5) days of the date of this order, schedule a 
CT scan as ordered by Dr. Petersen.  The CT scan itself need not to occur 
within five (5) days of the date of this order.
