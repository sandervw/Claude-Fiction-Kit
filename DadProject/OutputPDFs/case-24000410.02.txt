STATEMENT OF THE CASE

ISSUE 
The issue presented for resolution is whether the claimant is entitled to alternate 
medical care in the form of: 
 Authorization to treat with Morgan Laholt, M.D., at the Madonna 
Rehabilitation Center, or in the alternative the care recommended by 
Douglas Martin, M.D., which includes a neuropsychological assessment, 
MRI, and medication management.  
FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned, having considered all the evidence in the record, finds: 
On December 12, 2023, claimant sustained a work-related injury to his head. 
(See Petition; Answer).  According to the claimant’s testimony, he was in the process of 
unloading his work truck when the back gate swung open and the door latch struck him 
in the face. (Testimony).  The impact fractured his left eye socket and nose. (Id.).  
Defendants admitted liability for the injury and authorized medical care. (See Answer; 
Testimony). 
According to the limited medical records in evidence, following the incident 
claimant was taken to the emergency room (ER) at CHI Health in Omaha, Nebraska.  
(Cl Ex. 1, p. 2).  CT scans were taken of his brain and face. (Id.).  He was diagnosed 
with a mildly displaced and comminuted left maxillary and orbital floor fracture, a left 
nasal fracture, and a concussion. (Id. at 2, 5).  After being discharged, claimant was 
evaluated by a plastic surgeon. (Id. at 2).  He did not recommend surgery. (Id.).  
Claimant was also seen by a dentist and an eye doctor. (Id.).  Both specialists indicated 
there were no structural issues or abnormalities. (Id.).   
Claimant testified that he continued to experience symptoms after these 
evaluations, including sensitivity on the left side of his face, headaches, memory loss, 
light sensitivity, and mental issues such as suicidal thoughts. (Testimony).  In February 
2024, defendants authorized additional treatment with Todd Caze, II, Ph.D. (Id.).  Dr. 
Caze is a neuropsychologist. (Def. Ex. A, p. 4). He runs the Caze Concussion Clinic in 
Omaha, Nebraska. (Id.)  According to the medical records, Dr. Caze evaluated the 
claimant on February 6, 2024. (Id. at 3).  He diagnosed him with a concussion and 
prescribed a home exercise  plan and as well as a two-week work conditioning/physical 
therapy program. (Id.).  A follow-up note from Dr. Caze, dated February 7, 2024, also 
states that the claimant would be attending behavioral health appointments three times 
a week. (Id. at 4).  Dr. Caze provided the claimant with work restrictions and indicated 
he should follow up in two weeks. (Id.).  
The claimant attended a work hardening/work conditioning evaluation at Athletico 
on February 13, 2024. (Ex. B, p. 1). The report noted the claimant was still experiencing 
sensitivity to bright lights and headaches with exertion. (Id.).  The evaluation indicated 
that claimant could perform 5/5 of his job demands as a driver, but still recommended

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Under Iowa law, an employer who has accepted compensability for a workplace 
injury has a right to control the care provided to the injured employee.  Ramirez-Trujillo 
v. Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 N.W.2d 759, 769 (Iowa 2016).  The relevant statute provides 
as follows: 
For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish 
reasonable services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has the 
right to choose the care. . . . The treatment must be offered promptly and 
be reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the 
employee. If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the care 
offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such dissatisfaction 
to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the employer and 
the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited to treat the 
injury. If the employer and employee cannot agree on such alternate care, 
the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable proofs of the 
necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
Iowa Code § 85.27(4).   
Defendants’ “obligation under the statute is confined to reasonable care for the 
diagnosis and treatment of work-related injuries.” Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 
N.W.2d 122, 124 (Iowa 1995) (emphasis in original).  In other words, the “obligation 
under the statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.”  (Id.). 
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  See Iowa Code § 85.27(4). By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment 
and seeking alternate care, claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care 
is unreasonable.  See Iowa R. App. P 6.904(3)(e); Long, 528 N.W.2d at 124.  
Ultimately, determining whether care is reasonable under the statute is a question of 
fact.  Long, 528 N.W.2d at 123. 
Under Iowa Code section 85.27, claimant bears the burden of providing 
“reasonable proofs of the necessity” to order alternate care.  This includes submitting 
evidence for all of the statutory requirements outlined in Iowa Code section 85.27.  
Code section 85.27(4) requires an employee notify defendants of his or her 
dissatisfaction with care before applying to the agency for alternate medical care.  
According to the statute, this requirement is intended to give the parties an opportunity 
to reach an understanding on future care for the employee, before asking the agency to 
intervene.  There is no evidence in the hearing record that the claimant contacted 
defendants and requested care with Dr. Laholt and/or for defendants to authorize the 
care recommended by Dr. Martin before he filed this alternate care action.  That is a

requirement under the statute.  Claimant has not met it.  The undersigned cannot order 
the alternate care requested.  
With that said, Dr. Caze recommended claimant see a neurologist back in 
February of 2024. (Ex. A, p. 5).  Dr. Martin recommended that the claimant get a 
neuropsychological assessment, an MRI, and medication management in July 2024. 
(Ex. 1, p. 5).  A significant amount of time has passed since those recommendations 
were made, and defendants just now authorized care with Dr. Gregory at the start of 
this hearing. (Testimony).  At the hearing defendants’ counsel indicated some of this 
delay was caused by claimant’s failure to supply a signed patient’s waiver and/or prior 
records.  The undersigned cannot find any authority for this stance.  An employer’s right 
to select the provider of medical treatment for an injured worker does not include the 
right to determine how an injured worker should be diagnosed, evaluated, treated, or 
other matters of professional medical judgment.  Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 
866389 (Declaratory Ruling, May 18, 1988).  Defendants are not entitled to interfere 
with the medical judgment of their own treating physician, even if they lack a signed 
patient’s waiver and/or prior records. Pote v. Mickow Corp., File No. 694639 (Review-
Reopening, June 17, 1986).  Defendants should proceed accordingly in the future.     
Under the record currently before the undersigned, claimant’s request for 
alternate medical care is denied at this time.  If defendants fail to meet their obligations 
under Iowa Code 85.27 in the future, claimant has the right to re-file his petition for 
alternate medical care.   
THEREFORE IT IS ORDERED: 
Claimant's petition for alternate medical care is denied at this time.
