On June 4, 2024, the undersigned filed an arbitration decision in this case.  On 
June 19, 2024, the claimant filed an application for rehearing pursuant to 876 IAC 4.24. 
Claimant’s application urges reconsideration of his entitlement to healing period 
benefits between September 21, 2017, and December 15, 2022, or the proper 
commencement date for permanent partial disability benefits. 
Claimant correctly points out that the decision reached contradictory conclusions 
as to when he reached maximum medical improvement for his low back condition.  
When discussing the commencement date, the undersigned determined that the 
claimant did not reach maximum medical improvement for his low back condition until 
December 27, 2022.  Then, when discussing claimant’s entitlement to additional healing 
period benefits, the undersigned provided, 
Dr. Binegar placed claimant at maximum medical improvement for his low 
back on September 27, 2017.  Although his low back complaints continued, 
the persistence of pain does not prevent a finding that the healing period 
has ended if the underlying condition is stable.  I find claimant’s initial 
healing period ended on September 27, 2017. 
The earliest claimant’s healing period could have recommenced is 
November 27, 2019, the day after he reached maximum medical 
improvement for the right hip condition.  However, no physician restricted 
claimant from working due to his low back between November 27, 2019, 
and December 27, 2022.  Moreover, it appears from claimant’s testimony

The undersigned committed a scrivener’s error by failing to revise the paragraph 
pertaining to the proper commencement date in the final draft of the arbitration decision.  
It was and remains my intention to find that claimant reached maximum medical 
improvement for both the left shoulder and low back conditions on September 20, 2017.  
I conclude that an order nunc pro tunc is an allowable means to correct this error to 
remedy this situation and enter an order that reflects my actual intention at the time of 
filing this arbitration decision. 
The phrase, “nunc pro tunc” means “now for then.”  See:  Black’s Law Dictionary, 
page 1218 (Revised 4th Edition 1968).  The definition in Black’s Law Dictionary further 
provides:  “A phrase applied to acts allowed to be done after the time when they should 
be done, with a retroactive effect, i.e., with the same effect as if regularly done.”  Black’s 
at 1218.  A nunc pro tunc order “is not for the purpose of correcting judicial thinking, a 
judicial conclusion, or a mistake of law.”  Headley v. Headley, 172 N.W.2d 104, 108 
(Iowa 1969).  The nunc pro tunc order can be employed to correct obvious errors or to 
make an order conform to the judge’s original intent.  Graber v. District Court for 
Washington Cty, 410 N.W.2d 224, 229 (Iowa 1987).  Brinson v. Spee Dee Delivery 
Service, No. 8-754/06-2074 (Iowa App. Nov. 13, 2008).   
I conclude that an Order Nunc Pro Tunc is more appropriate than a rehearing.  
Therefore, the application for rehearing is denied; however, an Order Nunc Pro Tunc is 
appropriate. 
THEREFORE, IT IS ORDERED:  
Page 15 of the arbitration decision is modified as follows: 
The parties dispute the proper commencement date for permanent 
partial disability benefits. The commencement date for permanent partial 
disability benefits is the date of maximum medical improvement. Iowa 
Code section 85.34(2)  Houdek’s left shoulder and low back conditions 
reached maximum medical improvement on September 20, 2017.  
The arbitration decision shall be modified to include the following order: 
Defendants shall pay claimant two hundred fifty (250) weeks of 
permanent partial disability benefits commencing on September 20, 2017.
