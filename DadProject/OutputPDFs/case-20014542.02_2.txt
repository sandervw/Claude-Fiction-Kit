On January 8, 2025, the undersigned issued an arbitration decision in this case. 
The claimant applied for nunc pro tunc regarding parts of that decision so that Section 
VI of the decision, “Order,” matched the penalty determination on page 45. In reply to an 
email the undersigned sent to counsel for the parties, defense counsel stated the 
defendants would not resist the motion for order nunc pro tunc. 
The phrase “nunc pro tunc” means “now for then.”  See Black’s Law Dictionary, 
1218 (rev. 4th ed. 1968).  The definition in Black’s Law Dictionary further provides, “A 
phrase applied to acts allowed to be done after the time when they should be done, with 
a retroactive effect, i.e., with the same effect as if regularly done.”  Id.  A nunc pro tunc 
order “is not for the purpose of correcting judicial thinking, a judicial conclusion, or a 
mistake of law.”  Headley v. Headley, 172 N.W.2d 104, 108 (Iowa 1969).  Rather, the 
nunc pro tunc order can be employed to correct obvious errors or to make an order 
conform to the judge’s original intent.  Graber v. Iowa Dist. Court for Washington 
County, 410 N.W.2d 224, 229 (Iowa 1987); Brinson v. Spee Dee Delivery Service, No. 
8-754/06-2074 (Iowa App. 2008).  “[T]he intent of the trial judge is crucial to the 
determination of whether a nunc pro tunc order is appropriate to ‘correct’ a record.” 
Freeman v. Ernst & Young, 541 N.W.2d 890, 893 (Iowa 1995) (citing McVay v. Kenneth 
E. Montz Implement Co., 287 N.W.2d 149, 151 (Iowa 1980)). 
THEREFORE, IT IS ORDERED: 
1) The motion is GRANTED.
