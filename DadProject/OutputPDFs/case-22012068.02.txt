Kevin Koeller, claimant, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from Cardinal Logistics Management Corp., employer, and ACE 
American Insurance Co., insurance carrier, as defendants.  The hearing was held on 
November 15, 2023.  Pursuant to an order from the Iowa Workers’ Compensation 
Commissioner, this case was heard via videoconference using Zoom with all parties 
and the court reporter appearing remotely.   
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  Those 
stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration decision and 
no factual issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised or discussed in this 
decision.  The parties are now bound by their stipulations.  
Kevin Koeller was the only witness to testify live at trial.  The evidentiary record 
also includes joint exhibits 1-3, claimant’s exhibits 1-9, and defendants’ revised exhibits 
A1-A3.  All exhibits were received into the record without objection.  
The parties submitted post-hearing briefs on December 13, 2023, at which time 
the case was fully submitted to the undersigned.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 
employment.  Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. Dial 
Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309 (Iowa 1996).  The words “arising out of” refer to the cause or 
source of the injury.  The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 
circumstances of the injury.  2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124 (Iowa 1995).  
An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists between the

injury and the employment.  Miedema, 551 N.W.2d 309.  The injury must be a rational 
consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely incidental to 
the employment.  Koehler Electric v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1 (Iowa 2000); Miedema, 551 
N.W.2d 309.  An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it happens within a 
period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may be when 
performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties or doing 
an activity incidental to them.  Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143. 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa Ct. App. 1997); 
Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony.  The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question.  The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances.  The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); 
Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995).  Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994).  Unrebutted expert medical 
testimony cannot be summarily rejected.  Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 
N.W.2d 910 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
When an expert's opinion is based upon an incomplete or incorrect history, it is 
not necessarily binding on the commissioner or the court. It is then to be weighed, 
together with other facts and circumstances, the ultimate conclusion being for the finder 
of the fact.  Musselman v. Central Telephone Company, 154 N.W.2d 128, 133 (Iowa 
1967); Bodish v. Fischer, Inc., 257 Iowa 521, 522, 133 N.W.2d 867, 870 (1965).  The 
commissioner as trier of fact has the duty to determine the credibility of the witnesses 
and to weigh the evidence, together with the other disclosed facts and circumstances, 
and then to accept or reject the opinion.  Dunlavey, 526 N.W.2d 845. 
Under the Iowa Workers' Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is 
compensated either for a loss or loss of use of a scheduled member under Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(a)-(u) or for loss of earning capacity under section 85.34(2)(v). The 
extent of scheduled member disability benefits to which an injured worker is entitled is 
determined by using the functional method. Functional disability is “limited to the loss of 
the physiological capacity of the body or body part.” Mortimer v. Fruehauf Corp., 502 
N.W.2d 12, 15 (Iowa 1993); Sherman v. Pella Corp., 576 N.W.2d 312 (Iowa 1998).  At 
the start of the hearing, both parties stipulated that Koeller’s injury was a scheduled

member injury to the left shoulder.  (See Hearing Report).  Thus, his permanent 
impairment is determined under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(n).  Iowa Code section 
85.34(2)(n) limits compensation for a shoulder injury to four hundred weeks.  Iowa Code 
§ 85.34(2)(n) (2021).   
In all cases of permanent partial disability described in paragraphs “a” through

solely by utilizing the guides to the evaluation of permanent impairment, published by

Koeller argues the facts of his case are distinguishable from those presented in 
Jay and therefore its holding should not apply.  He contends application of the multiplier 
was permissible in Jay because there were no expert opinions in the hearing record 
addressing the proper calculation of impairments listed on Table 16-27. (Claimant’s 
Post-Hearing Brief, p. 7).  In this case, Koeller submitted two expert reports arguing that 
the multiplier is not supposed to be applied to any of the ratings listed on Table 16-27.  
(Id. at 8).  He argues those reports are unrebutted and must be accepted by the agency. 
(Id.).  The undersigned does not find this argument convincing.  While courts have 
indicated the agency should not summarily reject expert testimony when it is the only 
medical evidence presented, the Commissioner remains the finder of fact, and has a 
duty to reject expert testimony when there are valid reasons to do so. See Poula, 516 
N.W.2d at 911-912; See also Catalfo v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co., 213 N.W.2d 506, 
510 (Iowa 1973); James v. Sheller-Globe Corp., 510 N.W.2d 170, 174 (Iowa App. 
1993).  Under the statute, failure to comply with the language of the AMA Guides 
remains a valid reason to reject an expert opinion.  See Iowa Code § 85.34(2)(x).  
Additionally, the opinions offered by Koeller do not distinguish his injury from that

The parties have a dispute about Koeller’s correct weekly rate.  This 
disagreement originates from differences of opinion about his entitlement to tax 
exemptions.  Koeller alleges he is entitled to three exemptions.  Defendants contend he 
is only entitled to two exemptions.  An injured worker’s marital status and the number of 
exemptions he or she is entitled to claim, are considered when calculating rate.  This is 
because, under the language of Iowa Code section 85.37, the worker’s weekly benefit 
amount is calculated as eighty percent of the employee’s weekly spendable earnings. 
Weekly spendable earnings are the amount remaining after payroll taxes are deducted 
from gross weekly earnings.  Iowa Code § 85.61(9).  Iowa Code section 85.61(6) 
defines payrolls taxes as follows: 
“Payroll taxes” means an amount, determined by tables adopted by the 
workers' compensation commissioner pursuant to chapter 17A, equal to the 
sum of the following: 
a. An amount equal to the amount which would be withheld pursuant to 
withholding tables in effect on July 1 preceding the injury under the 
Internal Revenue Code, and regulations pursuant thereto, as amended, 
as though the employee had elected to claim the maximum number 
of exemptions for actual dependency, blindness and old age to which 
the employee is entitled on the date on which the employee was injured. 
Id.  The agency has long established precedent that the actual exemptions claimed on 
the employee’s income tax return controls when determining how many exemptions a 
claimant is entitled to claim for purposes of the weekly compensation rate.  Webber v. 
West Side Transport, Inc., File No. 1278549 (App. December 20, 2002); DeRaad v. 
Fred's Plumbing and Heating, File No. 1134532 (App. January 16, 2002), Rhoades v. 
Torgerson Construction Co., File No. 1012085 (App. January 31, 1995), Keeling v. 
Cedar Rapids Community Schools, File No. 891809 (App. February 26, 1993).  In 
essence, the agency recognizes a presumption that the claimant is entitled to the 
number of exemptions that were actually claimed on his or her tax returns for the injury 
year.  This presumption, however, is rebuttable.  The party seeking to overcome the 
presumption must present sufficient evidence at hearing to rebut the presumption.

not properly claimed as a dependent on her father’s tax returns.  As support for this 
assertion defendants cite to Iowa Code section 422.12 and Internal Revenue Code 
section 152(c)(1)-(3).  (Defendants’ Post-Hearing Brief, p. 9).  Internal Revenue Code 
152(c) articulates when a child qualifies as a dependent for tax purposes.  Iowa Code 
section 422.12 adopts the definition of dependent contained in the Internal Revenue 
Code.  To be considered a dependent under Internal Revenue Code section 152(c), a

Koeller asserts a claim for penalty benefits. Specifically, Koeller claims he is 
entitled to penalty benefits because: (1) defendants initially sent him a denial letter on 
October 28, 2022, and did not accept the claim and/or pay benefits until November 3, 
2022, and (2) defendants underpaid his rate based upon their assertion that he was 
only entitled to two exemptions.  (Claimant’s post-hearing brief, pp. 10-11).  Iowa Code 
section 10A.315(4) governs penalty benefits.  It provides: 
a. If a denial, a delay in payment, or a termination of benefits occurs without 
reasonable or probable cause or excuse known to the employer or 
insurance carrier at the time of the denial, delay in payment, or termination 
of benefits, the workers' compensation commissioner shall award benefits 
in addition to those benefits payable under this subchapter, or chapter 85, 
85A, or 85B, up to fifty percent of the amount of benefits that were denied, 
delayed, or terminated without reasonable or probable cause or excuse. 
b. The workers' compensation commissioner shall award benefits under this 
subsection if the commissioner finds both of the following facts: 
(1) The employee has demonstrated a denial, delay in payment, or 
termination of benefits. 
(2) The employer has failed to prove a reasonable or probable cause or 
excuse for the denial, delay in payment, or termination of benefits. 
Id.  Benefits that are underpaid, as well as late-paid benefits, are subject to penalties, 
unless the employer establishes reasonable and probable cause or excuse. See 
Robbennolt v. Snap-on Tools Corp., 555 N.W.2d 229, 237 (Iowa 1996) (finding an 
underpayment in the absence of an excuse required the commissioner to apply a 
penalty).  Koeller has shown late payment and underpayment of benefits in this case.  
The question is whether defendants had a reasonable cause or excuse for those 
delayed payments. The Iowa Supreme Court has explained this second statutory 
requirement as follows: 
A reasonable cause or excuse exists if either (1) the delay was necessary 
for the insurer to investigate the claim or (2) the employer had a reasonable 
basis to contest the employee's entitlement to benefits. A “reasonable 
basis” for denial of the claim exists if the claim is “fairly debatable.” 
Keystone Nursing Care Ctr. v. Craddock, 705 N.W.2d 299, 307 (Iowa 2005). The 
reasonableness of the employer's actions “does not turn on whether the employer was 
right. The issue is whether there was a reasonable basis for the employer's position that 

KOELLER V. CARDINAL LOGISTICS MANAGEMENT CORP. 
Page 14 
 
no benefits were owing.” Id. at 307–08. Stated another way, the “focus is on the 
existence of a debatable issue, not on which party was correct.” Bellville v. Farm Bureau 
Mut. Ins. Co., 702 N.W.2d 468, 473–74 (Iowa 2005). In Rodda v. Vermeer Mfg., 734 
N.W.2d 480, 483 (Iowa 2007), the court explained: 
A reasonable basis for denying insurance benefits exists if the claim is “fairly 
debatable” as to either a matter of fact or law. A claim is “fairly debatable” 
when it is open to dispute on any logical basis. Whether a claim is “fairly 
debatable” can generally be determined by the court as a matter of law. If 
the court determines that the defendant had no reasonable basis upon 
which to deny the employee's benefits, it must then determine if the 
defendant knew, or should have known, that the basis for denying the 
employee's claim was unreasonable. 
Id. (Internal citations and quotations omitted.) In City of Madrid v. Blasnitz, 742 N.W.2d 
77, 84 (Iowa 2007), the Iowa Supreme Court stated that when there are facts that 
create a genuine dispute between the parties, the claimant’s case is fairly debatable as 
a matter of law.  A review of the records in evidence shows that Koeller did not seek 
medical treatment until October 19, 2022, and no doctor provided an opinion on the 
cause of Koeller’s left shoulder complaints until December 9, 2022. (See JE 1, p. 1; JE 
2, p. 17).  On that date, Dr. Bollier indicated that the work incident on October 5, 2022, 
was a significant factor in causing Koeller’s rotator cuff tear and shoulder diagnosis. 
(Id.).  Koeller received care from Peggy Barton, ARNP, prior to that, but she never 
provided a causation opinion.  (JE 1, pp. 4, 12).  Additionally, x-rays taken on October 
19, 2022, showed only moderate AC joint osteoarthritis, a condition that is often non-
work related. (Id. at 6).  The parties were not aware of Koeller’s traumatic rotator cuff 
tear until they received the MRI results on November 3, 2022. (Id. at 7-8).   
Given this record, defendants’ delay in volunteering benefits appears reasonable 
in order to investigate his claim and determine compensability.  I do not believe the 
imposition of a penalty for failure to volunteer benefits prior to November 3, 2022, fifteen 
days after Koeller’s first medical appointment, is appropriate.  I also find that Koeller’s 
entitlement to three exemptions was fairly debatable.  Beyond his tax returns, he did not 
provide defendants with any documentary evidence that AK met the definition of a 
dependent in 2022.  Because they bore the burden of proof, defendants ultimately lost 
on this issue.  However, whether every full-time college student living on campus meets 
the Internal Revenue Code’s definition of a dependent is debatable and very fact 
specific.  With this backdrop, I find Koeller’s rate was debatable; he has not proven 
entitlement to penalty benefits for defendants’ underpayment.

Iowa Code section 85.27(4) provides, in relevant part:  
For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish reasonable 
services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has the right to 
choose the care. . . .  The treatment must be offered promptly and be 
reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the 
employee. If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the care 
offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such dissatisfaction 
to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the employer and 
the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited to treat the 
injury. If the employer and employee cannot agree on such alternate care,

Id.; see also, Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 433, 436-37 (Iowa

By challenging the employer's choice of treatment - and seeking alternate care - 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable. See Long 
v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122, 124 (Iowa 1995).  Determining what care is 
reasonable under the statute is a question of fact. Id. The employer's obligation turns on 
the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability. Id. In this instance, defendants 
are not offering any care for Koeller’s numbness symptoms.  I find that is unreasonable.  
Koeller has met the requirements of Iowa Code section 85.27;5 he has proven 
entitlement to alternate care, specifically an evaluation with a nerve or brachial plexus 
specialist.  Defendants retain the right to select the authorized provider for this 
treatment provided they authorize a provider promptly.  See Iowa Code § 85.27(4).  
Defendants shall designate an appropriate provider to evaluate Koeller’s numbness 
complaints, and defendants shall be responsible for payment of treatment 
recommended by that provider.
