STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Cleighton Barber, claimant, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from Amazon.com (“Amazon”), employer and LM Insurance 
Corporation, insurance carrier, as defendants.  Hearing was held via Zoom 
videoconference on June 18, 2024.     
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing.  On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations.  At the time 
of the hearing, numbered paragraph 4 of the hearing report was amended to state the 
time period claimant was seeking benefits for was October 28, 2022 through April 17, 
2024.  Additionally, numbered paragraph 5 (d) was amended to “stipulated” rather than 
“disputed.”  In numbered paragraph 8, claimant seeks reimbursement for an 
independent medical examination.  At the outset of the hearing defendants agreed to 
reimburse claimant’s attorney for the IME.  Therefore, there are no longer any issues 
related to paragraph 8 to decide.  Regarding numbered paragraph 9, defendants stated 
that prior to hearing they made payments as listed and clarified that those payments 
were ongoing.  Claimant agreed that defendants had made those payments but did not 
stipulate that those payments were for permanency benefits.  (Hearing Transcript, page 
11)  Numbered paragraph 11 (a) defendants amended to state “stipulated.”  All of those 
stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration decision.  
The parties are now bound by their stipulations.  
Cleighton Barber was the only witness to testify live at trial.  The evidentiary 
record also includes Joint Exhibits 1-8, Claimant’s Exhibits 1-7, and Defendants’

ISSUES 
 
The parties submitted the following issues for resolution: 
 
1. The nature and extent of permanent disability claimant sustained as the result 
of the stipulated October 28, 2022 work injury.   
 
2. Whether claimant is entitled to healing period benefits from October 28, 2022 
through April 17, 2024. 
3. Whether penalty benefits are appropriate for unreasonable delay in payment 
of benefits. 
4. Assessment of costs. 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
The undersigned, having considered all the evidence and testimony in the 
record, finds: 
 
 Claimant, Cleighton Barber, was 32 years old at the time of the hearing.  (Tr. p. 
53)  He began working full-time at Amazon in September 2022.  The parties stipulated 
that Cleighton sustained a work injury on October 28, 2022.  (Hearing Report, 
numbered paragraph 2)  He alleges he sustained an injury to his left shoulder and neck.  
(Tr. p. 28)

not assigned to a set schedule.  Cleighton was a flex employee so he had to pick up his 
own schedule.  (Tr. pp. 27-28)  There was an app that Amazon employees used to 
select shifts.  Amazon would release a bunch of shifts on a certain day of the week.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 

BARBER V. AMAZON.COM SERVICES, INC. 
Page 17 
 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 
621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 
(Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
Since claimant has an impairment to the body as a whole, an industrial disability 
has been sustained.  Industrial disability was defined in Diederich v. Tri-City Ry. Co. of 
Iowa, 219 Iowa 587, 591, 258 N.W. 899, 902 (1935) as follows:  "It is therefore plain that 
the Legislature intended the term 'disability' to mean 'industrial disability' or loss of 
earning capacity and not a mere 'functional disability' to be computed in terms of 
percentages of the total physical and mental ability of a normal man." 
Functional impairment is an element to be considered in determining industrial 
disability which is the reduction of earning capacity, but consideration must also be 
given to the injured employee's age, education, qualifications, experience, motivation, 
loss of earnings, severity and situs of the injury, work restrictions, inability to engage in 
employment for which the employee is fitted and the employer's offer of work or failure 
to so offer.  McSpadden v. Big Ben Coal Co., 288 N.W.2d 181, 192 (Iowa 1980); Olson 
v. Goodyear Service Stores, 255 Iowa 1112, 1120, 125 N.W.2d 251, 256 (1963); Barton 
v. Nevada Poultry Co., 253 Iowa 285, 110 N.W.2d 660, 663 (1961). 
Considering claimant’s age, proximity to retirement, educational background, 
employment history, ability to retrain, motivation to seek work, length of healing period, 
permanent impairment, and permanent restrictions, and the other industrial disability 
factors set forth by the Iowa Supreme Court, I find that he has sustained a 30          
percent loss of future earning capacity as a result of his work injury with the defendant 
employer. 
Compensation for permanent partial disability shall begin at the termination of the 
healing period.  Compensation shall be paid in relation to 500 weeks as the disability 
bears to the body as a whole.  Section 85.34.  I conclude claimant has demonstrated 
entitled to 150 weeks of permanent partial disability benefits at the stipulated rate of 
$471.66.  The parties have stipulated that the appropriate commencement date of any 
permanent partial disability benefits is April 18, 2024. 
We now turn to the issue of healing period benefits.  Claimant is seeking healing 
period benefits from October 28, 2022 through the stipulated commencement date of 
April 17, 2024.  (Hearing Report, numbered paragraph 4) 
Healing period compensation describes temporary workers’ compensation 
weekly benefits that precede an allowance of permanent partial disability benefits.  
Ellingson v. Fleetguard, Inc., 599 N.W.2d 440, 446 (Iowa 1999).  Section 85.34(1) 
provides that healing period benefits are payable to an injured worker who has suffered 

BARBER V. AMAZON.COM SERVICES, INC. 
Page 18 
 
permanent partial disability until the first to occur of three events.  These are: (1) the 
worker has returned to work; (2) the worker medically is capable of returning to 
substantially similar employment; or (3) the worker has achieved maximum medical 
recovery.  Maximum medical recovery is achieved when healing is complete and the 
extent of permanent disability can be determined.  Armstrong Tire & Rubber Co. v. 
Kubli, 312 N.W.2d 60, 65 (Iowa Ct. App. 1981).  Neither maintenance medical care nor 
an employee's continuing to have pain or other symptoms necessarily prolongs the 
healing period. 
Claimant contends that he had work restrictions placed on him since shortly after 
his October 28, 2022 work injury until he was released by his doctor with no restrictions 
on April 17, 2024 and therefore he should be paid healing period benefits for this period.  
(Cl. Brief, pp. 6-7)   
The Amazon wage records demonstrate that claimant worked and received 
wages after the injury on October 28, 2022 through January 28, 2023.  (CE6, p. 61)  
During this same time period, claimant received temporary partial disability benefits.  
(DE B, p. 13)  Under Iowa law, an employee is entitled to appropriate temporary partial 
disability benefits during those periods in which the employee is temporarily, partially 
disabled.  An employee is temporarily, partially disabled when the employee is not 
capable medically of returning to employment substantially similar to the employment in 
which the employee was engaged at the time of the injury but is able to perform other 
work consistent with the employee's disability.  Section 85.33(2).  Because claimant 
worked during this timeframe, an award of healing period benefits would not be 
appropriate.  Therefore, I conclude that claimant has failed to demonstrate entitlement 
to healing period benefits from October 28, 2022 through January 28, 2023.   
As noted above, the workers’ compensation benefit payment log demonstrates 
claimant was paid weekly benefits from January 29, 2023 through July 17, 2023.  (DE 
B, p. 13)  The record is void of any evidence to show that claimant is entitled to 
additional healing period benefits during this timeframe.  Therefore, I conclude claimant 
has failed to demonstrate entitlement to any additional healing period benefits during 
this time.   
The next period which claimant is seeking healing period benefits is from July 18, 
2023 to January 18, 2024.  Based on the above findings of fact, I conclude that claimant 
returned to full-time employment on May 1, 2023.  Under Iowa Code section 85.34(1), 
claimant’s entitlement to healing period benefits ended when he returned to work.  
Based on the above findings of fact, I conclude that claimant was already compensated 
for the time he lost from his security job after his neck surgery.  Thus, I conclude 
claimant has failed to prove by a preponderance of the evidence that he was entitled to 
any additional healing period benefits on or after May 1, 2023.

If weekly compensation benefits are not fully paid when due, section 10A.315(4) 
requires that additional benefits be awarded unless the employer shows reasonable 
cause or excuse for the delay or denial.  Robbennolt v. Snap-on Tools Corp., 555 
N.W.2d 229, 236-37 (Iowa 1996).  
In Christensen v. Snap-on Tools Corp., 554 N.W.2d 254 (Iowa 1996) and 
Robbennolt v. Snap-on Tools Corp, 555 N.W.2d 229 (Iowa 1996) the supreme court 
said: 
Based on the plain language of section 86.13,3 we hold an employee 
is entitled to penalty benefits if there has been a delay in payment unless 
the employer proves a reasonable cause or excuse.  [Iowa Code § 86.13 
(2015).]  A reasonable cause or excuse exists if either (1) the delay was 
necessary for the insurer to investigate the claim or (2) the employer had a 
reasonable basis to contest the employee’s entitlement to benefits.  A 
“reasonable basis” for denial of the claim exists if the claim is “fairly 
debatable.”  Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 260. 
The supreme court has stated: 
(1) If the employer has a reason for the delay and conveys that 
reason to the employee contemporaneously with the beginning of the delay, 
no penalty will be imposed if the reason is of such character that a 
reasonable fact finder could conclude that it is a "reasonable or probable 
cause or excuse" under Iowa Code section 86.13 [2015].  In that case, we 
will defer to the decision of the commissioner.  See Christensen, 554 
N.W.2d at 260 (substantial evidence found to support commissioner’s 
finding of legitimate reason for delay pending receipt of medical report); 
Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 236. 
(2) If no reason is given for the delay or if the “reason” is not one that 
a reasonable fact finder could accept, we will hold that no such cause or 
excuse exists and remand to the commissioner for the sole purpose of 
assessing penalties under section 86.13.  See Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at

(3) Reasonable causes or excuses include (a) a delay for the 
employer to investigate the claim, Christensen, 554 N.W.2d at 260; 
Kiesecker v. Webster City Custom Meats, Inc., 528 N.W.2d at 109, 111 
(Iowa 1995); or (b) the employer had a reasonable basis to contest the 
claimthe “fairly debatable” basis for delay.  See Christensen, 554 N.W.2d

(4) For the purpose of applying section 86.13, the benefits that are 
underpaid as well as late-paid benefits are subject to penalties, unless the 
employer establishes reasonable and probable cause or excuse. [Iowa 
Code § 86.13 (2015);] Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 237 (underpayment

(5) For purposes of determining whether there has been a delay, 
payments are “made” when (a) the check addressed to a claimant is mailed 
(Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 236; Kiesecker, 528 N.W.2d at 112), or (b) the 
check is delivered personally to the claimant by the employer or its workers’ 
compensation insurer.  Robbennolt, 555 N.W.2d at 235.   
(6) In determining the amount of the penalty, the commissioner is to 
consider factors such as the length of the delay, the number of delays, the 
information available to the employer regarding the employee’s injury and

N.W.2d at 238. 
(7) An employer’s bare assertion that a claim is “fairly debatable” 
does not make it so.  A fair reading of Christensen and Robbennolt, makes 
it clear that the employer must assert facts upon which the commissioner 
could reasonably find that the claim was “fairly debatable.”  See

If the employer fails to show reasonable cause or excuse for the delay or denial, 
the commissioner shall impose a penalty in an amount up to 50 percent of the amount

Based on the above findings of fact, I found that defendants did underpay and 
delay weekly benefits to the claimant.  As set forth above, the defendants’ payment log 
demonstrates that there were weekly benefits which were underpaid and unreasonably 
delayed.  I find that the record is void of any contemporaneous explanation as to why 
these benefits were underpaid and unreasonably delayed.  I conclude claimant has 
demonstrated that penalty benefits are appropriate for the benefits which were 
unreasonably delayed and that accrued prior to May 1, 2023.  Additionally, the weekly 
benefits paid after May 1, 2023, but before the June 18, 2024 hearing were underpaid 
by $21.29 per week. 
Considering the length of the delays, the number of delays, the information 
available to the employer and the employer’s lack of a past record of penalties, I find 
that a penalty in the amount of $6,300.00 is appropriate.  This is less than 50 percent of 
the benefits that were unreasonably delayed, but enough of a penalty to hopefully deter 
future such delays in payments.   
Finally, claimant is seeking an assessment of costs in the amount of $128.75.  
(CE7, p. 62)  Costs are to be assessed at the discretion of the Iowa Workers 
Compensation Commissioner or by the deputy hearing the case.  876 IAC 4.33.  I find 
that claimant was generally successful in his claim and therefore exercise my discretion 
to assess costs against the defendants.  I find that the costs claimant seeks are for the 
filing fee and are appropriate under 876 IAC 4.33(7).  Thus, defendants are assessed 
costs totaling $128.75.
