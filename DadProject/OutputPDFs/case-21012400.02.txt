STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A.  
Claimant, Nickolas King, invokes the expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48.  
Claimant appeared personally and through his attorney, Laura Pattermann.  Defendants 
appeared through their attorney Bryan Brooks.

17, 2025.  The proceedings were digitally recorded.  That recording constitutes the 
official record of this proceeding.  Pursuant to the Commissioner’s July 21, 2023 Order,

ISSUE 
 
The issue presented for resolution is whether the claimant is entitled to an 
alternate medical care order directing care to be provided through Jaclyn Jones, D.O. 
and/or Mark A. Oatman, M.D. 
 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
The undersigned having considered all the evidence in the record finds: 
 
Claimant, Nickolas King, sustained a work-related injury on September 16, 2021.  
Claimant sustained his injury in the State of Iowa.   As a result of that injury, claimant 
required medical care. 
 
Defendants initially authorized medical care in Iowa.  However, claimant required 
assistance with daily activities and financial assistance while off work.  Therefore, he 
chose to move from Iowa to Oklahoma to be near family that could assist him.  Claimant 
moved to Oklahoma in October 2022 and subsequently requested medical care be 
provided in Oklahoma. 
 
Defendants apparently agreed to authorize care in Oklahoma but requested 
claimant identify a medical provider that would agree to provide care through the 
worker’s compensation system.  Claimant identified Jaclyn Jones, D.O., and defendants 
authorized care through Dr. Jones. 
 
 
Dr. Jones evaluated claimant on June 14, 2023, and diagnosed claimant with 
complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS).  Dr. Jones recommended referral to a pain 
specialist.  Claimant started treatment at Precision Pain Management and obtained 
treatment at that facility through Mark A. Oatman, M.D.  However, claimant testified that 
defendants did not authorize or pay for care at Precision Pain Management. 
 
Mr. King also testified that he received a prescription from Precision Pain 
Management.  However, defendants did not authorize the prescription and he was not 
initially able to secure that prescription medication.  Claimant also testified that he 
received unpaid medical bills from Utica Park Clinic for the orthopedic care provided by 
Dr. Jones.

After numerous communications, defendants eventually scheduled an evaluation 
with M. Stephen Wilson, M.D.  Claimant was not aware of the status of Dr. Wilson and 
assumed the evaluation was a defense medical evaluation.  However, upon evaluation, 
Dr. Wilson had claimant sign a pain contract and provided treatment recommendations.  
Claimant presented to Dr. Wilson twice for treatment. 
 
Mr. King testified he was not comfortable receiving care from Dr. Wilson.  He 
testified that he had to drive a distance for this care and that his mother drove him 
because he would experience pain if he tried to drive to Dr. Wilson’s office.  Claimant 
testified that Dr. Wilson seemed distracted and dismissed claimant during his 
evaluations.  Mr. King testified that Dr. Wilson did not listen to everything that he 
provided as a history and that the receptionist at Dr. Wilson’s office told him that the 
physician did not have all of claimant’s medical records to review in advance of his 
evaluation. 
 
Dr. Wilson recommended a medication review as well as a nuclear bone scan be 
performed.  Dr. Wilson also recommended a neuropsychological evaluation.  Claimant 
asserts he is not making a depression or other mental health claim as part of this 
litigation.  He says no other physician has recommended a neuropsychological 
evaluation.  He believes a neuropsychological evaluation is intrusive under the facts and 
circumstances of this case and is reluctant to submit to such an evaluation.  
 
Dr. Wilson recommends a bone scan for claimant as well.  Claimant wishes to 
proceed with this recommended bone scan. Yet, no other physician has recommended 
a bone scan.  Claimant’s concerns about the treatment recommendations made by Dr. 
Wilson do not seem reasonable and well founded.   
 
I find nothing in this record to suggest that the treatment recommendations made 
by Dr. Wilson are unreasonable.  They are more extensive than recommendations 
made by other medical providers.  Dr. Wilson is a licensed pain specialist and his 
recommendations appear to be within the scope of his practice and reasonable. 
 
On the other hand, claimant asserts he lacks confidence in Dr. Wilson’s 
treatment.  Claimant asserts he does not trust Dr. Wilson.  Claimant specifically cites 
disciplinary and legal actions that have been taken against Dr. Wilson.  Claimant 
testified about and introduced records to establish that Dr. Wilson is under disciplinary 
action and subject to indefinite probation for alcohol and drug consumption by the 
Oklahoma State Board of Medical Licensure and Supervision.  (Claimant’s Ex. 1) 
 
Claimant also introduced evidence establishing that Dr. Wilson settled a suit with 
the United States government to resolve a False Claims Act charge of receiving 
kickback payments from a pharmacy in exchange for prescribing specific compounded 
medications produced by the pharmacy.  Those claims involved treatment of injured 
workers in the federal workers’ compensation system (Federal Employees 
Compensation Act Program).

Mr. King asserts that he does not trust and has no confidence in Dr. Wilson given 
the federal settlement and the ongoing probation and disciplinary action against Dr. 
Wilson.  Defendants offer no contrary evidence to refute or explain the above 
challenges.  Claimant’s testimony about his concerns and lack of trust or confidence in 
Dr. Wilson are credible and founded based on the evidence.   
 
I find that defendants’ offer of care through a pain specialist, Dr. Wilson, is

REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Section 85.27.  Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening October 16, 1975). 
 
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See Iowa 
R. App. P 6.904(3)(e); Bell Bros. Heating and Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d 
193, 209 (Iowa 2010); Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  
Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.  Long v. 
Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  The employer’s obligation turns on 
the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.  Id.; Harned v. Farmland Foods, 
Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983).   
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).   
 
In this case, I found that the defendants’ offer of care through a pain specialist 
was reasonable and appropriate.  I also found that the treatment recommendations 
made by Dr. Wilson were more extensive than treatment offered by other physicians 
and that the treatment recommendations were reasonable and appropriate for treatment 
of claimant’s injuries.  By way of further explanation, claimant’s counsel asserted from 
prior experience that a neuropsychological evaluation was not commonly ordered for 
CRPS.  However, claimant offered no evidence to prove that the recommended

neuropsychological evaluation was unreasonable or inappropriate treatment for 
claimant’s injuries.  If another physician adopted this recommendation or made a similar 
recommendation, I would be inclined to find that treatment recommendation reasonable 
on this evidentiary record.  I conclude that claimant failed to prove the treatment offered 
by defendants is unreasonable or inappropriate.   
 
There were allegations of untimeliness of treatment, authorizations, and payment 
of medical expenses.  However, the timeliness of defendants’ offer of the current 
treatment were not the significant basis upon which this alternate medical care claim 
was brought.  Defendants are encouraged to provide prompt authorizations and 
treatment for claimant moving forward, but this is not the basis of an order for alternate 
medical care under the current record. 
Nevertheless, I conclude that an order of alternate medical care is appropriate in 
this case.  Alternate medical care can be ordered when there is a breakdown in a 
physician-patient relationship.  Seibert v. State of Iowa, File No. 938579 (September 14, 
1994); Neuaone v. John Morrell & Co., File No. 1022976 (January 27, 1994); Williams v. 
High Rise Const., File No. 1025415 (February 23, 1993); Wallech v. FDL, File 
No. 1020245 (September 3, 1992) (aff’d Dist Ct June 21, 1993).  In this case, Mr. King 
has legitimate bases and concerns about Dr. Wilson.  Dr. Wilson’s legal problems and 
adverse disciplinary action create a reasonable objective basis for concern about Dr. 
Wilson’s practice abilities and I accepted Mr. King’s testimony that he has lost 
confidence and trust in Dr. Wilson’s ability to treat him as credible.  Therefore, I 
conclude claimant has proven a breakdown in the doctor-patient relationship such that 
alternate medical care should be awarded. 
Claimant seeks an order directing care back to Dr. Jones and/or Dr. Oatman.  
Defendants presented a convincing argument that an order authorizing care through Dr. Jones 
at this time would be inappropriate.  Dr. Jones evaluated claimant on June 14, 2023.  In that 
evaluation, Dr. Jones noted that conservative care was appropriate and indicated that claimant 
“has no obvious organic orthopedic pathology within the shoulder.”  (Claimant’s Ex. 9)  Dr. 
Jones continued, “I do not feel that he needs care from an orthopedic surgeon but rather from a 
pain management physician.”  (Claimant’s Ex. 9)  Given this opinion from the orthopedic 
surgeon, I perceive no basis for an order directing care back through Dr. Jones or another 
orthopedic surgeon at this time.  Claimant has not established that further orthopedic care is 
reasonable or necessary at this time.  Therefore, I deny the request to transfer care back to Dr. 
Jones at this time. 
Alternatively, claimant seeks an order authorizing care through Dr. Oatman.  Dr. Oatman 
practices at Precision Pain Management.  Dr. Jones specifically referred claimant to Precision 
Pain Management for further care. 
Defendants argue that authorization of care through Dr. Oatman is not appropriate 
because Dr. Oatman signs his October 10, 2023 office note as “Primary Provider.”  Defendants 
essentially contend that continuing care with Dr. Wilson, a pain specialist, is superior to 
transferring care back to a “Primary Provider.”  
I am not convinced that Dr. Oatman is a primary provider in the sense defendants now 
argue.  Rather, it appears to the undersigned that Dr. Oatman is likely the pain specialist that is

assisted by Chelsea Ferrier and the office notes that Dr. Oatman should be considered the 
primary provider for billing purposes; not that he is a primary provider instead of a pain 
specialist. 
Regardless, Dr. Oatman clearly practices medicine at a pain clinic, and I find that he is 
likely a pain specialist.  However, if defendants are accurate and Dr. Oatman is a primary 
provider and not a pain specialist, he clearly works in a pain clinic and can make appropriate 
referrals for evaluation and treatment through pain specialists.  The care offered and 
recommended by Dr. Oatman appears to have been appropriate.  Given the breakdown in the 
doctor-patient relationship with Dr. Wilson, I conclude it is appropriate to transfer care back to 
and authorize Dr. Oatman for further evaluation and treatment of claimant. 
 
ORDER 
 
THEREFORE IT IS ORDERED: 
 
The claimant's petition for alternate medical care is granted.   
 
Defendants shall immediately authorize and pay for all medical care through and 
recommended by Dr. Oatman at Precision Pain Management related to claimant’s 
September 16, 2021 work injury.
