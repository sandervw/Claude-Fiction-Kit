This matter came before the Court on a Petition for Judicial Review from a 
final decision of the Worker’s Compensation Commissioner.  Counsel appeared for 
oral argument on November 8, 2024. Petitioners, Western Express, Inc. and PA 
Manufacturers Association Inc. Co. (collectively referred to as “Western Express”) 
appeared through Timothy Wegman.  Respondent, Austin Adams (“Austin”) by and 
through Roseann Davis (“Adams”)1 appeared through Bruce Stoltze. Upon review of 
the court file and applicable law, the Court enters the following order: 
 
I. 
BACKGROUND FACTS AND PROCEDURAL POSTURE 
Gary Griffey was killed on May 22, 2015, while on duty in a truck owned and 
operated by Western Express. At the time of his death, Mr. Griffey had four children, 
including Austin Adams. Austin’s mother, Roesann Adams, was Austin’s trustee and 
guardian. An arbitration decision filed April 30, 2020, awarded Austin dependent 
benefits that were to continue until: (1) December 15, 2022, when Austin reached the 
age of 18; (2) until Austin reached the age of 25 if he was enrolled as a full-time 
student; or (3) if Austin was determined in the future to be incapacitated from earning 
for as long as he remained incapacitated from earning. Workers’ Compensation 
Commissioner Cortese affirmed the arbitration decision on November 5, 2020, and 
judicial review was not sought on that decision.

II. 
STANDARD OF REVIEW 
Final decisions rendered by the Iowa Workers’ Compensation Commission are 
reviewed under Iowa Code Chapter 17A, the Iowa Administrative Procedures Act.5 
“Under the Act, [a court] may only interfere with the [agency]’s decision if it is 
erroneous under one of the grounds enumerated in the statute, and a party’s 
substantial rights have been prejudiced.”6 The agency’s decision is binding “if it is 
supported by substantial evidence and not based upon incorrect conclusions of law.”7 
The standard of review depends on the type of error alleged by the Petitioner.8 
If the alleged error is one of fact, the standard of review is whether the findings 
are supported by substantial evidence.9 “In determining if there is substantial

“Because review is not de novo, the court must not reassess the weight to be 
accorded various items of evidence. Weight of evidence remains within the agency’s

reviewing an agency decision, the court only determines whether substantial 
evidence supports a finding “according to those witnesses whom the [agency] 
believed.”15

III. 
WESTERN EXPRESS’S PETITION FOR JUDICIAL REVIEW 
Western Express raises two issues with the Commissioner’s decision: (1) 
Austin’s claim of incapacity from earning needed to be determined based on the facts 
existing at the time of the original injury; and (2) the factual finding that Austin is 
incapacitated is unsupported by substantial evidence. 
A. INCAPACITY AT TIME OF THE ORIGINAL INJURY 
Iowa Code section 85.31(1)(a) directs that in the case of an employee death “the 
employer shall pay the dependents who were wholly dependent on the earnings of the 
employee for support at the time of the injury”: to any child under the age of eighteen; 
“to any child who was physically or mentally incapacitated from earning at the time 
of the injury causing death for the during of the incapacity from earning”; and to all 
dependents as defined in section 85.44 for the duration of the incapacity from earning. 
Section 85.44 provides “a dependent shall be one actually dependent or mentally or 
physically incapacitated from earning” and further directs “[s]uch state shall be 
determined in accordance with the facts as of the date of the injury.” 
The Court has explained that section 85.44 “signals two distinct classes of 
eligible claimants: those who are ‘actually dependent’ and those who are ‘mentally or 
physically incapacitated from earning.’”20 The Court clarified that the phrase 
“incapacity from earning” relates to the duration of benefits, not the recipient’s initial 
entitlement; in discussing the different subsections of section 85.31, the Court 
differentiated benefits paid to a child until the age of twenty-five “if actually 
dependent” from “benefits payable to a child ‘who was physically or mentally 
incapacitated from earning at the time of the injury causing death.’”21 The Court 
indicated “it is logical to assume the legislature intended a different meaning for

‘incapacity from earning’ when applied to a healthy but dependent person than when 
applied to one already classified as mentally or physically incapacitated from 
earning.22 If this action was an original action, it is true that Austin’s status—either 
as a healthy but dependent person, or one who is mentally or physically 
incapacitated—could only be made based upon facts at the time of his father’s death. 
However, the decision subject to this judicial review is a review-reopening decision. 
The worker’s compensation code allows “a proceeding to reopen an award for 
payments” and directs “inquiry shall be into whether or not the condition of the 
employee warrants an end to, diminishment of, or increase of compensation so 
awarded . . .”23 In the original action, the Commissioner determined Austin was 
entitled to benefits as he was solely dependent on the deceased employee. The 
Commissioner used Austin’s age to make the requisite finding that he was a 
dependent, then expressly reserved the ability to consider incapacity at a future 
date—presumably because a fifteen year old is student is incapable of self-sustaining 
earnings; this deferred decision only affected the duration of the benefits.  
The Commissioner made the necessary finding that Austin was initially 
entitled to benefits, and expressly noted three scenarios which respect to the duration 
of those benefits:  (1) December 15, 2022, when Austin reached the age of 18; (2) until 
Austin reached the age of 25 if he was enrolled as a full-time student; or (3) if Austin 
is determined in the future to be incapacitated from earning for as long as he 
remained incapacitated from earning. The Commissioner was within the scope of 
their authority under the worker’s compensation statutes and the original decision to 
defer the incapacity decision for a reopening-review proceeding.
