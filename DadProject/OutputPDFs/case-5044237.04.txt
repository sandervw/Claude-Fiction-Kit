On December 3, 2024, claimant filed an application for alternate medical care 
requesting payment for mileage, authorization for a prescription for a compound sav 
called GACK, and scheduling and payment for claimant’s treatment at the Shirley Ryan 
Institute. These requests were the subject of an alternate care proceeding filed on June 
20, 2024, and an alternate care proceeding filed on April 12, 2024, both of which were 
resolved by a consent order. 
The July 2, 2024, consent order stated that claimant sought authorization for 
treatment with the recommended new counselor/psychologist Dr. Kerrie Armstrong, 
authorization to be seen at the Shirley Ryan Institute, and for renewal and continued 
payment for the claimant’s gym membership at River Plex. Defendants agreed to these 
requests. 
The April 18, 2024, consent order stated claimant sought authorization for a 
compound sav (GACK) which was recommended and prescribed by authorized treating 
physician, Dr. Lisa Snyder. Defendants agreed to authorize and pay for the prescribed 
compound sav via Essential Wellness Pharmacy. 
At the hearing for the alternate care petition filed on December 3, 2024, the 
parties agreed that claimant was still entitled to this care, but that due to lack of 
communication and the changes in how claimant’s case was handled by the defendant 
insurer, this care has not been timely provided. 
There is a single prescription for GACK which was issued in April 2024 and is 
valid for one year. The GACK prescription must be refilled each month. However,

defendants have not paid for the GACK prescription since the first month’s prescription 
in April. Defendants maintain that they have not received any billing for this prescription. 
Around November, defendants issued claimant a prescription card that is managed by a 
company called Mitchell. Because the GACK prescription is not FDA approved for 
claimant’s condition, Mitchell requires pre-authorization. The process, as described by 
defendants during hearing, requires the pharmacist to contact Mitchell for authorization. 
Mitchell then contacts the insurer. Defendants maintain the pharmacist never contacted 
Mitchell for authorization. Regardless of the procedure, all parties agree claimant is 
entitled to the compound sav which needs to be refilled monthly. It is the defendants’ 
obligation to pay for this both by agreement and by law. 
Claimant has also not been reimbursed for mileage to medical visits. Defendants 
assert that they have not received any documentation from Dr. Armstrong that claimant 
attended appointments and therefore they cannot confirm that the mileage is valid. 
Claimant has provided contact information for Dr. Snyder and Dr. Armstrong 
since July 2, 2024. 
As it relates to the Shirley Ryan Institute, defendants stated that they issued the 
authorization twice via facsimile, but in verifying this, defendants realized that the 
authorization was sent to the wrong provider using an incorrect fax number. 
As claimant is seeking relief in this case, claimant bears the burden of proof to 
show by a preponderance of the evidence that the offered medical treatment is not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the employee. 
See Lawyer and Higgs, Iowa Practice, Workers’ Compensation, §15-4 and cases cited 
therein. 
Under Iowa law, the employer is required to provide care to an injured employee 
and is permitted to choose the care.  Pirelli-Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562  N.W. 
2d 433 (Iowa 1997).  Iowa Code section 85.27 provides, in relevant part: 
     For purposes of this section, this employer is obliged to furnish 
reasonable services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has 
the right to choose the care.  The treatment must be offered promptly and 
be reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the 
employee.  If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the care 
offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such 
dissatisfaction to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the 
employer and the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited 
to treat the injury.  If the employer and employee cannot agree on such 
alternate care, the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable 
proofs of the necessity therefore, allow and order other care. 
The question of reasonable care is a question of fact.  An application for alternate 
medical care is not granted simply because the employee is dissatisfied with the care 
the employer has chosen.  Mere dissatisfaction with the care is not sufficient grounds to

grant an application for alternate medical care.  The employee has the burden of 
proving that the care chosen by the employer is unreasonable.  Unreasonableness can 
be established by showing that the care was not offered promptly, was not reasonably 
suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the claimant.  
West Side Transport v. Cordell, 601 N.W.2d 691 (Iowa 1999);  Long v. Roberts Dairy 
Company, 528 N.W. 2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  Unreasonableness can be established by 
showing that the care authorized by the employer has not been effective and is “inferior 
or less extensive” than other available care requested by the employee.  Pirelli-
Armstrong, at 437. 
An employer’s statutory right is to select the providers of care and the employer 
may consider cost and other pertinent factors when exercising its choice.  Long, at 
124.  An employer (typically) is not a licensed health care provider and does not 
possess medical expertise.  Accordingly, an employer does not have the right to control 
the methods the providers choose to evaluate, diagnose and treat the injured 
employee.  An employer is not entitled to control a licensed health care provider’s 
exercise of professional judgment.  Assman v. Blue Star Foods, Declaratory Ruling, File 
No. 866389 (May 18, 1988).  An employer’s failure to follow recommendations of an 
authorized physician in matters of treatment is commonly a failure to provide reasonable 
treatment.  Boggs v. Cargill, Inc., File No. 1050396 (Alt. Care, January 31, 1994). 
In the case before us, the parties have agreed claimant is entitled to mileage 
reimbursement, payment and authorization of the compound sav (GACK), and payment 
and authorization of claimant’s treatment with Shirley Ryan Institute. 
Thus, claimant has carried her burden as it relates to her petition for alternate 
medical care. However, there is an ongoing issue of attentiveness and care by the 
defendants. Claimant has filed three alternate medical care petitions this year. There is 
no dispute over the care to which she is entitled, but she is not receiving the care 
agreed upon in a timely fashion. 
Claimant related that her case used to be managed by a nurse case manager, 
but this is no longer the case. Defendants failing to follow up and determine whether 
they are fulfilling their statutory obligations is creating ongoing problems for claimant. To 
that end, along with the authorization granted in this order, defendants are ordered to 
provide monthly status reports to be filed on the first of each month for the next six 
months affirmatively stating the steps they have taken to fulfill their obligations under the 
consent order of April 18, 2024, the consent order of July 2, 2024, and the order herein. 
THEREFORE IT IS ORDERED, claimant’s petition for alternate care is 
GRANTED. Defendants shall file a status report on the first of each month for the next 
six (6) months affirmatively stating that they have fulfilled, or are fulfilling, their statutory 
obligations in regard to the issues stated above.
