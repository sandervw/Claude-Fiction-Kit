This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A. The 
expedited procedures of rule 876 IAC 4.48, the “alternate medical care” rule, are 
invoked by claimant, John Henry.  
This alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on May 9, 2024. The 
proceedings were recorded digitally and constitute the official record of the hearing.  By 
an order filed by the Workers’ Compensation Commissioner, this decision is designated 
final agency action.  Any appeal would be by petition for judicial review under Iowa 
Code section 17A.15(1).  
The record in this case consists of claimant’s exhibits 1-4 and the testimony of 
claimant. 
Defendants contend this matter should be dismissed as they have denied liability 
for claimant’s “right knee pain starting in 2022.” 
ISSUE 
The issue presented for resolution in this case is whether claimant is entitled to 
alternate medical care consisting of authorization for surgery on the right knee. 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
Defendants accept liability for an injury to the right knee occurring on September 
11, 2019. They deny liability for claimant’s “right knee pain starting in 2022.”

On May 15, 2022, an Agreement For Settlement was approved by the 
Commission for an injury to claimant’s right lower extremity occurring on September 11, 
2019.  Under that agreement, the parties agreed claimant was entitled to future medical 
care for the injury.  (Exhibit 2) 
On March 4, 2024, claimant was evaluated by Jordyn Roddy, PA-C. Claimant 
had failed conservative treatment for the right knee.  Claimant was assessed as having 
moderate right knee osteoarthritis.  Notes indicate, “He has a work related/worker’s 
comp injury on this knee.”  A total knee replacement for the right knee was discussed 
and chosen as a treatment option.  (Ex. 1) 
In an April 2, 2024, letter, claimant’s counsel requested defendants authorize 
claimant’s right knee surgery.  The letter indicated claimant was scheduled for surgery 
on April 30, 2024, and that surgery was not yet authorized.  (Ex. 3)  There is no 
evidence defendants responded to this request. 
In an April 25, 2024, email, claimant’s counsel again requested defendants 
authorize claimant’s knee surgery.  The email indicated because the surgery was not 
yet authorized by defendants, the surgery was rescheduled to May 21, 2024.  The email 
indicates that if surgery was not authorized, the provider would cancel the surgery.  (Ex. 
4)  There is no evidence defendants responded to this request. 
Claimant testified his right knee has never healed since the date of injury.  He 
said he had a right meniscus surgery with David Field, M.D., in approximately 2019.  He 
said in the past several years his right knee has gotten progressively worse, and his 
pain has increased.  Claimant said he has gotten his right knee drained several times 
since his right knee surgery. 
CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3).  
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers’ compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Section 85.27.  Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening October 16, 1975).          
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable. See Iowa 
Rule of Appellate Procedure 6.904(3); Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 
(Iowa 1995). Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of

fact. Id. The employer’s obligation turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not 
desirability. Id.; Harned v. Farmland Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983). 
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995). 
An employer does not have the right to control the methods the providers choose 
to evaluate, diagnose and treat the injured employee. An employer is not entitled to 
control a licensed healthcare provider’s exercise of professional judgment.  Assmann v. 
Blue Star Foods, File No. 866389 (Declaratory Ruling, May 18, 1988).  An employer’s 
failure to follow recommendations of an authorized physician in matters of treatment is 
commonly a failure to provide reasonable treatment.  Boggs v. Cargill, Inc., File No. 
1050396 (Alt. Care, January 31, 1994). 
The Iowa Supreme Court has applied the principles of judicial estoppel to 
alternate medical care proceedings in the workers’ compensation context.  See 
Winnebago Industries, Inc. v. Haverly, 727 N.W.2d 567 (Iowa 2006); Tyson Foods, Inc. 
v. Hedlund, 740 N.W.2d 192 (Iowa 2007); Judicial estoppel is “a commonsense doctrine 
that prohibits a party who has successfully and unequivocally asserted a position in one 
proceeding from asserting an inconsistent position in a subsequent proceeding.” 
Hedlund, 740 N.W.2d at 196.  Its purpose is “to protect the integrity of the judicial 
process.”  Id.  For judicial estoppel to apply, the party’s inconsistent position must be 
“judicially accepted by the commissioner at the first alternate medical care hearing.” 
Judicial acceptance means that the “previous, inconsistent assertion was material to the 
holding in the first proceeding.”  Id. at 197.  
One court has summarized the principle of judicial estoppel in the context of 
alternate medical care proceedings as follows: 
Indeed, to allow an employer to control the medical care of an employee 
while simultaneously allowing that same employer to free itself of any 
obligation to later pay for medical care or benefits is a contravention of 
justice and fairness. Such a result, as the Iowa Supreme Court related, 
“simply does not advance the policy goal of avoiding inconsistent and 
misleading results.” Hedlund, 740 N.W.2d at 198. It does not respect judicial 
integrity.  Generally, once an employer admits liability for a claim in an 
alternate medical care proceeding, it is bound by that assertion.  See 
Winnebago, 727 N.W.2d at 575 (explaining it would take a “significant 
change in the facts after the admission of liability” in order to later deny 
liability); Hedlund, 740 N.W.2d at 198-99 (describing the critical role that 
admitting liability plays in an alternate medical care proceeding). This is 
because “[l]iability is normally an important component of the course of an

alternate medical care proceeding.”  Hedlund 740 N.W.2d at 198. Without 
first admitting liability, the workers’ compensation commissioner cannot 
hear the case.  Iowa Admin. Code r. 876—48.7.  
Spencer v. Annett Holdings, Inc., 905 F.Supp.2d 953, 985 (S.D. Iowa 2012). 
Defendants accepted liability for claimant’s right knee injury of September 11, 
2019, under the April 20, 2022, Agreement For Settlement.  Under that agreement 
defendants stipulated they would provide future medical care for claimant.  Defendants 
can only change their position regarding liability if there is a “. . . significant change in 
the facts after the admission of liability.”  Winnebago, 727 N.W.2d at 575 
A provider has indicated claimant had a work-related injury to his right knee that 
now requires a total knee replacement.  (Ex. 1)  Defendants deny liability for claimant’s 
“right knee pain starting in 2022.”  The Agreement For Settlement in this matter was 
approved by this agency on May 18, 2022.  Defendants have not submitted any 
evidence indicating claimant’s need for the total knee replacement on the right is not 
caused by the September of 2019 work injury.  There has been no change in facts since 
defendants agreed to provide claimant with future medical care for his work injury under 
the Agreement For Settlement. 
Because they have failed to provide any evidence there has been a significant 
change in the facts after the admission of liability, defendants are estopped from 
denying liability for the requested medical treatment.  Hormel Foods Corp. v. Tamayo-
Perez, File No. 23-0212, filed December 20, 2023 (Iowa Ct. App.); 2 N.W.3d 66 (Table). 
Defendants agreed to provide claimant with future medical care for the accepted 
injury of September 11, 2019.  A provider has indicated claimant had a work-related 
right knee injury that now requires a total knee replacement.  Defendants have denied 
that recommendation.  Defendants have not provided any new evidence there is a 
significant change in the facts since the admission of liability.  Given this record, it is 
found defendants’ denial of the requested medical care is unreasonable.  Claimant has 
carried his burden of proof he is entitled to the requested medical care. 
ORDER 
Therefore, it is ordered that claimant’s petition for alternate medical care is 
granted.  Defendants shall authorize and pay for claimant’s total knee replacement on 
the right knee as recommended.
