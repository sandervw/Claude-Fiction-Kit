This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A. The 
expedited procedures of rule 876 IAC 4.48, the “alternate medical care” rule, are 
invoked by claimant, Joshua Hanawalt. 
 
This alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on January 25, 2024. 
The proceedings were recorded digitally and constitute the official record of the hearing. 
By an order filed by the Workers’ Compensation Commissioner, this decision is 
designated final agency action. Any appeal would be by petition for judicial review under 
Iowa Code section 17A.15(1). 
 
The record in this case consists of Claimant’s Exhibit 1, Defendants’ Exhibits A-C 
and the testimony of claimant 
 
ISSUE 
 
The issue presented for resolution in this case is whether claimant is entitled to 
alternate medical care consisting of authorization for a gym membership where 
childcare is offered. 
 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
 
Defendants accept liability for a work-related injury to claimant’s low back on 
June 25, 2020.

A prior alternate medical care hearing was held in this case.  In a decision, filed 
on December 19, 2023, the undersigned ordered defendants, in part, to authorize and 
pay for a gym membership for claimant, as recommended by Shawn Spooner, M.D. 
 
In a January 4, 2024, email, claimant’s counsel asked defendants to authorize 
and pay for a gym membership for claimant as ordered by the December 19, 2023, 
decision.  (Exhibit 1).  In a response sent the same day, defendants indicated they 
would authorize and pay for a gym membership for claimant to Planet Fitness.  (Ex. A) 
 
In a January 10, 2024 email, claimant’s attorney requested the “Altoona Campus” 
be approved for claimant’s gym membership as it was close to claimant’s home and 
offered childcare.  (Ex. B) 
 
In a January 19, 2024, email, defendants again indicated they authorized a gym 
membership for claimant with Planet Fitness.  (Ex. C) 
 
Claimant testified he wanted a gym membership at the “Altoona Campus” as they 
offered childcare.  Claimant said he would be more likely to use a gym if childcare were 
provided. 
 
CONCLUSION OF LAW 
Iowa Code section 85.27(4) provides, in relevant part: 
For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish 
reasonable services and supplies to treat an injured employee and has the 
right to choose the care. . . The treatment must be offered promptly and 
be reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the 
employee. If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the care 
offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such 
dissatisfaction to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the 
employer and the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited 
to treat the injury. If the employer and employee cannot agree on such 
alternate care, the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable 
proofs of the necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable. See Iowa 
Rule of Appellate Procedure 6.904(3)I; Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 
(Iowa 1995). Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of 
fact. Id. The employer’s obligation turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not 
desirability. Id.; Harned v. Farmland Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983). In Pirelli-
Armstrong Tire Co. v. Reynolds, 562 N.W.2d 433 (Iowa 1997), the court approvingly 
quoted Bowles v. Los Lunas Schools, 109 N.M. 100, 781 P.2d 1178 (App. 1989):

[T]he words “reasonable” and “adequate” appear to describe the same 
standard. 
[The New Mexico rule] requires the employer to provide a certain 
standard of care and excuses the employer from any obligation to provide 
other services only if that standard is met. We construe the terms 
“reasonable” and “adequate” as describing care that is both appropriate to 
the injury and sufficient to bring the worker to maximum recovery. 
The order of the prior alternate medical care decision in this case required 
defendants to authorize and pay for a gym membership, as recommended by Dr. 
Spooner.  Defendants have authorized a gym membership for claimant with Planet 
Fitness.  Claimant wants a membership with the Altoona Campus as it is closer to his 
home and offers childcare.  In short, claimant contends the offer of a gym membership 
with Planet Fitness is unduly inconvenient and unreasonable as it does not offer 
childcare. 
 
I appreciate claimant’s position as I understand the difficulties of having children 
and trying to exercise without childcare.  However, there is no precedent indicating it is 
unreasonable to not offer childcare as a part of medical care.  No doctor has ordered 
that claimant have childcare with his gym membership.  Defendants have authorized 
claimant to have a gym membership with Planet Fitness.  There is no evidence in the 
record that travel to a Planet Fitness gym is unduly inconvenient to claimant.  Given this 
record, claimant has failed to carry his burden of proof the care offered by defendants is 
unreasonable.   Because claimant has failed to carry his burden of proof the care 
offered by defendants is unreasonable, claimant’s petition for alternate medical care is 
denied.  
 
ORDER 
 
Claimant’s petition for alternate medical care is denied.
