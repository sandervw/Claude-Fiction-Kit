This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A. The 
expedited procedures of rule 876 IAC 4.48, the “alternate medical care” rule, are 
invoked by claimant, Lee Gebert.  
This alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on April 22, 2024. The 
proceedings were recorded digitally and constitute the official record of the hearing. By 
an order filed by the Workers’ Compensation Commissioner, this decision is designated 
final agency action. Any appeal would be by petition for judicial review under Iowa Code 
section 17A.15(1).  
The record in this case consists of the testimony of claimant. 
ISSUE 
The issue presented for resolution in this case is whether claimant is entitled to 
alternate medical care consisting of an order from this agency to see a shoulder 
specialist for follow-up care. 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
Defendants accept liability for an injury to the right shoulder occurring on 
February 28, 2020. 
On March 27, 2023, claimant underwent a right shoulder surgery performed by 
Matthew Bollier, M.D.  Surgery consisted of, in part, a reconstruction of a shoulder 
rotator cuff with extensive debridement.  The record regarding this surgery is from an

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3).  
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Section 85.27.  Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening October 16, 1975).          
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See Iowa 
R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e); Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995). 
Determining what care is reasonable under the statute is a question of fact. Id.  The 
employer’s obligation turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.  Id.; 
Harned v. Farmland Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983).            
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).     
Defendants accept liability for a right shoulder injury to clamant occurring on 
February 28, 2020.

Defendants authorized surgery for claimant with Dr. Bollier for the right shoulder.  
Claimant’s credible testimony is that Dr. Bollier told him he would probably require 
follow-up care for the right shoulder approximately a year after surgery and that 
claimant should seek follow-up care with a shoulder specialist in Des Moines, Iowa. 
Given this record, claimant has carried his burden of proof that care is not being 
offered promptly and that he is entitled to alternate medical care. 
The undersigned apologizes to the parties for the delay in issuing the arbitration

involved in this alternate medical care petition.  An arbitration decision in these files will 
be issued shortly. 
ORDER 
Therefore, it is ordered that claimant has carried his burden of proof he is entitled 
to the requested alternate medical care.  Defendants shall authorize a follow-up 
appointment for claimant regarding his right shoulder injury.
