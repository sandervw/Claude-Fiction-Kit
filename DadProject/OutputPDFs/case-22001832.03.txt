STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Dylan Doyle, filed a petition in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits against ALPLA, Inc., employer, and Sentry Insurance, insurance 
carrier, both as defendants.  
This case was heard on January 7, 2025, and considered fully submitted on 
February 4, 2025, upon the simultaneous filing of briefs.  
The record consists of Joint Exhibits 1–5, Claimant’s Exhibits 1, 2, and 5, 
Defendants’ Exhibits B and D, and the testimony of claimant and Jodie Doyle.  
ISSUES 
1. Whether claimant sustained a permanent disability and, if so, the extent;  
 
2. The commencement date of permanent partial disability benefits;  
 
3. Costs.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury actually occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 
employment. Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143, 150 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. 
Dial Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309, 311 (Iowa 1996). The words “arising out of” refer to the 
cause or source of the injury. The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 
circumstances of the injury. 2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124, 128 (Iowa 
1995).  An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists 
between the injury and the employment. Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. The injury must 
be a rational consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely 
incidental to the employment. Koehler Elec. v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1, 3 (Iowa 2000); 
Miedema, 551 N.W.2d at 311. An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it 
happens within a period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may 
be when performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties 
or doing an activity incidental to them. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d at 150.

The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 
621 N.W.2d 410, 420 (Iowa 2001); St. Luke’s Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 
(Iowa 2000); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995). Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted 
expert medical testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & 
Ceiling, Inc., 516 N.W.2d 910, 911-12 (Iowa Ct. App. 1994). 
Claimant asserts he has sustained a mental injury from the January 24, 2022, 
work injury. In support of this claim, he relies primarily on the expert opinion of Dr. Kim 
who opined that claimant sustained a mild impairment, class 2, manifested through 
mental and behavior disorders as a result of the work injury. Dr. Kim bases the opinion 
on the results of the tests Dr. Kim administered as well as claimant’s past history and 
current presentation. Dr. Cook also agreed claimant suffered PTSD as a result of the 
work injury. Dr. Spooner acknowledged that trauma such as what claimant suffered 
and/or a concussive injury of any severity could cause material exacerbation to 
claimant’s mental state, but felt that it was not possible to determine within a reasonable 
degree of medical certainty to what degree that material exacerbation may exist. Dr. 
Spooner does not deny that the work injury could have caused a material exacerbation, 
but that he could not determine to what degree it existed in claimant.  
Therefore, based on the opinion of Dr. Kim and Dr. Cook, which is unrebutted by 
Dr. Spooner or any other expert in the record, it is found claimant sustained a mental 
injury in the form of PTSD and aggravation of pre-existing anxiety and depression 
arising from the work injury of January 24, 2022, in addition to the physical injuries to his 
jaw, face, teeth, and burns on the back of the neck.  
 Dr. Kim did not provide a specific impairment rating, nor did Dr. Kim opine that 
claimant sustained any loss of access to the labor market. Dr. Spooner also 
acknowledged the difficulty of assigning what degree of material exacerbation existed.  
Defendants argue that Dr. Kim’s opinion is too vague as Dr. Kim did not indicate 
to what extent that there was an aggravation of a pre-existing condition and provided no

analysis as to the level of impairment beyond that claimant was in the class 2 category 
of impairment. Defendants’ position is that because Dr. Kim provided only a broad 
category classification, no impairment rating can be given as it would require the use of 
agency expertise which is specifically prohibited by the 2017 statutory changes. See 
Iowa Code § 85.34(2)(x). 
However, the AMA Guides directs the physician to not assign numeric 
percentages to mental impairment. See AMA Guides, 5th Edition, p. 361. The Guides 
state that “there are no precise measures of impairment in mental disorders.” Instead, 
the Guides offer four main categories to assess areas of function. (Id.) This has been 
adopted by the Agency in Clark v. Arconic, Inc., File No. 5061553.01 at 3 (App. June 
28, 2022).  
The four categories include:  
1) 
Ability to perform activities of daily living; 
2) 
Social functioning;  
3) 
Concentration, persistence, pace;  
4) 
Deterioration or decompensation in work or worklike settings.  
AMA Guides, p. 361.  
While Dr. Kim did not lay out a precise explanation of the four categories, Dr. Kim 
did administer a number of tests to assess those four categories. Dr. Kim assessed 
neurocognition and placed claimant in the 42nd percentile (CE 1:6) Claimant placed 
68th percentile for Memory Domain. (Id.) His psychomotor speed, which was a 
cumulative index of motor speed, coordination, and visual perceptual ability, was in the 
63rd percentile. (CE 1:6) His reaction time was in the 25th percentile. (CE 1:6) This 
measured, among other things, claimant’s ability to attend to a conversation, tracking 
and responding to a set of simple instructions, and taking longer to decide what 
response to make. (CE 1:6) Claimant testified that he sometimes struggles with 
instructions at work. (Tr., pp. 28-30) His complex attention score was 37th percentile, 
his cognitive flexibility was 23rd percentile, his processing speed was 68th percentile, 
his executive function was 25th percentile, his simple visual attention was 40th 
percentile, his motor speed domain score was 55th percentile, his reasoning was 75th 
percentile, sustained attention was 66th percentile, and working memory was 63rd 
percentile. (CE 1:7-8) There were no pre-injury test scores to measure against.  
In reviewing the claimant’s testimony, it appears that his primary mental and 
behavioral complaint, at this time, is primarily one of slight memory loss as well as 
difficulty sleeping due to nightmares. (Tr., pp. 33-34; Tr., p. 23) He also testified that he 
has recurrent intrusive thoughts, negative ideation and some difficulty in following 
instructions. (Tr., pp. 28-30)

His activities of daily living are impacted in that from time to time he has 
nightmares and trouble sleeping. He struggles at times in caring for his pet. (Tr., pp. 48-
53)  Otherwise, the activities of daily living such as self-care, personal hygiene, 
communication, ambulation, travel, and sexual function are not affected.  
Outside stressors can affect his social functioning which trigger negative ideation 
and intrusive thoughts. He testified that he had ongoing explosive outbursts with 
coworkers and supervisors. (Tr., pp. 28-29) He also had these reactions with family and 
friends.  
He testified that he sometimes has difficulty processing instructions his 
supervisors give him, but he has returned to the same work and is performing his work 
without deterioration or decompensation in work or work-like settings. He forgot to lock 
out machinery which resulted in a minor injury. (Tr., p. 42)  
Overall, Dr. Kim placed claimant in class 2 or mild impairment level compatible 
with most useful functioning. Claimant refers to the Clark v. Arconic case where the 
injured worker was found to have sustained a 30 percent impairment. However, in 
Clark, the experts placed the injured worker in classes 3, 4 or 5. See Clark pp. 10-12. In 
this case, claimant’s impairment is mild and exhibited by occasional nightmares, 
outbursts with coworkers, friends, and family, negative ideations, and reduced ability to 
comprehend instructions or direction. Based on this, it is found claimant has sustained a 
10 percent impairment of the whole person. The commencement date of PPD 
commences when it is medically indicated that MMI from the injury has been reached 
and that the extent of loss or percentage of permanent impairment can be determined 
using the AMA Guides. Iowa Code Section 85.34(2).  
Dr. Kim assigned April 19, 2024, as the date of MMI; however, claimant stopped 
treating with Ms. Cook after May 12, 2023. His mental condition was deemed stable and 
under control via medications by Mr. Drahos on November 20, 2023. Based on 
claimant’s decision to not return to further counseling after May 12, 2023, and no 
change in his medication levels after that date, it is determined that the commencement 
date of PPD benefits is May 13, 2023.  
Claimant seeks an assessment of costs of $213.75. Because it is found that 
claimant has prevailed, the costs in Exhibit 5, p. 21, are awarded herein. 876 IAC 4.33.
