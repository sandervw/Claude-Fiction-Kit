STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A. The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Ann Marie French.   
The alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on April 16, 2025.  
Claimant appeared personally and through her attorney Nathan Willems.  Defendants 
appeared through their attorney Alison Stewart.  The proceedings were digitally 
recorded. That recording constitutes the official record of this proceeding.  
Pursuant to the Commissioner’s July 21, 2023, Order, the undersigned has been 
delegated authority to issue a final agency decision in this alternate medical care 
proceeding. Therefore, this ruling is designated final agency action, and any appeal of 
the decision would be to the Iowa District Court pursuant to Iowa Code section 17A. 
The hearing record consists of: 
 Claimant’s Exhibits 1-5 
 Defendants’ Exhibit A 
Claimant testified at the hearing.  Counsel for both parties provided argument.  
The record closed at the end of the alternate medical care telephonic hearing.

ISSUE 
The issue presented for resolution is whether the claimant is entitled to alternate

 An infrared sauna and a hot tub installed in her home.  
FINDINGS OF FACT 
The undersigned having considered all the evidence in the record finds: 
On December 9, 2019, claimant sustained work-related injuries to her bilateral 
upper extremities.  Defendants admitted liability for the injuries and authorized medical 
care. (See Answer; See French v. Rockwell Collins, Inc., File No. 20001174.01 (Arb. 
Decision, July 15, 2024).  An arbitration hearing was held for this injury date on January 
23, 2023. Following several stays, the undersigned issued an arbitration decision on 
July 15, 2024.  See French, File No. 20001174.01.   That decision outlines the 
treatment claimant received for her bilateral upper extremity injuries prior to the hearing 
on January 23, 2023.  The majority of that history will not be re-outlined in this decision.  
Id.  
According to that decision, defendants authorized Dr. Pospisil and Dr. Pardubsky 
to treat claimant’s bilateral upper extremity injuries.  See French, File No. 20011774.01. 
Dr. Pardubsky performed a left elbow ulnar neurolysis and left trigger thumb release on 
February 9, 2021, as well as a right elbow ulnar neurolysis and trigger thumb release on 
April 13, 2021. Id. On February 21, 2022, Dr. Pardubsky placed claimant at maximum 
medical improvement (MMI) for both upper extremities. Id.  According to the decision, 
claimant continued to complain of numbness, sensitivity to cold, and pain in both hands 
with use after her surgeries. Id. Claimant had a follow-up appointment with Dr. Pospisil 
on November 14, 2022. Id.  At that appointment, claimant complained of intense pain, 
numbness in her fingers, tingling, an inability to feel temperature changes and skin color 
changes in her fingers. Id. Dr. Pospisil diagnosed her with bilateral upper extremity 
symptoms similar to CRPD. Id.  
Claimant also alleged a mental health sequela as a result of the December 9, 
2019 injury date. See French, File No. 20001174.01.  On February 7, 2023, claimant 
attended an evaluation with Amy Mooney, Ph.D.  Id.  Following that evaluation, Dr. 
Mooney diagnosed claimant with major depressive disorder with anxious distress, 
PTSD, a history of trauma, and somatic symptom disorder with predominant pain. Id. 
For these conditions, she recommended 12-18 sessions of mental health 
psychotherapy, as well as a chronic pain therapy group. Id. Defendants have admitted 
liability for claimant’s mental health sequela and authorized medical care. Id; see also 
Answer).   
The arbitration decision awarded claimant 30 percent permanent impairment to 
the right upper extremity and 30 percent permanent impairment to the left upper 
extremity. See French, File No. 20001174.01.  It also found that claimant had not yet

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
Under Iowa law, an employer who has accepted compensability for a workplace 
injury has a right to control the care provided to the injured employee.  Ramirez-Trujillo 
v. Quality Egg, L.L.C., 878 N.W.2d 759, 769 (Iowa 2016).  The relevant statute provides 
as follows: 
For purposes of this section, the employer is obliged to furnish 
reasonable services and supplies to treat an injured employee, and has the 
right to choose the care. . . . The treatment must be offered promptly and 
be reasonably suited to treat the injury without undue inconvenience to the 
employee. If the employee has reason to be dissatisfied with the care 
offered, the employee should communicate the basis of such dissatisfaction 
to the employer, in writing if requested, following which the employer and 
the employee may agree to alternate care reasonably suited to treat the 
injury. If the employer and employee cannot agree on such alternate care, 
the commissioner may, upon application and reasonable proofs of the 
necessity therefor, allow and order other care. 
Iowa Code § 85.27(4).   
Defendant’s “obligation under the statute is confined to reasonable care for the 
diagnosis and treatment of work-related injuries.” Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 
N.W.2d 122, 124 (Iowa 1995) (emphasis in original).  In other words, the “obligation 
under the statute turns on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.”  (Id.). 
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care she has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  See Iowa Code § 85.27(4). By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment 
and seeking alternate care, claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care 
is unreasonable.  See Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3)(e); Long, 528 N.W.2d at 124.  
Ultimately, determining whether care is reasonable under the statute is a question of 
fact.  Long, 528 N.W.2d at 123. 
Under Iowa Code section 85.27, claimant bears the burden of providing 
“reasonable proofs of the necessity” to order alternate care.  At the hearing, claimant 
made a number of different arguments.  She indicated that she cannot drive to a gym 
that contains the equipment she seeks, she claims she cannot ride as a passenger to a 
gym that contains the equipment she seeks, she claims going to a gym would be 
counterproductive or make the treatment less beneficial than she hopes, and she 
testified that she cannot put on a swimsuit.  There is not sufficient medical evidence to 
support these assertions. No medical provider has restricted the claimant from driving, 
and in fact claimant does currently drive.  No medical provider has restricted the

claimant from riding in a car. No medical provider has ordered defendants provide 
assistance with daily ADLs.  No medical provider has indicated that an infrared sauna 
and/or a hot tub would be ineffective if provided at a gym.  In fact, the opposite is true. 
Dr. Mathew, her authorized provider, agreed that supplying a membership to a gym with 
the requested equipment would meet her needs if it was within a reasonable travel 
distance.  Finally, there is no evidence in the record that claimant even tried these 
treatments in a clinical setting and found them effective in relieving her symptoms 
before asking defendants to install the equipment in her personal residence. Given this, 
her testimony concerning the counterproductivity of providing the treatments in a gym is 
afforded little weight.  
Past decisions from the Iowa Supreme Court and this agency have generally 
found care located within a 50-mile radius of the claimant’s residence to be reasonable.  
See Bitner v. Cedar Falls Construction Co., File No. 5013852 (Alt. Care, September 24, 
2004); see also, Trade Professionals, Inc. v. Shriver, 661 N.W.2d 119, 124 (Iowa 2003) 
(more than 100 miles is an undue inconvenience); Schrock v. Corkery Waste Disposal, 
Inc., File No. 1133725 (Alt. Care, June 26, 1996) (120-mile round trip excessive); 
Schulte v. Vocational Services of Area Residential Care, File No. 1134342 (Alt. Care, 
September 6, 1996) (care more than 70 miles away unreasonable).  At the hearing, 
claimant testified that there is a gym with multiple locations that are 30 minutes or less 
from her house which offer a pool, hot tub and infrared sauna.  Defendants have offered 
her a membership at such a gym.  Under these facts, that is reasonable.  Claimant 
argues that she cannot drive herself to this gym, but she did not provide medical proof 
of this assertion. Plus, she did not even request defendants provide transportation to 
those services before filing this alternate care action.  At hearing, defendants’ counsel 
admitted transportation is available to the claimant under the statute.  
Defendants can take cost into consideration when providing medical care. See, 
e.g., Long, 528 N.W.2d at 124.  They have done so in this case.  They have offered 
claimant a gym membership for a facility that contains a hot tub and an infrared sauna, 
instead of installing that equipment in her personal residence.  They have also indicated 
that transportation is available under the statute.  This might not be claimant’s 
preference, but it is not unreasonable.  Claimant’s request for alternate medical care is 
denied.  
THEREFORE IT IS ORDERED: 
Claimant's petition for alternate medical care is denied.
