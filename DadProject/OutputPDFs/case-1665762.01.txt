STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Claimant, Michael A. Franck, has filed a petition for workers’ compensation 
benefits against Rudd Sanitation, Inc., employer, and PinnaclePoint Insurance 
Company, insurance carrier, both as defendants. 
In accordance with agency scheduling procedures and pursuant to the Order of 
the Commissioner the hearing was held on October 6, 2023, via Zoom.  
The record consists of Joint Exhibits 1-7, Claimants Exhibits 1-8, Defendants’ 
Exhibits A-D, along with the testimony of the claimant. The case was considered fully 
submitted on October 27, 2023, upon the simultaneous filing of briefs.  
STIPULATIONS 
 
The parties filed a hearing report at the commencement of the arbitration 
hearing. On the hearing report, the parties entered into various stipulations. All of those 
stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration decision and 
no factual or legal issues relative to the parties’ stipulations will be raised or discussed 
in this decision. The parties are now bound by their stipulations. 
The parties stipulated the claimant sustained an injury on May 23, 2019 which 
arose out of and in the course of his employment to his right shoulder and right hip. 
They further agree the injury was the cause of some temporary disability entitlement 
which is no longer in dispute.

ISSUES 
1. Whether the accepted work injury of May 23, 2019, resulted in injuries to 
claimant’s neck, right elbow, and right arm;  
2. Whether the claimant sustained a permanent disability to his neck, right 
elbow, right hip and right arm as a result of the injury alleged on May 23, 
2019; 
3. Whether the claimant is entitled to penalty benefits; and  
4. Whether the claimant is entitled to reimbursement for $4,800.00 for his IME.  
FINDINGS OF FACT 
At the time of the hearing claimant, Michael A. Franck, was a 69-year-old person. 
He did not graduate from high school but did undergo truck driving school at Kirkwood 
Community college, receiving a CDL in approximately 1980 or 1981. (Claimant Exhibit  
3, page 84) He went on to obtain a GED in 1982. Id. His past work history includes 
factory work as an assembler, a parts cleaner, and from 2009 to 2022, a sanitation truck 
driver and hauler for defendant employer. 
Claimant’s past medical history is significant for heart disease, neck surgery and 
arthritis in the back and hips. In a September 24, 2009, medical report issued to clear 
claimant for his commercial driver’s license, claimant reported a history of heart disease 
and regular alcohol use. (Defendants’ Exhibit A, page 1) Claimant underwent surgery in 
2009 for his neck. (CE 1:15; Hearing Transcript, pages 19-20) On July 29, 2016, 
claimant was observed moving garbage cans and recycling tubs while on light duty. (DE 
A:2) On August 18, 2016, claimant was seen by David S. Tearse, M.D., regarding left 
shoulder pain. (DE A:7) Claimant did have signs of impingement as well as give way 
weakness throughout the rotator cuff secondary to pain. Id. Dr. Tearse diagnosed 
claimant with rotator cuff strain and possible rotator cuff tear. Id. Claimant was treated 
with physical therapy and injections as well as work restrictions of no above waist level 
lifting and no lifting more than fifteen pounds. (DE A:8) Per the history taken by Dr. 
Segal, claimant also suffered right shoulder pain prior to his injury of May 23, 2019, and 
received treatment in the form of injections. (CE 1:15) Claimant also suffered from 
chronic low back pain prior to his May 23, 2019, injury, re-injured himself in May 2022 
and had surgery in June 2022. (CE 1:15)

At the time of his injury, claimant was working as a sanitation truck driver for 
defendant employer. His job duties included driving the garbage truck, stopping, 
climbing in and out of the truck, and throwing the contents of the trash containers into 
the back of the truck. He worked anywhere from 4 to 10 hours a day and was paid by 
the hour. His daily work hours were dictated by the route and whether he had help. On 
Mondays, he performed the route by himself. From Tuesday through Friday, he had at 
least one assistant. Claimant testified that the job was very physical. The average 
trashcan weighed between 20 to 60 pounds despite a 40-pound weight limit per can. If 
the workers could lift and dump it, it was accepted. From time to time, two people would 
need to lift and dump a can.  
On May 23, 2019, claimant was cleaning out his sanitation truck when he fell and 
injured himself. Because he had an assistant that day, he continued to do his route. 
However, the following day he reported to his employer that he needed medical care. 
On May 24, 2019, claimant was seen at Marion Urgent Care by Jeffrey F. Jones, 
M.D. (Joint Exhibit 1, page 1) He reported falling off a truck the previous day. Id. 
Claimant believed he suffered a direct blow to the hip, possible injury to the shoulder, 
and possible injury to the head. He reported pain in the shoulder and pain with arm 
movement. Id. He did not have neck or back pain and no numbness in the leg or arm. 
Id. He exhibited decreased range of motion, tenderness over the AC joint, crepitus and 
pain in the right shoulder. (JE 1:1) For the right hip he exhibited slight posterior 
tenderness, normal range of motion, normal strength, no bony tenderness, and no 
swelling. Id. 
X-rays showed progressive degenerative changes of the AC and glenohumeral 
joint. (JE 1:2) Dr. Jones diagnosed claimant with a hip contusion and a shoulder 
separation with a possible rotator cuff injury. Id. Claimant was kept off work and 
instructed to recheck with Occupational Medicine.  (JE 1:3, 6)

limit his lifting and carrying to no more than 5 pounds in the right arm and avoid any 
repetitive use of the right upper extremity including no forceful grasping, pinching, 
pulling, or pushing with the right arm. Id.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established has the burden 
of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 6.904(3). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the alleged injury actually occurred and that it both arose out of and in the course of the 
employment.  Quaker Oats Co. v. Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143 (Iowa 1996); Miedema v. Dial 
Corp., 551 N.W.2d 309 (Iowa 1996).  The words “arising out of” refer to the cause or 
source of the injury.  The words “in the course of” refer to the time, place, and 
circumstances of the injury.  2800 Corp. v. Fernandez, 528 N.W.2d 124 (Iowa 1995).  
An injury arises out of the employment when a causal relationship exists between the 
injury and the employment.  Miedema, 551 N.W.2d 309.  The injury must be a rational 
consequence of a hazard connected with the employment and not merely incidental to 
the employment.  Koehler Elec. v. Wills, 608 N.W.2d 1 (Iowa 2000); Miedema, 551 
N.W.2d 309.  An injury occurs “in the course of” employment when it happens within a 
period of employment at a place where the employee reasonably may be when 
performing employment duties and while the employee is fulfilling those duties or doing 
an activity incidental to them.  Ciha, 552 N.W.2d 143. 
 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based. A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause. A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible. George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996). 
 
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability. 
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); 
Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995). Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994). Unrebutted expert medical 
testimony cannot be summarily rejected. Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 
N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 1994). 
 
A personal injury contemplated by the workers’ compensation law means an 
injury, the impairment of health or a disease resulting from an injury which comes about, 

FRANCK V. RUDD SANITATION, INC. 
Page 14 
 
not through the natural building up and tearing down of the human body, but because of 
trauma.  The injury must be something that acts extraneously to the natural processes 
of nature and thereby impairs the health, interrupts or otherwise destroys or damages a 
part or all of the body.  Although many injuries have a traumatic onset, there is no 
requirement for a special incident or an unusual occurrence.  Injuries which result from 
cumulative trauma are compensable.  Increased disability from a prior injury, even if 
brought about by further work, does not constitute a new injury, however.  St. Luke’s 
Hosp. v. Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); Ellingson v. Fleetguard, Inc., 599 N.W.2d 
440 (Iowa 1999); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 
1995); McKeever Custom Cabinets v. Smith, 379 N.W.2d 368 (Iowa 1985).  An 
occupational disease covered by chapter 85A is specifically excluded from the definition 
of personal injury.  Iowa Code section 85.61(4)(b); Iowa Code section 85A.8; Iowa Code 
section 85A.14. 
Claimant seeks a finding that he has sustained a significant whole body disability 
as a result of slipping and falling on May 23, 2019. Defendants accept that claimant’s 
right shoulder and hip were injured, but deny permanent injuries to claimant’s hip, right 
elbow, neck, and right arm. 
Claimant relies on his testimony and the opinions of Dr. Segal to support the 
claims he sustained injuries to his hip, right elbow, neck, and right arm as a result of his 
fall on May 23, 2019.  
Claimant’s contemporaneous medical records support a finding that claimant’s 
right hip pain from the May 23, 2019, fall resolved by June 17, 2019. After June 17, 
2019, there were no other mentions of right hip pain until July 24, 2020. In the history 
portion of the July 24, 2020, note, it stated that the pain in claimant’s groin was of a 
longstanding nature and that he was getting up and down out of his truck several times 
a day, implying that this pain was more chronic in nature developing from the 
biomechanics of climbing in and out of the cab of the sanitation truck versus an impact-
related trauma on May 23, 2019. (See e.g. JE 4:30) The pain claimant described at that 
visit was greater on the left than the right and was not radicular in nature which is 
different than the complaints recorded by Dr. Segal. History of the left hip pain is not 
recorded. (CE 1:12-13) The right hip pain is noted as an average of 2 to 3 on a 10 scale 
with the highest pain rating a 5-6 on a 10 scale. (CE 1:12) Claimant reportedly has pain 
in the ball of his right hip joint radiating down his leg to his toes. (CE 1:13) Dr. Segal 
also attributes claimant’s right hip pain to a traumatic injury on May 23, 2019, that was 
exacerbated over time. (See CE 1:25 and 1:33-34) This, too, is inconsistent with the 
claimant’s contemporaneous medical notes indicating that the traumatic right hip pain 
resolved within a few weeks of the May 23, 2019, fall, but then later developed from 
climbing up and down out of the truck or as a result of claimant’s low back pain. He 
testified that he began using his cane around the time that he had a low back injury. 
(Tr., p. 37) The low back pain was primarily on the right side. Id. Based on claimant’s 
testimony, the contemporaneous medical records, the symptom inconsistency between 
the reports of hip pain leading up to the scheduling of the left hip replacement surgery, it 
is found that the right hip pain that claimant currently suffers is not causally related to

Page 15 
 
the work injury of May 23, 2019. Claimant did sustain a right hip contusion which 
resolved on or about June 17, 2019.  
Dr. Segal diagnosed claimant with ulnar neuropathy following the May 23, 2019, 
injury. There are no EMG tests confirming that claimant even suffers ulnar neuropathy. 
The contemporaneous medical records show claimant complained of right arm 
numbness and pain to the elbow along with weakness in grip strength. Dr. Segal posits 
that the claimant’s arm being in a sling likely contributed to his ulnar neuropathy and 
that the mechanism of injury resulting in injury to the shoulder likely means he sustained 
a direct injury to his ulnar nerve as well. Much of Dr. Segal’s report in this section as 
well as others relies on a presentation of possible causes of pain in the elbow in medical 
literature but not as much on the actual tests, reports of injury by claimant, and medical 
notes. The finding of ulnar neuropathy is not supported by the medical records but 
based on supposition by Dr. Segal.  
Dr. Segal also rates the biceps separately from the shoulder but the biceps 
tendon is considered part of the shoulder. Chavez v. MS Tech. LLC, 972 N.W.2d 662, 
668 (Iowa 2022).  
For the neck, claimant has had regular and ongoing complaints of neck pain from 
the initial fall through the present time. He complained about neck pain to Ms. Hart-
Fernandez shortly after the injury on May 31, 2019. While he did feel that the neck pain 
resolved, unlike the right hip injury, claimant repeated pain complaints about his neck in 
early October to his therapist and continued to complain about neck pain and 
headaches throughout therapy even after his second shoulder surgery. Based on 
claimant’s continuous and consistent complaints of neck pain following the May 23, 
2019, injury, as well as Dr. Segal’s expert opinion that the neck pain is connected to the 
work injury, it is found claimant has sustained an injury to his neck as a result of the 
May 23, 2019, fall from his work truck and that the symptoms of pain, loss of mobility 
and headaches are attributable to the neck injury suffered on May 23, 2019.  
Because the neck injury is an injury to the body as a whole, the claimant’s 
impairment is industrial in nature.  
Dr. Bollier assigned an 8 percent right upper extremity impairment rating for the 
right shoulder but did not provide a rating for the neck, as he did not treat any neck 
injury. Dr. Segal assigned a 37 percent right upper extremity impairment to the right 
shoulder or a 22 percent whole person impairment. His measurements for range of 
motion deficits were greater than that of Dr. Bollier, and he issued a rating for motor 
deficit of 10 percent right arm impairment, and 10 percent for distal clavicle excision. 
Range of motion and strength deficits are not separate ratable considerations when 
there are objective anatomic findings upon which to base a rating. See section 16.8a.  
Dr. Segal also adds an additional 10 percent for the distal clavicle excision as well as 
applying the appropriate modifier which is required by the AMA Guides. See Brown v. 
Estes Express, File Number: 2001413.01, 2023 WL 5952035, at *4 (Sept. 6, 2023).

instruments and repeated three times which are more precise than the non-instrument 
measurements taken by Dr. Bollier, and thus the 13 percent whole person rating is 
accepted as a factor to be considered in the claimant’s overall industrial disability. 
Dr. Segal assigns an 8 percent whole person rating for the neck injury finding 
claimant fits the DRE Cervical Category II. See AMA Guides 5th Edition, Table 15-5. 
DRE Cervical Category II requires clinical history and examination findings compatible 
with a specific injury. Id. Findings may include muscle guarding or spasms, asymmetric 
loss of range of motion or nonverifiable complaints of radicular pain. Id. Dr. Segal stated 
he would attribute hand numbness and weakness to the ulnar neuropathy and therefore 
any radiculopathy from the neck would be considered nonverifiable radicular symptoms. 
(CE 1:43) Problematically, there are no other radicular symptoms to be attributed to the 
neck if the hand numbness and weakness were attributable to the ulnar neuropathy 
which was found to not be a compensable injury in this case. Claimant’s primary 
complaints regarding his neck are headaches and unspecified neck pain and some 
limited turning motion of his neck. The claimant’s symptoms place him in the DRE 
Cervical Category I which is for no significant clinical findings, no observed muscle 
guarding or spasm, no documentable neurologic impairment, no documented alteration 
in structural integrity, and no other indication of impairment related to injury or illness; no 
fractures. AMA Guides 5th Edition, Table 15-3, p. 384. Based on Table 15-3, no 
impairment rating is appropriate for the neck pain and headaches.  
  Since claimant has an impairment to the body as a whole, an industrial disability 
has been sustained. Industrial disability was defined in Diederich v. Tri-City Ry. Co. of 
Iowa, 219 Iowa 587, 258 N.W. 899 (1935) as follows: "It is therefore plain that the 
Legislature intended the term 'disability' to mean 'industrial disability' or loss of earning 
capacity and not a mere 'functional disability' to be computed in terms of percentages of 
the total physical and mental ability of a normal man." 
Functional impairment is an element to be considered in determining industrial 
disability which is the reduction of earning capacity, but consideration must also be 
given to the injured employee's age, education, qualifications, experience, motivation, 
loss of earnings, severity and situs of the injury, work restrictions, inability to engage in 
employment for which the employee is fitted and the employer's offer of work or failure 
to so offer. McSpadden v. Big Ben Coal Co., 288 N.W.2d 181 (Iowa 1980); Olson v. 
Goodyear Service Stores, 255 Iowa 1112, 125 N.W.2d 251 (1963); Barton v. Nevada 
Poultry Co., 253 Iowa 285, 110 N.W.2d 660 (1961). 
Compensation for permanent partial disability shall begin at the termination of the 
healing period. Compensation shall be paid in relation to 500 weeks as the disability 
bears to the body as a whole. Section 85.34. 
Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(v) also states, in relevant part:  
A determination of the reduction in the employee’s earning capacity 
caused by the disability shall take into account the permanent partial

The last issue is the reasonableness of the fee charged by Dr. Segal. Iowa Code 
section §85.39 limits the amount to be reimbursed for an examination to a “reasonable” 
fee. Iowa Code Section 85.39.  “A determination of the reasonableness of a fee for an 
examination made pursuant to this subsection shall be based on the typical fee charged 
by a medical provider to perform an impairment rating in the local area where the 
examination is conducted.” Id.  
The claimant has the burden of proving the reasonableness of the expenses 
incurred for the impairment examination. MidAmerican Constr. LLC v. Sandlin, 992 
N.W.2d 237 (Iowa Ct. App. 2023). Defendants proffer the charges from Dr. Bollier as 
evidence that Dr. Segal’s fees were unreasonable. Dr. Segal’s fee for the examination 
section was $1,333.34. This is very close to the fee charged by Dr. Bollier. Dr. Segal’s 
fee of $1,333.34 is deemed reasonable and is awarded under Iowa Code Section 85.39.  
Claimant seeks reimbursement for the remainder of the charges under 876 IAC 
4.33. Agency rule 876 IAC 4.33(6) permits assessment of “the reasonable costs of 
obtaining no more than two doctors’ or practitioners’ reports. The Iowa Supreme Court 
has interpreted 876 IAC 4.33(6) and concluded that only the cost of the expert’s written 
report can be taxed as a cost. The rationale provided by the Court was that “a 
physician’s report becomes a cost incurred in a hearing because it is used as evidence 
in lieu of the doctor’s testimony.” Des Moines Area Regional Transit Authority v. Young, 
867 N.W.2d 839, 846 (Iowa 2015). Any expense related to the examination provided by 
a physician must be compensated, if possible, only under Iowa Code section 85.39 and 
is not considered a cost pursuant to Iowa Code section 86.40 or 876 IAC 4.33(6). Id. 
In this instance, Dr. Segal provided detailed explanation of his time spent on this 
case. He spent 1 hour reviewing the records and 3 hours drafting the impairment rating 
and report along with additional time considering future treatment and restrictions. (CE 
8:128) Dr. Segal’s report was 49 pages long. Accompanying the report were annotated 
pictures of claimant’s range of motion measurements. The fees charged appear 
reasonable and should be taxed under 876 IAC 4.33(6).
