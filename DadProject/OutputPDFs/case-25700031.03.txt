STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A.  The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Jessica Macumber.  
On February 7, 2025, claimant filed an alternate medical care petition against SBH 
Davenport and Safety National Casualty Corp.   
The alternate medical care claim came on for telephone hearing on February 18, 
2025.  The proceedings were digitally recorded.  That recording constitutes the official 
record of this proceeding.   
Pursuant to the Commissioner’s July 21, 2023 Order, the undersigned has been 
delegated authority to issue a final agency decision in this alternate medical care 
proceeding.  Therefore, this ruling is designated final agency action, and any appeal of 
the decision would be to the Iowa District Court pursuant to Iowa Code section 17A. 
The record consists of Claimant’s Exhibits 1 through 4 and Defendants’ Exhibits 
A and B.  All exhibits were offered without objection and received into evidence.  No 
witnesses were called to testify.  Argument of counsel was heard and considered.  The 
evidentiary record closed at the conclusion of the hearing.

ISSUE 
The issue presented for resolution is whether the defendants have provided 
reasonable treatment to the claimant without undue delay and, if not, the appropriate 
remedy. 
FINDINGS OF FACT 
Jessica Macumber sustained a work-related injury on June 28, 2024.  
Defendants admit liability for the alleged left hip condition; however, they deny liability 
for the claim relating to the alleged low back condition. 
Defendants authorized and directed medical care for claimant’s left hip condition.  
According to the medical records in evidence, claimant sustained a left hip labral tear.  
She received a hip injection, which relieved 80 percent of her pain from September to 
December 2024. (See Exhibit 4, page 10) 
According to claimant, medical treatment for the left hip condition was placed on 
hold while defendants investigated the alleged low back condition.  Ultimately, 
defendants denied liability for the low back condition and any treatment 
recommendations related to the same. 
On January 28, 2025, claimant presented to Pierce Peters, M.D., and reported 
left leg and hip pain. (Ex. 4, p. 10)  Dr. Peters assessed claimant with a left hip labral 
tear and L5-S1 spondylolisthesis. (Id.)  Given claimant’s positive response to the last left 
hip injection, Dr. Peters recommended treating the left hip condition before addressing 
the spondylolisthesis. (Id.) 
Following the denial of liability for the low back treatment, claimant’s counsel 
requested a follow-up appointment with “Dr. Hussain” to discuss further treatment for 
the accepted left hip condition.  The request was made via e-mail on  January 31, 2025. 
(Exhibit 1, p. 6)  Claimant’s counsel followed up on his January 31, 2025, e-mail, and 
requests for follow-up care, on February 6, 2025.  Later that afternoon, defense counsel 
responded to claimant’s attorney via e-mail and relayed that she would discuss 
claimant’s request with her client. (Ex. 1, p. 4) 
When defense counsel had not followed up by February 11, 2025, claimant’s 
counsel requested an update on the status of his client’s request. (Ex. 1, p. 3)  
Claimant’s counsel followed up on his February 11, 2025, e-mail, and requests for care, 
on February 13, 2025, and February 14, 2025. (Ex. 1, pp. 1-3) 
On the morning of February 18, 2025, defense counsel responded to claimant’s 
attorney via e-mail.  Defense counsel reassured claimant’s attorney that the left hip 
condition was still an accepted claim, care for the left hip condition would continue to be 
authorized, and the claimant was free to seek care for the same. (Exhibit B)

In a subsequent e-mail, which was not submitted into evidence, defendants 
relayed that they had authorized and attempted to schedule claimant for a follow-up 
appointment at ORA Orthopedics; however, the provider unexpectedly canceled the

Through her February 7, 2025, Petition, claimant seeks an order directing

REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The defendants have explicitly denied liability for the alleged low back condition. 
Before any benefits can be ordered, including medical benefits, compensability of 
the claim must be established, either by admission of liability or by adjudication.  The 
summary provisions of Iowa Code section 85.27, as more particularly described in rule 
876 IAC 4.48, are not designed to adjudicate disputed compensability of a claim. 
The Iowa Supreme Court has held:  
We emphasize that the commissioner’s ability to decide the merits 
of a section 85.27(4) alternate medical care claim is limited to situations 
where the compensability of an injury is conceded, but the 
reasonableness of a particular course of treatment for the compensable 
injury is disputed. . . .  Thus, the commissioner cannot decide the 
reasonableness of the alternate care claim without also necessarily 
deciding the ultimate disputed issue in the case:  whether or not the 
medical condition Barnett was suffering at the time of the request was a 
work-related injury.  
. . . . 
Once an employer takes the position in response to a claim for 
alternate medical care that the care sought is for a noncompensatory 
injury, the employer cannot assert an authorization defense in response to 
a subsequent claim by the employee for the expenses of the alternate 
medical care.  
R. R. Donnelly & Sons v. Barnett, 670 N.W.2d 190, 197-198 (Iowa 2003) (fn 2). 
Thus, the portion of claimant’s petition seeking medical treatment for the low 
back must be dismissed.  Given the denial of liability for the condition sought to be

treated in the petition for alternate medical care, defendants lose the right to control the 
medical care claimant seeks during the period of denial and the claimant is free to 
choose that care.  Brewer-Strong v. HNI Corp., 913 N.W.2d 235 (Iowa 2018); Bell Bros. 
Heating & Air Cond. v. Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d 193 (Iowa 2010).   
As a result of the denial of liability for the condition sought to be treated in this 
proceeding, claimant may obtain reasonable medical care from any provider for this 
treatment – but at claimant’s expense – and seek reimbursement for such care using 
regular claim proceedings before this agency.  Haack v. Von Hoffman Graphics, File 
No. 1268172 (App. July 31, 2002); Kindhart v. Fort Des Moines Hotel, I Iowa Industrial 
Comm’r Decisions No. 3, 611 (App. March 27, 1985).  Defendants are precluded from 
asserting an authorization defense as to any future treatment during their period of 
denial.  Brewer-Strong v. HNI Corp., 913 N.W.2d 235 (Iowa 2018). 
Claimant has the right to be notified as to the reason(s) for the denial of liability.  
“When liability on a claim is denied, a letter shall be sent to claimant stating reasons for 
the denial.” 876 IAC section 3.1(2).  Defendants contend they have complied with the 
compliance provision; however, claimant asserts new information has been brought to 
light that may require additional investigation by defendants. 
Defendants have accepted liability for the left hip condition.  With regard to this 
condition, I make the following conclusions: 
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Iowa Code Section 85.27 (2013). 
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See 
Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  Determining what care is 
reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.  Id.  The employer’s obligation turns 
on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.  Id.; Harned v. Farmland 
Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983).   
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995). 
An employer’s statutory right is to select the providers of care and the employer 
may consider cost and other pertinent factors when exercising its choice. Long, at 124. 
An employer (typically) is not a licensed health care provider and does not possess

medical expertise. Accordingly, an employer does not have the right to control the 
methods the providers choose to evaluate, diagnose and treat the injured employee. An 
employer is not entitled to control a licensed health care provider’s exercise of 
professional judgment. Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 866389 (Declaratory 
Ruling, May 19, 1988). An employer’s failure to follow recommendations of an 
authorized physician in matters of treatment is commonly a failure to provide reasonable 
treatment. Boggs v. Cargill, Inc., File No. 1050396 (Alt. Care January 31, 1994). 
In this instance, the claimant requested care for the accepted left hip condition 
following defendants’ investigation into the alleged low back condition.  Defendants did 
not promptly respond to claimant’s request.  Claimant then communicated her basis for 
dissatisfaction to the employer and made reasonable efforts to resolve the dispute prior 
to filing the current petition for alternate medical care.  At the time claimant filed her 
petition, defendants had not authorized a follow-up appointment with an authorized 
treating physician.  Eventually, defendants scheduled the claimant for a follow-up 
appointment with ORA Orthopedics.  My understanding is that the appointment was 
subsequently cancelled by ORA Orthopedics.  Defendants notified claimant’s counsel of 
the appointment – and its subsequent cancellation – on the date of hearing. 
Defendants contend that claimant cannot carry her burden of proving the 
authorized care is unreasonable, as they have agreed to authorize the care claimant 
seeks.  While defendants have agreed to schedule claimant for a follow-up 
appointment, this is the second instance in which claimant requested medical care and 
defendants did not communicate their position regarding the same until the date of 
hearing.  Moreover, as of the hearing date, the claimant was not scheduled to present 
for any follow-up medical treatment related to her left hip. 
I acknowledge defendants’ assertion that they have not communicated a denial 
regarding the left hip condition and claimant has been free to schedule the medical 
treatment she is seeking through her alternate medical care petition.  However, it is 
entirely reasonable for claimant’s attorney to request that the defendants schedule 
claimant’s appointments.  While the Iowa Code does not explicitly require defendants to 
schedule medical appointments on behalf of the injured worker, this is often the 
practical outcome, as medical providers often require confirmation that the treatment is 
authorized before scheduling an appointment for a work-related injury.  This is 
especially true in cases like the present one, where there has been a gap in medical 
treatment for the condition at issue.  Even in this case, where the defendants eventually 
scheduled the claimant’s medical care, the appointment was later cancelled by the 
provider.  This demonstrates that even the defendants, who have direct control over 
medical care and established relationships with the providers, can face challenges 
when securing appointments.   
Having reviewed all evidence in the record, and after weighing the interests of all 
parties, I find that the most appropriate way to deal with this dispute is to grant the 
claimant’s request for alternate medical care.  Both parties, however, are ordered 
heretofore to engage in reasonable communication in all disputes regarding authorized 
care.

ORDER 
THEREFORE IT IS ORDERED: 
The claimant's petition for alternate medical care is GRANTED.  Defendants shall 
authorize and coordinate an examination for treatment purposes for claimant’s left hip 
condition.  Defendants shall schedule the examination as soon as possible, but no later 
than March 3, 2025.  The actual examination may take place on or after that date. 
IT IS FURTHER ORDERED that the claimant's claim for low back treatment 
should be and is hereby dismissed without prejudice. 
IT IS FURTHER ORDERED that if claimant seeks to recover the charges 
incurred in obtaining the care for which defendants deny liability, defendants are barred 
from asserting lack of authorization as a defense for those charges. 
FURTHER, if defendants have not already done so, defendants are ordered to 
file a Subsequent Report of Injury “denial of liability” through the EDI system, and send 
a letter to the claimant which states the reason(s) for the denial of the condition denied, 
pursuant to 876 IAC 3.1(2). The defendants shall do this within ten (10) days from the 
date of this order.
