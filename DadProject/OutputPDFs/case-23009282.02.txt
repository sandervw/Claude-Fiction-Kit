STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
This is a contested case proceeding under Iowa Code chapters 85 and 17A.  The 
expedited procedure of rule 876 IAC 4.48 is invoked by claimant, Nicholas Noel.  
Claimant appeared personally and through his attorney, MaKayla Augustine.  
Defendants appeared through their attorney, Stephen Brown. 
The alternate medical care claim came on for hearing on December 5, 2024.  
The proceedings were digitally recorded.  That recording constitutes the official record 
of this proceeding.  Pursuant to the Commissioner’s Order, the undersigned has been 
delegated authority to issue a final agency decision in this alternate medical care 
proceeding.  Therefore, this ruling is designated final agency action and any appeal of 
the decision would be to the Iowa District Court pursuant to Iowa Code section 17A. 
The record consists of claimant’s exhibit 1 and defense exhibits A and B, which 
were received without objection.  The defendants do not dispute liability for claimant’s 
April 2023, work injury.  It is noted that the claimant has also alleged that the condition 
extends into his right leg, hip and body as a whole, as well as “gait derangement.”  The 
defendants are still investigating this portion of Mr. Noel’s claim.  In any event, at the 
present time, Mr. Noel is seeking treatment for his right foot and ankle, which is 
admitted. 
ISSUE 
The issue presented for resolution is whether the claimant is entitled to an 
evaluation with a certified orthotist.

FINDINGS OF FACT 
Nicholas Noel sustained an injury to his right foot and ankle on or about April 15, 
2023.  The defendants accepted the claim and provided treatment.  His last authorized 
treating physician saw him in May 2024, and released him from care at that time.  Mr. 
Noel has not sought authorized care since this time. 
In October 2024, Mr. Noel was evaluated by Robert Rondinelli, M.D., at the 
request of his attorney for purposes of an independent medical examination (IME) under 
Section 85.39.  Dr. Rondinelli diagnosed “residual dynamic instability about his ankle 
and hindfoot, which is due to weakness, inefficiency of gait, rapid fatigue, and 
associated ankle and foot pain.”  (Claimant’s Exhibit 1, page 2)  Dr. Rondinelli 
recommended an ankle-foot orthosis (AFO) and referred Mr. Noel to a specific orthotist 
for examination.  Claimant’s counsel then forwarded the IME to defendants and 
requested this treatment on November 7, 2024, and specifically requested the care 
recommended by Dr. Rondinelli.  (Cl. Ex. 1, p. 1)  Mr. Noel testified that he believes Dr. 
Rondinelli’s diagnosis is correct, and that he strongly desires to see an orthotist for 
treatment.  I find his testimony credible. 
In response, defense counsel stated on the record that an appointment has been 
arranged for Mr. Noel to see a podiatrist on December 13, 2024.  This evaluation is for 
treatment purposes.  Defense counsel stated this in his answer and on the record, 
further clarifying that the defendants would authorize any treatment recommended by 
the podiatrist. 
REASONING AND CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law.  The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services.  The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury.  Iowa Code Section 85.27 (2013). 
By challenging the employer’s choice of treatment – and seeking alternate care – 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable.  See 
Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).  Determining what care is 
reasonable under the statute is a question of fact.  Id.  The employer’s obligation turns 
on the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability.  Id.; Harned v. Farmland 
Foods, Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98 (Iowa 1983).   
An application for alternate medical care is not automatically sustained because 
claimant is dissatisfied with the care he has been receiving.  Mere dissatisfaction with 
the medical care is not ample grounds for granting an application for alternate medical 
care.  Rather, the claimant must show that the care was not offered promptly, was not 
reasonably suited to treat the injury, or that the care was unduly inconvenient for the 
claimant.  Long v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122 (Iowa 1995).

An employer’s statutory right is to select the providers of care and the employer 
may consider cost and other pertinent factors when exercising its choice. Long, at 124. 
An employer (typically) is not a licensed health care provider and does not possess 
medical expertise. Accordingly, an employer does not have the right to control the 
methods the providers choose to evaluate, diagnose and treat the injured employee. An 
employer is not entitled to control a licensed health care provider’s exercise of 
professional judgment. Assmann v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 866389 (Declaratory 
Ruling, May 18, 1988). An employer’s failure to follow recommendations of an 
authorized physician in matters of treatment is commonly a failure to provide reasonable 
treatment. Boggs v. Cargill, Inc., File No. 1050396 (Alt. Care, January 31, 1994). 
I find that it would be perfectly reasonable for the defendants to simply authorize 
Mr. Noel’s referral to a certified orthotist.  This, however, is not the issue.  The issue is 
whether the claimant has proven by a preponderance of evidence that the treatment 
offered by the defendants is unreasonable. 
Based upon the record before me, I cannot make such a finding.  Mr. Noel had 
been released from care since the end of May 2024.  The authorized treating provider 
had made no additional treatment recommendations.  Mr. Noel, through counsel, had 
sought out a second opinion through an IME with Dr. Rondinelli.  Dr. Rondinelli is not an 
authorized treating provider; rather, he is the claimant’s medical expert.  The defendants 
are not required, under Iowa law, to accept Dr. Rondinelli’s report as true or authorize 
the care he recommends. 
Upon being presented with this report, within a couple of weeks, the defendants 
authorized and then secured an appointment with a foot specialist for December 13, 
2024.  The defendants acknowledged that this appointment was for the purpose of 
determining a course of treatment for Mr. Noel.1  In other words, it is not merely a 
defense IME.  I find that this treatment is prompt, convenient and reasonably suited to 
treat Mr. Noel’s injury.  In my estimation, this is precisely how the alternate medical care 
process should work when both sides are doing their jobs.  While I find that the 
defendants are offering reasonable care at this time, I do note that this agency expects 
the defendants to follow through with treatment recommendations for the right foot and 
ankle, if any, from their chosen podiatrist. 
ORDER 
THEREFORE IT IS ORDERED: 
The claimant's petition for alternate medical care is DENIED.
