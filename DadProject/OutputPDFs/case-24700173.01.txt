STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
Mark Carlson, claimant, filed 2 petitions in arbitration seeking workers’ 
compensation benefits from Emerson Automation Solutions, employer and Liberty 
Mutual Insurance Group, insurance carrier, as defendants.  Hearing was held via Zoom 
videoconference on April 22, 2025.     
For the arbitration hearing the parties filed a separate hearing report in each file.  
On the hearing reports, the parties entered into various stipulations.  All of those 
stipulations were accepted and are hereby incorporated into this arbitration decision.  
The parties are now bound by their stipulations.  
Mark Carlson and Tim Brown were the only witnesses to testify live at trial.  The 
evidentiary record also includes joint exhibits 1-8, claimant’s exhibits 1-5, and 
defendant’s exhibits A-G.  All exhibits were received without objection.  The evidentiary 
record closed at the conclusion of the arbitration hearing.       
The parties submitted post-hearing briefs on June 16, 2025, at which time the 
case was fully submitted to the undersigned.

The central dispute surrounding the November 4, 2022 date of injury is whether 
the injury caused any permanent disability to his left shoulder and/or left arm.   
First, we will address the left shoulder.  Dr. Ash, Dr. Chen, and Dr. Kuhnlein all 
agree that the injury on November 4, 2022 affected Mark’s left shoulder.  However, Dr. 
Ash and Dr. Chen opined that the November 4, 2022 event was merely a temporary 
aggravation. Although Dr. Ash indicated this was merely a temporary aggravation, he 
restricted Mark to 35-pound lifting limit with his left arm and no work above shoulder 
level; a restriction that Mark did not have prior to November 4, 2022.  (JE6, pp. 69-74) 
Mark relies on the opinions of Dr. Kuhnlein and his own lack of symptoms in the five 
years before the accident to support his contention that the injury did cause permanent 
disability. Defendants rely on the opinions of Dr. Ash and Dr. Chen to support their 
contention that the November 4, 2022 work injury was merely a temporary aggravation.  
However, neither Dr. Ash nor Dr. Chen cite to any evidence that Mark experienced left 
shoulder problems in the five years before the November 2022 work injury. Additionally, 
there are no records during that time to indicate that claimant sought any medical 
treatment for his left shoulder during those five years.   
There is no dispute that Mark had severe arthritis in his left shoulder prior to 
November 4, 2022.  The medical records also demonstrate that in 2017 Dr. Sneller 
noted that Mark would require a total shoulder replacement at some point.  (JE3) 
However, I find that the evidentiary record demonstrates that Mark was able to perform 
his regular job duties without restrictions or difficulty in the years leading up to 
November 4, 2022.  I further find that Mark did not seek medical treatment for his left 
shoulder in the approximately five years leading up to the November 2022 work injury.  
Additionally, I find that the records support Mark’s testimony that his symptoms have 
been ongoing since the November 4, 2022 work injury.  Dr. Kuhnlein opined that the 
November 4, 2022 crush injury served to light up and materially aggravate Mark’s 
previously asymptomatic left shoulder arthritis changes.  (Cl. Ex. 1, p. 13) I find Dr. 
Kuhnlein’s rationale that he set forth in his report to support his opinion to be 
persuasive.  I find his opinion to be more persuasive and to carry greater weight than 
the opinions of Dr. Ash and Dr. Chen.  Therefore, I find that Mark has carried his burden 
of proof to demonstrate by a preponderance of the evidence that he sustained 
permanent disability as the result of the injury he sustained to his left shoulder on 
November 4, 2022. 
I further find Dr. Kuhnlein’s opinion regarding permanent partial disability carries 
greater weight than Dr. Ash’s or Dr. Chen’s opinions.  As noted above, under the Fifth 
Edition of the Guides, Dr. Kuhnlein assigned 20 percent permanent functional 
impairment of the left upper extremity for deficits in range of motion and motor deficits.  
He also assigned 1 percent whole person impairment for the trapezius muscle; 
however, I found this 1 percent was not based solely on the Guides.  Therefore, I find 
that Mark has demonstrated by a preponderance of the evidence that he sustained 20

percent permanent functional impairment of the left upper extremity as the result of the

Next, we turn to the left arm.  There are no medical opinions that causally

demonstrate that he sustained an injury to his left upper extremity as the result of the

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The party who would suffer loss if an issue were not established ordinarily has 
the burden of proving that issue by a preponderance of the evidence.  Iowa R. App. P. 
6.904(3)(e). 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148 (Iowa 

CARLSON V. EMERSON AUTOMATION SOLUTIONS 
Page 13 
 
1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154 (Iowa App. 1997); Sanchez v. 
Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283 (Iowa App. 1996).   
The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony.  The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability.  
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question.  The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances.  The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part.  St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410 (Iowa 2001); 
Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845 (Iowa 1995).  Miller v. 
Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417 (Iowa 1994).  Unrebutted expert medical 
testimony cannot be summarily rejected.  Poula v. Siouxland Wall & Ceiling, Inc., 516 
N.W.2d 910 (Iowa App. 1994). The well-established law is clear, however, that an 
expert's opinion is not necessarily binding when it is based on an incomplete or 
inaccurate history. Dunlavey v. Economy Fire & Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995) (citing Bodish v. Fischer. Inc., 133 N.W.2d 867, 870 (1965)). 
 
Based on the above findings of fact, I conclude that claimant has carried his 
burden of proof to demonstrate by a preponderance of the evidence that he sustained 
permanent disability as the result of the injury he sustained to his left shoulder on 
November 4, 2022. 
 
Under the Iowa Workers' Compensation Act, permanent partial disability is 
compensated either for a loss or loss of use of a scheduled member under Iowa Code 
section 85.34(2)(a)-(u) or as an unscheduled injury pursuant to the provisions of section 
85.34(2)(v). An injury to the shoulder is considered a scheduled member injury under 
Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(n). The extent of scheduled member disability benefits to 
which an injured worker is entitled is determined by using the functional method. 
Functional disability is “limited to the loss of the physiological capacity of the body or 
body part.” Mortimer v. Fruehauf Corp., 502 N.W.2d 12, 15 (Iowa 1993); Sherman v. 
Pella Corp., 576 N.W.2d 312 (Iowa 1998). 
          Iowa Code section 85.34(x) permanent disabilities states:

not loss of earning capacity, the extent of loss or percentage of permanent

evaluation of permanent impairment, published by the American medical 
association, as adopted by the workers' compensation commissioner by 
rule pursuant to chapter 17A. Lay testimony or agency expertise shall not 
be utilized in determining loss or percentage of permanent impairment 
pursuant to paragraphs “a” through “u”, or paragraph “v” when determining 
functional disability and not loss of earning capacity.

Claimant is seeking reimbursement for the IME conducted by Dr. Kuhnlein.  Iowa 
Code section 85.39 permits an employee to be reimbursed for subsequent examination 
by a physician of the employee's choice where an employer-retained physician has 
previously evaluated “permanent disability” and the employee believes that the initial 
evaluation is too low.   Iowa Code § 85.39(2) (2023).  The section also permits 
reimbursement for reasonably necessary transportation expenses incurred and for any 
wage loss occasioned by the employee attending the subsequent examination.  Iowa 
Code § 85.39 (1), (2).   
The Iowa Supreme Court has provided a literal interpretation of the plain 
language of Iowa Code section 85.39, stating that section only allows the employee to 
obtain an independent medical evaluation at the employer’s expense if an evaluation of 
permanent disability has already been made by an employer retained physician.  Des 
Moines Area Reg’l Transit Auth. v. Young, 867 N.W.2d 839, 847 (Iowa 2015). Under 
the Young decision, an employee can only obtain an IME at the employer's expense if 
an evaluation of permanent disability has been made by an employer-retained 
physician. Id. 
 
Defendants dispute claimant’s entitlement to reimbursement, but the basis for the
