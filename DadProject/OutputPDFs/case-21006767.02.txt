STATEMENT OF THE CASE 
 
Immanuel “Manny” Hoppe, claimant, filed a petition in arbitration seeking 
workers’ compensation benefits against Kraft Heinz Company and Indemnity Insurance 
Company of North America.  
 
In accordance with agency scheduling procedures, a hearing occurred on July 
12, 2024, via Zoom.  The case was considered fully submitted on September 6, 2024, 
upon the filing of briefs.

The evidentiary record consists of Joint Exhibits 1 through 13, Claimant’s 
Exhibits 1 through 19, and Defendants’ Exhibits A through L.  Claimant testified on his 
own behalf.  Rachel Ball testified on behalf of defendants.

CONCLUSIONS OF LAW 
The claimant has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that 
the injury is a proximate cause of the disability on which the claim is based.  A cause is 
proximate if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the result; it need not be the only 
cause.  A preponderance of the evidence exists when the causal connection is probable 
rather than merely possible.  George A. Hormel & Co. v. Jordan, 569 N.W.2d 148, 153 
(Iowa 1997); Frye v. Smith-Doyle Contractors, 569 N.W.2d 154, 156 (Iowa Ct. App. 
1997); Sanchez v. Blue Bird Midwest, 554 N.W.2d 283, 285 (Iowa Ct. App. 1996). 

HOPPE V. KRAFT HEINZ COMPANY

The question of causal connection is essentially within the domain of expert 
testimony. The expert medical evidence must be considered with all other evidence 
introduced bearing on the causal connection between the injury and the disability. 
Supportive lay testimony may be used to buttress the expert testimony and, therefore, is 
also relevant and material to the causation question. The weight to be given to an 
expert opinion is determined by the finder of fact and may be affected by the accuracy 
of the facts the expert relied upon as well as other surrounding circumstances. The 
expert opinion may be accepted or rejected, in whole or in part. St. Luke’s Hosp. v. 
Gray, 604 N.W.2d 646, 652 (Iowa 2000); IBP, Inc. v. Harpole, 621 N.W.2d 410, 420  
(Iowa 2001); Dunlavey v. Economy Fire and Cas. Co., 526 N.W.2d 845, 853 (Iowa 
1995); Miller v. Lauridsen Foods, Inc., 525 N.W.2d 417, 421 (Iowa 1994). 
While a claimant is not entitled to compensation for the results of a preexisting 
injury or disease, its mere existence at the time of a subsequent injury is not a defense. 
Rose v. John Deere Ottumwa Works, 247 Iowa 900, 76 N.W.2d 756, 760-61 (1956). If 
the claimant had a preexisting condition or disability that is materially aggravated, 
accelerated, worsened or lighted-up so that it results in disability, claimant is entitled to 
recover. Nicks v. Davenport Produce Co., 254 Iowa 130, 115 N.W.2d 812, 815 (1962); 
Yeager v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co., 253 Iowa 369, 112 N.W.2d 299, 302 (1961). 
It is well-established in workers' compensation that “if a claimant had a 
preexisting condition or disability, aggravated, accelerated, worsened, or “lighted up' by 
an injury which arose out of and in the course of employment resulting in a disability 
found to exist,” the claimant is entitled to compensation. Iowa Dep't of Transp. v. Van 
Cannon, 459 N.W.2d 900, 904 (Iowa 1990). 
Claimant’s pre-existing arthritis was not causing him problems prior to the 
January 28, 2021, work injury.  Claimant sustained an acute injury on January 28, 2021, 
which caused pain in the right shoulder and resulted in arthroscopic surgery.  His pain 
did not resolve after the injury or arthroscopic surgery and, ultimately, he underwent a 
total shoulder replacement.  In this case, I found the medical opinions of Dr. Bansal and 
Dr. Munns to be the most convincing and credible evidence in the record as to 
causation for the right total shoulder replacement.  As such, I find that the work injury 
materially and substantially aggravated claimant's preexisting condition to the point 
where he required a right total shoulder replacement. 
With respect to permanent disability, I found Dr. Bansal’s impairment ratings to 
be reasonable and reliable.  Under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(n), compensation for the 
loss of a shoulder is based on a percentage of 400 weeks of benefits.  Permanent 
partial disability benefits for 29 percent right upper extremity impairment is equal to 116 
weeks of benefits.   
Compensation for permanent disability shall begin when it is medically indicated 
that MMI from the injury has been achieved and the extent of loss or percentage of 
permanent impairment can be determined by using the Guides. Iowa Code section 
85.34(2). Based on the opinions of Dr. Munns and Dr. Bansal, I conclude the 
appropriate date for commencement of permanent partial disability benefits is March 2, 
2024.

Claimant asserts the reverse total shoulder replacement affects structures 
outside of the glenohumeral joint and shoulder area. Specifically, claimant asserts the 
right total shoulder replacement involves anatomic structures outside of, and beyond, 
those considered in Chavez v. MS Technology, L.L.C., 972 N.W.2d 662 (Iowa 2022). 
Therefore, claimant asserts his injury should convert from a right shoulder injury 
compensated as a scheduled member injury into a body as a whole, unscheduled injury 
compensated with industrial disability benefits pursuant to Iowa Code section 
85.34(2)(v). 
In 2017 the Iowa Legislature amended Iowa Code section 85.34. Before the 2017 
changes, shoulder injuries were considered proximal to the arm and compensated as a 
body as a whole injury, under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(u). Prior to the 2017 
changes, a shoulder injury was compensated as an unscheduled injury, and based on 
industrial disability. See Alm v. Morris Barick Cattle Co., 240 Iowa 1174, 38 N.W.2d 161 
(1949). Through the 2017 legislative changes, the legislature added the shoulder to the 
list of scheduled members. Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(n) states: “[f]or the loss of a 
shoulder, weekly compensation during four hundred weeks.” Iowa Code section 
85.34(2)(n). This amendment went into effect on July 1, 2018. The legislature did not 
define the term “shoulder.” 
The Iowa Supreme Court in Chavez v. MS Tech. LLC, 972 N.W.2d 662 (Iowa 
2022), and Deng v. Farmland Foods, Inc., 972 N.W.2d 727 (Iowa 2022), affirmed that 
the shoulder was construed in a functional sense to include the glenohumeral joint as 
well as all of the muscles, tendons, and ligaments that were essential for the shoulder to 
function. The Iowa Supreme Court acknowledged that the rotator cuff tendons that were 
injured and repaired in those cases attach proximally to the glenohumeral joint. 
However, the fact that those tendons attached proximally to the joint was not the 
controlling factor in determining if an injury is considered a “shoulder” injury.  Instead, 
the Iowa Supreme Court fashioned a “functionality” test to determine whether an injury 
is confined to the shoulder or extends beyond the shoulder. The Court held that 
“Defining ‘shoulder’ in the functional sense under section 85.34(2)(n) best achieves the 
statute's purpose.” Id. at 669. 
In Ojeda v. Sivyer Holdings, File Nos. 20008020.01 and 21005432.01 (App. July 
8, 2024), this agency addressed Hoppe’s argument and found a total shoulder 
arthroplasty is to be compensated as a scheduled member under Iowa Code section 
85.34(2)(n).  I find no reason to change the outcome for a reverse total shoulder 
arthroplasty as both procedures involve surgical alteration of the glenoid. 
In the present case, claimant failed to demonstrate by a preponderance of the 
evidence that his January 28, 2021, injury extends beyond the right shoulder into the 
body as a whole.  Considering and applying the Iowa Supreme Court's analysis and 
functionality test, I find that the mere fact that a total shoulder replacement affects 
structures outside of the glenohumeral joint is not controlling in defining whether the 
injury is a shoulder injury.

odd-lot claim is only applicable in unscheduled, industrial disability claims. Cook v.

Newton Correctional Facility, File No. 5064180 (App. September 11, 2020). In fact, 
when the Iowa Supreme Court adopted the odd-lot doctrine, it did so because the

Claimant sustained a prior work injury in 2018 to his right bicep.  The defendants

injury to the right arm pursuant to Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(m).  The instant case 
involves a shoulder injury under Iowa Code section 85.34(2)(n).  Dr. Bansal’s

stemming from the 2018 injury, but only holds defendants liable for new permanent 
partial disability for the January 28, 2021, work injury.  I find defendants are not entitled 
to apportionment for the 2018 right arm injury. 
Claimant is seeking healing period benefits from June 15, 2023, through March 
1, 2024.   
Healing period compensation describes temporary workers' compensation 
weekly benefits that precede an allowance of permanent partial disability benefits. 
Ellingson v. Fleetguard, Inc., 599 N.W.2d 440 (Iowa 1999). Section 85.34(1) provides 
that healing period benefits are payable to an injured worker who has suffered 
permanent partial disability until the first to occur of three events. These are: (1) the 
worker has returned to work; (2) the worker medically is capable of returning to 
substantially similar employment; or (3) the worker has achieved maximum medical 
recovery. Maximum medical recovery is achieved when healing is complete and the 
extent of permanent disability can be determined. Armstrong Tire & Rubber Co. v. Kubli, 
(Iowa Ct. App.) 312 N.W.2d 60 (1981). Neither maintenance medical care nor an 
employee's continuing to have pain or other symptoms necessarily prolongs the healing 
period. 
The parties stipulate that claimant was off work during this period of time.  
Claimant underwent a right total shoulder replacement procedure on June 15, 2023.  I 
found the need for this procedure was causally related to the January 28, 2021, work 
injury.  Dr. Munns and Dr. Bansal opined that claimant reached MMI on or about March 
1, 2024.  Claimant has carried his burden of proving his entitlement to healing period 
benefits from June 15, 2023, through March 1, 2024. 
The parties stipulated that defendants are entitled to a credit under Iowa Code 
section 85.38(2) for short-term disability benefits paid between June 15, 2023, and 
December 22, 2023. (Hearing Report)

Claimant also seeks an award of past medical expenses related to treatment he 
obtained for the January 28, 2021, work injury.   
The employer shall furnish reasonable surgical, medical, dental, osteopathic, 
chiropractic, podiatric, physical rehabilitation, nursing, ambulance, and hospital services 
and supplies for all conditions compensable under the workers' compensation law. The 
employer shall also allow reasonable and necessary transportation expenses incurred 
for those services. The employer has the right to choose the provider of care, except 
where the employer has denied liability for the injury. Section 85.27. Holbert v. 
Townsend Engineering Co., Thirty-second Biennial Report of the Industrial 
Commissioner 78 (Review-Reopening October 1975). 
The claimed medical expenses in Exhibit 14 appear to be for medical services 
provided to claimant by ORA Orthopedics. Defendants denied liability and declined to 
authorize medical care as of March 7, 2023.  Having found the January 28, 2021, work 
injury and subsequent medical treatment materially aggravated claimant’s arthritis and 
accelerated the need for a total shoulder replacement, and that the medical expenses 
for treatment of the same were reasonable and necessary, I conclude that claimant has 
proven entitlement to satisfaction of his medical expenses contained in Exhibit 14. 
Next, claimant seeks an order for alternate medical care.  First, he seeks ongoing 
treatment of his right shoulder with Dr. Munns. 
By challenging the employer's choice of treatment - and seeking alternate care - 
claimant assumes the burden of proving the authorized care is unreasonable. See Long 
v. Roberts Dairy Co., 528 N.W.2d 122, 123 (Iowa 1995). Determining what care is 
reasonable under the statute is a question of fact. Id. The employer's obligation turns on 
the question of reasonable necessity, not desirability. Id.; Harned v. Farmland Foods. 
Inc., 331 N.W.2d 98, 101 (Iowa 1983). 
An employer's statutory right is to select the providers of care and the employer 
may consider cost and other pertinent factors when exercising its choice. Long, at 124. 
An employer (typically) is not a licensed health care provider and does not possess 
medical expertise. Accordingly, an employer does not have the right to control the 
methods the providers choose to evaluate, diagnose and treat the injured employee. An 
employer is not entitled to control a licensed health care provider's exercise of 
professional judgment. Assman v. Blue Star Foods, File No. 866389 (Declaratory 
Ruling, May 19, 1988). 
In this instance, defendants disputed liability for claimant’s ongoing right shoulder 
symptoms and need for the total right shoulder replacement.  “The statute contemplates 
that an injured employee may select his or her own medical care when the employer 
abandons the injured employee through the denial of compensability of the injury.” Bell 
Bros. Heating & Air Conditioning v. Gwinn, 779 N.W.2d 193, 204 (Iowa 2010).  Given 
defendants’ denial of liability for the ongoing right shoulder symptoms and shoulder 
replacement recommendations, I conclude that the defendants have no right to assert 
the authorization defense or challenge claimant’s assertion of a claim for alternate 
medical care.

Second, claimant requests that the undersigned order defendants to authorize 
the additional hyperbaric therapy treatments recommended by Dr. Hartsuch.  Outside of 
claimant’s testimony, there is little evidence that the hyperbaric therapy treatments have 
been reasonable and necessary.  Along the same lines, there is little evidence in the 
record to establish that the treatments have objectively improved claimant’s condition.   
Luxury-type items and treatments are viewed with skepticism, particularly when 
the treatment was first the idea of the patient. See Stephenson v. Furnas Electric Co., 
522 N.W.2d 828 (Iowa 1994). The only physicians to explore the necessity of the 
hyperbaric treatment are Dr. Hartsuch and Dr. Bollier. 
The fact that claimant has reportedly received relief due to the hyperbaric 
treatments is fortunate to both the claimant personally, and likely to the defendants as 
well.  However, as discussed by Dr. Bollier, there is no definitive evidence in the 
orthopedic shoulder research literature that hyperbaric treatment significantly improves 
shoulder pain.  In addition, although Dr. Hartsuch is now recommending ongoing 
hyperbaric treatment, claimant first sought out the hyperbaric treatment on his own.  
Without a recommendation for hyperbaric therapy treatments from one of claimant’s 
treating physicians, I decline the opportunity to order this additional care.
