Listen up, buddy. There’s a tower up the hill, stone-cold, and a bell hanging there like a busted green moon. You take the rope—rough as penance—and you yank. Click. Rasp. That sound rolls down the rock like a slow train, leaves iron on your tongue.

Thing is, you dropped it once. Old man’s line slipped through your hands. Ever since, the tower keeps score. Get the pull wrong—too high, too tight, wrong pitch—and you come apart. Knees buckle. Or you wake up swinging. The other boys learn to grab you quick. Learn to keep their fingers clear. Then you laugh—sharp, kid-laugh—and your eyes stay shut like a bar sign with the power cut.

And when the noise dies, you sit under the big bell with a little book on the stone. No blaming. Just tally marks. Wind. Footsteps. Who climbed the stairs. Scratch-scratch, like a bug in the wall. You write like ink can make it mean something. But all you ever get back is the next hour, waiting.
