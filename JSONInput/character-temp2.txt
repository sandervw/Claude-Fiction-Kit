The rope remembers. It hangs in the bell tower like a vein drained of purpose, grey-brown and fraying where his hands caught it, where they failed, where the fibers bit into his palms and then simply—gave. The bell above it breathes with the wind, a low brass murmur that hums against his teeth when he stands too close.
He rings the hours. His hands move with the jerky precision of someone who has forgotten why but cannot stop, the motion worn into muscle like a groove in stone. His face has gone somewhere else—not blank, but vacated, a room someone left in haste with the candle still burning. The other monks look past him now. There is nothing behind his eyes to meet.
But the laugh. It comes without warning: high and thin as a child's, cutting through the bronze drone. It does not touch his mouth. It simply escapes, then dies, and he continues pulling as though the sound belonged to someone else entirely.
He keeps a journal beneath the largest bell, where the metal's shadow pools like something spilled. He writes what the wind did that day. Who stood where. The angle of the sun on the wet stones. He does not know what he is building—only that his hands must move, must pull, must hold onto something, even if it is only questions that lead nowhere, even if the rope gives again and again in his dreams.
