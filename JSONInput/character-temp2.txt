The bell hangs in the tower like a tarnished moon, greened at the seams, and when the apprentice comes to it the metal seems to listen. He wraps both hands around the rope—hemp rough as a hairshirt—and pulls; fibers rasp, the pulley clicks, and the sound blooms downward through stone in a slow shudder that leaves the air tasting of iron and dust. Light slips in by narrow slits, skates across the bell’s lip, and dies, leaving his face not calm but erased—like a doorway that opens onto a wall—while his beard knots itself into a wild record of neglect.

He once caught his master’s falling rope and could not hold it. The tower remembers. At certain heights, at certain tensions, at the wrong pitch, his body unthreads and drops, or snaps awake into violence; brothers learn to catch him, and learn, too, to keep their hands away. Then, without warning, a laugh cracks out—high, sharp, almost childlike—and it never reaches his eyes.

Still, beneath the largest bell, he keeps a journal pressed to cold stone. He does not accuse. He worries the facts with insect-care: thumb on a fray, ear to a draft, questions about wind and footsteps and who was in the tower, the quill scratching like a small claw. He writes as though ink might prove the fall meant something, but the only reply is the next hour, waiting.
