The Swaying Interrogation — During a violent windstorm, the apprentice corners a monk who was near the tower that day. The fortress lurches, chains groaning, and he has to choose between pressing for answers or bracing the man before they both slide toward the edge.
The Wrong Note — A bell cracks mid-ring, hitting a pitch identical to the one that sounded when his master fell. He collapses in the tower. When he wakes, he's alone, and someone has taken three pages from his hidden journal.
Anchor Inspection — He volunteers to descend the chains to check the crumbling anchors—the one job no monk wants. Down in the poison-thick air, rope in hand, he finds tool marks on the stone that shouldn't be there.
The Laughing Funeral — At a memorial for another fallen brother, that high, childlike laugh escapes him at the worst possible moment. The silence afterward is a character in itself.
Night Visitor — A monk comes to his cell after midnight, claiming to have seen something the day his master died. Before the man can finish, the apprentice's startle reflex kicks in—and now he has an unconscious witness and a decision to make.
The Rope Market — A supply caravan arrives via bridge-chains from a neighboring fortress. He examines their rope stock obsessively, comparing fibers to the frayed ends he's kept hidden. The merchant notices.
Below the Clouds — The poison fog clears for the first time in years, revealing the choked world below: ruins, movement, lights. The monks gather to stare. He's the only one who doesn't look down.
The Blank Mirror — The abbot requests a private meeting and gently suggests the apprentice has become "unsettling to the brothers." He catches his reflection in a polished chalice and genuinely doesn't recognize his own face.
The Second Fall — Another monk falls from a high place—but survives, clinging to a window ledge. The apprentice is first on scene. Saving him means grabbing rope, feeling the weight, holding on. The flashback hits mid-rescue.
The Bell's Confession — Alone in the tower at the hour of his master's death, he speaks to the bells as if they witnessed it. One of them—the largest, the one his journal hides beneath—rings once. There's no wind.
